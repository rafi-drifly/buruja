Caption

Surat At-Tariq opens like a knock at the door in the dead of night, and turns that knock into an argument you can't sit still under.

Sheikh Abu Bakr Zoud draws it out: at-Tariq is the one who arrives at night and knocks, and Allah names it the piercing star (an-najm ath-thaqib), light that bores a hole through the dark curtain of the night to reach your eye. Why swear by it? Because the answer to the oath is that no soul is without a guardian set over it: angels who shield you from undecreed harm and scribes who record even a blink, appointed since the womb. The surah closes on a decisive word that is not amusement, and a promise that while they plot, Allah plots, and gives them only a little while.

At the heart of the surah Allah turns you to your own creation as the proof: the One who drew a living child out of a lifeless drop, from between the backbone and the ribs, can just as easily bring you back. And on the Day the secrets are put on trial, no soul keeps any power of its own or any helper.
