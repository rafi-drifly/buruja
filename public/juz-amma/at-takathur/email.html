The counting that does not stop

Preview: At-Takathur catches you mid-count, adding up a world you will leave. Here is what the Sheikh says it is really asking.

Assalamu alaikum,

Surat At-Takathur opens by catching you doing something you did not even notice: counting. Adding up what you have, measuring it against the next person, quietly keeping score.

Sheikh Abu Bakr Zoud sets the scene with two Makkan tribes who boasted to one another over wealth and men and horses, and could not stop, until the counting ran out of the living and they walked into the graveyard to count their dead. The surah names that whole frenzy in one word: at-takathur, the rivalry for more.

He is careful to stop us misreading it. The crime is not the wealth. The crime is the wealth that distracts. A pocket full of money is not the problem; a heart dragged off by it is. Dawud and Sulayman, peace be upon them, held kingdoms and armies and were not pulled away from their prayer for a moment.

Then comes a verb worth pausing on. The counting goes on, the surah says, until you visit the graves. Not until you are buried, the Sheikh notes, but until you visit. A visit is short. You do not move into a house you are only visiting. Your time in the grave is exactly that, brief, and you are coming back out of it. So the cure for all the counting is to visit the graves now, while you still can, and feel what waits.

And the surah ends on the question that follows you home: you will be asked, that Day, about the blessing. The Sheikh lets it land through the famine in Madinah, when the Prophet ﷺ ate cold water, fresh dates, and meat at a generous man's home and wept, saying this very comfort is from the blessing you will be asked about. Three things on a table, and they were a matter for tears.

The more you reach for, the more you stand answering for. Take only what you need, and prepare the answer now.
