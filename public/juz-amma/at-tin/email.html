The surah that swears by your dinner

Preview: At-Tin opens on a fig, an olive, and two mountains. Here is what all of it was swearing toward.

Assalamu alaikum,

Surat At-Tin opens in a strange place for a chapter about your own worth: a plate of food and a map. A fig. An olive. Mount Sinai. The city of Makkah. Allah swears by all four.

Sheikh Abu Bakr Zoud says to hold a question in your chest as you read, because an oath in the Qur'an always swears toward a point. The fig and the olive are blessed foods, and also a nod to holy lands, Jerusalem, the mountain where Nuh's ark rested. Mount Sinai is where Allah spoke to Musa, told him to remove his sandals, and unveiled Himself until the mountain shattered. Makkah is the secure city, where even old enemies would not lift a hand, because the place itself disarms the violence in a person.

And then the oath lands on its point, and the point is you: 'We have certainly created man in the best of stature.' The Sheikh draws out the double emphasis at the front of the verse, the kind you only use when people refuse to believe a thing. You were made beautiful outside and balanced within, fashioned exactly for your purpose. Which is why worship is never beyond you. You were shaped to fit it.

But the very next verse is a drop: 'Then We return him to the lowest of the low.' You were placed at the top of creation, and you carry, every day, the freedom to climb or to fall. The ones never brought low, Allah says, are the ones who believe and do righteous deeds, the inside and the outside of the same engine, kept together.

Then the surah turns and asks, after all of this, what still makes you deny? The Sheikh's answer is uncomfortable: most who mock the religion are not confused by it. They simply do not want to face that they are choosing wrong, and tearing it down is easier on the conscience than admitting it.

May Allah keep us from debasing what He raised.
