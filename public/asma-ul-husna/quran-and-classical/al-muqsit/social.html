Al-Muqsit.

The Equitable. The One who does justice.

This name is about the One who does.

One honest note first.

Al-Muqsit, in that exact name-form, does not appear in the Qur'an. It comes from the scholars' lists of the 99 names.

But the root q-s-t (equity) is all over the Book. We keep that honest throughout.

How is it different from Al-Adl?

Al-Adl = justice as His very nature.
Al-Muqsit = justice as an act, the doing of equity between parties.

Al-Adl is the justice He is. Al-Muqsit is the justice He does.
03 / 09
Where the root lands on Allah:

'...maintaining [creation] in justice (qa'iman bil-qist).' (3:18)

Al-Sa'di: He has never ceased to be described by equity, in all He commands, forbids, creates and decrees.
04 / 09
Then He makes it a command:

'Say, my Lord has ordered equity (amara rabbi bil-qist).' (7:29)

Before a single rule of prayer or trade, the headline is equity itself. The God who does justice wants it done.
05 / 09
And He says, three separate times, that He loves the ones who do it:

'Indeed, Allah loves those who act justly.' (5:42, 49:9, 60:8)

The judge who will not be bought. The parent who plays no favourites. The witness who tells the truth against himself.
06 / 09
But settle fairly, not just quietly.

Al-Sa'di on 49:9: a peace built on wronging one side is NOT the reconciliation Allah commanded.

Equity is not the absence of conflict. It is the presence of fairness inside the resolution. 🤍

Ibn Kathir, from Sahih Muslim: the just will be on pulpits of light at the right of the Most Merciful, those fair in their judgement, with their families, and in what they governed.

(A hadith of the Prophet ﷺ, not a verse.)

Ya Muqsit.
09 / 09
Caption

There is a wrong that does not just need a verdict, it needs someone to step in and settle it. Two people locked against each other, a thing taken and never returned, and no one fair enough to make it right between them. Most of the time on this earth, no one comes.

Al-Muqsit, the Equitable, is the answer the Qur'an gives. One honest note: the name in that exact form does not appear in the Qur'an; it comes from the scholars' lists of the ninety-nine names. But the root q-s-t (equity) is all over the Book, and we keep that distinction honest throughout. Grounded in classical tafsir (Ibn Kathir, al-Sa'di), this name differs from its sister Al-Adl in a beautiful way. Al-Adl is justice as His nature. Al-Muqsit is justice as an act: the One who maintains it (qa'iman bil-qist, 3:18), orders it (7:29), settles between parties, and loves, three separate times, the people who do equity (5:42, 49:9, 60:8).

And He wants it done right. On 49:9, al-Sa'di warns that a peace built on wronging one side is not the reconciliation Allah commanded. Equity is not the absence of conflict, it is the presence of fairness inside the resolution. The reward is one of the most striking promises in the books: Ibn Kathir carries from Sahih Muslim that the just will be on pulpits of light at the right of the Most Merciful, those fair in their judgement, with their families, and in what they governed. (A hadith of the Prophet ﷺ, not a verse.)

So lay the unsettled case down. The One who settles it cannot be bought and never tilts the scale by a hair.

What wrong have you been carrying that no one ever stepped in to fix? The settlement is coming. 🤍
