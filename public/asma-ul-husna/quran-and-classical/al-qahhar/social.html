Al-Qahhar.

The Subduer. The One who prevails over all.

When life feels out of control, we reach for a quiet comfort: it is in bigger hands now. A power nothing can overrule.

That instinct has a name, and the Qur'an puts it on Allah.

Here is something striking.

The name al-Qahhar appears six times in the Qur'an.

All six times, it is joined to another name: al-Wahid, the One.

You never read it alone. Always al-Wahid al-Qahhar, the One, the Subduer.
02 / 09
That pairing is not decoration. It is an argument.

Al-Sa'di: oneness and overpowering require each other. There can never be two powers that each prevail absolutely, because each could check the other.

Total power can only belong to One.

The argument that reframes every fear:

Look at anything that overpowers, al-Sa'di says, and you'll find something above it that overpowers it in turn. The chain has to end somewhere.

It ends at al-Wahid al-Qahhar. 🤍

So sit with this.

Everything you are afraid of is itself afraid of something.

05 / 09
Of all places, hold on to where Yusuf says this name.

He is in prison. Betrayed, forgotten, powerless over his own life.

And the name on his tongue is al-Wahid al-Qahhar (12:39).

He trusted the Subduer while everything said otherwise, and was proved right.
06 / 09
Then the Day comes when every power drops its mask.

The earth is changed, and all of creation steps out barizun, laid bare, nothing to cover or hide them (14:48, 40:16).

Title, wealth, reputation, gone. The tyrant and the beggar in the same naked light.
07 / 09
Ibn Kathir relates that Allah rolls up the heavens and earth and asks:

Where are the kings of the earth? Where are the tyrants?

Then, when no one remains but Him: 'To whom belongs the kingdom today?'

And He answers Himself: 'To Allah, al-Wahid al-Qahhar.'
08 / 09
The name cuts two ways, and you choose which edge you feel.

To the one who fights Allah: the wall he breaks against.
To the one who runs to Allah: the wall nothing breaks through to reach him.

Same power. Where you stand is everything.

Caption

Al-Qahhar, the Subduer, the One who prevails over all. Grounded in classical tafsir (Ibn Kathir and al-Sa'di), this name is the answer the Qur'an gives to a fear we all carry: that our lives are at the mercy of powers we cannot control.

Notice something. The name appears six times in the Qur'an, and all six times it is joined to al-Wahid, the One: al-Wahid al-Qahhar. As al-Sa'di explains, that pairing is an argument. Oneness and overpowering require each other, because two beings could never each prevail absolutely. Total, unanswerable power can belong only to One.

And here is the thought that reframes every fear: look at anything that overpowers, al-Sa'di says, and you will find something above it that overpowers it in turn. The chain has to end somewhere, in a Subduer who is subdued by nothing. Everything you dread is itself afraid of something. The power over you is being held by something larger, all the way up, until you reach the One above the whole ladder, the One you pray to.

Yusuf preached this name from a prison floor, powerless over his own life, and was proved right (12:39). And on the Day every creature stands barizun, laid bare with nothing to hide them, Ibn Kathir relates that Allah asks, to whom belongs the kingdom today, and answers Himself: to Allah, al-Wahid al-Qahhar (40:16). Every crown that was ever worn will be on the floor.

The same power that humbles the tyrant is the one that shelters the believer. Where you stand in relation to it is everything.

What power in your life feels too big right now? It is a rung on a ladder, and Someone stands above it. 🤍
