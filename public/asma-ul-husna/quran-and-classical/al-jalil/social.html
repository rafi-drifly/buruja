Al-Jalil.

The Majestic.

We spend our lives feeling slightly too big: too sure of our opinions, our plans, our offence at being crossed.

This name cuts the ego back down to its real size. And it goes infinitely further than any night sky.
01 / 09
One honest note first.

The definite form al-Jalil does not actually appear in the Qur'an.

What the Qur'an gives, twice, is Dhul-Jalal, the Owner of Majesty (55:27, 55:78). The scholars draw the name from there. So we ground it where He grounds it: in His jalal.
02 / 09
What is jalal?

From the root j-l-l: greatness, grandeur, awe.

Ibn Abbas glossed it in two words: the Owner of greatness and grandeur.

It is the majesty of a King before whom you simply do not slouch.
03 / 09
How big is it?

Al-Sa'di: before His greatness the mightiest created things have dwindled, and the seven heavens and all in them have become small before His grandeur.

Not small like a person beside a mountain. Small like the whole night sky beside the One who holds it.
04 / 09
Everything bows. Everything shrinks.

Ibn Kathir on 6:18: before the greatness of His jalal, all things have humbled themselves, and they grow still and shrink small between His hands.

The arrogant and the humble are the same size here.
05 / 09
The Day jalal becomes visible:

'...and all voices will be stilled before the Most Merciful, so you will not hear except a whisper.' (20:108)

Al-Sa'di: the kings and the common people, silent together, eyes lowered, on their knees.
06 / 09
And this awe is not meant to paralyse you. It is meant to free you from sin.

'But as for he who feared the standing before his Lord and prevented the soul from desire, then indeed, Paradise will be his refuge.' (79:40-41)

Sin always needs a small God. Al-Jalil makes Him large again.
07 / 09
The Sunnah hands you His own name to cling to.

After every prayer the Prophet ﷺ would say:

'...blessed are You, Ya Dhal-Jalali wal-Ikram.' O Owner of Majesty and Honour.

Call the Majestic by His majesty.
08 / 09
The ego is exhausting. The constant work of being big, being right, never bowing.

Al-Jalil offers to take it off you.

Get the size of God right, and you no longer have to be big. You get to be small, and held, and quiet. 🤍

Caption

We spend our lives feeling slightly too big, and Al-Jalil, the Majestic, is the name that returns us to our true size.

One honest note: the definite form al-Jalil does not appear in the Qur'an. What the Qur'an gives, twice in Surah Ar-Rahman, is Dhul-Jalal, the Owner of Majesty (55:27, 55:78), and the scholars draw the name from there. So we ground it in His jalal: greatness, grandeur, awe.

Grounded in classical tafsir (Ibn Kathir, al-Sa'di), here is what that majesty does. Al-Sa'di writes that before His greatness the mightiest created things dwindle, and the seven heavens and all in them become small before His grandeur. Ibn Kathir, on 'He is the subjugator over His servants' (6:18), says all things humble themselves and shrink small between His hands. On the Day of Standing, the Qur'an says, every voice falls to a whisper before Him (20:108), and al-Sa'di pictures the kings silent beside the common people, all on their knees.

And this awe is not meant to crush you. It is the engine of taqwa: 'he who feared the standing before his Lord, and prevented the soul from desire' wins Paradise (79:40-41). Sin always needs a small God to happen. Al-Jalil makes Him large again, and the temptation shrinks to the small thing it always was.

The ego is exhausting. Get the size of God right, and you finally get to put it down. You no longer have to be big. You get to be small, and held, and quiet, before the only One whose majesty is real. 🤍
