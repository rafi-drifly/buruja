Caption

Look around and notice how much you call mine: the home, the savings, the name you built, even the body you are reading this with. Now ask the question we spend our lives avoiding: where does all of it go when you let go? Not if. When.

Al-Warith, the Inheritor of all, is the Qur'an's answer, and it is gentler than the question sounds. Grounded in classical tafsir (Ibn Kathir, al-Sa'di, al-Muyassar), this name means the One to whom everything returns when every owner dies. As Allah says, 'it is We who will inherit the earth and whoever is on it' (19:40). The land and the landlord both revert to Him. We were never owners, only stewards holding things in trust for a while.

Ibn Kathir explains that the whole of creation perishes while Allah remains, with no one left to claim any ownership at all, for He is the Inheritor of all His creation. And al-Sa'di draws out the freedom in it: since everything in your hands is leaving you anyway and returning to Allah, there is no sense clutching it. So give while it is still yours.

Then the name turns tender. Zakariyya, old and childless and afraid of the silence after him, prayed, 'My Lord, do not leave me alone, while You are the best of inheritors' (21:89). He handed his deepest fear to the One who outlasts everything, trusting Him to be a better guardian of all he loved than he could be.

And the most hopeful turn: to the believer, Allah hands an inheritance that never changes hands again, a home in Firdaus, forever (23:10-11).

What are you clutching that was only ever borrowed? Open your hands. 🤍
