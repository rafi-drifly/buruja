Everything you call 'mine' is borrowed

Preview: Al-Warith, the Inheritor of all. Loosen your grip on what was never yours to keep, and take the comfort Zakariyya leaned on.

Assalamu alaikum,

Look around the room you are in and notice how much of it you call mine. The phone in your hand, the keys, the home, the savings, even the body you are reading this with. Now ask a question we spend most of our lives avoiding: where does all of it go when you let go of it? Not if you let go. When.

One of the names of Allah is the answer, and it is far gentler than the question sounds: Al-Warith, the Inheritor of all. The One to whom everything returns when every owner dies. Every fortune ever counted, every acre ever fenced, every body ever buried, all of it reverts to Him, because He is the One who remains after the holders are gone.

The Qur'an gives you this name in a single sentence: 'Indeed, it is We who will inherit the earth and whoever is on it, and to Us they will be returned' (Maryam 19:40). Not just the earth, the soil people fight over the deeds to. Whoever is on it. The owners themselves are part of the inheritance. Commenting on this verse, Ibn Kathir explains that the whole of creation perishes while Allah remains, with no one left to claim any ownership or control, for He is the Inheritor of all His creation, the One who endures after them. We are not really owners at all. We are people holding things in trust for a while.

And that one shift, from owner to steward, changes everything about how you hold your life. Commenting on the people who hoard what Allah gave them, al-Sa'di makes a freeing point: since all of it returns to Allah, who is the best of inheritors, there is no sense in being stingy with a thing that is leaving you anyway and passing to someone else. So you give while it is still yours to give. You hold your blessings loosely, as trusts and not possessions, and you are spared the agony of clutching what was always going to leave.

Then this name turns tender. An old, childless prophet, afraid of the silence after him, lifts his hands and prays, 'My Lord, do not leave me alone, while You are the best of inheritors' (Al-Anbiya 21:89). Feel how beautiful that is. He wants an heir, and so he asks the Heir of everything. Al-Sa'di explains what he meant by 'the best of inheritors': You remain, You are the best to succeed me with good, and You are more merciful to all I love than I am. Even without an heir, Allah remains a better guardian of everything Zakariyya treasured than he could ever be. That is the comfort this name holds out to anyone afraid of what comes after them.

And there is one more turn, the most hopeful of all. The Qur'an, having told you that everything reverts to Allah, then says that Allah, out of pure grace, hands the believers an inheritance of their own: 'Those are the inheritors, who will inherit al-Firdaus. They will abide therein eternally' (23:10-11). You give back the borrowed thing that was always leaving you, and He gives you, to keep, the one thing that never does.

So here is the invitation of this name. Open your hands today, while you still hold something. Give freely, hold loosely, and send your true wealth ahead to the One who never loses what He inherits. Nothing you love is truly disappearing. It is going home.

Ya Warith, teach us to hold Your gifts as trusts, do not leave us alone, and be our Guardian over all we love and cannot keep.
