Why does the hand that can give everything sometimes hold back?

Preview: Al-Qabid, the Withholder. The hand that closes is the same hand that opens, and learning to trust the closing is one of the deepest things faith asks.

Assalamu alaikum,

Something in your life has closed. A door that used to be open, a provision that used to come easily, a strength you used to have, a person who used to be there. You prayed, and it still closed. And underneath the disappointment sits a harder question than we usually let ourselves ask: if Allah is good, why does He withhold?

One of the names of Allah does not flinch from that question: Al-Qabid, the Withholder, the One who constricts and draws in.

Let us be honest with you from the start, because this name deserves it. Unlike most of the names we write about, Al-Qabid does not appear in the Qur'an in its definite form. What the Qur'an gives instead is the living verb. In Surah Al-Baqarah, Allah says of Himself: 'wa-Llahu yaqbidu wa-yabsut,' and it is Allah who withholds and grants abundance (2:245). The name is how the scholars of the tradition gathered that verb, and they did not do it lightly. Ibn 'Ashur lists Al-Qabid and Al-Basit among the names of Allah at this very verse, and al-Tabari records that when prices rose in Madina and the people asked the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, to fix them, he answered that Allah is 'Al-Basit, Al-Qabid, Ar-Razzaq,' the Expander, the Withholder, the Provider. He would not set the prices, because the tightening and widening of provision are in a Hand higher than any market.

Notice what the verse refuses to do. It will not let you hear 'He withholds' on its own. In the same breath comes 'and He expands.' The tradition has carried them as a pair ever since, Al-Qabid beside Al-Basit, because the same Hand that closes is the very Hand that opens. He is not sometimes generous and sometimes withholding. He is always wise, and wisdom looks like opening in one season and closing in another. Commenting on this verse, the scholar al-Sa'di says a person might fear that giving will make him poor, so Allah repels that thought, and then al-Sa'di writes a line worth memorizing: holding back does not expand your provision, and giving does not constrict it.

And the withholding is never blind. On a verse in Surah Al-Isra, Ibn Kathir names Allah as the One who enriches and restricts 'by a wisdom He has,' and cites a hadith that can change how you read every withheld thing in your life: among Allah's servants are those whom only wealth sets right, and those whom only poverty sets right. The thing you are begging to be given might be the one thing that would quietly ruin you, and the One who can see that loves you too much to hand it over.

If the idea still feels cold, the Qur'an gives you a picture. Look up at a bird in flight, 'with wings outspread and folded in' (67:19), and that folding, from the very root of this name, is the only reason it stays in the air. The fold is not the fall. The seasons when your life draws in may be half of how you are being carried, held up, the verse says, by none but the Most Merciful.

So when the constriction comes, you do not have to force a smile over it. You bring it back to Him, the way everything in the verse returns to Him: You are Al-Qabid and Al-Basit. You closed this, and in Your Hand is all good. Open for me what is better, and if You keep this closed, let me trust the Hand more than I wanted the thing.

Ya Qabid, ya Basit, withhold from us only what would corrupt us, and spread open for us what would set us right.
