The name of Allah the Qur'an speaks only once

Preview: Al-Muqit, the Nourisher and the Keeper. The One who measures your morsel and weighs your deed, in a single name said one time.

Assalamu alaikum,

Some of the names of Allah arrive on almost every page of the Qur'an. This one comes once. A single word, near the close of a single verse, and then it is gone: 'And ever is Allah, over all things, a Keeper' (An-Nisa 4:85). The word is muqita. You could read past it in a second. But the early scholars stopped on it and found, inside one word, a God who is holding your whole life at once.

An honest note first. Most famous names of Allah appear in the Qur'an in their definite form, with the al- attached. Al-Muqit, in that exact shape, is not one of them; what the Book gives is the word muqitan, one time, at the end of this verse, and the scholars draw the name from it. We say that plainly, because this name deserves to be met for what it actually is.

The word comes from qut, a person's sustenance, the small daily portion of food that keeps them alive. Commenting on the verse, al-Qurtubi and the early grammarian al-Farra' explain Al-Muqit as the One who gives every human being his qut, his portion. And here is something striking: that root appears in only one other place in the whole Qur'an, a verse about Allah stocking the entire earth. In Surah Fussilat, describing the making of the world, He 'determined therein its sustenance' (aqwat, the plural of qut), before there was a single mouth to feed. Ibn Kathir explains these as the provisions the earth's people need; Ibn Zayd reads it to mean Allah measured it all in accord with the need of everyone who would ever need it. The portion on your plate today is a thread running back to that apportioning.

And it widens to every breathing thing: 'There is no creature on earth but that upon Allah is its provision' (Hud 11:6). Al-Sa'di says Allah has taken the sustenance of every land and sea creature upon Himself, and then he draws out the comfort: let hearts be at peace with the One who guaranteed their provisions and whose provision is wide enough for them all.

But the verse Al-Muqit actually seals is not about food. It is about deeds, about a good word spoken for someone and an evil one. So the scholars read a second sense in the same word. Al-Sa'di says it means Allah is a Witness, a Keeper and a Reckoner over our deeds, recompensing each person with exactly what they deserve. Ibn Abbas read it as hafiz, a Guardian; Mujahid as shahid, a Witness. And al-Suddi, Ibn Zayd and al-Tabari read a third sense, qadir, the All-Able, over all things without exception, so that this provider can never run short and this keeper can never be overruled.

Hold the senses together and the rare word opens up. The God measuring out your daily bread is the God measuring out your daily deeds. He is provisioning your body and auditing your soul in the same breath, with the same total, unbroken attention, and not one portion of either has ever been lost.

So here is the invitation of this name. Stop carrying your provision as though you were its source; you never were. Work, and tie your camel, but let the quiet arithmetic of whether there will be enough finally rest somewhere. And let the knowing that the One who feeds you also sees you keep your tongue clean and your hands open to the portions of others.

Ya Muqit, nourish us with what is lawful, keep the fear of not-enough far from our hearts, and let us trust the measure You have weighed for us.
