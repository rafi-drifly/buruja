The name that does not just forgive, it erases

Preview: Al-Afuw, the Pardoner. The difference between a sin covered over and a sin wiped clean, and the dua the Prophet ﷺ built Laylat al-Qadr around.

Assalamu alaikum,

Most of us know what it is to be forgiven and still carry the thing. The other person says it is fine, they have let it go, and yet the memory of it sits there between you, a small permanent mark on the record. Forgiveness covered it. It did not erase it. And quietly we assume that is the best even God offers: that our worst moments are pardoned but never quite gone, still technically on the books.

One of the names of Allah answers that assumption directly: Al-Afuw, the Pardoner.

This name does not only mean the One who forgives. The scholars of the language tell us that afw means effacement, the wiping away of a thing until no trace of it is left. So where the names Al-Ghaffar and Al-Ghafur cover the sin (their root means to veil, to conceal), Al-Afuw goes further and removes it. He does not only hide the record, He erases it, as though it had never been written. Commenting on Surah Al-Hajj 22:60, the scholar al-Sa'di says that Allah pardons the sinners and forgives their sins, and then the key phrase: He removes them, and He removes their very traces from them. Not a stain covered over with the scar still showing, but a stain washed out completely.

And He does this while fully able to do otherwise. In Surah An-Nisa the name is paired with power: 'indeed, Allah is ever Pardoning and Competent' (4:149). Ibn Kathir explains that among Allah's attributes is that He pardons His servants although He is fully able to punish them. This is no helpless overlooking. He erases what He had every right and every power to hold against you, and that is exactly what makes it mercy.

If you want to feel how central this name is, look at what the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, taught Aisha to say on the greatest night of the year. She asked him what to say if she knew it was Laylat al-Qadr, the Night of Decree. Of all the things a person could beg for on a night better than a thousand months, he gave her one short prayer, built entirely on this name: 'Allahumma innaka afuwwun tuhibbu al-afwa faʿfu anni,' O Allah, You are Pardoning, You love to pardon, so pardon me (reported by at-Tirmidhi). Notice it does not lead with a list of requests. It approaches Allah by this name, then says something astonishing about Him, that He loves to pardon, that erasing our sins is something He delights in, and only then asks: so erase mine.

And because He erases, He asks you to. Al-Sa'di, on that same verse in Al-Hajj, turns to the one who has been wronged and says: pardon and overlook and forgive, so that Allah will deal with you as you deal with His servants. The standard you set is the standard you invite.

So here is the invitation of this name. You do not have to live as someone permanently marked by your worst day. You do not approach Al-Afuw by first cleaning yourself up enough to deserve pardon. You approach Him because He loves to pardon. Bring Him the thing you are most ashamed of, and ask the One who delights in erasing to erase it.

Ya Afuw, You love to pardon, so pardon us, and wipe from our record the things we would give anything to undo.
