The name of Allah that is not in the Qur'an

Preview: Al-Mani, the Withholder. An honest look at where this name stands, and the mercy hidden inside a no.

Assalamu alaikum,

We are going to do something a little different with today's name, because the name itself asks for honesty.

Al-Mani, the Withholder, is not found in the Qur'an. There is no verse that calls Allah by it, and the Arabic root behind it never names Him in the Book. Unlike As-Sami, the All-Hearing, or Ar-Razzaq, the Provider, which the Qur'an states outright, this name reaches us through two other channels: the agreed list of the ninety-nine beautiful names that the scholars compiled, and a saying of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, that he taught us to repeat after every prayer. We would rather tell you that plainly than dress it up, because a name of God is not something to overstate.

But here is the thing. Honesty about the word is not doubt about the meaning, because the truth this name carries runs straight through the Qur'an from end to end. 'Whatever Allah grants to people of mercy, none can withhold it; and whatever He withholds, none can release it thereafter' (Surah Fatir 35:2). And in Surah Al-Mulk, a question with no answer: 'Or who is it that could provide for you if He withheld His provision?' (67:21). Ibn Kathir, explaining that verse, says it simply: no one gives, and no one withholds, except Allah alone. The reality of Al-Mani is everywhere in the Book, even where the title is not.

The hadith is the second pillar, and it is one you may already know. Ibn Kathir relates that the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, would say after the prayer: 'O Allah, none can withhold what You have given, and none can give what You have withheld' (la mani'a lima a'tayta wa la mu'tiya lima mana'ta, recorded by al-Bukhari and Muslim). So a Muslim affirms this reality many times a day, on the authority of the Sunnah, in words the Prophet himself chose. The name is anchored, just not in a verse.

And notice what that hadith does: it never says Withholder on its own. It says given and withheld, in the same breath. The classical scholars follow the Prophet in this and never let the name stand alone. Al-Sa'di, commenting on Surah Yunus, names them together, the Giver and the Withholder, al-mu'ti al-mani', as two sides of one truth. So we read Al-Mani beside His giving names, never as if Allah were stingy or withholding by nature.

Why, then, does He withhold? Surah Fatir ends by calling Him al-Hakim, the Wise, and al-Sa'di explains that name as the One who puts everything in its proper place. When Allah withholds, He is not failing to give. He is placing a thing exactly where wisdom decides it belongs, which is sometimes far from your open hand. And the Qur'an turns this straight toward your heart: 'Perhaps you hate a thing and it is good for you; and perhaps you love a thing and it is bad for you. And Allah knows, while you know not' (2:216).

That is the freeing part of this name. The door you pounded on that never opened, the prayer you made for years that was met with silence: the One who withheld it was not deaf and was not cruel. He was the Wise, placing it where it belonged, often on the way to giving you something better than the thing you mourned. The hand that opens is the only hand that closes. So no gatekeeper can keep from you what Allah has sent, and no closed door is ever a verdict on His care.

O Allah, make everything You withhold from us a mercy, and a choosing of what is best for us.
