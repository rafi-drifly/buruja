Caption

There is a kind of greatness that pushes you away: glorious but cold, impressive but unreachable. A lot of us quietly assume God is like that, only bigger.

Al-Majid, the All-Glorious, takes that apart. Grounded in classical tafsir (Ibn Kathir, al-Sa'di, al-Muyassar), the meaning of its root, majd, is the breadth and greatness of every one of Allah's perfect attributes, with nothing missing. But the same scholars pour nobility and generosity into the very same word: Ibn Kathir glosses the related majeed as 'great and noble,' azim karim. This is glory that gives.

A note in honesty, because grounding a name means telling you where it stands: the Qur'an uses the form al-Majeed (مجيد), in 85:15 and 11:73. Al-Mājid is the sister name the scholarly tradition pairs with it, from the very same root.

Feel it in Surah Al-Buruj, where the glory is framed by tenderness: 'And He is the Forgiving, the Affectionate, the Glorious Owner of the Throne, the Doer of whatever He wills' (85:14-16). Al-Sa'di notes that al-Wadud is set beside al-Ghafur on purpose, so you know that when you repent Allah does not only erase the sin, He loves you for coming back. Forgiveness, love, glory, and limitless power, describing one God.

So come to Him standing taller, and ask Him generously, because small du'a does not honour a glory this large.

Where have you been assuming God is too grand to be gentle? 🤍
