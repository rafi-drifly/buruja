The name the Prophet ﷺ whispered at Badr

Preview: Al-Hayy and Al-Qayyum: the One who never dies, never sleeps, and holds your whole life in His hand.

Assalamu alaikum,

The Prophet ﷺ told us that Allah has a greatest name, and that whoever calls on Him by it, from the bottom of the heart, is never turned away. He did not tell us which name it is. One of the strongest answers the scholars give is not one name but two, joined together, sitting at the opening of the greatest verse in the whole Qur'an, Ayat al-Kursi: Al-Hayy and Al-Qayyum, the Ever-Living and the Maintainer of all.

Al-Hayy comes from the root meaning to be living. He is the One who never dies. The day the Prophet ﷺ passed away, the Companions were in shock, and Abu Bakr steadied them with a single truth: whoever used to worship Muhammad, he has died, but whoever worships Allah, know that Allah is Ever-Living and never dies. And to be living is to be active. Movement is life. Every single day, the Qur'an says, He is bringing about a matter. He is never idle, never asleep, never, to borrow a human word, offline.

Ustadh Hisham Abu Yusuf clears away an illusion we all live under. We are like the dog who hears the bell, sees the food arrive, and thinks the bell brought it, when one hand was ringing and the same hand was feeding. We think the boss brings the paycheck, the spark brings the fire. But Allah is the One acting in every moment, linking each cause to its effect. So when you need to lean on something, lean on the One who is always living: rely on Him, trust Him, ask Him, because people will let you down and Al-Hayy never will.

Al-Qayyum comes from the word for standing, and means the One who holds all of existence in place and never tires. The next words of Ayat al-Kursi guard the meaning: neither drowsiness nor sleep overtakes Him. A human guard eventually drifts off. Mothers tire, fathers tire. Allah never reaches that point. If gravity paused in your room for a single second, you would float like an astronaut. He never pauses.

And the other side of that truth is our need. You are the poor one, the Qur'an says, and Allah is the Free of need. A drowning man screaming for Allah is no more in need of Him than you are right now; he simply feels it. Everything you hold, your health, your wealth, the people you love, is borrowed from Al-Qayyum, who can ask for it back whenever He wills. When the Prophet ﷺ lost his own child he said, to Allah belongs what He took and what He gave. The one who thinks he owns it all lies awake in fear of losing it. The one who knows Allah is holding it can do the work and hand over the outcome, the way the birds leave hungry in the morning and return full.

At Badr, in the hardest hours, the Prophet ﷺ was found in prostration repeating only this: Ya Hayyu, ya Qayyum, by Your mercy I seek relief. He gave us a fuller version to read morning and evening: O Ever-Living, O Sustainer of all, by Your mercy I seek relief, set right all my affairs, and do not leave me to myself for the blink of an eye.
