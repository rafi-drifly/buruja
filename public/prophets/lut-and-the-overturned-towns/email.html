The prophet who would not call the forbidden normal

Preview: Day 14 of 29: Lut, Ibrahim's nephew, and the towns Allah turned upside down.

Assalamu alaykum,

There was a young man who watched Ibrahim, peace be upon him, walk out of a fire unburned, and believed when almost no one else on earth would. His name was Lut, the Lot of English Bibles, and he was Ibrahim's nephew. Ibrahim sent him north to a cluster of towns near the Dead Sea, and there Lut found a people doing something no nation in the history of the world had done before them.

The men had turned away from the women Allah created as their mates and turned toward each other, openly, until it became the custom of the place. Allah's own word for what they did is not a gentle one: al-khaba'ith, the foul deeds (Surah al-Anbiya 21:74). And the most striking thing about that society is how completely it had inverted right and wrong. When they wanted Lut gone, their reason was: 'Evict them from your city. Indeed, they are men who keep themselves pure' (Surah al-A'raf 7:82). Decency itself was the offence. They had even passed a law that Lut was forbidden to host a single guest.

Here is where Mufti Menk turns the story on us, and so does today's entry. As Muslims we are not free, and we say that as a boast. We chose to surrender, and that means we accept a code. You can sin and remain a Muslim, weak and in need of repentance. But the moment you take a thing Allah forbade and rebrand it as permitted, you have tried to edit the law you swore to keep. This is the whole heart of halal and haram: they are Allah's to define, not the crowd's, not the era's, not the syllabus a society decides to teach its children. A whole nation calling a sin normal does not move it one inch on Allah's scale.

Lut stood almost alone, three believers in a city of thousands, and held. Allah answered his du'a with three guests of impossible beauty, angels in the form of young men. His own wife, no believer, betrayed him to the mob. And at dawn the angel Jibril lifted the towns on the tip of one wing and turned them over, top to bottom, and rained down marked stones of baked clay. One was for the wife who looked back. Allah left the ruin on a road people still travel, so we would see it and remember.

Tonight, one du'a, the one Lut himself made: My Lord, save me and my family from what they do.
