One prophet is named in the Qur'an more than any other. His life is told in more stages than any other.

Musa, peace be upon him. Tonight it begins, not with the staff or the sea, but with a baby and a river.

A man ruled Egypt and told the world: I am your lord, the most high.

But he ate, slept, fell sick, and asked his advisors for help.

A god who consults. A god who is afraid. It gives him away.

He dreamed a boy of Bani Israil would pull down everything he built.

So he ordered every newborn son killed.

And Allah announced, before any rescue: We will make the crushed ones leaders. We will make them the inheritors. (28:5)

A mother was inspired: nurse him, and when you fear, place him in the river. Do not be afraid. We will return him to you. (28:7)

Who sets their own baby on the Nile and lets go?

Only a heart Allah had filled with certainty.

The basket floated past every checkpoint.

It came to rest at the one address she'd never have dared: the bank behind Pharaoh's own palace.

The safest place for the hunted child was the house of the hunter.

'I cast upon you love from Me, that you would be raised under My eye.' (Ta-Ha 20:39)

Pharaoh's wife, childless for years, begged to keep him.

The baby then refused every nurse, until they brought his own mother. And he drank.

He grew up powerful, and never once believed the man who called himself god.

One day he struck an Egyptian to stop a fight. One blow, and the man was dead. He had never meant to kill.

Word spread. A man ran to warn him: they mean to kill you. Get out.

He fled to Madyan with worn-out shoes, watered two women's flock, and prayed: my Lord, whatever good You send, I need it. (28:24)

The river that was meant to drown him had carried him the whole way.

When your river runs the wrong way, remember whose hand is on the basket. Al-Latif.
