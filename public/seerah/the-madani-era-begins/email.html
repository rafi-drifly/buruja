He built Allah's house before his own ﷺ

Preview: Day 30 of 104: the Madani era begins with a command, a camel, and a masjid of palm trunks.

Assalamu alaykum,

The hijrah is complete, and today the Madani era truly begins. Day 30 of Buruja's seerah, retold faithfully from Dr. Yasir Qadhi's series, opens not with a parade but with a command: hijrah became obligatory on every Muslim still in Makkah, down to the women and children.

One of them was an old man, blind, unable to walk, who heard the verse warning those who stayed and said: I am not of those who have an excuse. I will not spend another night in Makkah. He died at the city's edge, his right hand pressed into his left: O Allah, this is my pledge. The companions said he almost made it. Allah answered by revealing his pardon, and Ibn Abbas taught that when Allah says perhaps, it is a promise.

Then the arrival. On a Friday, more than five hundred of the Ansar marched out in armor to walk the Prophet ﷺ in (and the famous song of the little girls, the Sheikh shows, belongs to another day entirely). Every clan grabbed at his camel's halter, and he gave one answer: let her be, she is under command. She knelt on a date-drying yard owned by two orphans, beside the home of his distant cousin Abu Ayyub, who would spend half a year terrified of so much as walking above his guest's head.

The orphans begged to gift the land. He paid them full price. Then he joined the labor line himself, passing bricks and singing with the builders: there is no good but the good of the akhirah. Two weeks later the masjid stood, palm trunks and clay, its roof low enough to touch. His own house could wait. Allah's house came first.

And when a proper minbar finally replaced the old palm stump he used to stand on, the stump wept aloud like a baby camel, and he ﷺ came down mid khutbah and hugged it until it went quiet. A piece of wood could not bear distance from him. The early scholars asked: can your heart really bear it more easily?
