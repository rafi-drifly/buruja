The Companions

Abu Muwayhibah

The Witness of the Final Choice

Some companions are remembered for years of battles and long lists of narrations. This one is remembered for a single night, and a single conversation, that he was the only person on earth to witness.

This is the story of Abu Muwayhibah (may Allah be pleased with him): a freed slave of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, a man of the masjid, and the companion who walked beside him in the dark on one of his final nights. He has almost no biography. He has one moment, and it is among the most tender in the whole seerah.

Abu Muwayhibah

The Witness of the Final Choice

A freed man from Muzaynah

Abu Muwayhibah came from Muzaynah, an Arab tribe whose lands lay toward the coast near Yanbu, a couple of hours from Madinah. Like many of the freed slaves who lived close to the Prophet ﷺ, he had most likely first been taken as a captive of war. That was how a great many of these men entered the story: not by choice, and not from positions of strength.

What we know for certain is how that chapter ended. He was freed by the Prophet ﷺ, and from that point on his whole identity in the books is simply this: Abu Muwayhibah, the freed slave of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. One early scholar tried to record a fuller name for him, Muhabbir ibn Muadh, but no one else confirms it, so even his own name has slipped through the cracks of history.

He is one of those names that is easy to pass over. We tend to measure a life by what it leaves behind: titles, wealth, a long record of deeds. By that measure there is almost nothing here.

And yet the deeper you look, the clearer it becomes that the Prophet ﷺ held a place for each of these freed men that the records can only hint at. As small and unnoticed as they may seem, he had a special love for them, and they for him.

A life lived in the masjid

In Madinah, Abu Muwayhibah settled among the people of al-Suffa: the companions who lived in the masjid of the Prophet ﷺ and were cared for by the believers. Many of them had no family in the city and no wealth to fall back on. The masjid was their home.

But it was more than a shelter. It was a kind of seminary in the heart of the mosque. There the people of al-Suffa learned hadith, learned knowledge, and learned the manners of the Prophet ﷺ and his companions, simply by being near them day after day.

This was the school Abu Muwayhibah grew up in. He did not study the Prophet ﷺ from a distance. He lived in his courtyard, watched how he prayed, how he spoke, how he treated people, and absorbed it the way you absorb the habits of someone you love.

The one campaign we find him taking part in was the expedition of al-Muraysi, also known as Banu Mustaliq, in the fifth year after the hijrah. It is reported that he was the man who normally led the camel of our mother Aisha (RA), the very camel at the center of the events that followed that journey. Picture him there: an ordinary man, walking, holding the reins, trusted with that quiet duty and asking for no more than that.

A last visit to Uhud

A few days before he passed away, the Prophet ﷺ did something that still moves anyone who hears it. He went out, one final time, to the martyrs of Uhud.

He had often visited them over the years. But this time he went out to them as if to say goodbye. The companion Uqbah ibn Amir (RA) narrated that the Prophet ﷺ prayed over the people of Uhud the way one prays over the dead, almost a decade after they had fallen on that field.

Think about what that loyalty looks like. Years had passed. The grief could have faded into a memory. Instead he returned to them and honoured them as if it were the day they were buried. That kind of faithfulness, kept warm for people who can no longer thank you, tells you something about the heart that carried it.

Then he turned to the living and spoke words of comfort. He said that he was going ahead of them, that he was a witness over them, and that he could already see his fountain: the place he had promised the believers he would wait for them. Be patient, the promise went, and you will find me there.

Up to this point he was still walking on his own, still strong enough to make the journey. The illness had not yet shown itself as something final. That would come within days, and a single man would be at his side when it did.

A summons in the middle of the night

Then came the night that gave Abu Muwayhibah his place in this story. The Prophet ﷺ called him, late, and said: I have been commanded to seek forgiveness for the people of al-Baqi. So come with me.

Abu Muwayhibah rose, made himself ready, and went out with him into the depth of the night. There were no lamps, no crowd, nothing but the two of them and the darkness over the graves of Madinah. Try to hold that image: just you and the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, alone, on what would turn out to be one of his last nights walking the earth.

He would later say it plainly: it was the middle of the night. He was the only person to share that walk. Of all the great companions, the scholars, the warriors, the leaders, it was this freed man who was chosen for the moment.

His entire legacy rests on this one narration. He has no others. And it is enough.

The choice, and the answer

At al-Baqi the Prophet ﷺ greeted the people of the graves: peace be upon you, O people of the graves. He stood with them and prayed for them. Then, Abu Muwayhibah said, he turned toward me and began to speak to me.

First the buried, then the living man at his side. Who was Abu Muwayhibah, that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ would turn to him in the dark and give him this? And what he said was no small thing. He told him: I have been given the choice between the keys and treasures of this world, and to live in it until its very end, and then Paradise, or to meet my Lord now.

Abu Muwayhibah understood at once what was being weighed. He pleaded the only way a loving heart knows how. May my mother and father be sacrificed for you, he said. Take the first. Stay in this world with us, with all of it, and then enter Paradise after. Why hurry? Stay with us. Do not go.

It is a plea anyone who has loved and feared losing someone will recognise. He was not reasoning about the next life. He simply did not want the Prophet ﷺ to leave.

The Prophet ﷺ answered gently and without doubt: No, Abu Muwayhibah. I have chosen the meeting with my Lord. Offered the whole world and a long life within it, he turned toward his Lord instead. Then he turned back to the graves, sought forgiveness for them once more, and the two of them walked home.

Only a few days after

It was from that night that the illness of the Prophet ﷺ deepened toward its end. He passed away only a few days later. The walk to al-Baqi was among the last things he did while still able to walk out into the night.

Abu Muwayhibah lived on, but the records fall silent. We do not know the date, the place, or any of the circumstances of his own death. This single moment is all that history kept of him.

There is something quietly beautiful in that. He is not remembered for what he achieved, but for whom he stood beside, and for the love that made him beg the Prophet ﷺ to stay.

What this one night tells us

In the end, Abu Muwayhibah's narration is not really about him. It is a window onto the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

The choice he was offered is striking. The treasures of this world, and the keys to it, and a long life lived in its fullness, all laid before him. He set them down without a second thought. He had spent his life calling people away from chasing exactly that, and at the final crossroads he showed them, in his own person, what he had meant.

His answer also carried knowledge. He was aware, in a way his companions were not yet, of the depth of trial and difficulty that would come after him. He was not fleeing the world. He was choosing, with open eyes, the meeting that mattered more.

And he chose it calmly. No drama, no fear, only a settled heart turning home. That is the picture Abu Muwayhibah carried for us out of the dark: a man offered everything, answering quietly that he would rather meet his Lord.

What Abu Muwayhibah's story teaches us

He left us one night and one conversation. Held up to the light, that is more than enough to live by, and most of what it teaches has nothing to do with fame.

  • No one is too small to be chosen. A freed slave, a man of the masjid floor, was given the most intimate moment of the Prophet's final days. Standing before Allah, your station among people is not the measure that counts. He sees the heart, not the title.
  • Nearness is built quietly. He earned that night not through fame or wealth but by living close to the Prophet ﷺ, learning his manners, and being faithful in small, unseen duties. The same is true for us: closeness to Allah grows in the ordinary moments no one else sees.
  • Choose the lasting over the passing. Offered the entire world and immortality within it, the Prophet ﷺ chose the meeting with his Lord. Every ordinary day asks us a smaller version of that same question, between what fades and what remains.
  • Loyalty does not expire. He returned to the fallen of Uhud and honoured them as on the day they were buried. Faithfulness to those we have lost, in our prayers and our memory, is part of faithfulness itself.

Questions

Who was Abu Muwayhibah?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the tribe of Muzaynah, most likely first taken as a captive of war and then freed by the Prophet ﷺ. He lived among the people of al-Suffa in the masjid of Madinah.

Why is Abu Muwayhibah remembered?

He was the only companion who walked with the Prophet ﷺ to the graveyard of al-Baqi on one of his final nights, when the Prophet ﷺ said he had been given the choice between this world and meeting his Lord, and chose to meet his Lord.

What was the choice the Prophet ﷺ described that night?

He told Abu Muwayhibah he had been offered the keys and treasures of this world and life in it until its end followed by Paradise, or to meet his Lord at once. He chose the meeting with his Lord, and passed away a few days later.

What can we learn from Abu Muwayhibah?

That no one is too small to be honoured by Allah, that nearness is built through quiet faithfulness, and that the lasting meeting with our Lord is worth more than anything this world can offer.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be remembered for a long list of deeds. We live ordinary days, do small duties, and stay close to what is good when no one is watching. Abu Muwayhibah's whole legacy was one faithful night, and it was enough to carry his name through the centuries.

His story leaves us with two quiet invitations. Be faithful in the small things, because nearness is built there, not on a stage. And when the world offers you its keys, remember the man who was offered everything and answered, without hesitation, that he would rather meet his Lord. Carry one of those into today, and the story has done its work.
