Abu Dujana

The Red Bandana

Almost everything we know about him happens on a battlefield. He was a young man trained for war his whole life, and he is remembered, more than anything, for a single day and a single act of courage.

This is the story of Abu Dujana (may Allah be pleased with him): the warrior of Madinah who was handed the Prophet's own sword at Uhud, and who later said it was not his courage that gave him hope, but something much quieter.

The Companions

Abu Dujana

The Red Bandana

A young man groomed for battle

His real name was Simak ibn Kharasha, but the Muslims have always known him by his nickname, Abu Dujana. He came from Banu Sa'ida, a sub-tribe of al-Khazraj and one of the more prominent families of Madinah, with a long sense of leadership before and after Islam.

From boyhood he practiced every art of war. He trained with the spear and the sword, he learned to ride horses and camels, and he prepared himself, day after day, for the moment he would be called on to fight. He grew up expecting the petty tribal wars of Arabia, the kind in which fathers killed each other over small things.

Then Islam came to Madinah, and Allah had other plans for him. When the people embraced the faith, Abu Dujana was among the three young men who went through the city and broke its idols. Everything about him was confidence and readiness for the moment of war.

The Prophet ﷺ used to pair the emigrants of Makkah with their counterparts among the Ansar, matching them by character rather than by tribe. He paired Abu Dujana with a man who was among the earliest Muslims, who had made both migrations and fought every battle, and who later helped defeat the Persian empire. So a youth shaped for battle now lived alongside another, both of them waiting for a very different summons.

The sword, and its right

By the time the Battle of Uhud came, Abu Dujana was only about twenty. Before the fighting began, the Prophet ﷺ did what he often did: he singled out one honour for one man. He held out his own sword and asked, "Who will take this sword from me?"

This was no banner and no piece of cloth. It was the sword of the Messenger of Allah. Hands shot up at once. Everyone wanted it. Then the Prophet ﷺ added a condition: "Who will take it with its right?" The hands came back down. They understood that a responsibility was being attached, and they hesitated.

Abu Dujana put his hand forward and said, "I will take it, O Messenger of Allah, with its right." The Prophet ﷺ explained the right of the sword: that its bearer would not strike a fellow believer with it, and would not flee from the enemy, no matter what happened. With that, the sword was his.

The red bandana

Az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (may Allah be pleased with him) admitted he was stung when the sword went to Abu Dujana and not to him. He was the Prophet's cousin, always near, and he resolved to watch this young man closely and see what made him worthy of such an honour.

What he saw first was a red bandana. Abu Dujana pulled it out and tied it on his head, and all the Ansar grew excited, for they knew this man and his habit. When the red bandana came out, they said, the bandana of death was on. He then strutted between the two armies, twisting the sword, showing the enemy no fear though the Muslims were badly outnumbered.

This was not how the Prophet ﷺ taught people to walk. He taught humility. Yet watching Abu Dujana, he said this was a way of walking that Allah hates, except in a moment like this one. There is a time and a place for everything. He understood the psychology of the battlefield, and rather than break the young man's spirit, he let it lift the morale of the Muslims around him.

Abu Dujana also chanted as he went, reciting that he had taken an oath with his beloved friend that he would never stand in the back rows of the army, but would fight the enemy with the sword of Allah and His Messenger. The young Ansar cheered him on, and the show he put on began, all on its own, to unsettle the larger force in front of them.

Cutting through the ranks

When the fighting started, az-Zubayr kept his eyes on him. Abu Dujana had broad shoulders, and he was finishing his opponents one by one, carving a path forward into an army three times the size of his own. From the other side, a fierce fighter was cutting his own way through, and the two of them drew closer and closer.

When they finally met, it was over in a moment. Abu Dujana blocked the man's blow and struck him down with a single hit. He kept moving forward. Another companion, lying wounded on the ground, watched a large, well-armed enemy advance, a man slaughtering people as if they were animals, and feared the worst for the Muslim who faced him. That Muslim was Abu Dujana, and he dropped the bigger man too.

He cut all the way through to the very back of the enemy lines, where the women were chanting to rile up their army. There, suddenly, was a woman who shrieked and revealed her face: Hind, the wife of Abu Sufyan, an active and ferocious participant in the battle. Abu Dujana raised the Prophet's sword over her, and then he pulled it back.

Too noble to strike

Az-Zubayr came up to him afterward and said he had loved everything he saw, and that he finally understood why the Prophet ﷺ had given him the sword. But one thing puzzled him: with Hind beneath his blade, an active enemy, why had Abu Dujana held back?

His answer stayed with them. "I realised she was a woman," he said, "and the sword of the Messenger of Allah is too noble to strike a woman." He would not taint the trust he had been given. The Prophet ﷺ had taught them not to kill the women, not to kill the innocent, not to harm anyone who was not fighting, and Abu Dujana held to it even in the heat of the moment.

Up to this point the Muslims had the upper hand. Outnumbered three to one, they had pushed the enemy back, and had the battle ended there, Abu Dujana would already have been counted among its heroes for the way he broke the enemy's spirit.

Then the battle turned. The archers left their post, the enemy circled the mountain, and the Muslims were struck from behind. In that chaos, while others were chased and scattered, a handful of young men from the Ansar chose to surround the Prophet ﷺ rather than flee. Abu Dujana was one of them.

The shield of arrows

He turned his own back into a shield. He crouched over the Prophet ﷺ and let the arrows rain down on him until, in his own words, his back looked like a hedgehog. Those nearby had to look past Abu Dujana just to see whether the Messenger of Allah was still alive, so completely did he cover him.

When an attacker pressed in, poking at the Prophet ﷺ around him, Abu Dujana did not move from his post. With his back still littered with arrows, he struck and brought the man down. Miraculously, he survived. Of those who shielded him that day, the Prophet ﷺ said, "O Allah, be pleased with him as I am pleased with him."

After the battle, the Prophet ﷺ took back his sword and gave it to his daughter Fatimah to wash the blood from it, praising that it had been used well, that Abu Dujana had done justice to the trust he was given. Every battle after this, he was on the front line. At Hunayn he changed the momentum by cutting down the enemy's flag-bearer, and in the Farewell Hajj the Prophet ﷺ left him as governor of Madinah.

Two things at the end

His last battle was Yamamah, against the false prophet Musaylimah, who had crucified Muslims for refusing to call him a prophet. To break the deadlock, the companions launched Abu Dujana over the wall into the garden where the enemy was fortified. He landed so hard that he broke his leg, fought on, and was struck in the chest. His eyes turned to the sky, and he left this world doing the one thing he was known for.

He had named his only son Khalid, after the great warrior. But the most astonishing thing about him is not on any battlefield. When he lay sick with a fever and people came to pay their last respects, they found his face glowing like the moon, and they asked him what was giving him such hope.

This was the man of the red bandana, the man who had taunted armies and carried the Prophet's sword. He did not mention his sword, his hand, or his heroics. He said two things gave him hope before meeting Allah: "I never used to talk about things that did not concern me," and, "my heart toward all my brothers and sisters was always pure."

What Abu Dujana's life teaches us

His courage is what draws you in. But it is the quiet things he hoped in at the end that he would want you to remember.

  • Zeal can be guided, not crushed. The Prophet ﷺ did not scold the young man for his fire. He gave it a worthy direction. The same energy that fuels us can serve something good if it is aimed well.
  • An honour comes with a duty. Abu Dujana did not just take the sword; he took its right. Whatever we are trusted with carries responsibilities we are meant to keep.
  • Strength knows where to stop. At the height of battle he pulled his blade back from a woman. Real strength is not only what we are willing to do, but what we refuse to do.
  • A clean tongue and a clean heart. A famous warrior's last hope was that he minded his own business and held nothing against anyone. That is a struggle harder than any battle, and open to all of us.

Questions

Who was Abu Dujana?

Abu Dujana (RA), whose real name was Simak ibn Kharasha, was a young companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah. He was a renowned warrior, best known for carrying the Prophet's own sword at the Battle of Uhud.

Why is Abu Dujana known for a red bandana?

Before every battle he would tie on a red bandana, and the Ansar came to recognise it as his signal that he was entering a different mode of fighting. They called it the bandana of death. At Uhud he wore it and strutted before the enemy to show no fear.

What happened with the sword of the Prophet at Uhud?

The Prophet ﷺ offered his sword to whoever would take it with its right, meaning its full responsibility: not to strike a believer and not to flee. Abu Dujana accepted that condition, used the sword to cut deep into the enemy lines, and spared Hind when he realised she was a woman, saying the sword was too noble to strike her.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Dujana?

That courage should be guided by ethics, that any honour carries a duty, and that even a celebrated warrior placed his deepest hope not in his bravery but in a clean tongue and a heart free of resentment.

Why this story stays with us

It would be easy to remember Abu Dujana only for the red bandana and the sword, for the strut before the enemy and the back turned into a shield of arrows. That is the part that thrills us. But when he thought he was dying, he reached past all of it.

Most of us will never stand on a battlefield. We will be tested in smaller, daily ways: by the urge to talk about people, by the quiet grudges we let settle in our hearts. Abu Dujana, of all people, hoped in Allah for a tongue that minded its own business and a heart that held nothing against anyone. Choose one of those for today, and his story has done its work.
