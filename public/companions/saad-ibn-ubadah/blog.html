Sa'd ibn Ubadah

The Generous Chief

In Madinah, before Islam arrived, there was a man who would climb onto the roof of his house each evening and call out into the streets: whoever wants a good meal tonight, come to me. Not barley. Not a handful of dates. The best of what he had.

This is the story of Sa'd ibn Ubadah (may Allah be pleased with him): a chief of the Khazraj, a man known to everyone for one thing above all, and a host whose table seemed to have no end.

The Companions

Sa'd ibn Ubadah

The Generous Chief

A chief from a house of givers

Sa'd was the son of Ubadah, the chief of Banu Sa'idah, one of the clans of the Khazraj. Madinah in those days was held by two tribes, the Aws and the Khazraj, often at war with each other. His father led before him, and his mother, Amra, would live long enough to accept Islam.

They called him al-Kamil, the complete one. He was said to be striking to look at and skilled at almost everything: reading, writing, poetry, calligraphy, swimming, archery, the throwing of the spear. People said there was no quality they prized that Sa'd did not carry more fully than the rest.

But the trait that defined him was not any of these. It was generosity. His father was known for it, he was known for it, and his son would be known for it after him. Generosity, the scholars note, is a quality that tends to pass down through a family until a whole house wears it as its name.

Ask a generous person where their habit came from, and they will often point back to a parent. Sa'd grew up watching his father give, and he gave the same way, and his children watched him. A house can pass down many things. This one passed down an open door.

The host of Yathrib

His giving was so well known that poets wrote about it. When the rains failed, Sa'd would supply crops to the city. When travellers came through, poor or stranded, whoever they were, his house was the one that took them in. When people fell into debt, even distant relatives he was barely tied to, he would write off what they owed.

Every day he stood on his roof and offered not the leftovers people usually gave away, but the good food: the meat, the part that fills and nourishes. His door was open every single night.

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ later taught that people are like precious stones, that the best of them before Islam are the best of them in Islam if they understand, Sa'd was exactly the kind of man he meant. His good was already there. Islam would polish it and aim it at something lasting.

The last of five to believe

A small group in Madinah heard the message first, and one by one they accepted it in a single day. Sa'd heard the commotion, came to see what was happening, was told to sit and listen, and believed before that day was out. He was the last of the five, but he came in all the same.

It mattered who these men were. There was a saying that spread as far as Makkah: if the two Sa'ds, Sa'd ibn Ubadah and Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, accepted this religion, then the Prophet ﷺ had nothing left to fear. These were the men the whole city rested on. When they believed, everyone understood that Islam had found a home.

Sa'd pledged himself to the Prophet ﷺ at the second pledge of Aqabah, among the seventy, and was named one of the twelve leaders of Madinah. In time, after Sa'd ibn Mu'adh passed away, he came to be seen as the leader of the Ansar as a whole.

Taken and tortured

The pledge had been made in secret. When word reached Makkah that the people of Yathrib had given themselves to the Prophet ﷺ, Quraysh went after them. The only one they caught was Sa'd. They held him, starved him, and beat him for days.

He remembered being dragged out in chains, dehydrated and hungry, when a handsome man stepped toward him. Something in the face made Sa'd think: if there is any good left in these people, it will be in this one, surely he has come to help me. The man came close, raised his hand, and struck him as hard as anyone had. There is no good in them at all, Sa'd thought.

In the end it was not strength that freed him. Sa'd had hosted powerful men of Makkah when they passed through his city, and trade and hospitality bound them to him. He was urged to name those ties, and when he did, the men were asked and confirmed it: yes, this is our host, a noble man who opens his home to us when we travel.

And so he was released. The generosity he had shown for years, with no thought of any return, quietly came back to carry him home.

The kitchen that fed Madinah

Once in Madinah, Sa'd carried on exactly as before. His kitchen ran like a soup kitchen for the city, open every single night. In the hardest stretches, it was his stores of dates and food that fed the Muslim army when nothing else was left.

When new arrivals came from Yemen and far places, with no home yet and no footing, they would sleep in the mosque until they could stand on their own. Each night people came to take one or two of them home to feed. Sa'd would take eighty. Whoever was left after everyone else had chosen, he gathered up and brought to his house.

He did not give in order to be seen. He once gathered the best parts of forty animals and set them before the Prophet ﷺ, who barely ate, saying simply: take the share you want first, and I will give the rest to the people of Madinah. He even taught his son never to hold back at the door, because feeding people was who they were. When others once told the young man to wait and keep some of the household's food back, Sa'd was displeased that anyone would try to make his son cautious with what was meant to be given away.

His giving reached the Prophet ﷺ so often that the Prophet ﷺ once raised his hands at the door of Sa'd's house and made a prayer for him and his family. From a meal at Sa'd's table came the supplication the Prophet ﷺ taught for a host: that those who are fasting may break their fast with you, that the righteous may eat your food, and that the angels send their peace upon you.

Honour, and a heart that could weep

Sa'd had the temperament of a chief: a strong sense of honour and a fierce protectiveness over his family and his people. When he spoke of defending what was his, the Prophet ﷺ did not break that in him. He said he was not surprised by Sa'd's sense of honour, that he himself had more of it, and that Allah had more than them both.

Yet the same proud man was the one who once asked the Prophet ﷺ, when he saw him weep over a dying grandchild, what this was. His people were not used to such tears from a leader. He learned the answer and carried it: that weeping is a mercy Allah places in the hearts of His servants, and that Allah is merciful to those who show mercy. Strength and tenderness, he saw, were not opposites.

When the Prophet ﷺ himself fell ill near the end, Sa'd had been ill before him. The Prophet ﷺ had gone to visit him, found him slipping between consciousness, and wept until those around him wept too. The companions saw on that day how dear Sa'd was to him.

A charity that outlived him

Sa'd loved his mother deeply. While he was away on an expedition, she died in Madinah, and he came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked him to pray over her. Her last words had been that whatever she left should go to Sa'd, for she trusted him to spend it well.

So Sa'd kept giving on her behalf. He asked whether charity would reach her after death and was told yes, and he began handing over the best of their gardens and wealth in her name. He asked whether he could fulfil an oath she had left, and was told to fulfil it.

Then he asked the question he is most remembered for: what is the best charity I can give for her? The answer was water. So Sa'd dug wells. The practice of giving water for a parent who has passed traces back to this man and his mother.

He lived a quieter life afterwards and died in Sham. His son, Qays, went on to become a known leader. But Sa'd had set something in motion that did not stop with his death: a way of giving that others would follow long after he was gone.

What Sa'd's life teaches us

His story is not built on a single battle or a single speech. It is built on an open door, kept open for a lifetime, and on what that habit became.

  • Generosity is a habit you build, not a moment you have. Sa'd gave every single night, in good years and hard ones. The giving became who he was, and it shaped his son after him.
  • Give people your best, not your leftovers. He refused to hand out only what was easy to spare. He offered the good food, the best of forty animals, the finest of his gardens.
  • Quiet good comes back in ways you cannot plan. The hospitality he had shown for years was the very thing that freed him from his captors. Kindness rarely returns the way we expect.
  • The best gifts keep giving after you are gone. Sa'd dug wells for his mother so the reward would continue. A life can be measured by what keeps flowing once it ends.

Questions

Who was Sa'd ibn Ubadah?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and a chief of the Khazraj tribe of Madinah. He was one of the twelve leaders chosen at the pledge of Aqabah and, in time, came to be regarded as a leader of the Ansar. He is best known for his extraordinary generosity.

Why is Sa'd ibn Ubadah called the generous chief?

His family was known for generosity across generations. Sa'd fed travellers, the poor, and the homeless of Madinah every night, opened his stores in times of hardship, and is remembered as one of the great hosts of his city.

What is the connection between Sa'd ibn Ubadah and giving water?

After his mother died, Sa'd asked the Prophet ﷺ what charity would benefit her most. He was told water, so he dug wells in her name. The practice of donating wells on behalf of a deceased parent is often traced to this account.

What can we learn from the life of Sa'd ibn Ubadah?

That generosity is a habit worth building, that we should give people our best rather than our leftovers, and that the gifts which keep giving after we are gone are among the most lasting.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to think of generosity as something for the wealthy, or for a special occasion: a large gift, a good year, a moment when we feel we can spare it. Sa'd's life says otherwise. His giving was nightly, ordinary, and built into the shape of his day. He kept a door open, and over a lifetime that open door became his name.

Most of us will never feed eighty people. But we can decide that someone leaves our presence better off, today and tomorrow and the day after, until it stops being a decision and becomes who we are. Sa'd dug wells so his giving would outlast him. His story asks a gentle question of our own day: what are we putting in motion that will still be giving when we are gone?
