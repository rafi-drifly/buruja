The Companions

Al-Fadl ibn al-Abbas

The Rider Behind the Prophet

On the day of Eid, in the middle of the only Hajj the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ever performed, a young man stood holding the reins of his camel while the crowds pressed in with their questions. He was handsome, and he knew it, and his eyes wandered to a woman in the crowd who was looking back at him.

This is the story of al-Fadl ibn al-Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him): the eldest cousin of the Prophet ﷺ from the house of al-Abbas, the rider who sat behind him on his camel, and the young man the Prophet ﷺ taught, with one quiet hand, to lower his gaze.

The Companions

Al-Fadl ibn al-Abbas

The Rider Behind the Prophet

A family that seemed to have everything

Al-Fadl was the eldest son of al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. If you read the seerah and look for a family that seemed to have it all, you find the house of al-Abbas. They were known for their beauty and their presence. When they walked into a gathering, people noticed.

It is not easy to be the brother of someone like Abdullah ibn Abbas, the great scholar of this ummah. You could spend a lifetime in his shadow. Yet the brothers of al-Fadl were not jealous of one another the way the brothers of Yusuf once were. Their story is one of admiration. Each one looked at the gem the family already had and asked a simple question: what is left for me to carry? What place can I take in serving the Prophet ﷺ and this religion?

That question is part of why al-Fadl matters. He was not the most learned of his brothers, and he did not need to be. He found the work that was his to do, and he did it close to the Prophet ﷺ.

Al-Fadl was born around the year 614, about four years after the first revelation. He was the oldest, and the oldest carries certain responsibilities. His biography is short, sometimes only a line here and there, but it is woven through some of the most tender moments in the life of the Prophet ﷺ. If you let your mind picture those moments, you see the religion itself being handed down, person to person.

Raised to step into his father's place

Al-Fadl grew up in Makkah at his father's side, not in Madinah. Al-Abbas took him everywhere: to the tribal meetings, to the important negotiations, learning the affairs of the family so that one day he could take his father's place. He was being raised to be his father's right hand.

Like his father, he did not announce his Islam openly until the Prophet ﷺ returned to Makkah victorious. The Prophet ﷺ was in Madinah in those years, and al-Fadl was in Makkah with al-Abbas. How much faith he carried quietly before the conquest, we cannot say for certain. His public story begins on the day the Prophet ﷺ entered the city in triumph.

From that day, the Prophet ﷺ drew al-Fadl close. He made him something like his spokesman and his messenger to the people around him. If you wanted to reach the Prophet ﷺ, you went through al-Fadl. Messages came and went through him.

And it was then that the Prophet ﷺ began to make him his riding companion. Of all the sons of al-Abbas, al-Fadl is the one you most often see seated on the camel behind the Prophet ﷺ. It was a small, daily closeness, the kind that does not make headlines but shapes a life.

The young man who stood firm at Hunayn

Soon after the conquest came the Battle of Hunayn. The Muslims went out in large numbers, the believers of Madinah joined by the new Muslims of Makkah, and for once they were many. The Qur'an records what their numbers did for them:

It was a hard day. They had gone out to face Bedouin tribes who were plotting against them, and even with their numbers, things nearly fell apart. The lesson was an old one, learned again: it was never the numbers that won anything, but the help of Allah.

When the fighting turned and people scattered in every direction, the Prophet ﷺ was left standing almost alone, calling out without fear, "I am the Prophet, this is no lie. I am the son of Abd al-Muttalib." Only a small group held their ground around him. Al-Fadl was one of them.

In that same battle, al-Fadl became a first: he was the first to carry the standard of Banu Hashim after Islam. He had earned a new layer of the Prophet's love, not with words, but by staying when others fled. The Prophet ﷺ had longed to have his family near him, and here was his young cousin proving his courage at the hardest moment.

God has helped you [believers] on many battlefields, even on the day of the Battle of Hunayn. You were well pleased with your large numbers, but they were of no use to you: the earth seemed to close in on you despite its spaciousness, and you turned tail and fled.Quran 9:25

Riding behind the Prophet on Hajj

The most famous moments of al-Fadl's life come from the farewell Hajj. Imagine the scene: more than a hundred thousand people, and one young man chosen to ride behind the Prophet ﷺ on his camel. It was an honour beyond counting.

Al-Fadl rode behind him from Arafah, moving quickly toward Muzdalifah, then on toward Mina. He held on as the Prophet ﷺ went to throw the pebbles at the Jamarah. Because he was right there, he heard and remembered the words and the order of the rites that millions of Muslims now follow every year.

It tells you something about the Prophet ﷺ that he was mindful, even on such a day, of giving each member of his family a turn near him. There were more than a hundred thousand people around him, and still he noticed the young ones and drew them close.

So much of how we perform Hajj was carried to us by people like al-Fadl, who simply held on and paid attention. One pilgrimage of the Prophet ﷺ has guided the ummah for over a thousand years, and a quiet young man on the back of a camel was one of those who passed it down.

Lower your gaze

It happened on the day of sacrifice, the day of Eid. The Prophet ﷺ had come down from his camel and was answering the people's questions one by one, and al-Fadl was holding the camel for him. In Hajj, his brother Abdullah ibn Abbas said, every Muslim becomes like a child again, asking the smallest of questions, and the Prophet ﷺ patiently answered them all.

A young woman from the tribe of Khath'am came to ask a question. She was beautiful, and al-Fadl was a handsome young man, and the two of them began to look at one another. The Prophet ﷺ noticed. He did not shout. He did not shame him. He simply reached up, placed his hand on al-Fadl's cheek, and gently turned his face the other way.

Al-Fadl turned back, and the Prophet ﷺ turned his face again. It happened three times before al-Fadl understood: lower your gaze, keep your eyes down. The woman's question, it turned out, was a serious one. Her father was now very old and could no longer ride or perform Hajj. Could she perform it for him? "Yes," the Prophet ﷺ told her, "you may perform Hajj on his behalf."

It is narrated that afterward the Prophet ﷺ spoke to al-Fadl kindly: "O son of my brother, this is a mighty day. Whoever guards his eyes and his ears and his tongue on this day will be forgiven." He was not humiliating the young man. He was protecting his reward.

Beside the Prophet to the very end

Al-Fadl stayed close to the Prophet ﷺ in his last days. When the Prophet ﷺ could no longer walk and had to be carried, it was Ali and al-Fadl who carried him. Al-Fadl was there when the Prophet ﷺ passed away.

Of all the sons of al-Abbas, al-Fadl is the one who took part in every part of the Prophet's funeral, because he was the eldest. He walked the Prophet ﷺ home. He helped wash his body. He entered the grave, received the body, and helped lay him to rest. He was present for every step of that farewell. The young man who had ridden behind him was now among the last hands to hold him.

Afterward, the courage he had shown at Hunayn carried him into the great campaigns into Syria. He fought at Yarmuk carrying the shield of the Prophet ﷺ. Think of that: a young man going out to battle holding the armour of the man he had buried. It is said that he and another companion fought in the front lines as if each man were a thousand.

He took part in the opening of the lands that are now Jordan, on the front lines against the Byzantine soldiers, until those lands were opened. He died not long after: some say in battle, others in the plague of Amwas, which took many companions who had expected to fall on a battlefield instead. He is buried in that land, far from Makkah, the cousin who had once ridden so close to the Prophet ﷺ.

What al-Fadl's life teaches us

His biography is short, but it holds a few things that are easy to carry into an ordinary day.

  • A correction can be gentle. The Prophet ﷺ did not shame al-Fadl in front of the crowd. He turned his face away with one quiet hand. Truth and kindness are not opposites.
  • Lowering the gaze is care, not punishment. The Prophet ﷺ was protecting al-Fadl's reward on a sacred day, not taking something from him. Guarding the eyes is a gift you give yourself.
  • Stay when others scatter. At Hunayn, al-Fadl earned a deeper place in the Prophet's heart simply by standing firm when the crowd ran. Loyalty shows itself in hard moments.
  • Find your own place to serve. He did not envy his famous brother. He asked what he could carry, and carried it well. There is room for everyone to be useful.

Questions

Who was al-Fadl ibn al-Abbas?

He was the eldest son of al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, and so a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He is best known as the young man who rode behind the Prophet ﷺ on his camel during the farewell Hajj.

What is the story of lowering the gaze?

During the Hajj, a beautiful young woman came to ask the Prophet ﷺ a question, and al-Fadl began to look at her. The Prophet ﷺ gently turned al-Fadl's face away with his hand, teaching him to lower his gaze without shaming him.

Why is al-Fadl called the riding companion of the Prophet?

The Prophet ﷺ would often seat him on the camel behind him, especially during the rites of Hajj. Because he rode so close, al-Fadl heard and passed down many of the details of how the Prophet ﷺ performed the pilgrimage.

What can we learn from the life of al-Fadl?

How to correct others gently, why guarding the eyes is a form of self-care, the value of staying firm when others scatter, and the contentment of finding your own way to serve.

Why this story stays with us

Most of our slips are small and quiet, like a glance that lingers a moment too long. What stays with us in al-Fadl's story is not the slip but the hand on the cheek: a correction that carried no shame, only care. The Prophet ﷺ did not want to embarrass the young man. He wanted to guard his reward.

There is something to imitate here on both sides. When we are corrected, we can receive it the way al-Fadl did, as protection rather than insult, a sign that someone is guarding our good rather than exposing our fault. And when it falls to us to correct someone we love, a child, a friend, a younger sibling, we can do it the gentle way, turning a face softly instead of raising a voice. Most of the people in our lives do not need to be shamed. They need a hand placed kindly on the cheek. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
