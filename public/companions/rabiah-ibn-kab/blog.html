Rabiah ibn Kab al-Aslami

The One Who Asked for Jannah

A teenager from the edge of the city saw a man enter Madinah and, in his own words, loved him with a love that filled every part of him. He had no home there, no family there, no reason to stay. He stayed anyway, just to be near.

This is the story of Rabiah ibn Kab al-Aslami (may Allah be pleased with him): the young man who served the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ for the love of it, and when he was finally offered anything he wanted, asked for the one thing that would never run out.

Rabiah ibn Kab al-Aslami

The One Who Asked for Jannah

A teenager and a single look

Rabiah came from Banu Aslam, a tribe living on the outskirts of Madinah. He was barely past the age of maturity, still a teenager, when he first came into the city to see the Prophet ﷺ. He was unmarried, with no wealth and no household of his own.

He later told his son what that first moment felt like. He said that when Allah opened his heart to faith, the very first time he laid eyes on the Prophet ﷺ, he loved him with a love that took hold of his whole being. Not only his heart, he said: every part of him.

It was a strange thing to feel for a stranger. He knew almost nothing about the man. He simply looked, and he was certain, and from that moment he had only one thought: he had to find a way to get close to him.

How does a stranger get close?

The problem was simple. The Ansar of Madinah were competing to host the Prophet ﷺ, each offering a home and a place at his side. The Muhajirun who had migrated with him were already his closest companions, drawn closer still by the road they had travelled together. Rabiah was neither of these. He was a young outsider with no obvious place in any of it, asking himself how he could ever wedge his way into the circle around the messenger of Allah.

So he made a place. He joined the young men who slept in the back of the mosque, the ones with no home yet, later known as Ahl as-Suffah, the People of the Bench. There he noticed something. Whenever a gift of food reached the Prophet ﷺ, he did not keep it for himself. He would carry it out, sit down among these young men, and share it with them.

That was the door. The ones a city might have overlooked, the poor and the homeless of the mosque, were already close to him without trying. Rabiah was one of them, and simply by being there, eating beside the Prophet ﷺ, he began to gain a quiet nearness.

Living in his shadow

One day Rabiah said to himself that being nearby was not enough. He would go and offer himself completely. He told the Prophet ﷺ his name and said he wished to be in his service, ready for anything he needed. He braced for a gentle refusal. It did not come. The Prophet ﷺ accepted him.

From that day, Rabiah said, he lived in his shadow. Wherever the Prophet ﷺ went, he was there. Wherever he looked, Rabiah was within his glance, waiting for a single request. But the Prophet ﷺ did things for himself and spent his care on others, so the request rarely came.

Rabiah would stay with him until the last prayer of the night, then think about returning to the shelter of the mosque, then stop himself: what if the Prophet ﷺ needed something? So he would fetch water, set it at the door, and sit on the doorstep. He would listen to the recitation inside drift long into the night, doze, wake to the sound of it, and wait for the dawn.

Ask me

It was the Prophet's ﷺ way that no one did something for him without him wanting to give back something greater. He came to Rabiah one day and said simply: ask me. Ask me for anything, and I will give it to you.

Here was a poor young man with no wife, no wealth, no home, told he could ask for any of it. He did not answer at once. He asked for time to think, and the Prophet ﷺ gave it to him. That night he turned it over. He could ask to be lifted out of poverty. He could ask for a wife, for children.

Then another thought stopped him. This world is going to pass, he told himself. Whatever sustenance Allah has written for me will reach me anyway. But the Prophet ﷺ has a station with his Lord, and a request he makes of Allah will not be refused. So Rabiah decided. He would not waste the asking on something temporary.

Your company in Paradise

He came back. The Prophet ﷺ was expecting the usual request of a young man: marry me off, give me something, settle me. Instead Rabiah said: I ask for your companionship in Paradise.

The Prophet ﷺ was surprised. It was a mature answer from a young face, so much so that he asked whether anyone had told him to say it. Rabiah swore that no one had. He had looked at himself, seen that this world fades, and chosen the next.

The Prophet ﷺ fell quiet for a long time. Then he said he would ask Allah for what Rabiah wanted, and he added one thing: help me, by increasing your prostrations. This was not a wish granted and forgotten. The path to that closeness was more worship, more prayer in the night. So Rabiah went back, threw himself into the worship of Allah, and kept pushing himself to do more.

A wedding the Prophet built

Sometime later the Prophet ﷺ asked him: do you not want to get married? Rabiah, still guarding his single wish, said he did not want anything to take him away from serving him. The Prophet ﷺ let it rest. When he asked a second time, Rabiah admitted the truth: he had nothing to offer as a dowry even if he did marry. He was a poor young man of the mosque, with nothing to show for himself.

So the Prophet ﷺ took over. He told Rabiah to go to a particular family and say that the Prophet ﷺ had instructed them to marry their daughter to him. Rabiah knocked, delivered the message, and the family, astonished but trusting, welcomed it at once. When he worried about the dowry, the Prophet ﷺ asked his clansmen to gather a little gold. When he worried about the wedding feast, the Prophet ﷺ arranged an animal to slaughter and sent him to ask his own household for what barley it had. They emptied their cupboard for him.

Then, just like that, there was bread and meat, and the Prophet ﷺ came with his companions and ate at the wedding. Afterwards he gifted Rabiah a piece of land beside Abu Bakr. The young man who had slept on his doorstep with nothing now had a wife, a home, and the dearest of neighbours, all of it arranged by the very person he had only wanted to serve.

One palm tree, and the fear of Allah

The new comfort unsettled Rabiah. After years of wanting nothing but to serve, he felt himself becoming attached to the world. Then came the one ordinary dispute in his whole story. He and Abu Bakr argued over a single palm tree near the boundary of their land, and in the heat of it Abu Bakr said a word that hurt him.

Realising what he had done, Abu Bakr asked Rabiah to say the same word back, so they would be even. I do not want to meet Allah, he said, with a word that hurt you on my record. Rabiah refused: he would never return a hurtful word to a man of Abu Bakr's standing. When his own tribe arrived urging him to stand his ground, he sent them away quickly, afraid that gathering them would look like turning people against Abu Bakr, and that the Prophet ﷺ might be displeased.

When the Prophet ﷺ heard the matter, he told Rabiah he had done well: do not answer Abu Bakr with that word, but say instead, may Allah forgive you. Rabiah said it, and Abu Bakr wept until his beard was wet. Here were two believers in a small quarrel, and both were more afraid of a careless word on the Day of Judgement than of losing the argument. It is the kind of fear that makes an ordinary disagreement feel suddenly serious.

The quiet that followed

Rabiah stayed at the Prophet's ﷺ side through everything that came after. He was his shadow in the years that remained, present through the battles and the days in between. And when the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Rabiah did not become a famous teacher or fill the gatherings with narrations the way many companions did. He went back to his people and lived a quiet life.

The loss seemed to settle over him. All that survives from him is a handful of narrations passed to his son, among them the story of the asking, told as his own private thoughts on the night he made his choice. He waited, in his quiet, for the reunion he had been promised.

He was killed years later in the tragedy that struck Madinah, attaining martyrdom. He had begun as a teenager who loved a man at first sight and slept at his door. He ended having asked for, and been promised the means to, his company in the Garden.

What Rabiah's life teaches us

His whole story turns on a single choice: offered the world, he asked for what lasts. The rest is the kind of devotion that choice grows from.

  • Aim past the temporary. Given one open request, Rabiah skipped the wealth and the comfort and asked for nearness to Allah and His Prophet ﷺ. He measured the world against the next life and chose accordingly.
  • Love shows up as service. He did not just feel love; he carried water, kept watch at the door, and made himself useful. Devotion that stays a feeling is not yet devotion.
  • Wishing is not enough; work for it. The Prophet ﷺ promised to ask, then told him to increase his prostrations. What we hope for in the next life, we build with what we do in this one.
  • Guard the tongue, even when you are right. Rabiah would not return a hurtful word to Abu Bakr, and both feared meeting Allah with it. A dispute is rarely worth a wound.

Questions

Who was Rabiah ibn Kab al-Aslami?

A young companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the tribe of Banu Aslam. He served the Prophet ﷺ closely in Madinah and is best known for asking, when offered anything he wished, for the Prophet's companionship in Paradise.

What did Rabiah ask the Prophet ﷺ for?

When the Prophet ﷺ told him to ask for anything, Rabiah did not ask for wealth or marriage. He asked to be his companion in Paradise. The Prophet ﷺ agreed to ask Allah for it and told him to help by increasing his prostrations.

Why did Rabiah sleep at the Prophet's door?

He had no home in Madinah and slept among the young men of the mosque. He chose to stay at the Prophet's ﷺ doorstep at night in case he needed anything, wanting to serve him in any way he could.

What can we learn from the life of Rabiah ibn Kab?

To aim past the temporary toward what lasts, to let love express itself as quiet service, to pair our hopes for the next life with real effort, and to guard the tongue even in the heat of being right.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are not offered a single open wish. But we make small versions of Rabiah's choice all the time, in where we put our hope, our time, and our worry. He had every reason to ask for an easier life and chose nearness instead, then quietly did the work it asked of him.

His example is an invitation into an ordinary day: to serve without being asked, to want the lasting thing more than the comfortable one, and to hold the tongue when winning would cost a brother or sister a wound. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
