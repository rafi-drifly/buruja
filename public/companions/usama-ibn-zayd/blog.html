Usama ibn Zayd

The Beloved, Son of the Beloved

Some people are born into the heart of a family. Usama was carried into one. His mother had been a freed slave who became like a mother to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. His father had been a freed slave the Prophet ﷺ loved like a son. And when their child was born, the Prophet ﷺ held him the way a grandfather holds the first grandchild he has ever known.

This is the story of Usama ibn Zayd (may Allah be pleased with him): the boy whose skin was darker than his father's, who bore a cruel rumour with patience, and who learned from the Prophet ﷺ that the law of God does not bend for the people we love.

Usama ibn Zayd

The Beloved, Son of the Beloved

A family gathered by Allah

Nothing about Usama's family came together in an ordinary way. His mother, Baraka, was a young woman from Abyssinia who had been sold in the slave market of Makkah and given to the Prophet's father, Abdullah. She was the only person in the room when the Prophet ﷺ was born, and she was there to console his mother in her grief and her death. The Prophet ﷺ later freed her and called her his mother after his own mother, saying she was what remained of his family. She is known to us as Umm Ayman.

Usama's father, Zayd ibn Harithah, was a free child kidnapped on a journey and sold into the same market. He was bought, then gifted to Khadijah, and the Prophet ﷺ grew so attached to him that when Zayd's own father came to take him home, the boy refused to leave. The Prophet ﷺ raised him as his own son.

Then the Prophet ﷺ married these two to each other, and out of that household of love and loyalty came a child. The risk of it all is worth pausing on: a girl carried across a sea into slavery, a boy stolen from his parents, both brought to the same market, both raised in the same house. From that, Usama was born into the closest circle there was.

The first grandchild

Usama was older than Hasan and Husayn. In a real sense he was the first child the Prophet ﷺ knew as a grandfather, and he carried all the memories that come with that: being lifted and kissed, riding on the Prophet's back, sitting behind him on his mount, walking beside him to the prayer.

His nickname tells you how he was held. The Prophet ﷺ is al-Habib, the beloved. Zayd was called the beloved of the Prophet ﷺ. So Usama became the beloved, the son of the beloved. His whole place in the family was made of affection.

There are small, tender scenes that have nothing to do with greatness and everything to do with love. Once a child of the household had a hurt or a mess on his face, and others hesitated to clean it. The Prophet ﷺ said, leave him to me, and cleaned it himself. Of Usama he said plainly, love him, for I love him.

Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) remembered him in the home as a small boy she helped to raise, though she said she did not always know how, having no children of her own. Once the Prophet ﷺ took Usama from her to wash him gently himself, and he said that had Usama been a girl, he would have dressed and adorned the child himself until the day of marriage. It is hard to imagine a softer picture of how a grandfather loves.

A slander, and a quiet answer

Usama had very dark skin, darker than his father's lighter brown. People who wanted to wound the Prophet ﷺ found their opening there. They whispered that a boy who looked so different could not really be Zayd's son. It was a low rumour, beneath answering, and the Prophet ﷺ never dignified it in public. But it hurt, and it hurt the one it was aimed at.

The answer, when it came, came without anyone asking for it. A man skilled in reading family resemblance once entered the Prophet's house while Usama and Zayd were asleep under a single cloak, their heads covered and only their feet showing. Unprompted, he looked at the two pairs of feet and said these clearly belong to one another, father and son.

The Prophet ﷺ was visibly happy. Not because he needed the verdict, but because the truth had answered the rumour on its own, in front of everyone who had been suggesting otherwise. Usama carried the slander with patience, and Allah carried the answer for him.

Inside the Kaaba

When the Prophet ﷺ returned to Makkah in victory, he could have taken back the home he was driven out of. He did not. He pitched a tent instead, choosing humility over the posture of a conqueror.

Then the door of the Kaaba was opened, and the Prophet ﷺ went inside. He entered with only two people: Bilal, the freed slave, and Usama, the son of freed slaves who was treated as his grandson. They stayed inside a long while, and the doors were shut behind them.

Think of what that scene says in a society built on tribe and status. The two people brought into the most sacred space were a former slave and a dark-skinned boy others had mocked. The Prophet ﷺ was changing his people not with a speech but by who he chose to keep beside him.

If Fatima were to steal

A woman of a powerful clan was caught stealing, and her people could not bear to see the punishment carried out on one of their own. They looked for someone with the standing to ask the Prophet ﷺ to let it go, and the one they sent was Usama. It shows how the tables had turned: the boy they once mocked was now the one whose word might reach the Prophet's heart.

When Usama interceded, the Prophet's face changed. "Are you interceding against one of the punishments of Allah?" Then he stood and addressed the people. He told them that what destroyed the nations before them was exactly this: when a noble person stole, they let it pass, and when a weak person stole, they came down on him with full force.

Then he said the line that has echoed for fourteen centuries. By the One in whose hand is my soul, if Fatima herself were to steal, I would carry out the punishment on her. His most beloved daughter would not be spared. Usama learned, in that moment, that loving the family of the Prophet ﷺ does not place anyone above the book of Allah.

Did you open his heart?

The Prophet ﷺ sent Usama on an expedition, and in the heat of battle he cornered a man. As Usama was about to strike, the man said the words of faith. Usama judged it a last attempt to escape death, and killed him.

When he reported it, the Prophet ﷺ asked him, did you kill him after he said there is no god but Allah? Usama explained that the man had only said it to save himself. The Prophet ﷺ pressed the question again and again: did you open his heart to know? He repeated it until Usama wished he had never become Muslim before that day, so that this would not be on his record.

The lesson stayed with him for life. When Muslims later fell into fighting one another, Usama refused to take part. He said he would not raise his sword against anyone who said there is no god but Allah. A single rebuke from the Prophet ﷺ shaped the rest of his choices.

A trust the Prophet ﷺ would not undo

Near the end of his life, the Prophet ﷺ appointed Usama to lead an army, though he was only about seventeen and was being sent against the Romans. Some of the companions questioned giving such young shoulders such a weight. The Prophet ﷺ stood and answered: if you doubt his leadership, you doubted his father's before him, and by Allah, both were fit for it, and both were among the most beloved of people to me.

Even as he lay dying, slipping in and out of consciousness, the Prophet ﷺ kept asking, has the army of Usama set out? When Usama heard how grave the illness was, he rushed back to Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ could no longer speak. He raised his hands to the sky and then pointed to Usama, and Usama knew he was being prayed for. That was the last he saw of him.

After the Prophet's death, some asked that an older man replace Usama. Abu Bakr refused, gripping the situation with a firmness that was not usually his manner, and said he would not loosen a knot the Prophet ﷺ had tied. The army went out, and it returned successful. Word of it even reached the Romans: their people had lost their Prophet and still kept their promise to march.

Years later Usama lived quietly. When strife broke out among the Muslims and they began to fight one another, he kept out of it entirely, telling an old companion that he would jump into the mouth of a lion beside him out of love, but would never lift his sword in that kind of fighting. He fasted every Monday and Thursday into old age, because he had heard the Prophet ﷺ say that deeds are presented to Allah on those days. When he died, he was carried into Madinah and buried near the one who had loved him so much.

What Usama's life teaches us

His story is not built on conquest. It is built on being loved well, bearing insult with patience, and learning where the line of justice truly falls.

  • Worth is not skin or status. The boy others mocked for his colour was the one the Prophet ﷺ brought into the Kaaba and trusted to lead. Honour, in his house, was measured by faith and character.
  • Patience answers slander better than anger. Usama bore a cruel rumour without lashing out, and the truth defended him in its own time. Not every insult needs a reply from us.
  • Justice cannot have favourites. "If Fatima were to steal, I would carry out the punishment on her." The people we love do not get a private set of rules.
  • Be slow to judge another's heart. "Did you open his heart to know?" We see words and actions; we do not see what is inside. Usama never forgot it.

Questions

Who was Usama ibn Zayd?

He was the son of Zayd ibn Harithah and Umm Ayman, both of whom the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ loved dearly. Raised in the Prophet's household and treated as his grandson, he was known as the beloved, son of the beloved.

Why was Usama slandered?

Usama had very dark skin, darker than his father Zayd's lighter complexion. People who wished to hurt the Prophet ﷺ whispered that he could not be Zayd's son. The Prophet ﷺ did not dignify the rumour, and a man skilled in reading resemblance later affirmed, unasked, that the two were clearly father and son.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ make Usama lead an army so young?

Near the end of his life the Prophet ﷺ appointed Usama, about seventeen, to lead an expedition against the Romans. When some questioned his age, the Prophet ﷺ insisted he was fit for it and among the most beloved of people to him. Abu Bakr later refused to replace him, unwilling to undo what the Prophet ﷺ had decided.

What can we learn from the life of Usama?

That a person's worth is not in colour or class, that patience can answer an insult better than anger, that justice must apply equally to everyone, and that we should be slow to judge what is in another person's heart.

Why this story stays with us

Usama was loved without earning it and judged without deserving it, and he answered both with the same steadiness. He did not let affection make him proud, and he did not let an insult make him bitter. When he was wrong, he carried the correction for the rest of his life and let it change how he treated people.

Most of us will be praised more than we have earned and criticised more than we deserve. His life is a quiet invitation: to hold the love we are given with humility, to leave a cruel word unanswered when patience serves better, to refuse a private set of rules for the people close to us, and to be slow, always, to decide what lives in another heart. Carry one of those into your day, and this story has done its work.
