He had already escaped. Then one sentence turned him back.

Preview: Ayyash loved his mother, and his enemy knew it.

Assalamu alaikum,

Ayyash ibn Abi Rabiah (may Allah be pleased with him) had already made it out. He was on the road to Madinah, safe, with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ahead of him and his old life behind. Then his half-brother caught up to him.

The brother was Abu Jahl, the fiercest enemy the early Muslims had. He was too clever to attack Ayyash directly. So he used words instead. Your mother, he said, has sworn she will not eat, will not drink, will not even sit in the shade. She will starve in the Makkan sun until you return. And she is dying because of your Islam.

It was a lie, shaped precisely to wound a man who loved his mother. Umar ibn al-Khattab, traveling with him, saw what it had done and warned him: when the heat is unbearable she will go inside, when she is hungry she will eat. Do not go with them.

Ayyash went anyway. I have to obey my mother, he said, but I will not give up my faith. He meant only to reassure her and return. On the road, they tricked him down off his camel and put him in chains.

He was held in Makkah for years. He missed the migration he had set out to make. He missed Badr. And yet he was not forgotten: in the last part of his prayer, the Prophet ﷺ would raise his hands and call out for the believers trapped in Makkah, naming Ayyash among them.

The road finally opened after the treaty of Hudaybiyah. Ayyash reached Madinah at last, fought in the great campaigns, and was martyred at Yarmuk. Khalid ibn al-Walid wept as he led the prayer over him.

There is something quiet and steadying in his story. His path was crooked and slow and full of chains. He honoured his mother without surrendering his deen, and the years he seemed to lose were seen, and counted, and not wasted.

May Allah be pleased with him, and grant us patience on our own long roads.
