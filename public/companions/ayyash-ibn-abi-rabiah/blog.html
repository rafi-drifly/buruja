Ayyash ibn Abi Rabiah

The Long Road Home

He had already made it out. He had reached safety on the road to Madinah, with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ahead of him and his old life behind. Then a man rode up and told him his mother was dying because of him.

This is the story of Ayyash ibn Abi Rabiah (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the first Muslims, a man of two migrations, whose love for his mother led him into chains, and whose faith held through years he could not escape.

The Companions

Ayyash ibn Abi Rabiah

The Long Road Home

A son of two powerful houses

Ayyash ibn Abi Rabiah came from strength on both sides. Through his father he belonged to a powerful clan of Quraysh. Through his mother he was tied to another. He was a man with standing in Makkah, the kind of person who, by the reckoning of his society, had little to fear.

He was also the half-brother of Abu Jahl, who shared the same mother. Abu Jahl became the fiercest enemy of the Muslims, the man the early community called the Pharaoh of this ummah. The two brothers would end up on opposite sides of everything that mattered.

And there was a third bond in his life that the rest of this story turns on: Ayyash loved his mother deeply. Picture a single woman with two sons, one a companion of the Prophet ﷺ, the other the chief persecutor of the believers. That was the home Ayyash came from.

Among the first to believe, and to leave

Ayyash accepted Islam very early, soon after the first quiet years of the message. He is counted among the first Muslims. When the persecution in Makkah grew heavy, he left, joining the migration to Abyssinia with his wife, Asma, who is herself remembered among the first to believe.

He did not go on the very first migration, but on the second. Some say he embraced Islam a little after the first group had left. Others say that, given his lineage and his place in society, he had no pressing need to flee at first, and only made the journey when the pressure rose. Either way, both he and Asma gave up safety for faith.

In Abyssinia their son, Abdullah ibn Ayyash, was born, far from the city of his parents. He grew up to narrate the story of his mother and father. They were one of those families whose children first opened their eyes in a land of refuge.

Then word came that the believers were gathering with the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah, and Ayyash and Asma made their way back toward Makkah to join the migration. He was now a man of two journeys for the sake of his faith, and the hardest one was still ahead of him.

The guilt that turned him back

When the time came to migrate to Madinah, Ayyash set out alongside Umar ibn al-Khattab, his close relative. They had already reached safety on the road when Abu Jahl caught up with them, with another brother, al-Harith ibn Hisham, beside him.

Abu Jahl knew better than to confront Umar directly. So he turned to Ayyash with words instead of force. Your mother, he said, has sworn she will not eat, will not drink, will not even sit in shade. She will sit out in the Makkan sun and starve and go thirsty until you come back to her. She is wasting away, he told him, because of your Islam.

It landed exactly where it was meant to. This was a man who loved his mother, now told she was dying by his hand. Think of what that does to a heart: the person you love most, perishing, and you the cause.

Umar saw what the words had done, and he kept warning him. Your mother, he said, when the heat grows unbearable and the discomfort creeps over her, she will go inside. When hunger and thirst press hard, she will eat and she will drink. Do not go with them. He understood that Abu Jahl and al-Harith were up to no good.

A camel, a plot, and chains

Umar and the others even offered to help. Stay with us, they said, and whatever your mother needs will be sent to her. But Ayyash made up his mind. This is my mother, he said. I have to go to her. I will not renounce my faith, but let me reassure her that I am well, let me convince her to eat and to come in out of the sun, and I will return. There is something tender and true in his reasoning: to honour a parent is a command, and he would not abandon his mother to her grief. He only drew the one line he would not cross. He would obey her in kindness, but not into leaving his religion.

Umar stayed wary of Abu Jahl. If you must go, he said, then take my camel: it is healthy and fast, and if they make a move on you, ride hard and get away. So Ayyash took Umar's camel and turned toward Makkah.

On the way, Abu Jahl and al-Harith worked a trick that got Ayyash down off the camel. The moment his feet touched the ground, they were on him. They had not dared attack him beside Umar; now they could outnumber him. They bound him in chains and carried him back to Makkah a prisoner.

The pressure on him was severe, and for a moment, under it, he relented. Some of the scholars of tafsir say it was for people in his situation that this promise was sent down:

Say, '[God says], My servants who have harmed yourselves by your own excess, do not despair of God's mercy. God forgives all sins: He is truly the Most Forgiving, the Most Merciful.Quran 39:53

The years he could not escape

Ayyash held on to his faith, and he remained in Makkah, held there under torture. He could not get out. From the moment he was captured on the road until long afterward, he stayed in the city. He missed the migration he had set out to make. He missed Badr. He missed Khandaq. He missed the years in which the community he loved was being built without him.

He was not forgotten. Abu Hurairah narrated that in the last part of his prayer the Prophet ﷺ would raise his hands and call out for the weak and oppressed believers held in Makkah, naming Ayyash among them, asking Allah to rescue them. The Prophet ﷺ prayed for the captives, and he prayed against those who held them, that a drought be sent upon the city as it once was upon the people of Yusuf. And a drought came.

The first verse of Surah al-Ankabut, the scholars say, speaks to this group above all: the ones tested so hard for their faith, held in the city, unable to reach Madinah, who still did not break.

Do people think they will be left alone after saying 'We believe' without being put to the test?Quran 29:2

The road that finally opened

His way out came from an unexpected direction. After the treaty of Hudaybiyah, one of its terms was that anyone who escaped Makkah to the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah had to be returned. A man named Abu Basir, who had escaped and could not be kept, was quietly told to settle elsewhere.

Abu Basir gathered the fugitives the treaty had trapped: men who had fled torture in Makkah but could not be received in Madinah. They camped outside the city and pressed the caravans that passed between Makkah and Syria, until the Makkans themselves begged the Prophet ﷺ to simply take these men in. Ayyash was among those who finally reached Madinah this way.

Years later, fighting in the cause, Ayyash saw a man who had once tortured him in Makkah and, not knowing the man had since become Muslim, struck him down. He learned only afterward that the man had believed. We trust that Allah is just, and the final judge of such things. The scholars connect his case to the verse on a believer who takes a believer's life by mistake:

Never should a believer kill another believer, except by mistake.Quran 4:92

Buried beside the brother he suffered with

Ayyash went on to take part in the great campaigns. He fought at Yarmuk against the Byzantines, the battle that decided so much against the Romans, under the command of Khalid ibn al-Walid. There, alongside Salama, another of the believers once held in Makkah, he was martyred.

On that field there is a famous scene. As the wounded lay dying and a little water was carried among them, each man, asked first, turned it toward his brother: take it to him, he is in greater need. So it passed from one to the next until it reached none of them, every one of them gone. Ayyash and Salama were among those martyrs.

Khalid ordered that the two be buried together, in one grave, because they had lived together, been tortured together, and died together. He wept as he led the funeral prayer over them, and said these were the best of their clan, the remnant who had believed from the very house of Abu Jahl, and that there would never be men like them from that house again.

What the life of Ayyash teaches us

His story is not a straight line. It is a man who tried to do right by everyone he loved, took the longer road, and still arrived.

  • Love can be used against you. The thing that nearly turned Ayyash back was not hatred. It was love for his mother, and good people made it a weapon. Hold on to what is right even when your softest feelings are being pulled.
  • Obey, but not into sin. He chose to honour his mother and care for her, while refusing to give up his faith. Kindness to parents and loyalty to God are not opposites, until someone asks you to trade one for the other.
  • A delayed path is still a path. He missed the migration, missed Badr, lost years to captivity. Yet he reached Madinah, fought in the great battles, and died a martyr. What looks like a detour can still lead home.
  • You are remembered when you cannot help yourself. While Ayyash sat helpless in chains, the Prophet ﷺ was raising his hands for him by name. No one who is sincere is ever truly forgotten.

Questions

Who was Ayyash ibn Abi Rabiah?

An early Muslim from a powerful family of Quraysh and the half-brother of Abu Jahl. He migrated to Abyssinia and is counted among those who made two migrations, though years of captivity in Makkah delayed his arrival in Madinah.

Why did Ayyash turn back on the way to Madinah?

Abu Jahl told him his mother had sworn to starve herself in the sun until he returned, and that her illness was caused by his Islam. Moved by love for her, Ayyash went back to reassure her, refusing to give up his faith, and was captured on the road.

Which verses of the Quran are connected to his story?

Scholars of tafsir link several verses to Ayyash and those in his situation: the promise that Allah's mercy is not lost to those who repent (39:53), the reminder that belief is tested (29:2), and the ruling on a believer who kills another by mistake (4:92).

What can we learn from the life of Ayyash?

That love for family is a strength that can also be exploited, that honouring parents must never mean abandoning faith, and that a delayed or difficult path can still end well. He reached Madinah at last and died a martyr.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are not tested by armies. We are tested by the people we love most, who can ask of us, gently and sincerely, the one thing we should not give. Ayyash felt that pull at full strength, and it nearly cost him everything. His way back was long and full of chains, and still he held the line that mattered: he honoured his mother, and he kept his faith.

His life is an invitation to be patient with our own crooked road. To care for our parents without surrendering our deen. To trust that the years we seem to lose are seen, and counted, and not wasted. Carry that quiet steadiness into today, and his story has done its work.
