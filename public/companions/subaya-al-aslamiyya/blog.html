The Companions

Subay'a al-Aslamiyya

The Question That Became a Ruling

A young widow, two weeks after burying her husband, holding a newborn and a difficult question. A man told her she had no right to remarry yet. Instead of arguing, she went and asked the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ himself.

This is the story of Subay'a al-Aslamiyya (may Allah be pleased with her): one of the early Muslims, one of the women remembered as jurists among the companions, and a woman whose single question became a ruling the ummah still follows.

The Question That Became a Ruling

A woman the scholars learned from

We do not know much about Subay'a al-Aslamiyya's early years. What the books preserve is what came of her life: she is counted among the fuqaha of the companions, the ones trusted to explain the religion and to give a ruling on a difficult matter.

Not every companion was a scholar of their generation. There were categories among them. Some were known as narrators, careful keepers of what they had heard. Others were the people you went to when you needed a question answered, the ones capable of giving a fatwa in their area. Subay'a was among that second kind.

The jurists of Madinah and Kufa studied what she carried and passed it on after her. It is a quiet thing to say about a person, that scholars came to her to learn. But it tells you the weight she held. Her knowledge did not stay with her. It travelled into two of the great centres of learning in early Islam, and it is still taught today.

Among the first, and among the displaced

Subay'a was one of the first people to accept Islam, alongside her husband, Sa'd ibn Khawla. They were among the early couples to believe, and among those who would make hijra twice: first to Abyssinia, then to Madinah. They were people of the two migrations, beloved to the Prophet ﷺ for the sacrifices they made when belief was costly.

Sa'd was, by some accounts, a Persian man from Yemen, an ally of the tribe of Amir, counted among the weak and the unprotected of Makkah. Some of the scholars place him among the very people Allah spoke of when He told the Prophet ﷺ not to send away the poor who called on their Lord morning and evening, seeking His pleasure. The elites of Quraysh wanted those people pushed aside as the price of their attention. The Prophet ﷺ was told not to turn them away, for they were significant in the sight of Allah even as the city looked through them, and victory would come through them.

When persecution made Makkah unbearable, Sa'd and Subay'a left it. As people with no tribe to protect them, they were among the most exposed. They waited out the years in Abyssinia, and when the call came to migrate to Madinah, they were among the very first of the believers to arrive. They had only the Prophet ﷺ and the truth they had chosen.

A young couple who gave everything

When they reached Madinah, they stayed in the home of one of the early Ansar, Kulthum, who was among the first to receive the migrants from Makkah. To be hosted by him is itself a sign of how early they came. They were not stragglers who arrived once the road was safe. They were near the front of the migration.

Sa'd fought at Badr when he was only twenty-five. So this was a young couple, newly arrived, with no children that we know of, pouring themselves into the cause of the Prophet ﷺ without anything held back. They had no wealth or standing to fall back on. What they had, they gave.

Sa'd went on to stand with the Prophet ﷺ in his battles. His life moves in a wide circle: a man called weak in Makkah, a refugee in Abyssinia, a settler in Madinah, a soldier at Badr, and at last a pilgrim returning to the very city that had once driven him out. The unprotected man of Makkah ended his days on Hajj beside the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

A death in Makkah, and a child two weeks later

The Prophet ﷺ had a particular wish for the companions who left Makkah: that they would not return there to die. He wanted their migration completed, their resting place in Madinah where they had built their new lives, not in the city they had been forced to flee. He used to pray that for them.

But Allah had decreed otherwise for Sa'd. He died in Makkah during the Farewell Hajj, the final pilgrimage, with the Prophet ﷺ present. The Prophet ﷺ was saddened that Sa'd, who had given so much to leave Makkah, passed away within it. Yet to die on Hajj is a noble end in itself, and Allah keeps His own reward for each soul in whatever place and circumstance He takes it.

It is worth pausing on that care. The Prophet ﷺ did not only want his companions to live well; he thought about where they would die, and he wished for them the completion of the journey they had begun. That a man like Sa'd, who had left everything to follow him, should pass away back in Makkah was a quiet sorrow. And yet Allah had chosen for him an end on Hajj, in the company of the Messenger ﷺ, which is its own honour.

Subay'a was pregnant when her husband died. Two weeks after his death, she gave birth. And with that birth, a question opened that the companions would return to for years afterward.

The question that settled the matter

The Qur'an sets the waiting period of a widow at four months and ten days. But what of a woman who is pregnant when her husband dies, and who gives birth before that time is up? Does her waiting end when the child is born, or must she still complete the longer term? For a while this was a matter the companions sought to understand clearly.

Subay'a's case became the answer they pointed back to. When she gave birth, her waiting period ended. She did not have to wait out the rest of the four months and ten days. The shorter term, the birth of the child, was what released her. Years later, when scholars discussed the question, hers was the example that settled it.

There is a tenderness in how the Qur'an frames this. The law was made lighter, not heavier, for a woman already carrying grief and a newborn. The verse names her situation directly, and closes with a promise that reaches past the ruling itself:

If you are in doubt, the period of waiting will be three months for those women who have ceased menstruating and for those who have not [yet] menstruated; the waiting period of those who are pregnant will be until they deliver their burden: God makes things easy for those who are mindful of Him.Quran 65:4

She did not argue. She asked.

Soon after she gave birth, while still in the days of postnatal bleeding, Subay'a prepared herself for marriage. Two men came to propose, both young, one a little older than the other. The older was a man named Abu al-Sanabil.

She was inclined toward the younger of the two. Abu al-Sanabil, wanting to slow things down, told her she had no business making herself available when, in his view, her waiting period was not yet complete. He measured her by the four months and ten days, and one account says he hoped that if her family returned, they might persuade her to choose him instead.

Subay'a did not quarrel with him, and she did not simply take his word for something so important. She went to the Prophet ﷺ and asked him directly. He confirmed to her that her waiting period had ended the day she gave birth. She was free to remarry. And in the end, for reasons of her own, she chose to marry Abu al-Sanabil after all.

It is a small, human turn in the story, and easy to pass over. But notice what she did with a confident man's mistake. She did not let it shrink her, and she did not let it pull her into an argument. She carried it to the one place where it could be answered, and came back with the truth.

Grief does not run on someone else's clock

There is a habit of judging a widow or a widower who remarries soon, of reading it as a lack of love or a lack of patience, of laying our own ideas of grief on them. None of that judgement belongs to this religion. Subay'a married while still in her postnatal days, and her readiness took nothing away from what she had felt for Sa'd.

Among the companions you find both kinds of people: some who never married again after losing a spouse, and some who married again quickly. Neither was a measure of how much they loved the one they lost. Both responses are honoured, and to look down on either is to add a burden the religion never placed on the grieving.

There is one more thing Subay'a carried. She is the narrator of a saying of the Prophet ﷺ: whoever among you can die in Madinah, let him do so, for I will intercede for whoever dies there. There is a gentle symmetry in this. The woman whose own husband died far from Madinah is the one who preserved the Prophet's wish for his people to rest in his city, a wish she could not see fulfilled in her own loss.

What Subay'a's life teaches us

Her story turns on small, ordinary choices: where to take a question, how to treat the grieving, what to do when someone speaks over you with confidence.

  • Take your question to the right place. A man told Subay'a she was wrong. She did not argue and did not assume. She went to the source and asked, and the answer set her free.
  • Knowledge is meant to be passed on. Subay'a became one whom scholars came to learn from. What she understood did not stay with her; it shaped the law of generations after her.
  • Do not measure others by your own idea of grief. Some mourn for years, some begin again soon, and both can be sincere. Judging a grieving heart by an outside clock is not from this religion.
  • Allah makes the burden lighter, not heavier. A widow with a newborn was given the shorter waiting period, not the longer. The law bends toward mercy for those already carrying weight.

Questions

Who was Subay'a al-Aslamiyya?

An early Muslim woman and one of the companions remembered as jurists. The scholars of Madinah and Kufa narrated knowledge from her. Her husband was Sa'd ibn Khawla, and together they were among the first to accept Islam and to migrate to Abyssinia and then Madinah.

Why is the story of Subay'a important in Islamic law?

Her husband died while she was pregnant, and she gave birth about two weeks later. Her case became the proof the companions returned to: that a pregnant widow's waiting period ends when she gives birth, rather than her having to complete the full four months and ten days.

What did Subay'a do when told her waiting period was not over?

A man proposing to her claimed she could not yet remarry. Rather than argue, she took the question to the Prophet ﷺ, who confirmed that her waiting period had ended at the birth of her child.

What can we learn from the life of Subay'a?

To take our questions to those with knowledge instead of acting on assumption, to pass on what we understand, and not to judge a grieving person by our own idea of how grief should look.

Why this story stays with us

Subay'a was not in a battle or on a journey when her moment came. She was at home, newly bereaved, holding a newborn, when a confident voice told her she was in the wrong. What she did next is the whole lesson: she did not argue, and she did not simply obey. She went and asked the one who would know.

Most of us meet that same fork often. Someone speaks over us with certainty, and we either swallow it or fight back. Subay'a shows a third way: stay calm, withhold the assumption, and seek the truth from where it actually lives. She was a woman whom scholars came to learn from, and her name survived not because she was loud but because she was reliable. In an ordinary life, that is a quiet kind of greatness worth wanting: to be the person whose answer can be trusted, and who, when unsure, knows exactly where to turn. Carry one of those into today, and her story has done its work.
