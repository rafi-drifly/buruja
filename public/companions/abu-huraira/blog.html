Abu Huraira

The Preserver of Hadith

He came to Madinah with almost nothing: an old, blind mother, a borrowed servant who soon fled, and four years left in the life of the man he had crossed a desert to follow.

This is the story of Abu Huraira (may Allah be pleased with him): an orphan shepherd from a tribe known for all the wrong things, who would carry more of the Prophet's words to the world than any other human being.

The Companions

Abu Huraira

The Preserver of Hadith

The boy from a tribe with no good in it

Abu Huraira came from Daws, a tribe in Yemen famous for drinking, gambling, and every kind of wrongdoing. When their chief, Tufayl, gave up on calling them to Islam, he asked the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ to pray against them. Instead the Prophet ﷺ raised his hands and said: "O Allah, guide Daws, and bring them."

No one would answer that prayer more fully than the orphan boy Tufayl brought with him. When this young man first stood before the Prophet ﷺ and said he was from Daws, the Prophet ﷺ replied, "I thought there was no good in anyone from Daws." Yet here he was.

He was a shepherd, simple and introspective, the kind of person the world tends to overlook. He kept a kitten in his sleeve and carried it with him among the sheep. From that came the name the Prophet ﷺ gave him, the one he loved most: Abu Huraira, the father of the kitten.

The prayer for his mother

He reached Madinah the morning the Prophet ﷺ set out for Khaybar, with only his mother, Maymunah, beside him. She was old and blind, and he was her only child. When he told her he had become a Muslim and called her to it, she refused, and one day she said things about the Prophet ﷺ that wounded him deeply.

He went to the Prophet ﷺ in tears. The Prophet ﷺ held full authority in Madinah by then, but he did not send anyone to punish her. He simply raised his hands and prayed, three times: "O Allah, guide the mother of Abu Huraira."

Abu Huraira walked home and found the door shut and water running. His mother called out for him to wait. When she opened the door, the first words out of her mouth were the testimony of faith. He came back to the Prophet ﷺ weeping again, this time from joy.

Standing at his mother's door

From that day, a quiet habit shaped his life. Their home was two doors of one structure, and he reached hers from the outside. Every time he left the house and every time he returned, he stopped at her door first.

"Peace be upon you, my dear mother, and the mercy of Allah and His blessings," he would say. She would return the greeting. "May Allah have mercy on you," he would add, "as you raised me when I was young." And she would answer, "May Allah have mercy on you, my dear son, as you have shown me nothing but goodness in my old age."

It is no accident that he is the one who carried to us the famous words of the Prophet ﷺ about who deserves our best company: "Your mother, then your mother, then your mother, then your father." He did not perform Hajj and did not marry until after she died, because he wanted to stay near her.

Hunger on the bench of the mosque

Abu Huraira became one of the Ahl al-Suffa, the poor migrants who slept on a raised bench inside the Prophet's mosque. He had no tribe in the city and no settled work, and he refused to ask anyone for food. So he went hungry, sometimes for days, and tied a stone over his stomach to bear it.

Years later, when people said he narrated too much, he explained why he knew so much: "I used to stay close to the Messenger of Allah ﷺ at the cost of filling my stomach." He would stop a passing companion and ask about a verse he already understood, hoping they would notice his hunger and feed him. Ja'far ibn Abi Talib was the most generous to them, giving until there was nothing left in his house but an empty butter dish to scrape.

Once the Prophet ﷺ saw the hunger on his face and called him in to share a pitcher of milk. Then he told Abu Huraira to call all of the Ahl al-Suffa first. Abu Huraira served every one of them, sure nothing would remain, until at last only the two of them were left, and the Prophet ﷺ told him to drink his fill.

The memory that lost not a letter

Abu Huraira shadowed the Prophet ﷺ when others went home, and asked what others did not think to ask. Once he complained that he heard so much yet forgot some of it. The Prophet ﷺ asked for his cloak, spread it, made a prayer as if pouring something into it, and told him to gather it to his chest. After that, Abu Huraira said, he never forgot a single thing he heard.

Decades later the governor Marwan tried to test him. He hid scribes behind a curtain and had Abu Huraira narrate from morning till night while they wrote it all down. A year later he asked him to narrate it again. Not a word, they said, was out of place.

His gatherings became the largest in the mosque of the Prophet ﷺ. He prayed Fajr, then sat from the forenoon until midday, telling all who came what he had heard from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

Five things to live by

One day the Prophet ﷺ asked who would take some words from him and act on them, or teach others who would. Abu Huraira said at once, "I will, O Messenger of Allah." The Prophet ﷺ took his hand and counted five things off on his fingers.

Leave what is forbidden, and you will be the most devoted of worshippers. Be content with what Allah has given you, and you will be the richest of people. Be good to your neighbour, and you will be a true believer. Love for people what you love for yourself, and you will be a true Muslim. And do not laugh too much, for too much laughter deadens the heart.

He took those words to heart. He would say the remembrance of Allah thousands of times a day, treating each one as a ransom for himself. And he held to three personal counsels from the Prophet ﷺ: to fast three days of each month, to pray two units in the forenoon, and to pray witr before sleeping.

The house where the night was never empty

When his mother died, Abu Huraira married Busra bint Ghazwan, a woman of means whose sheep he had once tended, an echo of the way the Prophet ﷺ had married Khadijah. He would sometimes finish his prayer and say aloud, "Praise be to Allah, who took Abu Huraira and gave him a wife like mine, and changed his situation from what it was."

A man who stayed in his home for seven days called him the most generous host he had ever seen. He found that Abu Huraira, his wife, and their household divided the night into thirds: one would pray, then wake the next, so that someone in that house was always standing in prayer until dawn.

He was also a soldier who fought in major battles, and a governor trusted by Umar in Bahrain and later in Madinah. When Umar found he had saved a little money, Abu Huraira gave all of it to the treasury and refused to govern again, saying he feared to speak without knowledge or to judge without gentleness.

The inheritance being given out

Abu Huraira was loved across the community. He played hide and seek with the children of Madinah and taught them to recite Qur'an even inside the game. As governor he carried his own firewood through the market, calling out for people to make way, just to make them laugh. Humour and humility went hand in hand in him.

When civil strife broke out among the Muslims, he withdrew from it entirely and took no side. One day he walked into the marketplace and called, "Come to the mosque, the inheritance of the Prophet ﷺ is being distributed." People rushed over and found only circles of Qur'an, hadith, and learning. As the scholars say, when strife grows, the circles of knowledge shrink, and he was trying to call people back.

He died about sixty years after the Prophet ﷺ. Near the end, sick and surrounded by visitors, he looked up, smiled, and said, "O Allah, I love to meet You, so love to meet me." He asked that no fuss be made over his grave, and that they hurry his bier, for he had heard the Prophet ﷺ say the righteous soul calls out to be carried quickly to its Lord.

What Abu Huraira's life teaches us

He is remembered for the sheer volume of what he narrated. But the man behind the narrations is a lesson in himself: hungry, devoted, gentle, and faithful to the small things.

  • Presence is how knowledge is earned. He learned more than anyone because he stayed close when others went home, and paid for it in an empty stomach. What we love, we make time for.
  • Honour your parents while you can. He greeted his mother at her door coming and going, prayed for her by name, and delayed his own life to keep her company. He narrated that the mother comes first, and he lived it.
  • Contentment is its own wealth. Leave the forbidden, be content with your share, be good to your neighbour, and want for others what you want for yourself. He was given five plain things and built a life on them.
  • Gentleness and humility wear well. He carried his own wood, joked with children, and stepped away from conflict to call people back to learning. Strength did not need to be loud in him.

Questions

Who was Abu Huraira?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the tribe of Daws in Yemen. An orphan and former shepherd, he spent about four years at the Prophet's side and narrated more of his sayings than any other companion.

Why did he narrate so many hadith?

He stayed close to the Prophet ﷺ when others went home, asked questions others did not, and was known for an extraordinary memory. He himself said he accompanied the Prophet ﷺ even at the cost of going hungry, so that he heard what many others missed.

Where does the name Abu Huraira come from?

It means "father of the kitten." As a young shepherd he kept a small kitten in his sleeve, and the name the Prophet ﷺ gave him became the one he loved most. His given name was changed to Abd al-Rahman.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Huraira?

The value of staying present where knowledge is found, of honouring one's parents while there is still time, of contentment with little, and of carrying real devotion with humility and gentleness.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to assume the great narrator of hadith must have been a scholar from birth, born into ease and books. He was the opposite: an orphan shepherd with a kitten in his sleeve, often so hungry he tied a stone to his stomach, who simply refused to leave the side of the one teaching him. What he could not buy with wealth he paid for with presence.

Most of us are not asked for anything dramatic. We are asked to show up, again and again, for the people and the good we say we love: to greet a parent kindly, to stay a little longer where something true is being taught, to be content, to be gentle. Carry one of those into today, the way Abu Huraira carried his cat among the sheep, and his story has done its work.
