The orphan shepherd who remembered everything

Preview: He kept a kitten in his sleeve and refused to leave the Prophet's side. We know him as Abu Huraira.

Assalamu alaikum,

The companion who carried more of the Prophet's words to us than anyone in history was not born into books or ease. He was an orphan shepherd from a tribe known for all the wrong things, who kept a small kitten in his sleeve. From that came the name he loved most: Abu Huraira, the father of the kitten (may Allah be pleased with him).

He reached Madinah with almost nothing. An old, blind mother. A servant who soon ran off. And only four years left in the life of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, whom he had crossed a desert to follow.

When his mother refused Islam and spoke harshly of the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Huraira came to him in tears. The Prophet ﷺ did not send anyone to punish her. He simply raised his hands: "O Allah, guide the mother of Abu Huraira." His mother opened her door that day with the words of faith on her lips.

He slept on a bench inside the mosque with the other poor migrants. He had no tribe in the city and would not ask anyone for food, so he went hungry, sometimes for days, and tied a stone over his stomach to bear it. Later, when people said he narrated too much, he explained it simply: "I used to stay close to the Messenger of Allah ﷺ at the cost of filling my stomach." What he could not buy with wealth, he paid for with presence.

There is a lesson in him that is easy to miss. We tend to imagine that knowledge and nearness to good come to people who are gifted, or comfortable, or lucky. Abu Huraira had none of that. He had hunger and devotion and a refusal to leave the side of the one teaching him.

Most of us are not asked for anything dramatic. We are asked to show up, again and again, for the people and the good we say we love. To honour a parent. To stay a little longer where something true is being taught. To be content with our share, and gentle with everyone.

May Allah be pleased with Abu Huraira, and gather us in the company of those who stayed close.
