Abbad ibn Bishr

The Friend of the Qur'an

One dark night on a tense expedition, a man stood guard at the mouth of a valley. He began to pray, and the prayer carried him away. An arrow struck him, then another, then a third. He pulled each one out and kept reciting, because he could not bear to leave the verses he was in.

This is the story of Abbad ibn Bishr (may Allah be pleased with him): a young man of the Ansar, an early believer, and the companion they called the friend of the Qur'an.

The Companions

Abbad ibn Bishr

The Friend of the Qur'an

A young man who fell in love with the Qur'an

Abbad ibn Bishr came from the Ansar of Madinah, from the tribe of Banu Abdul Ashhal, a branch of the Aws. He was one of their leaders, and an early believer in a city that had not yet met the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He had a sister, Umm Amir, who also embraced Islam early, and through her marriage he was connected to the family of Muhammad ibn Maslamah.

His Islam came before many of the names we know best. He accepted it through Musab ibn Umayr, the gentle teacher the Prophet ﷺ had sent ahead to Madinah to recite the Qur'an and explain the faith. It was through Musab that Abbad first heard the Qur'an, and he fell in love with it. He memorised everything Musab taught him.

By the time the Prophet ﷺ arrived in the city, Abbad had already gathered every portion of the Qur'an that had reached the people of Madinah. That detail alone tells you something about him. Whatever revelation came within his reach, he held it close, the way some people hold on to the thing they love most in the world.

The friend of the Qur'an

His tribe held a high place among the Ansar. In one narration the Prophet ﷺ named Banu Abdul Ashhal among the best of the Ansar, and it was said that some of the most righteous of the companions would be found among them. Their small mosque still stands in Madinah, and Abbad was its imam, leading his people in prayer.

His people gave him two titles. One was simply al-imam, the one who led their prayers. The other was the name that stayed with him: the friend of the Qur'an. Everything in his life seemed to return to it. His own name, Abbad, points to intense and constant worship, and he lived up to it.

The Prophet ﷺ paired him with Abu Dujana, another lover of the Qur'an, and their home became known as a house of recitation, the verses passed back and forth through the night between two men who could not get enough of them.

He narrated only a single saying from the Prophet ﷺ. To the Ansar he reported these words: that they were like the inner lining of a garment, and everyone else like the outer part. It was a quiet way of telling them they were the closest people to him.

The voice the Prophet stopped to hear

One night the Prophet ﷺ was praying, and through the dark he heard a voice reciting the Qur'an nearby. He turned, moved by what he was hearing, and asked whether anyone knew whose voice it was.

It was the voice of Abbad. The Prophet ﷺ recognised it, and made a quiet supplication for him: O Allah, have mercy on him. O Allah, forgive him.

That recitation reached further than Abbad could have known. The companion narrating the moment was a young man named Abbad ibn Abdullah ibn Zubayr, who said plainly that his father had named him after this man. A life given to the Qur'an was being remembered in the names of children not yet born.

Three arrows, and a prayer he would not leave

On the expedition of Dhat ar-Riqa, a hard and dangerous journey, the Prophet ﷺ asked who would guard the camp through the night. Abbad and Ammar volunteered, and went to the mouth of the valley. They agreed to split the watch, and Ammar lay down to sleep first.

Alone in the quiet of the night, Abbad did what he always did. He stood to pray, reciting the Qur'an. An archer found him in the dark and struck him with an arrow. He pulled it out, set it down, and kept reciting. A second arrow came, then a third. Only then, soaked in blood, did he wake Ammar.

When Ammar saw the three arrows on the ground he was astonished. Why had Abbad not woken him at the first one? Abbad answered that he had been deep in verses that filled him with awe, and that had he not feared abandoning the post the Prophet ﷺ had set him, he would have let the recitation carry him until the man took his life.

It is one of the most loved scenes in the lives of the companions: a man so held by the words of his Lord that the pain of three arrows could not pull him out of them.

Trusted with the heavy things

The Prophet ﷺ also knew Abbad as a man of courage and integrity, and gave him work that needed both. He fought at Badr and Uhud, and stood among those sent to deal with Kab ibn al-Ashraf alongside his relative Muhammad ibn Maslamah. He was present in battle after battle.

He was sent out as a collector of charity, trusted to gather what was due and bring it back, and he did exactly as he was told. After the later victories he was given the task of distributing the spoils, a role that demanded a man whose honesty was beyond question.

At the difficult expedition of Tabuk, the Prophet ﷺ made Abbad his personal guard for twenty days, watching over him and over the Muslims. Wherever trust was required, this was a name the Prophet ﷺ returned to.

The dream before Yamama

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Abbad remained what he had always been: an imam, a reciter, a man the community could lean on. At the Battle of Yamama, against the false prophet Musaylimah, he led a large group of the Ansar.

The night before, he told Abu Saeed al-Khudri of a dream. He had seen the sky open like a gate, felt himself drawn up into it, and then the gate closed behind him. He said, calmly, that he understood it to mean martyrdom, and Abu Saeed prayed that Allah would grant him the good he had seen.

The next day Abbad rode between the ranks calling out to the Ansar to break the scabbards of their swords and to set themselves apart by how hard they fought. Around four hundred men gathered behind him, and they cut their way forward until they reached the wall of Musaylimah's garden.

There he was killed, at forty-five years old. His body was so badly struck that those who found him could only recognise him by a mark upon it. The body was broken, but the soul, as he had seen in his dream, had risen.

A name that came full circle

Abbad left no sons to carry his story forward, and so he appears only briefly in the books, often beside another companion. Gather the pieces, though, and a rare man comes into view.

Remember the child named after him: Abbad ibn Abdullah ibn Zubayr. His father, Abdullah ibn Zubayr, would himself be martyred years later near the Kaaba, struck from every side, refusing to leave his prayer.

There is something quietly beautiful in that. The man who would not break his prayer for three arrows was honoured by Allah with a namesake who, in turn, would not leave his prayer even at the end. Devotion does not always leave behind a lineage. Sometimes it leaves behind an example that other lives are shaped around.

What Abbad's life teaches us

His story is not about a single act of bravery. It is about one love, carried steadily into everything he did, until it shaped how he lived and how he died.

  • Let the Qur'an be a friend, not a task. Abbad did not merely read the Qur'an; he loved it. That is why it could hold him through pain that would have stopped almost anyone else.
  • Be the one who can be trusted with the heavy thing. Charity to collect, spoils to distribute, a Prophet to guard. He was given what mattered because his honesty was never in doubt.
  • Keep your post. Even lost in worship, he would not leave the place he had been told to hold. Devotion and duty were not in conflict in him; they moved together.
  • A good life leaves a mark even without an heir. He had no sons to narrate his story, yet a companion named his child after him, and that child grew into a man worth remembering too.

Questions

Who was Abbad ibn Bishr?

He was a companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah, an early believer and a leader of his tribe. He was known for his deep love of the Qur'an, his worship, and his courage, and was given the title the friend of the Qur'an.

Why is Abbad ibn Bishr called the friend of the Qur'an?

He accepted Islam through Musab ibn Umayr and fell in love with the Qur'an, memorising all of it that had reached Madinah. His worship and recitation became the most defining feature of his life, and so his people called him the friend of the Qur'an.

What is the story of Abbad and the three arrows?

On the expedition of Dhat ar-Riqa he was guarding the camp at night and stood to pray. An archer struck him with three arrows in turn. He pulled each one out and kept reciting, waking his companion only after the third, because he could not bear to leave the verses he was in.

How did Abbad ibn Bishr die?

He was martyred at the Battle of Yamama against Musaylimah, at around forty-five years old, after leading a large group of the Ansar to the wall of the enemy's garden. The night before, he had dreamed of the sky opening for him and understood it as a sign of martyrdom.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us have something we say we love but treat like a chore. Abbad's life asks a gentle question: what would it look like to truly love the words of Allah, so that opening them felt less like a duty and more like meeting a friend? He did not get there through grand gestures. He got there by returning to it, night after night, until it became the thing that held him.

We will not be tested by arrows. We are tested by distraction, by tiredness, by a hundred small reasons to cut the prayer short. His example is an invitation to stay a little longer in what is good, to guard the small post we have been given, and to let one steady love quietly shape the rest of our day.
