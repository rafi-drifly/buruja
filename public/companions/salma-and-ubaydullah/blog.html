Salma and Ubaydullah ibn Abi Rafi

The Hands That Served the Prophet's House

Some of the most important people in the Prophet's life are the ones history almost forgot to name. We do not even know who Salma's parents were. Yet her hands were the first to hold nearly every child born into his house.

This is the story of Salma and her son Ubaydullah ibn Abi Rafi (may Allah be pleased with them): a midwife who served the family of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ across three generations, and the son whose pen carried that family's words to the world.

The Companions

Salma and Ubaydullah ibn Abi Rafi

The Hands That Served the Prophet's House

A woman the records barely name

Salma begins her story as an enslaved woman in one of the great households of Makkah. She belonged to Safiyyah bint Abd al-Muttalib, the aunt of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the mother of the companion Az-Zubair ibn al-Awwam. That placed her, from the start, inside the wider family of the Prophet ﷺ.

We do not know her lineage. We do not know her parents. By the measure of her own society, she was someone easy to overlook, a name without a famous father, the kind of person whose life rarely makes it into the records at all.

And yet she was about to be placed at the very center of the household that would change the world. This is one of the quiet patterns of the early community: people the world counted as nothing were given a closeness to the Prophet ﷺ that the powerful would have envied. Their nearness was not bought; it was earned through trust and service.

Her husband, Abu Rafi, had walked a similar road. He had once been enslaved to al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, an uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, before he was freed by the Prophet himself. Two people who started at the margins were quietly drawn into the heart of the family.

The first hands to hold his children

In a time long before hospitals, it was the midwife who met every child at the threshold of life. She was the one who waited through the long hours, who steadied the mother, who first lifted the newborn into the world. Salma was that midwife for the house of the Prophet ﷺ.

One by one, she delivered the children born to him and Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her). All of them passed first through her hands. She was there when Fatima was born. Think about what that means: the same trusted woman returned for birth after birth, until she was less a visitor and more a part of the family itself.

There is something quietly moving in that picture. The household whose names we honour above almost all others had, at its most tender moments, a freed woman they trusted completely standing beside them.

It is the kind of service that leaves no monument. No one writes poetry about the woman who waits through the night and calms a frightened heart. Yet without her, the most cherished household in our history would have been a lonelier place.

The message that brought Hamza to Islam

Salma's place in the family put her close to one of its most beloved men: Hamza, the Prophet's uncle. Hamza was a hunter, away on his expeditions, and the men of Makkah would gather around him on his return to hear his news. Religion was not something that interested him. Safiyyah, who was near to Hamza in age and deeply attached to him, trusted Salma with an errand that would echo for centuries.

Abu Jahl had humiliated and hurt the Prophet ﷺ at the Kaaba. It was Salma who carried the news to Hamza: did you hear what Abu Jahl did to your nephew? A simple question, delivered by someone the family trusted, at exactly the right moment.

Hamza marched straight to the Kaaba, struck Abu Jahl, and declared himself on the religion of his nephew. By his own admission he did not yet understand Islam. He went to the Prophet ﷺ and said as much: he had felt something, he knew there was a God, but he did not know if his heart had truly entered. The Prophet told him to ask Allah for guidance.

Faith then settled into Hamza's heart, and he became one of the great defenders of the faith. And the spark that began it all was a message, carried quietly by Salma. She did not give the sermon or strike the blow. She simply delivered the news, and Allah used it to change a life.

Freed, and never far from his side

The Prophet ﷺ freed Salma early, and she did not drift away into a quiet life of her own. She traveled with him to Madinah. She was present through his journeys and accompanied him on nearly all of his battles, part of the moving household that followed the message wherever it went.

Her husband served in his own way. Abu Rafi pitched the tent, carried messages, and handled debts and charity on the Prophet's behalf: a trusted hand for the practical work that keeps a community running. He would go and collect, deliver, and settle affairs, so that the Prophet ﷺ was free for the weight he carried.

Between them, this was a marriage of service. Two freed slaves, asking for no title and no spotlight, simply made themselves useful to the people they loved, every single day. It is easy to picture how much steadier that household must have felt with the two of them in it.

This is a kind of greatness that is easy to walk past. There is no famous speech here, no single dramatic scene. There is only a couple who showed up, again and again, and did what needed doing.

Across three generations

What Allah gave Salma to witness is rare in all of history. Years later, when the Prophet's son Ibrahim was born in Madinah to Maria (may Allah be pleased with her), it was Salma who delivered him and Salma who ran to bring the Prophet ﷺ the glad news of a son.

She delivered his grandsons too, Hasan and Husayn. The same midwife who had held his children now held the children of his children. Across the generations of that blessed house, hers were among the first hands every newborn knew.

And she was there at the end as well. Salma nursed Fatima (may Allah be pleased with her) in her final illness, and it was Salma who washed her body for burial.

Sit with that for a moment. The hands that welcomed Fatima into the world were the hands that prepared her to leave it. Few people in any age are trusted with the whole arc of a life like that, from the first breath to the last washing. It is a measure of how deeply this woman was loved and relied upon.

The son who carried the words forward

Salma narrates two reports from the Prophet ﷺ. Her husband Abu Rafi narrates around forty. But the family's legacy of knowledge reached its fullest in their son, Ubaydullah ibn Abi Rafi, and Allah blessed this couple with several children.

Just as his father had served the Prophet ﷺ, Ubaydullah attached himself to the family of Ali (may Allah be pleased with him). He became Ali's scribe, the one who put words to paper, and was appointed to collect and distribute zakat and charity. The same trusted role his father had carried for the Prophet now passed to the son, in the service of the Prophet's household.

Ubaydullah did not stop at service. He narrated from Ali, from Abu Hurayrah, and devoted himself to sacred knowledge until he became a scholar in his own right, gifted, as Dr. Omar Suleiman notes, with the ability to write and to gather what others might have let slip away.

Then came the part that ties the whole story together. Ubaydullah taught some of the most important figures of the Prophet's household: Zayn al-Abidin Ali ibn al-Husayn, Muhammad al-Baqir, and Ja'far al-Sadiq. Ibn Hajar described him as reliable, very strong in hadith, and prolific in narration. His own children, among them Muhammad, Ja'far, Ali, and Uthman, grew up to be scholars as well, carrying the tradition another generation onward.

From the margins to the heart of the legacy

Trace the chains of narration that carry the sayings of the Prophet's family, and again and again they pass through this household of freed slaves. Scholars of hadith still recite their names today without always knowing the human story behind them: a midwife, a messenger, and the son who learned to write.

A family that society once looked down upon ended up so woven into the prophetic legacy that, in over a hundred narrations from the household of the Prophet ﷺ, their link still holds. Every time those reports are read, a little of the reward returns to people who once owned nothing of their own.

This is how Islam measured people. Not by where they began, but by what they gave. The world had ranked them near the bottom, and the message lifted them by the only standard that finally matters.

Salma and her family began with nothing the world counts, and ended at the very center of the most honoured house in history. It is worth remembering whenever we are tempted to measure ourselves, or anyone else, by the wrong scale.

What Salma and her family teach us

Their story is not built on famous battles or grand speeches. It is built on quiet, faithful service repeated over a lifetime, and that is exactly why it lasts. Here is what it leaves with us.

  • Quiet service is never small. Salma delivered children, carried messages, and washed the dead. None of it sought attention, and all of it mattered more than most things that do.
  • Where you start is not where you are measured. A family that began enslaved and overlooked ended at the heart of the prophetic legacy, honoured by the chains of narration that still carry it.
  • Faithfulness can be inherited. Abu Rafi served the Prophet ﷺ, his son served the family of Ali, and his grandchildren became scholars. One household's loyalty shaped generations.
  • Be the trusted one. Salma was given the whole arc of a life, from birth to burial, because people could rely on her completely. Reliability is its own quiet honour.

Questions

Who was Salma (RA)?

She was a freed servant in the household of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the midwife who delivered his children, his son Ibrahim, and his grandsons Hasan and Husayn. She also nursed and washed his daughter Fatima (RA).

Who was Ubaydullah ibn Abi Rafi (RA)?

He was the son of Salma and Abu Rafi. He became the scribe of Ali (RA), a collector and distributor of charity, and a respected scholar of hadith who taught leading figures of the Prophet's household.

How is this family connected to Hamza accepting Islam?

Safiyyah bint Abd al-Muttalib sent Salma to tell Hamza how Abu Jahl had mistreated the Prophet ﷺ. That news moved Hamza to confront Abu Jahl and then to embrace Islam.

What can we learn from Salma and her family?

That quiet, reliable service carries great weight, that a person is measured by what they give rather than where they began, and that faithfulness can be passed down through a family for generations.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think that only the loud lives count: the leaders, the warriors, the famous names. Salma's life answers gently. She held newborns through the night, carried a message that changed a man's heart, and prepared a beloved daughter for her grave. None of it was for show, and all of it became part of something that outlived her by centuries. We do not even know when or where she died, and yet her work is still bearing fruit.

Most of our own days are made of small, unseen acts of care: for our families, our neighbours, the people who quietly depend on us. Her story is an invitation to do that work well, to be the trusted hand others can lean on, and to trust that Allah counts what the world overlooks. Carry that into today, and her story has done its work.
