The Muslim Family of Abu Lahab

When Allah Guided a House of Enemies

In all the Quran, only one enemy of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ is named and condemned by name: his uncle Abu Lahab. Children memorise the short surah about him before they can read. It is easy to assume the whole house was lost with him.

This is the story of the family he left behind (may Allah be pleased with the believers among them): a wife and a son who shared his ruin, and the daughter, the sons, and the long line of descendants whom Allah quietly drew into faith.

The Muslim Family of Abu Lahab

When Allah Guided a House of Enemies

The uncle who was meant to be a father

Abu Lahab was about twenty years older than the Prophet ﷺ, old enough to stand in for the father the Prophet never knew. His real name was Abdul Uzza ibn Abdul Muttalib, and he was the brother of Abdullah, the Prophet's father. The two brothers had grown up close, and Abdullah died young, before he even knew his wife Aminah was carrying a child.

He does not fit the picture films give him. He was strikingly handsome, intelligent, wealthy, and well dressed, with a glow to his face that earned him his nickname: Abu Lahab, the father of the flame. He was from Banu Hashim, respected by his tribe, generous to the pilgrims in a way that drew people to him.

When the Prophet ﷺ was born, it was Abu Lahab who freed the servant Thuwaybah out of sheer joy at his nephew's arrival. He saw the child as an extension of the brother he had lost. For years the two families shared a wall. Two of his sons were engaged to two daughters of the Prophet ﷺ. By every appearance, this was a household made for love.

The day the door closed

When the Prophet ﷺ climbed Mount Safa and called his relatives to worship one God, his family gathered to hear him. They had just affirmed that he was the trustworthy one, the honest one. One word of welcome from Abu Lahab, his neighbour and the father-in-law of his daughters, could have changed everything for Banu Hashim, who looked to their elders.

Instead Abu Lahab cursed him and said, "Is this what you gathered us for?" He rejected the message as a bad deal. He sold idols to the pilgrims and made his living from them. "If you cannot put it in my hand," he told his nephew, "it does not exist." Then Allah answered him in words that are now recited across the world:

He left the Prophet ﷺ standing on Safa, bewildered and alone, with only a child, Ali (may Allah be pleased with him), still beside him. Just as he left the Prophet ﷺ alone to be humiliated, Allah left him alone, named and humiliated in the Quran in a way no other enemy ever was.

May the hands of Abu Lahab be ruined! May he be ruined too! Neither his wealth nor his gains will help him: he will burn in the Flaming Fire-- and so will his wife, the firewood-carrier, with a palm-fibre rope around her neck.Quran 111:1-5

A house that hardened

What began as words became cruelty. Abu Lahab's wife was Umm Jamil, Arwa bint Harb, sister of Abu Sufyan ibn Harb. She earned a name of her own in the Quran: the carrier of firewood. She would lay thorns and spikes at the Prophet's door so he had to step over them, and tip filth onto him as he came out. It is narrated that she would even loop a thorny rope around his neck.

Abu Lahab then turned on his sons. He swore he would disown them unless they divorced the Prophet's daughters, Ruqayyah and Umm Kulthum (may Allah be pleased with them). Utbah, the elder, divorced Ruqayyah quietly. Utaybah, the younger, made a public scene: he insulted the Prophet ﷺ, tore his shirt, and spat at him.

When his own brother Hamza, not yet a Muslim, defended their nephew, Abu Lahab cut himself off from him too. During the three years of the boycott he told Quraysh to starve and torture his own clan. His brother Abu Talib died of that hardship. His former neighbour Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her) died of it. None of it moved him.

Endings that mirrored the harm

No one in this family left the world quietly. Abu Lahab did not fight at Badr; he paid another man to go in his place and waited in Makkah for good news. When word came that the Muslims had won, and that angels had fought beside them, his rage spilled over and he beat a servant who rejoiced at the news. Days later a wound on his head turned septic and his body began to rot. His own family left him to die alone, afraid of what he carried, and his body lay three days before anyone would bury it.

Utaybah, the son who had spat at the Prophet ﷺ, had a prayer made against him: "O Allah, set one of Your dogs upon him." On a journey to Syria his companions slept in a ring around him for protection. In the night a lion walked past every one of them and went straight to him. "Muhammad has killed me," he cried out, "and he is in Makkah while I am in Syria."

Umm Jamil, grieving her husband and son, was later found dead with the same thorny rope she had used against the Prophet ﷺ wound around her own neck. Three lives ended in a shape that matched, almost exactly, the way each had harmed him. The deeper punishment, the lecture reminds us, belongs to the next life, not this one.

The daughter who knew

Everyone else on the family tree became Muslim. The first was a daughter, Durrah bint Abi Lahab (may Allah be pleased with her). She had been married off after the broken engagement to another man, who then died fighting the Prophet ﷺ at Badr. While the rest of her family burned for revenge, Durrah said simply, "I always knew he was a prophet."

She fled Makkah alone, carrying her children, and walked into Madinah. People did not know what to make of the daughter of Abu Lahab arriving at the Prophet's door. She gave her testimony of faith and became one of the early emigrants. But some of the women began to taunt her: "What good is your migration? You are the daughter of the man Allah cursed."

When she came to him in tears, the Prophet ﷺ stood up angry, prayed with the people, and then addressed them from the pulpit. He told them his intercession reached his family as long as they believed, and that no living person should be hurt over someone who has died. Then he chose a husband for her: Zayd ibn Harithah, known as the most handsome of the companions, the one the angel Jibril resembled when he came to earth.

The cousins he asked Allah for

Years later, when Makkah opened to Islam, the Prophet ﷺ looked around and asked, "Where are my two nephews, Utbah and Mu'attab? I do not see them." They had fled to the edges of the city, sure that the son who divorced his daughter could expect no welcome. The Prophet ﷺ sent for them anyway.

When they came, he walked between them, took both their hands, and spoke with them for a long time, putting them at ease. He let Mu'attab keep the very house he had seized from the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah. Afterward his face was full of joy. He said he had asked his Lord for his two cousins, and Allah had given them to him. At the battle of Hunayn, both stood firm beside him, and Mu'attab lost an eye in his defence.

From this house came a long line of believers. Mu'attab named his children Abdullah and Muhammad. Among Abu Lahab's descendants were scholars of hadith who carried Quran in their hearts, names that join the Prophet's family and his uncles in a single chain, every one of them reciting the very surah that names their forefather.

What his money and children could not buy

Abu Lahab once boasted that if his nephew turned out to be right, he would simply pay his own way out of the Fire on the Day of Judgement. The Quran answered: neither his wealth nor his gains will help him. He trusted his money, and his money did nothing for him.

Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) once parted two of Abu Lahab's children who were quarrelling and said of them, "These are what he earned." When the verse says his gains will not help him, he explained, it includes his children. He took pride in them, and they became Muslims who taught his disgrace to their own children and grandchildren.

There is a quiet ache in that. Had he believed, every one of those descendants would have been a continuing reward for him, a legacy flowing back to him long after his death. Instead his name survives only as a warning, recited by the faithful, including his own family line.

One drink of water

Al-Abbas, the Prophet's uncle, later saw his brother Abu Lahab in a dream, in a terrible state. He asked him what he had found in the next life. Abu Lahab answered that he had known no relief at all since leaving this world, except for a single small drink of water.

That one relief, he said, was for the moment he freed the servant Thuwaybah out of joy on the night the Prophet ﷺ was born. A whole life of enmity, and the only mercy he could point to was one act of honour toward the Prophet ﷺ, decades earlier, before any of it began.

It is a sobering picture, and a hopeful one. The same house that produced the man named in the Quran also produced believers, emigrants, and scholars. Guidance, in the end, was never about lineage. It was about who turned toward the truth when it reached them.

What this family teaches us

This is not one life but a whole household, and that is the point. Within a single family were the worst enemy of the message and some of its most devoted followers.

  • Guidance is not inherited. Abu Lahab's own children and grandchildren came to faith. No one is condemned by their family name, and no one is saved by it either.
  • Wealth secures nothing that matters. He thought his fortune could buy him out of anything. It bought him nothing. What we store with Allah is the only wealth that holds.
  • Mercy outlasts cruelty. The Prophet ﷺ took the hands of the sons who had wronged his house, and asked Allah for them. He chose to welcome rather than to settle scores.
  • Do not weigh people by their relatives. When Durrah was mocked for her father, the Prophet ﷺ defended her openly. A person is met for who they are, not for who came before them.

Questions

Who was Abu Lahab?

He was an uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, a wealthy and respected man of Quraysh whose real name was Abdul Uzza ibn Abdul Muttalib. He became the Prophet's harshest enemy and is the only opponent named and condemned by name in the Quran, in Surah Al-Masad.

Did anyone in Abu Lahab's family become Muslim?

Yes. Apart from his wife Umm Jamil and his son Utaybah, the rest of his children embraced Islam, including his daughter Durrah and his sons Utbah and Mu'attab (may Allah be pleased with them). His later descendants included scholars of hadith who memorised the Quran.

Who was Durrah bint Abi Lahab?

She was a daughter of Abu Lahab who accepted Islam, migrated alone to Madinah with her children, and is counted among the early emigrants. When some women taunted her for her father, the Prophet ﷺ defended her and said no living person should be hurt over someone who has died.

What does this story teach us?

That guidance is not passed down by blood, that wealth cannot buy what truly matters, and that mercy can reach even a house marked by enmity. Each person answers for their own turning toward the truth.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to read someone out of mercy because of where they come from: a hard family, a bad name, a past that seems to settle the matter. This family quietly refuses that logic. The same house held the man condemned by name in the Quran and the daughter who walked alone to faith, the sons whose hands the Prophet ﷺ took in his own. Nothing was decided by lineage. Everything turned on the choice each person made when the truth reached them.

Carry that into today with the people you are tempted to give up on, including, sometimes, yourself. Do not measure a soul by its worst relative or its worst year. Leave the door open the way the Prophet ﷺ left it open, and ask Allah for the ones you love rather than counting their faults. Guidance has surprised harder houses than ours.
