Zinneera and Aflah

The Tortured Ones

Some of the earliest believers left us no last name, sometimes no first name at all. They had no clan to protect them and no power in the eyes of their city. What they had was certainty, and they paid for it with their bodies.

This is the story of two of them: Zinneera and Aflah (may Allah be pleased with them), enslaved in Makkah, tortured for saying that there is only one God, and remembered for a faith that did not bend.

Zinneera and Aflah

The Tortured Ones

The ones with no one to protect them

When we look back at the first Muslims, the names we know best often belong to people of standing. But beneath them was another group: the enslaved, the poor, the ones with no tribe to shield them. Of many of these believers we have only a single line, or a first name, or nothing at all.

These were the people who put everything on the line in Makkah without seeing any light at the end of the tunnel. No protection. No promise that things would get easier in their lifetime. Only the conviction that what they had found was true. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ saw their suffering and longed to do more for them than he was able to do in those early years.

Some of them lived long, honoured lives and went on to Madinah, even past the death of the Prophet ﷺ. Others were killed under the persecution itself, dying as they were tortured, and a number of them died without leaving us their names.

Allah knows each of them. They were forerunners, among the very first of this ummah to give themselves to it. Two of these believers come down to us with stories worth holding onto.

Abu Bakr, who bought their freedom

Almost all of these stories pass through one man: Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). When he saw a person being tortured for the sake of Allah, especially the enslaved, he would go and pay whatever it took to purchase their freedom.

The disbelievers questioned his motives. Even his own father told him that these were people who could be of no use to him, that his money was wasted on them. Abu Bakr answered that he was not seeking anything from them at all. He was seeking something from Allah, a reward that only Allah could give and that no person of this world could ever repay.

It is said that he freed several people before he made his journey to Madinah. Some of their names are known to us. Among those he rescued from that cruelty was the man we will come to know as Aflah.

A handful of names we still keep

Before we come to the two, it is worth pausing on the others, because they fill in the world these believers lived in. Among the enslaved women freed for the sake of Allah was one who had been brutally mistreated by Umar ibn al-Khattab in the days before his Islam, beaten until her tormentor grew tired. Her freedom too was purchased.

There was a woman named Lubaynah who took the wrath of the powerful, all for the sake of Allah. There were others whose names survive only in passing, and many more whose names did not survive at all.

It is a sobering thing to sit with. A whole layer of the first believers gave everything, and history barely recorded that they existed. We do not know what they looked like or where they were buried, or what words passed their lips in their final moments. We only know that they held on, and that Allah, who needs no record, knows every one of them by name.

Zinneera, beaten until she could not see

Zinneera was an enslaved woman in Makkah, and to be enslaved there did not only put you at the mercy of any cruel person. It often put you at the mercy of the worst of them. She fell under the power of Abu Jahl, of the clan of Makhzum.

He singled her out. There are narrations from more than one person about the particular harshness he showed her, harassing her and beating her publicly and in ugly ways. This was the same hatred that took the life of the first martyr of this ummah, and Zinneera too was marked for that kind of cruelty.

He beat her until she lost her eyesight. He beat her until she became blind. Such was the severity of the blows that fell on this woman who had done nothing but believe.

Then he twisted even that. When people spoke about what had happened to her, he claimed it was not him. It was the idols she had rejected, he said, that had taken her sight away as punishment for turning from them.

They have no power

Picture her position for a moment. She had been beaten to blindness in the name of these idols. She held no rank in society and had no one to defend her. And still, blind and broken, she answered him.

She said they were lying. By Allah, she said, no harm comes from these idols. They do not benefit anyone. They have no power at all. It is Allah who holds all power, from whom every benefit comes, and in whose will alone any harm can take place.

Think of the strength in that. Stripped of her sight, with nothing left to lose and no audience to impress, she still refused to grant these stones a single ounce of the power her tormentor claimed for them.

When she said it, Allah returned her sight to her. She could see again. The ones who had mocked her now had no honest explanation. The same idols they had credited with her blindness could hardly be credited with her healing. So they reached for the only thing left and called it sorcery, a proof of the magic of the Prophet ﷺ. But it was no such thing. It was a quiet gift from Allah to a woman whose certainty had not wavered for an instant, an answer to her words from the only One who could give one.

Aflah, the twin in torture

The other believer is best described by who he resembled. Aflah was tortured in the same way as Bilal, by the same man, Umayyah ibn Khalaf. If you want to picture his ordeal, picture everything that was done to Bilal, and know that it was done to him too.

He was tortured in the scorching heat. He was forced to lie out in the desert with a heavy stone pressed onto his chest until it nearly killed him. He was deprived of food and water. He was deprived of sleep. He was lashed in public, again and again, as he held his ground and refused to leave his faith.

The last we hear of his torment is the worst of it. The pagans tied a rope around his feet and his neck and dragged his nearly lifeless body through the streets of Makkah until he was almost dead.

Abu Bakr saw him in that state and bought his freedom. Then, unlike Bilal who stayed with Abu Bakr in Makkah and went on to Madinah, Aflah was sent to Abyssinia to escape the heavy persecution. According to the few accounts we have, he died there soon after.

Names worth remembering

We carry very little information about Zinneera and Aflah. A few lines, a handful of narrations. Yet what they endured is part of the reason this beautiful religion reached us at all.

There is a small story Dr. Omar Suleiman tells about Aflah. He once met a man who had named his child after this tortured companion, and the man did not know who the companion was. When he was told that this was a person tortured the way Bilal was tortured, it brought him to tears.

That is the point. These are names worth knowing and worth using. We should say them and think about the people behind them: a woman who answered her torturer while blind, and a man who endured the desert and the rope rather than deny his Lord. To name a child after one of them, or simply to remember them, is to keep a thread unbroken back to the very first days of this faith.

Behind each of them is a level of certainty that held firm under the worst that Makkah could do, and a place with Allah that we can only try to imagine.

What their lives teach us

Their stories are short, only a few lines each, but they carry something most of us are quietly looking for: a faith that holds when there is no reward in sight and no one watching.

  • Certainty does not need an audience. Zinneera had no rank, no protector, and no platform. Blind and beaten, she still answered her torturer plainly: the idols have no power, only Allah does. Real conviction is what you hold when no one is impressed by it and nothing is gained from it.
  • Worth is not measured by usefulness. Abu Bakr was told plainly that these people could do nothing for him. He freed them anyway, seeking only Allah. The people the world steps over are often the ones nearest to God, and treating them as disposable is the error of those who tortured them.
  • Some are not spared the hardship. Aflah was rescued from the rope, then sent away, and died far from home soon after. Not every faithful life ends in visible relief or a happy turn. Their reward was never meant to arrive in this world, and that does not make it any smaller.
  • We inherited this from people we barely know. Much of what we practice today was carried through the bodies of believers whose names we have lost. We pray and fast in the light they bought with their suffering. Gratitude, and saying their names when we have them, is the least we owe them.

Questions

Who were Zinneera and Aflah?

They were among the enslaved early Muslims of Makkah who were tortured for accepting Islam. Zinneera (may Allah be pleased with her) was a woman beaten until she lost her sight, and Aflah (may Allah be pleased with him) was a man tortured in the same way as Bilal.

What happened to Zinneera's eyesight?

Abu Jahl beat her until she became blind, then claimed her own rejected idols had punished her. She answered that the idols had no power, that only Allah does. When she said this, Allah returned her sight to her.

How was Aflah connected to Bilal?

Aflah was tortured by the same man who tortured Bilal, Umayyah ibn Khalaf, and in the same brutal ways. Abu Bakr bought his freedom and sent him to Abyssinia, where he is reported to have died soon after.

What can we learn from their lives?

That sincere certainty needs no audience, that the people the world overlooks can be the closest to Allah, and that we received this faith through believers who suffered for it before us.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to imagine that real faith is for people with a voice, a following, some way to be seen. Zinneera and Aflah had none of that. They were enslaved and overlooked, and what they held onto was held in private, in the heat of the desert and under the blows of a tormentor, where only Allah was watching. That is exactly what makes it stay with us.

Most of our own tests are quiet ones too: a truth to hold when no one would notice if we let it go, a kindness no one will thank us for, a moment of patience that no one sees. Their lives ask a simple thing of us today. Be certain when it costs you something. Value the people the world ignores rather than measuring them by what they can do for you. And remember, in your prayer, the ones who carried this faith to you with their bodies and their names half forgotten, and ask Allah to be pleased with them as He was pleased with their patience.
