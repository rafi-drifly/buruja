Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah

The Trustworthy One

He led armies and conquered cities, yet when a visitor came to his home they found one cooking pot, one cushion, and a little dry bread. The leader of all of Greater Syria owned almost nothing, and he wanted it that way.

This is the story of Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the ten promised Paradise, the man the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ called the trustworthy one of this ummah, and a soul the world could not seem to change.

The Companions

Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah

The Trustworthy One

A name, and a quiet beginning

His given name was Amir, son of Abdullah, son of al-Jarrah. He belonged to Quraysh, but to a small and distant branch, not one of the powerful houses that ruled Makkah. He had the honour of the tribe without its influence, which suited a man who would never reach for either. We know him by his grandfather rather than his father, and one reason the scholars offer, painfully, is that the father had become known for something the family wished to leave behind.

He grew up in a family of archers and learned the full craft of war young. By six he could handle a bow. By twelve he could outmatch seasoned warriors. He could read the ground an army had crossed and tell you how many men and animals had passed, and when. He was being shaped for something, long before he knew what it was.

He was a teenager, eighteen or nineteen, when Islam reached him, accepted at the hands of Abu Bakr (RA) among the very first group of believers. He learned in the house of al-Arqam, was among the first to memorise the Quran as it was revealed, and made both the migration to Abyssinia and later the migration to Madinah. Of his years in Makkah we know little except two things: that he was persecuted, and that he was patient.

The day he chose the Prophet over his father

Badr was called the Day of the Criterion, the day faith was separated from blood. Companions found themselves facing brothers, uncles, and fathers across the field. For Abu Ubaydah the test came in its hardest form.

It is reported, in a difficult narration found in the later books, that his own father came to the battle with a single aim: to kill the son who had left the idols. Abu Ubaydah avoided him through the day, until his father cornered him and left him no choice. Defending his own life, he struck him down. It is one reason some say he is known by his grandfather's name, and not his father's.

The narration is not in the earliest sources, so we hold it gently. But the test it describes was real for many of the companions, and the Quran spoke to exactly that wound: that the messenger of Allah ﷺ had become dearer to these believers than their own kin, and that Allah had lifted the burden from their hearts.

لَّا تَجِدُ قَوْمًا يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱللَّهِ وَٱلْيَوْمِ ٱلْـَٔاخِرِ يُوَآدُّونَ مَنْ حَآدَّ ٱللَّهَ وَرَسُولَهُۥ وَلَوْ كَانُوٓا۟ ءَابَآءَهُمْ أَوْ أَبْنَآءَهُمْ أَوْ إِخْوَٰنَهُمْ أَوْ عَشِيرَتَهُمْ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ كَتَبَ فِى قُلُوبِهِمُ ٱلْإِيمَـٰنَ وَأَيَّدَهُم بِرُوحٍ مِّنْهُ ۖ وَيُدْخِلُهُمْ جَنَّـٰتٍ تَجْرِى مِن تَحْتِهَا ٱلْأَنْهَـٰرُ خَـٰلِدِينَ فِيهَا ۚ رَضِىَ ٱللَّهُ عَنْهُمْ وَرَضُوا۟ عَنْهُ ۚ أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ حِزْبُ ٱللَّهِ ۚ أَلَآ إِنَّ حِزْبَ ٱللَّهِ هُمُ ٱلْمُفْلِحُونَ[Prophet], you will not find people who truly believe in God and the Last Day giving their loyalty to those who oppose God and His Messenger, even though they may be their fathers, sons, brothers, or other relations: these are the people in whose hearts God has inscribed faith, and whom He has strengthened with His spirit. He will let them enter Gardens graced with flowing streams, where they will stay: God is well pleased with them, and they with Him. They are on God's side, and God's side will be the one to prosper.Quran 58:22

The teeth he gave at Uhud

Uhud was the day the battle turned and a small band of companions threw their bodies between the arrows and the Prophet ﷺ. He was struck again and again, knocked into a ditch, and two rings of his helmet were driven into the flesh of his cheek.

Abu Ubaydah came to him and did something no one else thought to do. Rather than risk hurting the Prophet's face with his hands, he gripped the iron rings with his teeth and pulled. The metal came free. His two front teeth came with it.

He carried a slight catch in his speech for the rest of his life. Those who heard him said it had only made his speech more beautiful than before. A man had given his teeth to spare the Prophet ﷺ pain, and was left more pleasant to listen to than ever.

The trustworthy one of this ummah

Two Christian leaders came from Najran to debate the Prophet ﷺ, then asked him to send back with them a man they could trust to teach and to judge fairly. Send us, they said, only a trustworthy man. The Prophet ﷺ promised them one who was strong and trustworthy.

The companions longed to be that man. They came forward, made themselves seen, hoping to be chosen, even Umar (RA) among them. The next morning the Prophet ﷺ called out, "Stand up, Abu Ubaydah," and said that every ummah has a trustworthy one, and the trustworthy one of this ummah is Abu Ubaydah.

It was rare praise. Al-Amin, the trustworthy, was one of the Prophet's own titles, and the scholars note that he never gave one of his own titles to anyone else. To hand it to Abu Ubaydah was to set him apart. Ibn Masud (RA) counted his closest companions as three: Abu Bakr, Umar, and Abu Ubaydah. Ibn Umar (RA) named the three of Quraysh whose character and gentle speech outshone the rest, and listed him among them.

Years later, when Aisha (RA) was asked who was most beloved to the Prophet ﷺ, she named Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Uthman, then Abu Ubaydah. Again and again he is placed in that same small company by the people who knew him best, and he seems never to have noticed or sought it.

The story of the whale

The Prophet ﷺ sent him out at the head of three hundred men on a mission toward the sea. Along the way their food ran low, and as their commander he shared it out fairly, a little less each day, until the daily ration was a single date for each man.

Jabir (RA), who was with them, was later asked how they survived on one date a day. We came to know its value, he said, only when even that ran out. They were a desert people who had never known the sea. Then Allah cast a whale onto the shore, vast as a mountain.

They ate from it for eighteen days. Abu Ubaydah had two of its ribs set upright in the ground, and a rider passed beneath them on his camel without touching the top. They sat together inside its eye sockets. It is recorded in Sahih al-Bukhari, and it is the kind of provision Allah sends to those who hold fast and stay patient.

A leader who never wanted to lead

He was tall, strong, and striking, yet easy-going and soft-spoken, never one to dominate a gathering. He wore rough clothes and cared nothing for the world. He married little and left almost no trace of a private life. He was, simply, a soldier in the service of the Prophet ﷺ, living from one mission to the next.

Because he never reached for leadership, leadership kept being placed on him. He fought in every battle, and in each he held some position as a general, carrying a banner beside the Prophet ﷺ. Sent out once alongside Amr ibn al-As, with no clear commander named, he let Amr lead and even prayed behind this newer Muslim rather than argue. We do not work for this world, he would say. We are brothers for the sake of Allah.

When Makkah was finally entered in triumph, the army came in three columns, and it was Abu Ubaydah who walked at the front of the centre, carrying the banner before the Prophet ﷺ. When they arrived, he was the one who tended the Prophet's mount and pitched his tent. He never once boasted of any of it. We know these things only because others told them about him.

There is a pattern worth noticing in that day of victory. Those who had been persecuted in Makkah in the early years, men like Abu Ubaydah and Bilal, were the ones honoured to lead when the city was finally opened. The Prophet ﷺ also praised him often, saying simply, what a man Abu Ubaydah is.

The man the world could not change

Under Abu Bakr and then Umar (RA), he was made the commander in Greater Syria. He built mosques and hospitals there and a dam to hold back the floods. When Umar replaced the famed general Khalid ibn al-Walid with him, he received the letter mid-campaign and quietly waited until the battle was won before telling Khalid, refusing to disrupt the army or wound his brother.

Umar came once to see how he lived. He found no furniture, no possessions, just a pot, a cushion, and a little bread. Umar wept. Abu Ubaydah had warned him: there is nothing here for you to see except what will make your eyes shed tears. And Umar said the line that became his epitaph: the world has changed all of us, except you, Abu Ubaydah.

When the plague struck Greater Syria, Umar tried to call him to safety in Madinah. Abu Ubaydah understood the kindness and refused it. I will not leave my army, he wrote, and I am pleased with the decree of my Lord; if I die in this plague it is martyrdom. He had himself narrated that one who dies of plague dies a martyr. He was struck soon after. As he lay dying he reminded his men that every child of Adam must die, and that the wisest is the one most humble before Allah and most ready for what is to come. Umar later said that had Abu Ubaydah lived, he would have made him his successor and met his Lord at peace with the choice.

What Abu Ubaydah's life teaches us

His was not a story of grand speeches. It was trust kept quietly, over a lifetime, until the word trustworthy belonged to him alone.

  • Trust is built in small, unseen choices. He earned the title al-Amin not in one moment but in a thousand: apportioning food fairly, holding back a letter to protect a brother, never once using a position for himself.
  • The best leaders do not chase leadership. He let a newer Muslim lead him and prayed behind him without complaint. Because he never reached for authority, authority could be trusted in his hands.
  • Let the world pass through your hands, not your heart. He commanded a province and owned a pot and a cushion. Umar wept and said the world had changed everyone but him. That is a freedom worth wanting.
  • Peace comes from accepting Allah's decree. Offered an escape from the plague, he stayed with his people, content with whatever Allah had written. He met death without fear because he had already made his peace.

Questions

Who was Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, one of the ten given glad tidings of Paradise, and a leading general. The Prophet ﷺ called him the trustworthy one (al-Amin) of this ummah, and he later commanded the Muslim armies in Greater Syria.

Why is Abu Ubaydah called the trustworthy one of the ummah?

When two leaders from Najran asked the Prophet ﷺ to send a trustworthy man back with them, he chose Abu Ubaydah and said that every ummah has a trustworthy one, and the trustworthy one of this ummah is Abu Ubaydah. Al-Amin was one of the Prophet's own titles, which made the praise especially rare.

What happened to Abu Ubaydah at the battle of Uhud?

When two rings of the Prophet's helmet were driven into his cheek, Abu Ubaydah pulled them free with his teeth rather than risk hurting him with his hands. He lost his two front teeth doing so, and it was said his speech became only more pleasant afterwards.

How did Abu Ubaydah die?

He died in the plague that struck Greater Syria. Umar (RA) tried to call him to safety, but he refused to leave his army and accepted Allah's decree, content to die a martyr. He himself had narrated that one who dies of plague dies a martyr.

Why this story stays with us

We tend to measure a life by what it gathers: titles, comfort, a name people remember. Abu Ubaydah gathered almost nothing and was trusted with almost everything. He held command lightly, owned little on purpose, and let every honour pass straight through him to Allah. The one thing he kept was his trustworthiness, and in the end that was the one thing worth keeping.

Most of us are not asked to lead armies. We are asked to be reliable in small things: to give a fair share, to keep a promise no one is checking, to hold a position without letting it change us. Today, ask where you can be the trustworthy one, the person others can lean on without fear. Live like that quietly enough, for long enough, and the word may one day belong to you too.
