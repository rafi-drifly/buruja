Addas of Ta'if

The First Fruit of Ta'if

On what the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ would later call the worst day of his life, he sat bleeding under a tree in a garden, alone, with no friend left to turn to. The next person to walk toward him was not a chief or a scholar. He was a slave carrying a plate of grapes.

This is the story of Addas (may Allah be pleased with him): a Christian servant from far away who recognised the truth in a single word, and became the first person to believe from the city of Ta'if.

The Companions

Addas of Ta'if

The First Fruit of Ta'if

The city that should have welcomed him

Ta'if was not like the rest of Arabia. Set up in the mountains, it had greenery, flowing water, and real seasons. It grew fruit and flowers of every kind, and its roses are famous to this day. Almost all the fruit that reached Makkah came from Ta'if, so its people were among the wealthiest and most comfortable in the region.

When the Prophet ﷺ was shown a dream of a place full of green, his mind went straight to Ta'if. It was the natural place to go: prosperous, respected, and close enough to matter. He even had a connection there, an elderly relative married to one of its chiefs, to ease his way in. Everything about the choice made sense.

He went carrying high hopes, and he went in the lowest year of his life, after the deaths of those who had protected and loved him. Hope that high, placed in a moment that low, can break a person. He did not ask why. He simply went.

It is worth holding this next to what came later. Years afterwards, a city far further away, full of people who had never even seen his face, would take him in and love him more than they loved themselves. Faith is not handed out by wealth or distance or reason. It is a gift, and Allah decides where it lands.

The worst day

He met the three chiefs of the city and put his request plainly. Accept this message, he said, or at least give me shelter and safety until Allah opens a way between me and my people. And if you will do neither, then at least do not tell Makkah that I came to you asking for refuge.

Their answer was worse than the stones that came after. One said, could Allah not find someone other than you to send. Another said he would tear the cloth of the Kaaba before he believed him. The third swore he would never even speak to him. Then they did the one thing he had begged them not to: they sent word to Makkah, and they lined up the children and the fools of the town to drive him out.

He left under a rain of stones, kicked and spat on along a road that ran for miles. Blood ran from the wounds and from the stones gathered in his sandals, and the beating came so fast he had no chance to clear them. By the time it stopped he barely knew where he was.

Along the way he passed the elderly woman he had hoped would help him, the wife of one of the chiefs. She felt for him and wanted to do something. He said only that all he had found from her family was beating and rejection and humiliation. There was no anger in it, just the plain truth of a man who had come asking for shelter and been given stones.

A plate of grapes

He came to rest in a garden outside the city and sat under a tree, weeping and turning to Allah, with no one in the world to help him. The garden belonged to two men of Makkah, both of them among his enemies, men who would one day die fighting against him at Badr. They were watching him from the house.

Even hard hearts can feel a moment open in them. They did not go to him, and they did not bring him into their home, and they offered no comfort to a man they had known for years. But they called their slave, a man named Addas, and told him to take some grapes out to the broken stranger under the tree.

Addas carried the plate over and set it before the weeping stranger. When the Prophet ﷺ reached for the food, he said, "Bismillah," in the name of Allah, and began to eat. It was the smallest of things, two words before a meal. But it stopped Addas where he stood, because he had never once heard them in that land.

The word from his own land

"The people of this land do not say those words," Addas said. In all his years of slavery in that place, he had never met a single person who worshipped one God. So where had this come from?

The Prophet ﷺ answered his question with a question. Where are you from? Nineveh, the man replied. "The land of my brother Yunus," the Prophet ﷺ said. Addas was startled. How do you know Yunus? Who are you? And the Prophet ﷺ told him: Yunus was a prophet, and I am a prophet, and Allah sent us both with the same message, to call people to worship Him alone.

From the house, the two men watched a slave they thought they owned bend down to kiss the hands of the man under the tree, reaching even for his feet. Addas had found, in the middle of a stranger's grief, the one thing he had been missing his whole life. When they called him back and mocked him, he only said: no one knows what this man knows except that he is a prophet of Allah.

A follower of Yunus

Of all the people Allah could have sent to comfort His Prophet ﷺ on that day, He sent a follower of Yunus. Not a follower of Musa, not a scholar of the elites, but a servant who carried in his heart the memory of the one prophet who had once walked away from his people too soon.

There is a reason that lands so gently. Among the earliest words revealed to the Prophet ﷺ was a command tied to that very prophet: to be patient, and not to be like the man in the whale. After the cruellest rejection of his life, when any of us might have given up on people entirely, Allah quietly sent him someone from Yunus, as if to say: do not walk away. You do not know who your people will turn out to be.

Yunus had given up and left, and yet Allah did not leave him.

Wait patiently [Prophet] for your Lord's judgement: do not be like the man in the whale who called out in distress: if his Lord's grace had not reached him, he would have been left, abandoned and blameworthy, on the barren shore, but his Lord chose him and made him one of the Righteous.Quran 68:48-50

And then he forgave them

There is one more turn to this story that is easy to miss. Years later, long after Ta'if had stoned him out of its streets, a delegation came to the Prophet ﷺ to accept Islam. Among them, by some accounts, was one of the very chiefs who had given him that cruel answer in the worst hour of his life.

He could have reminded the man. He could have asked him to remember the stones, the mockery, the children lined up to drive him out. He did none of it. He accepted the man's Islam and let it go, the way Yusuf forgave the brothers who had thrown him into a well.

So this is a story about being received when you are at your lowest, and it is also a story about forgiving those who put you there. The same heart that wept under that tree had room, in the end, for the people who made him weep.

The one we know almost nothing about

We know Addas for a single moment and almost nothing else. We do not know what happened to him afterwards, where he went, whether he married, or how his life ended. He appears once, gives his whole heart, and then is gone from the record.

But think of the road that brought him there. A man taken from Nineveh in Iraq, passed from owner to owner across the world, set down at last as a slave in a garden in Ta'if, on the exact day the Prophet ﷺ would come and sit beneath that tree. Sometimes Allah brings a person to faith in chains, after a journey no one could have planned. Faith, it turns out, is a kind of sustenance. Allah gives it to some and not to others, and He chooses the time, the place, and the person.

In every age the message tends to reach the weak before the powerful, because they carry the fewest barriers between themselves and the truth. The Prophet ﷺ himself said to look for him among the weak, that it is through them that a people is helped and given victory. The chiefs of Ta'if, with all their wealth, could not see what a slave saw in an instant.

The first fruit of that bitter city was not its richest man or its proudest name. It was Addas. And that is exactly how this message has always moved through the world.

What the story of Addas teaches us

His whole place in history is one short meeting in a garden. That is part of the point: a single sincere moment, given fully to Allah, is never wasted.

  • One sincere moment can be your whole story. Addas is remembered for a few minutes under a tree. He held nothing back in them, and that was enough to be written down forever.
  • Guidance reaches the humble first. A slave recognised in an instant what the chiefs of the city, with all their comfort, refused to see. Status is no advantage before the truth.
  • Keep your part, and leave the rest to Allah. The Prophet ﷺ did everything he could and was rejected, yet Allah sent a believer to him anyway, in the last place he would have looked.
  • Do not give up on people too soon. On his most painful day, Allah reminded him not to walk away. We rarely know who will turn toward the truth, or when.

Questions

Who was Addas of Ta'if?

Addas (may Allah be pleased with him) was a Christian slave, originally from Nineveh in Iraq, who served two men of Makkah who owned a garden in Ta'if. He met the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ there and is counted as the first person to believe from Ta'if.

How did Addas come to meet the Prophet?

After the people of Ta'if drove the Prophet ﷺ out with stones, he rested in a garden. The owners sent their slave Addas with a plate of grapes. When the Prophet ﷺ said "Bismillah," Addas was surprised, and a short conversation led him to recognise the Prophet ﷺ.

Why does the story connect Addas to the Prophet Yunus?

Addas was from Nineveh, the land of the Prophet Yunus. The Prophet ﷺ called Yunus his brother in prophethood. Among the earliest revelations to the Prophet ﷺ was a verse about Yunus, urging patience and warning against giving up on one's people.

What happened to Addas after that day?

The records do not tell us. We know him only for that single meeting in the garden. What became of him afterwards is part of what makes his story so quietly moving.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never have a famous life. We will not be written about, and our names will not be counted among the great ones. What we will have are moments: small openings where we can choose sincerity over indifference, where we can recognise something true and bend our hearts toward it before working out what it might cost us. Addas had one such moment, and he gave himself to it completely. That is all that was asked of him, and it was enough.

His story is a reminder that you are never too low, too unknown, or too far from the centre of things to be reached. Faith can find you in a garden, in a kitchen, in the middle of an ordinary day. When it does, the only question that matters is whether, like Addas, you will lean toward it without counting the cost.
