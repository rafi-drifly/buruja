The Companions

Uthman ibn Affan

Selfless Even When Slandered

A man was sent into a city that had once driven the Muslims out, to speak for them and then come home. A rumour reached the camp that he had been killed. What happened next was not a march to war. It was a quiet pledge under a tree, and one of the men being honoured was not even there.

This is the second part of the story of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him): the companion the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ represented with his own hand, the man he warned of his death more than ten times, and the one who, when that day came, would not let a single drop of blood be spilled for his sake.

Uthman ibn Affan

Selfless Even When Slandered

The hand that stood in for his

On the way to Makkah for a pilgrimage they would not be allowed to complete, the Muslims stopped at Hudaybiyyah. Quraysh blocked the road. The Prophet ﷺ needed someone to enter the city and negotiate, and he chose Uthman. Uthman had a tribe that would protect him, and old ties of respect among the people of Makkah from before Islam.

Uthman went in. Quraysh held him there and let the days drag on. Then word came back to the camp that he had been killed. Imagine the grief in those hearts: not over a stranger, but over a man they loved, killed, they believed, while carrying their trust.

The Prophet ﷺ gathered the people under a tree and took a pledge from them, one by one. It was a pledge unto death: that they would march on Makkah and not turn back. Around fourteen hundred people gave it, with only one hypocrite holding back. Of them the Prophet ﷺ said they were the best people on the face of the earth. And Allah testified to what was in their hearts:

God was pleased with the believers when they swore allegiance to you [Prophet] under the tree: He knew what was in their hearts and so He sent tranquillity down to them and rewarded them with a speedy triumphQuran 48:18

This is for Uthman

Here is the part that belongs to Uthman alone. The whole pledge was sparked by the rumour of his death, and yet he was the one man not present to give it. So when the Prophet ﷺ had taken the pledge of everyone there, he clasped one of his own hands with the other and said: this is for Uthman.

He took his own hand to stand in for the hand of an absent man. It is hard to imagine a gentler way to honour someone. Many would have given anything to be the hand of the Prophet ﷺ that day, and that honour was given to Uthman while he was miles away in Makkah.

Inside the city, Quraysh had offered Uthman a private favour: he could make tawaf around the Kaaba in peace, even if the other Muslims could not. He refused. He would not circle the House before the Prophet ﷺ did. When some wondered aloud whether Uthman might simply stay and complete it, the Prophet ﷺ answered for his character: he knew Uthman would not do tawaf until he did. Allah protected him, and he came home.

Told of his death, more than ten times

There is a quiet, heavy thread running through Uthman's life. By one count, the Prophet ﷺ told him on no fewer than ten occasions that he would one day be killed. Not the most hated man in Makkah, but the most beloved. The kindest and most generous of the Muslims. Who would ever want to harm Uthman? And still the warning came, again and again.

Abu Musa al-Ash'ari (RA) described one of them. He was guarding the gate of a garden where the Prophet ﷺ sat alone in thought, his feet over the edge of a well. Abu Bakr asked to enter, and the Prophet ﷺ said to let him in with the glad tidings of Paradise. Then Umar, the same. Then a third man asked permission, and this time the Prophet ﷺ sat up and said: let him in, and give him the glad tidings of Paradise after a hardship that will strike him.

It was Uthman. The other two were given Paradise plainly. For Uthman alone there was a price attached: Paradise, after a trial. He answered with one sentence: Allahul Musta'an, from Allah alone is help sought. He did not flinch and he did not ask to be spared. He simply walked in and sat down across from them.

The garment he was told not to remove

On the mountain of Uhud, with Abu Bakr, Umar and Uthman beside him, the Prophet ﷺ once felt the rock tremble and said: be firm, Uhud, for upon you are a prophet, a man of truth, and two martyrs. Two of the men with him were being told, plainly, how they would die.

To Uthman in particular the Prophet ﷺ gave a strange and tender instruction. Perhaps Allah will clothe you in a garment, he said, and if the hypocrites try to make you take it off, do not remove it until you meet me. The garment was leadership. Years later, when the trials closed in and Uthman wondered whether stepping down might end them, Abdullah ibn Abbas (RA) reminded him of exactly these words: do not take off a garment that Allah clothed you with.

There was another time he was called near and the Prophet ﷺ whispered something to him, and Uthman's face changed colour as he listened. Asked if he understood the weight of it, Uthman said his ears had heard and his heart had accepted. He knew what was coming. He spent the rest of his life carrying that knowledge quietly.

How a good man becomes a target

Uthman's leadership was long, and most of it was good: Islam spread, the community grew, and much was built. We tend to remember only the last years, when a fitna gathered against him. Why him, of all people? Because the surest way to halt all that good was not to fight it from outside, but to stir division within. When people are consumed by conflict, they stop building, and by the time they see the harm, it is already done.

It began with half-truths. A man from Egypt once cornered Abdullah ibn Umar (RA) at Hajj. Did you know Uthman fled at Uhud? Yes. Did you know he was not at Badr? Yes. Did you know he was not at the Pledge of Ridwan? Yes. The man was scandalised: how could you let this man lead? Each fact was true. Each conclusion was false.

Uthman was absent from Uhud and was forgiven for it in the Quran. He missed Badr because the Prophet ﷺ told him to stay and care for his sick wife, the Prophet's own daughter, and was counted among Badr's veterans anyway. And the Pledge of Ridwan was sparked by him: the Prophet ﷺ had stood in for his hand. Yet stripped of their context, these truths became a weapon, while companions were still alive to remember the truth. The rumours grew: nepotism, misused money, claims he had changed the call to prayer. Lie built on lie until his home was under siege.

He would not let blood be shed for him

Watch which qualities held when everything else fell away. The first was the Quran. Uthman used to say that if our hearts were truly pure, they would never tire of the words of Allah. He hated to let a day pass without looking into the pages of the Quran. Even with his house surrounded, one of his household called out to the crowd that this was a man who would revive whole nights in prayer with the Quran.

The second was his modesty, his hayah. The Prophet ﷺ once received him with a care he had not shown Abu Bakr or Umar, sitting up and straightening his clothes, and explained: should I not feel shy before a man the very angels feel shy before? That same modesty meant Uthman would never stand before the crowd listing what he had done for them. He had read the verse warning believers not to ruin their charity by boasting of it, and he lived it.

The third was his refusal to harm. He feared being an oppressor so deeply that he once begged a young servant he had accidentally hurt to take retribution and pinch him back, saying the pain of this world is easier than the pain of the next. So when Ali, al-Hasan, al-Husayn, Talha and al-Zubayr offered to fight for him, his answer was final: do not shed a single drop of blood in my cause. He would rather be the first caliph killed by members of this ummah than have one of them killed in his name.

On his last day he fasted, and slept, and saw the Prophet ﷺ in a dream. Have they kept you from water, Uthman? Yes. From food? Yes. From praying in my mosque? Yes. Then the Prophet ﷺ said: tonight you will break your fast with us. He woke calm and glad. He was killed soon after, the Quran open in front of him, reading still.

What the second part of Uthman's life teaches us

His worst years were the ones he never asked for. What they reveal is a man whose character did not bend when it was tested at the very edge.

  • Be careful what you repeat. The fitna against Uthman was built from true facts stripped of their context. Long before social media, half-truths passed by word of mouth were enough to harm one of the best of people.
  • Modesty keeps its own good quiet. Uthman could have silenced his critics by listing all he had given. His hayah would not let him boast, even to save himself.
  • Sincerity is steady when it costs you. Everything he had done for the community was repaid with a siege, and he did not turn bitter. His loyalty was to Allah and to the ummah, not to their gratitude.
  • A constant heart shows at the end. He died reading the Quran because he had lived reading it. The peace of a final moment is built quietly over a whole life.

Questions

What was the Pledge of Ridwan?

It was a pledge the Prophet ﷺ took from around fourteen hundred companions under a tree at Hudaybiyyah, after a rumour that Uthman had been killed in Makkah. They pledged not to turn back, and Allah praised them in the Quran for what was in their hearts.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ use his own hand for Uthman?

Uthman was the reason for the pledge but was absent in Makkah as the Prophet's envoy. So when the Prophet ﷺ had taken everyone's pledge, he clasped his own hands together and said, this is for Uthman, honouring him while he was away.

How did Uthman ibn Affan die?

After his home in Madinah was placed under siege by people misled by rumours against him, he was killed while reading the Quran. He had refused to let anyone fight or shed blood to defend him.

What can we learn from this part of his life?

How easily true facts can be twisted into slander, how modesty keeps a person from boasting even in their own defence, and how a life close to the Quran shapes the way a person meets hardship and death.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never face a siege. But we all know what it is to be misjudged, to hear a true thing about us repeated until it becomes a lie, to feel the pull to defend ourselves loudly and settle the score. Uthman shows another way. He kept reading. He kept quiet about his own good. He refused to let his pain become someone else's wound.

There is a kind of strength that does not look like strength at all: the strength to be wronged and not pass the harm along. Carry one piece of him into today. Guard what you repeat about others. Let your good deeds stay between you and Allah. And when you are wronged, ask for the steadiness to answer it without bitterness, the way a heart full of the Quran answers everything.
