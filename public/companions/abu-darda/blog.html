The Companions

Abu Darda

The Wise Man of the Ummah

He was the last of the great Ansar to accept Islam. While his closest friend was taking the pledge with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, he was still at home, dressing his idol in fine cloth and the most expensive perfume he owned.

This is the story of Abu Darda (may Allah be pleased with him): the merchant who walked away from his businesses, the man the Prophet ﷺ called the wise man of his ummah, and the scholar whose every saying still reads like a sermon.

The Companions

Abu Darda

The Wise Man of the Ummah

The man who came late

His name was Uwaymir, from the tribe of Khazraj in Yathrib. He was wealthy, beloved by his people, and known for his handsome appearance and his fine clothes. He owned businesses across the city. He was not a man who chased the conflicts and quarrels of his people, and they spoke well of him.

When Islam came, Abu Darda was not hostile to it. He simply was not interested. He had something the people of Makkah rarely had: he sincerely loved his idol. The Makkans had used their idols for power and barely believed in them. Abu Darda truly worshipped his, dressing it in his best garments and perfuming it, devoting himself to it for years.

His closest friend, Abdullah ibn Rawahah, accepted Islam early and was among those who took the pledge at Aqabah. He told Abu Darda what had happened. Abu Darda's reply was gentle and firm: good for you, but I am happy with my religion. He even checked on his friend's safety when Abdullah went out to Badr. He was the last major Ansari still holding back.

The broken idol

One day, while Abu Darda was out at work, Abdullah ibn Rawahah came to the house. He went to the idol and broke it, saying that everything called upon besides Allah is falsehood. Then he left.

Abu Darda came home to find his wife crying before the shattered idol. He began to speak to it: why did you not stop him, why could you not defend yourself. His wife answered with one quiet line that changed everything. If it could protect or benefit anyone, she said, it would have protected and benefited itself.

He asked for water, washed, put on his best clothes, and went to the Prophet ﷺ to accept Islam. When Abdullah ibn Rawahah saw him approaching, he feared his friend had come for revenge. But the Prophet ﷺ said that he was coming to become Muslim, and that Allah had promised him Abu Darda would believe. The brightest among us can be carried far, and then come home all at once.

Catching up

Abu Darda had missed years with the Prophet ﷺ, and he set out to make up every hour of it. He memorised the Quran. He stood in the front row of every prayer. He sat close to the Prophet ﷺ and asked the companions about everything he had missed. He became one of the few to memorise the entire Quran in the Prophet's lifetime and recite it back to him.

He had missed Badr, but at Uhud he was one of the handful who stayed close to the Prophet ﷺ when most had fled. He fought with his sword until he had no sword, then threw arrows until he had no arrows, then threw stones. He was wounded many times. The Prophet ﷺ asked who that was, and when told it was Abu Darda, he praised him and called him the wise man of his ummah.

There is a lesson in this that he himself drew. The man who once seemed furthest behind became the most knowledgeable of the Ansar. No one should feel they have fallen too far back to begin. The point is to start, and to push forward toward Allah.

A heart that could not be scattered

Abu Darda was honest about a struggle most people hide. He had been a well-known merchant, and when Islam came he tried to balance his trade with his worship. He could not. So he sold his businesses and gave himself to worship instead.

He explained it in words that still cut clean. I seek refuge in Allah, he said, from a scattered heart. When they asked what a scattered heart was, he said: that Allah had given him a business in every valley, so his mind was always in one valley or another, never whole, never present.

Other companions kept their wealth and their worship together, and that was the higher path. Abu Darda knew his own weakness and chose the path that kept his heart in one piece. His wife said it plainly: your brother Abu Darda has no need of this world. He became known as the ascetic of the Ansar.

He was not condemning trade, and he never pretended his choice was the only one. He was simply honest about himself. He had a conflict he could not resolve, and rather than let it quietly drain his worship, he named it and acted. There is a kind of self-knowledge in that which is rarer than wealth.

Beautiful character, learned by heart

The hadith Abu Darda narrated tended to gather around one theme: good character. He narrated that nothing is heavier on the scale on the Day of Judgement than good character. And then he lived it. His wife found him standing in prayer one night, weeping, repeating a single supplication until dawn: O Allah, You have made my outward form beautiful, so make my character beautiful too.

He narrated that when you pray for your brother in his absence, an angel says, and for you the same. So he kept 360 friends for the sake of Allah and prayed for every one of them by name each night, and for their parents too. When his wife asked why, he said the angels would be calling out for him in the last third of the night while he did it.

When he heard the verse about the man dragged toward the Fire, he went straight home and told his wife they would feed a poor person every single day from then on, so they would never fall into that description. He was a man who heard knowledge, memorised it, and turned it into something he actually did.

Wisdom seemed to pour out of him after that, in sayings so deep that it was said almost any one of them could stand as a Friday sermon. Asked what his most constant worship was, those close to him pointed not to a ritual but to reflection: he said sitting and contemplating for an hour was better than standing in prayer through the night. It is a quiet instruction for us, to put the phone away for ten minutes and simply think, with Allah as the focus.

The teacher of Damascus

When the people of Syria needed teachers, Abu Darda was sent among them, and he settled in Damascus as its scholar and judge. There he built what may be the first organised system of Quran circles in Islam. At Fajr the mosque held two hundred circles, each with ten students and a teacher he had trained, and he sat in the corner listening to the whole mosque hum with recitation, testing each one in turn.

He walked through the streets with people following him like a king, one asking about law, another about hadith, another about poetry, and he answered them all. Yet when he was made governor, his students came to congratulate him and found him grieving. Are you congratulating me, he asked, over a trust that Allah throws people into the Fire over?

He kept almost nothing. When the caliph Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) heard he was not keeping his salary, Abu Darda reminded him of the Prophet's words: take from this world only what a traveller takes. He said everything he owned could fit on a single camel, and that the one who carries the least will rise the quickest. When he narrated a hadith, he always smiled, because, he said, the Prophet ﷺ never narrated to them except with a smile.

A proposal in Paradise

His greatest fear, despite all of it, was hypocrisy. A student once found him in prayer seeking refuge from being a hypocrite and asked, in surprise, what someone like him had to fear. Abu Darda answered: who can feel safe? He feared standing before Allah and being asked what he had done with what he had learned.

When he was dying, his wife Umm Darda sat at his bedside. She reminded him that he had asked her father for her hand in this world, and asked him now to seek her hand from Allah in Paradise. He accepted. Then he turned to his students, still teaching, and asked who among them would prepare for a day like the one he was in.

He died in tranquillity, with no debt and no grudge left behind. For a man who had been a judge and a governor, it was remarkable that not one person came forward with a complaint against him. The character he begged Allah for every night had been granted. And when Umm Darda was later offered marriage by the powerful Muawiyah, she refused, saying she was already engaged to Abu Darda in Paradise.

What Abu Darda's life teaches us

He started late and ended ahead, and the reason was never talent. It was that he turned everything he learned into something he lived.

  • It is never too late to begin. He was the last of the great Ansar to believe, and he became the most knowledgeable of them. No one is too far behind to start moving toward Allah.
  • Knowledge is meant to be lived. He did not just memorise the hadith about good character and feeding the poor. He wept through the night for character, and fed a poor person every day for life.
  • Guard your heart from being scattered. He gave up businesses in every valley so his heart could be in one place. We can ask the same of our attention, our screens, and our days.
  • Pray for people by name. He kept a list of 360 friends and prayed for each of them at night, in secret. A quiet habit, and one anyone can begin tonight.

Questions

Who was Abu Darda (RA)?

Abu Darda, whose given name was Uwaymir, was a companion from the Ansar of Madinah. He became one of the foremost scholars among the companions and later served as the scholar, judge, and teacher of the Quran in Damascus.

Why is Abu Darda called the wise man of the ummah?

After he fought to defend the Prophet ﷺ at the battle of Uhud, the Prophet ﷺ praised him and called him the wise man of his ummah. He went on to be regarded as one of the most knowledgeable companions of his time.

Was Abu Darda really the last of the Ansar to accept Islam?

He is described as the last of the major Ansari figures to accept Islam. Before he believed, he was a sincere worshipper of his idol, until his friend Abdullah ibn Rawahah broke it and he came to the Prophet ﷺ soon after.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Darda?

That it is never too late to begin, that knowledge is meant to be acted upon, that a divided heart is worth guarding against, and that praying for others by name is a quiet habit with great weight.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to think the people ahead of us in faith were simply born that way. Abu Darda was not. He arrived last, behind everyone he knew, with years of worship spent on the wrong thing. What set him apart was not a head start. It was that he refused to let anything he learned stay as words. He heard about good character and wept for it through the night. He heard about feeding the poor and fed someone every day until he died.

Most of us already know more good than we practise. His life is a gentle question about the distance between the two. Pick one thing you believe but do not yet live, and live it today, even in a small way. That is the whole of his example, and it is within reach of an ordinary afternoon.
