Ali ibn Abi Talib

The First Young Believer

He was a boy of about ten when he woke one night and found two people praying in a way he had never seen. He did not know yet what it meant. He only knew that the home he lived in was quietly changing.

This is the story of Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him): raised in the house of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the first child to accept Islam, and the young man who would lie down in the Prophet's bed on the most dangerous night of his life.

The Companions

Ali ibn Abi Talib

The First Young Believer

The mother who raised a prophet

Ali was the youngest son of Abu Talib, the Prophet's uncle. His mother was Fatima bint Asad, and her place in this story is easy to overlook. She was the first woman of Banu Hashim, the Prophet's own clan, to accept Islam, counted among the first ten or eleven believers of all. When the message reached her, she did not hesitate.

Long before that, she had raised the Prophet ﷺ himself, from the age of six until he married, caring for him as one of her own. He remembered the years of hunger in that household, and how she would keep little for herself and give her food instead to him and to her children. The family of Abu Talib lived in deep poverty, and that poverty never really left them.

The Prophet ﷺ called her a mother and loved her dearly. When she was dying he came to her, wrapped her in his own shirt, and sat by her bed making supplication for her until she passed. He stepped into her grave himself, smoothing the earth, and prayed over her. This was the woman who shaped the home Ali was born into.

Taken into the Prophet's home

A famine struck Makkah, and large families in poverty felt it hardest. The Prophet ﷺ had recently married Khadijah, and his circumstances had eased. So he went to his uncle Abbas with a quiet plan: each of them would take one of Abu Talib's children to raise, to lighten the load on a struggling father.

They came to Abu Talib together. Abbas took Ali's older brother Jafar. The Prophet ﷺ lifted up Ali, still an infant, embraced him, and said he would take him. From that day, Ali grew up inside the household of the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah, a child of that house in everything but name.

There is something striking in how this unfolded. The Prophet ﷺ never formally adopted Ali, and Khadijah never nursed him, though both would have seemed natural. Had either happened, Ali could never later have married the Prophet's daughter Fatima. Without anyone planning it, the way was being kept open.

The first night he saw them pray

Ali was watching the household from the inside as everything began to change. He saw the Prophet ﷺ come home shaken in those early days. He sensed that their lives were no longer ordinary. And one night, before anyone had spoken to him about any of it, he saw the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah praying together.

He waited until they finished, then asked plainly what this was. The Prophet ﷺ told him: this is the religion of Allah, chosen for Himself, carried by His messengers. He called Ali to worship Allah alone, with no partner, and to turn away from the idols. He did not treat the boy as a child to be brushed aside. He gave him the full weight of it.

Ali answered with a maturity beyond his years. He said this was something he had never heard before, and that he could not decide without speaking to his father, Abu Talib. The Prophet ﷺ smiled and accepted that, and asked him only one thing: if you do not accept this, keep it quiet.

A heart that could not sleep

That night Ali sat back and could not rest. The words kept turning over inside him. He repeated them to himself, trying to understand what he had heard, until they settled into his heart. By morning the decision was no longer in question.

He went to the Prophet ﷺ and asked him to repeat the whole message once more. The Prophet ﷺ said it again: worship Allah alone, reject the idols. And Ali answered simply, "I submit." He never did go to his father first. He had heard the truth and chosen it.

From that day he prayed alongside the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah. He is remembered as the first child, the first young person, to accept Islam. He had grown up in a house that never worshipped idols, so he had never bowed to one in his life.

The boy who stood when no one else would

When the Prophet ﷺ first took his message to his wider family, Ali went along on those small gatherings, silent because he was only a boy, but always there. Then came the open call. The Prophet ﷺ stood and asked his people who would support him, and the crowd that had just called him truthful fell completely silent.

Ali looked around at that silence and raised his hand. He was the only one. A child standing up for an uncertain cause, surrounded by grown men who said nothing. The reaction was scorn, and the gathering broke apart. But Ali had stood.

There is a quiet scene from those years that stays with you. A traveller passing the Kaaba at midday saw a man with his hands raised, a boy beside him doing the same, and a woman standing behind them. He asked who they were. Abbas told him: that is my nephew, his wife, and the son of his uncle, and as far as I know there is no one else on earth who follows this religion. Three people, in the open, alone.

Ali had a part to play even then. He was good at reading faces, and the Prophet ﷺ would have him watch the crowds near the Kaaba for anyone who looked as though they were searching for the truth. It was through Ali, quietly noticing a seeker in the marketplace, that some came to the message. A boy turned his small gift into service.

The night of the bed

When the order came to migrate, the Prophet ﷺ faced a plot to kill him. Seven clans had agreed to share the act, so that no single family could be blamed and no blood feud could follow. On the eve of his departure, the Prophet ﷺ asked Ali to sleep in his bed and take his place.

Ali knew what waited outside. Men were coming to kill whoever lay there, and if they pulled back the cover and found him instead, they might well kill him anyway. He had no hesitation. He later said he slept that night as he had never slept before, calm and trusting in Allah, while the danger gathered at the door.

And the Prophet ﷺ, even while men plotted his murder, gave Ali one more task: to stay behind and return the belongings that people had left with him for safekeeping. Those same people who wanted him dead still trusted him with their property. Ali returned every trust, then set out for Madinah on foot, with no companion and no camel, too poor for either, walking by night to escape the heat.

A brother, and a friendship that joked

In Madinah the Prophet ﷺ paired the believers as brothers, and he took Ali as his own brother. Across about thirty years of age between them, the bond looked less like father and son and more like two brothers. The Prophet ﷺ would seek his counsel, and the two of them shared an ease that could even joke.

Ali still lived by the work of his hands. One day he was paid twelve dates for a full day of hard labour. Knowing the Prophet ﷺ was hungrier than he was, he set six in front of him. When the companions gathered, Ali teased that the Prophet ﷺ ate his share and left him hungry. The Prophet ﷺ answered that Ali swallowed his date stones whole. That was the warmth between them.

When Ali was once left behind to care for those who stayed in Madinah, and the hypocrites mocked it as a demotion, the Prophet ﷺ reassured him: are you not content to be to me as Harun was to Musa, except that there is no prophet after me. It was never a setting aside. It was trust placed in someone the Prophet ﷺ loved.

The companions came to know Ali by a simple sign. He himself narrated that the Prophet ﷺ told him only a true believer would love him, and only a hypocrite would hate him. At the battle of Khaybar, the Prophet ﷺ said he would give the banner to one who loved Allah and His Messenger and was beloved to them. Ali was suffering from his eyes and could barely see, so the Prophet ﷺ applied some of his own saliva to them, and Ali could see again. He carried the banner that day, and Allah granted victory through him. His standing was never about power or lineage. It was tied to faith itself.

What Ali's life teaches us

Ali's story begins with a child who took the truth seriously when no one expected him to. That seriousness is the thread through everything that follows.

  • Take the truth seriously, even when young. A boy of ten sat awake all night turning the message over until it reached his heart. He did not treat it as something to grow into later.
  • Courage is standing when others stay silent. When the whole gathering went quiet, Ali was the only one to raise his hand. Real courage is often simply being the one who does not look away.
  • Trust is shown in the hardest moment. He lay down in the Prophet's bed with killers at the door, and slept in peace. Conviction is what steadies the heart when the danger is real.
  • Be the one others can rely on. Even people who wished the Prophet ﷺ dead trusted him with their property, and he returned every trust through Ali. Honesty does not depend on who is watching.

Questions

Who was Ali ibn Abi Talib?

He was the cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the youngest son of Abu Talib, and was raised in the Prophet's own household from infancy. He is remembered as the first child to accept Islam.

Why is Ali called the first young believer?

Growing up in the Prophet's home, Ali saw him and Khadijah praying, asked what it meant, and after one sleepless night accepting it in his heart, he submitted. He is widely counted as the first child, or first young person, to embrace Islam.

What happened on the night of the migration?

Knowing several clans had plotted to kill him, the Prophet ﷺ asked Ali to sleep in his bed and take his place so he could leave. Ali did so without hesitation, then stayed behind to return the trusts people had left with the Prophet ﷺ.

What can we learn from the life of Ali?

To take the truth seriously even when young, to stand for what is right when others stay silent, to trust Allah in the hardest moments, and to be a person others can rely on completely.

Why this story stays with us

It would have been easy for a child to treat all of this as something for the grown-ups. Ali did the opposite. He listened as though every word weighed something, he lay awake until the truth settled in him, and from then on he carried it with a steadiness that did not waver, even with killers at the door.

Most of us first meet what is right while we are still young, or still unsure, or still afraid of what others will think. Ali's life is an invitation to take it seriously anyway: to be the one who stands when the room goes quiet, who can be trusted with what others leave in our hands, who rests in Allah when there is every reason to panic. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
