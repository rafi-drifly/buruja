The first doors that opened to the Prophet in Madinah

Preview: An old chief who believed at once, and a young man who would not trade Paradise for safety.

Assalamu alaikum,

Before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ reached the heart of Madinah, he stopped on its edge, in a small settlement called Quba. He stayed there for two weeks, and two ordinary homes opened their doors to him.

The first belonged to Kulthum ibn al-Hadm (may Allah be pleased with him), the elderly chief of his tribe. When he heard the Prophet ﷺ was coming, he did not pause to weigh what it might cost him. He believed at once, and said that everything he owned should be set aside for the Prophet's arrival. In Makkah it had been the tribal chiefs who fought hardest against the message. Here was a chief saying instead: let him come, and we will honour him.

The second home belonged to a young man named Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah. By night the Prophet ﷺ stayed with the elders in Kulthum's house, and by day he sat with the youth in Sa'ad's. He divided his time evenly between them, so that no one, old or young, was made to feel forgotten.

Sa'ad's own story reaches all the way to Badr. He and his father both longed to fight beside the Prophet ﷺ, but only one could go; the other had to stay and care for the family. The father asked to be the one. Sa'ad answered that if the matter were anything other than Paradise, he would have given way. So they cast lots for the chance to march out, and the lot fell to Sa'ad. He was martyred at Badr, among the very few Muslims who fell in a battle the Muslims won. His father, Khaythamah, followed him as a martyr at Uhud.

The scholars say the two of them are held within two verses of the Quran: the son among those who loved to purify themselves, the father among the believers who kept their pledge to Allah and never changed.

Both of the men who hosted the Prophet ﷺ in Quba died early, and they were the first two of the Ansar to be buried in the blessed ground of Madinah. They had simply opened their doors, and their names are remembered for it.

Most of us are tested the same quiet way: in how we welcome people, and in what we are willing to give up for what is right.
