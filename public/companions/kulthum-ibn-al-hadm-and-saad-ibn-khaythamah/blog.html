The Hosts of Masjid Quba

Before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ reached the heart of Madinah, he stopped on its edge, in a small settlement called Quba. For two weeks he stayed there, and two ordinary homes opened their doors to him.

This is the story of the hosts of Quba (may Allah be pleased with them): Kulthum ibn al-Hadm, the elder who welcomed the Prophet ﷺ at night, and Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah, the young man who gathered the youth by day.

The Hosts of Masjid Quba

A land that still carried its divisions

Madinah, in those years, was a place shaped by old quarrels. Each tribe held its own quarter, and the very ground reflected the rivalries between them. Two great tribes, the Aws and the Khazraj, had spent years at war, and the land itself was carved up between their many sub-tribes.

Into this the Prophet ﷺ arrived as a complete stranger, a man from Makkah now being embraced by people who had been trying to kill one another not long before. He was the most intentional of people: the way he spoke to one person, the words he chose for each tribe, the time he gave to each group, none of it was careless. He moved among them with great care, giving each tribe and even each age group its rightful place, never moving from one home to another without honouring the people he was leaving behind. Uniting old enemies was not an accident of his arrival; it was the work of a leader who refused to let anyone feel overlooked.

Quba sat on the outskirts, the settlement of a sub-tribe of the Aws. There were no hypocrites among its people in those days, only the sincere. One way to picture it is a camp holding the best of those who fled Makkah and the best of those who came out to receive them, with the Prophet ﷺ among them. For two weeks, that was Quba.

The people there were the first to receive him, and the homes that took him in belonged to two men whose names are tied to that place forever.

Kulthum ibn al-Hadm, the elder who believed at once

Kulthum ibn al-Hadm was the chief of his tribe in Quba, an old man known for his humility. When word reached him that the Prophet ﷺ was coming, he did not hesitate or weigh what it might cost him. He believed at once, and he said, simply, that everything he owned should be set aside for the Prophet's arrival.

This was not a small thing. In Makkah it was the tribal chiefs who had given the Prophet ﷺ the hardest time. Here was a chief, an elder, wise and humble, saying instead: we believe in him, let him come, we will receive him and honour him.

The Prophet ﷺ reached Quba on a Monday, the twelfth of the month, and stayed in the home of Kulthum through the first week. He stayed there in the evenings, holding his conversations with the tribal elders, and Kulthum became to him almost as a brother. The Prophet ﷺ himself helped lay the stones of Masjid Quba and prepared food alongside the people, an active leader from the very first day.

A mosque the Quran would honour

Masjid Quba is one of the most overlooked mosques in Islam, and yet the Quran honours both the place and the people who built it. The Prophet ﷺ said that to pray there was like performing a complete umrah, and that whoever made wudu at home and then prayed two units in Quba would have a full umrah written for them.

He kept going back. Even after his own mosque was built in the centre of Madinah, he would walk out to Quba on foot and pray two units there. In one narration he said that to pray in Quba was more beloved to him than something far greater, and that if people knew its worth they would hurry to it. The people of Quba, he said, were people who loved to purify themselves, and a verse came down naming exactly that quality.

When the verse was revealed, the Prophet ﷺ came to them and told them how beautifully Allah had praised them. They explained that, even before Islam, they had been careful to wash themselves for prayer, and that they had simply carried that habit with them. The praise, the scholars noted, reached both the outward washing and the purity of the heart that Allah loves most:

[Prophet], never pray in that mosque. You should rather pray in a mosque founded from its first day on consciousness of God: in this mosque there are men who desire to grow in purity- God loves those who seek to purify themselves.Quran 9:108

Two homes, the elders and the youth

The way the Prophet ﷺ arranged those two weeks tells you a great deal about him. By night he stayed with Kulthum, the elder, meeting the chiefs and the senior men. By day he went to a second home, which the people called the house of the unmarried, where the young single men of Quba stayed.

He spent his days with the youth and his evenings with the elders, and then did it again the next day, dividing his time evenly between the two. No group was made to feel like an afterthought. The leader they had waited months for was sitting with their old men in the evening and with their young men in the day.

The host of that second home was a young man named Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah. He was not a chief and he was not old, yet he held a place in this story that few could match.

Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah and the pledge at Badr

Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah had been among the very first from Madinah to meet the Prophet ﷺ. He was there at the first pledge, the only one from his tribe present to give his hand, young as he was. When the Prophet ﷺ came, the youth of Quba clung to him, and Sa'ad's home, the house of the singles, was where they gathered.

Then came Badr. Sa'ad and his father, Khaythamah, both wished to go out and fight beside the Prophet ﷺ, but one of them had to stay behind to care for the family. The father asked his son to let him be the one to go, and to stay back himself. Sa'ad answered that if the matter at stake were anything other than Paradise, he would have given way to his father. But this was Paradise, and he could not.

So they did the only thing left to them: they cast lots for the chance to march out with the Prophet ﷺ. The lot fell to Sa'ad. He went to Badr and was among the very few Muslims to be killed there, in a battle the Muslims won. He was a man who had given the pledge, hosted the Prophet ﷺ, and fallen as a martyr at Badr, all before his life had truly begun.

A father who followed his son

When the army returned from Badr, news of the victory had already reached Madinah. Khaythamah went out searching the ranks for his son, calling his name. They told him that Sa'ad had been martyred. The grief of it was sharpened by the memory of their last conversation, the casting of lots, the son who would not give up Paradise.

But the story did not end there. At the Battle of Uhud, Khaythamah carried the same longing his son had carried. He stayed firm with the Prophet ﷺ when others scattered in the chaos, and he too was granted what he had asked for. There is no other father and son known to have a martyr at Badr and a father who followed him in that way.

The scholars say that the two of them are held within two verses of the Quran. The son is among those who loved to purify themselves. The father is among the believers who were true to the pledge they had made with Allah:

There are men among the believers who honoured their pledge to God: some of them have fulfilled it by death, and some are still waiting. They have not changed in the least.Quran 33:23

The first to be buried in that land

Both of the men who hosted the Prophet ﷺ in Quba died early. Kulthum lived only long enough to believe, to welcome the Prophet ﷺ, to be his host, and then he was gone. Sa'ad was martyred at Badr soon after.

The scholars differ over which of them died first, but they agree on this: the first two of the Ansar to be buried in the blessed cemetery of Madinah were these two men. The two who had received the Prophet ﷺ in different ways, the elder and the youth, were the first to be laid in that ground.

There was a third host in Quba as well, a man so quietly devoted that the verse praising those who love to purify themselves was said to be about him. When he died, it was said over his grave that there was not a man left on earth who could claim to be better than the one buried there. These were not people who pushed themselves forward. They simply served, purely, and let Allah raise their names.

They had set the stage. The Prophet ﷺ would move on into the heart of Madinah and his own mosque would be built, but the first doors that opened to him belonged to a humble old chief and a young man who would not trade Paradise for safety.

What the hosts of Quba teach us

Their story is short, and most of it happens in the background. That is part of its quiet power.

  • Say yes before you count the cost. Kulthum was a chief with everything to protect, and he believed at once and offered all he had. Some openings in life ask to be answered quickly, not weighed forever.
  • Give everyone their rightful place. The Prophet ﷺ divided his days between the elders and the youth so no one felt forgotten. The people around us notice when we make room for them.
  • Purity begins on the inside. Quba was praised for washing carefully for prayer, but the praise reached the heart too. Cleaning out envy, gossip, and spite is the harder, deeper work.
  • Hold to what is true, and do not change. Sa'ad and his father longed to keep their pledge and never wavered. A faith that stays the same when no one is watching is the kind that lasts.

Questions

Who were Kulthum ibn al-Hadm and Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah?

Two companions from the settlement of Quba on the edge of Madinah. Kulthum ibn al-Hadm was the elder chief who hosted the Prophet ﷺ in his home by night, and Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah was the young man who hosted the youth by day. Both were among the Ansar, the helpers of Madinah.

Why is Masjid Quba significant?

It was the first mosque built after the migration, and the Quran honours both the mosque and its people. The Prophet ﷺ said that praying there was like performing a complete umrah, and he would walk out to pray there even after his own mosque was built.

What happened to Sa'ad ibn Khaythamah?

He and his father both wished to fight at Badr, but only one could go. They cast lots, the lot fell to Sa'ad, and he was martyred there among the very few Muslims who fell in that battle. His father, Khaythamah, was later martyred at the Battle of Uhud.

What can we learn from the hosts of Quba?

To answer a good calling without endless hesitation, to give every person around us their rightful place, to care for the purity of the heart and not only the outside, and to hold firmly to what is true.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never lead a tribe or fall in a famous battle. We will host quietly, welcome the people who pass through our days, and decide, again and again, whether to give our time to those who need it. The hosts of Quba did exactly that, and their names are remembered for it.

There is something to carry from each of them. From Kulthum, the readiness to say yes to what is right before fear has time to argue. From Sa'ad, the refusal to trade what matters most for what is merely comfortable. Take one of those into today, into your own home and your own prayer, and their story has done its work.
