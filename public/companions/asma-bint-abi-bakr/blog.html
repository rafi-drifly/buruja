Asma bint Abi Bakr

The Possessor of Two Belts

She was heavily pregnant when she climbed the mountain in the dark, again and again, carrying food to two men hiding in a cave. One of them was her father. The other was the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and half of Makkah was hunting for him.

This is the story of Asma bint Abi Bakr (may Allah be pleased with her): the daughter of the first man, the one who fed the Hijra, and a woman whose courage held from a slap at the door to a tyrant at the end of her long life.

The Companions

Asma bint Abi Bakr

The Possessor of Two Belts

A daughter of the first house

Asma was the daughter of Abu Bakr as-Siddiq, the closest companion of the Prophet ﷺ and the first free man to believe. Her full brother was Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr, and her younger half-sister was Aisha, who would become a mother of the believers. It was a family in which courage seemed to run in the blood, passed from parent to child.

She accepted Islam young, in the first small group of believers, at the hands of her own father. We hear almost nothing of her early years in Makkah. Those were the hidden, dangerous years, when the community was scattered and quiet, and little of what the believers endured was written down.

She married Zubair ibn al-Awwam, a man who had also embraced Islam at the hands of Abu Bakr. He owned almost nothing. Within the first year of their marriage she was expecting their first child, and it was then, while she was heavily pregnant, that the order to leave Makkah came.

The two belts

When the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr slipped out of Makkah and hid in the cave of Thawr, it was Asma who carried food to them in the dark. She had nothing to tie the provisions with, so she took her waist belt, tore it in two, and used one half to bind the food and the other to fasten her own dress.

The Prophet ﷺ saw what she had done and smiled. He told her that Allah had given her, in exchange for that torn belt, two belts in Paradise. From that day she was known as Dhat an-Nitaqayn, the possessor of two belts.

It was not only a title to be proud of. It was a promise of a specific reward, the clothing of Paradise, which meant she was among those given the glad tidings of Paradise itself. A small act of service in the dark had been answered from above.

The knock at the door

After the Prophet ﷺ and her father left, Asma stayed behind in Makkah, pregnant and largely alone. News reached Abu Jahl that she knew where they had gone. By then he was no ordinary enemy. He was the one who had set in motion the plan to kill the Prophet ﷺ, the man who had already killed believers, men and women, without mercy.

Sit with that for a moment. The Muslims have gone. There is a knock at the door, and she knows whose it is. She has seen this man take lives. She is heavily pregnant, and there is no one to protect her. He demands to know where her father and the Prophet ﷺ are. She refuses. His face reddens, and he asks again. She still refuses.

He struck her across the face so hard that her earring flew off, a blow she never forgot. And still she told him nothing. He left her standing there, alive, with the secret kept. The greatest struggle, the Prophet ﷺ taught, is a word of truth before a tyrant. She had just lived it, alone.

"He has left plenty for us"

As she prepared to leave for Madinah, her grandfather Abu Quhafa came to her. He had not yet accepted Islam, and he saw only that this man Muhammad ﷺ had taken her father and left her in hardship. He told her she had been deprived, abandoned, robbed of her father and his support.

She did not argue with his grief. She simply answered that, on the contrary, the Prophet ﷺ had left them a great deal. What they had been given could not be measured against money. To her grandfather it looked like loss. To her it was the most valuable thing a person could hold.

Then she made the long journey to Madinah, still carrying her child. At Quba she gave birth to a son, Abdullah ibn al-Zubair, one of the first children born to the believers who had migrated. The Prophet ﷺ took the baby, softened a date and touched it to his mouth, and made supplication for him.

A life of struggle in Madinah

Her mother, Qutaylah, who had not become Muslim, came to visit her in Madinah. Asma was unsure how to receive her, so she asked the Prophet ﷺ. He told her to keep a good and kind relationship with her mother. It was the same balance the Qur'an sets out: honour your parents, even if they call you to something Allah forbids.

Life in Madinah was poverty. Asma said that when Zubair married her he had no land, no money, no servants, only a horse and a camel for water. She fed the horse and groomed it, ground dates for the camel, carried water, patched the leather bucket, and kneaded dough. From Zubair's land, a gift from the Prophet ﷺ, she carried date stones on her head across a distance of two miles.

One day, carrying that load, she met the Prophet ﷺ with a group of his companions. He made his camel kneel so she could ride. But she felt shy before the men, and she knew her husband's sense of honour, so she declined, and the Prophet ﷺ understood without a word and rode on. When her father later sent a servant girl to help with the horse, she said it felt as if she had been set free.

Give, and do not count

Their hardship was so plain that when a verse came warning that people would be questioned about their blessings, the companions wondered aloud what blessings they even had. They had only the two black things, dates and water. They were told they would be asked about those too.

In that poverty, Asma came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked whether she could give in charity from what little Zubair brought home. He told her to give and not to hoard, or Allah would withhold from her. Another time he told her, even more simply: do not count what you give, or it will be counted against you.

She also took up a sword. At the battle of Yarmuk, when the Muslims were heavily outnumbered by the Romans, Asma was among the women who fought alongside the army. The ease she was promised was never the ease of this world. It was waiting in the next.

ثُمَّ لَتُسْـَٔلُنَّ يَوْمَئِذٍ عَنِ ٱلنَّعِيمِOn that Day, you will be asked about your pleasures.Qur'an 102:8

The womb that carried a legacy

Asma raised eight children. Among her sons were Abdullah, who would govern from Makkah, and Urwah, who became one of the great scholars of the next generation. Two of her daughters she named Khadijah and Aisha, after the most honoured women in the Prophet's household.

It was Urwah, sitting at the side of his aunt Aisha, who gathered much of what we know of the Prophet's life and passed it on. So a large share of the seerah we still read today flowed through this family, through the patient teaching of women, and through the household Asma built. She herself narrated many hadith, taught the rulings of the religion, and instructed pilgrims in Makkah for years.

There is a quiet weight in that. A woman who lived in poverty, who carried date stones on her head, raised a son whose learning still reaches us. Allah records the footprints people leave, even the ones the world never sees.

A word of truth, again, at a hundred

Her son Abdullah ibn al-Zubair declared himself caliph in Makkah and held it for years, until the armies of the tyrant al-Hajjaj came against the city and the Kaaba itself. By then Asma was about a hundred years old, and her son came to ask her advice. Should he surrender, or keep fighting?

She told him: a dignified strike with the sword is more beloved than a humiliating lash of the whip. When he asked if she would grieve for him, she said she would grieve only if he were killed upon falsehood. She asked him to come close so she could hold him and smell him one last time, for they both knew he was going to die. He kissed her hands, her feet, her head, and asked her not to stop praying for him.

He was killed beside the Kaaba and his body left hanging. Asma went to the tyrant who had done it. A hundred years old, she stood before al-Hajjaj and told him the truth to his face: the man he had killed was a man of prayer and fasting, a guardian of the faith, devoted to his parents. If he had ruined her son's world, her son had ruined his Hereafter. She had stood before Abu Jahl as a young woman, and now she stood before al-Hajjaj at the end of her life, fearing no one but Allah. She died not long after, still in worship, at the age of a hundred.

What Asma's life teaches us

Her story is bracketed by two tyrants, one at the start of her life and one at the end, and the same steady courage answers both. In between is a long, unglamorous service that never once asked for ease.

  • Courage is quiet before it is loud. Long before she stood before tyrants, she climbed a mountain in the dark and kept a dangerous secret. Bravery is built in the small, unseen acts first.
  • What faith gives cannot be measured in comfort. When her grandfather called her abandoned, she answered that she had been given a great deal. She had simply learned to count the right things.
  • Give without keeping score. In real poverty she was told to give and not to count it. Generosity that waits until there is plenty rarely arrives at all.
  • Raise people who outlast you. The home she built in hardship produced a scholar whose learning still reaches us. The quiet work of raising and teaching echoes far past one life.

Questions

Who was Asma bint Abi Bakr?

She was the daughter of Abu Bakr as-Siddiq and an older half-sister of Aisha (RA). She was among the first to accept Islam and is known for feeding the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and her father during the Hijra.

Why is she called Dhat an-Nitaqayn, the possessor of two belts?

On the night of the Hijra she tore her waist belt in two, using one half to tie the provisions for the cave and the other for her dress. The Prophet ﷺ told her Allah had given her two belts in Paradise in exchange.

What happened when Abu Jahl came to her door?

He demanded to know where the Prophet ﷺ and her father were hiding. She refused to tell him, and he struck her so hard that her earring fell. She still kept the secret, and he left her unharmed.

What can we learn from the life of Asma?

Courage that begins in small hidden acts, contentment that measures faith above comfort, generosity that keeps no count, and the lasting value of raising and teaching the next generation.

Why this story stays with us

Asma's life was not a single brave moment. It was a long line of them, most of them unseen: a secret kept under threat, a household held together in poverty, a son raised who carried knowledge forward. The famous stands before Abu Jahl and al-Hajjaj were only the surface of a courage that had been forming quietly for a hundred years.

Most of our own tests look more like the hidden ones. The patience no one notices, the giving that nobody counts, the steadiness we keep at home when it would be easier to complain. Her life invites us to build that kind of courage now, in the small things, so that it is already there when something larger asks for it. Carry one quiet act of it into today, and her story has done its work.
