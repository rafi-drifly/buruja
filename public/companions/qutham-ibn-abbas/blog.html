Qutham ibn Abbas

The Prophet's Likeness

He was the baby of a famous house. His father was beloved, his brothers were leaders and scholars, and at every turn there was someone larger ahead of him. It would have been easy to be lost in such a family.

This is the story of Qutham ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him): the youngest son of Abbas, the child who looked most like the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and a man who quietly found his own place at the very edge of the Muslim world.

The Companions

Qutham ibn Abbas

The Prophet's Likeness

The youngest in a house of giants

Qutham was the youngest child of Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ. To be born into that family was to begin life in the shadow of others. His father was so loved that he was almost a father to the Prophet ﷺ himself. His brothers, al-Fadl and Abdullah, were already on their way to greatness.

Think for a moment of what that must feel like. All these famous names ahead of you, each one a kind of star, and you arriving last. How does a child even begin to find his own place in a house like that?

His name was unusual even then. There is a famous poem about him, and the name Qutham is so rare that most people will go their whole lives without meeting one.

But the youngest carried something none of the others did. Of all the family, he resembled the Prophet ﷺ the most in his appearance. He was only a small child in the Prophet's lifetime, so the likeness showed itself fully later, as he grew into a man.

The faces that mirrored a face

The companions remembered who looked like the Prophet ﷺ the way you remember something precious. Ja'far ibn Abi Talib was told, "You resemble my character and my creation," and was sent as the ambassador to Abyssinia, a perfect representation of the one he resembled.

From the family there were others. Hasan, the grandson, looked so much like him that Abu Bakr once lifted the child into the air and said he resembled his grandfather more than his own father, Ali. There was Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith, the cousin and childhood friend who looked exactly like him, whose long absence from Islam hurt the Prophet ﷺ deeply, and whose return brought him real joy.

It is worth noticing how much this likeness mattered. Two of the men who resembled the Prophet ﷺ, Ja'far and Mus'ab ibn Umayr, were sent out as his ambassadors. When Mus'ab fell in battle, some of the enemy cried out that they had killed Muhammad ﷺ, because the face they saw was so close to his.

And then there was Qutham. The scholars would debate it, and some of them said it was Qutham who was the most identical of all to the Prophet ﷺ in his appearance. It was a quiet inheritance, given to the youngest, and it shaped how his family held him.

A dream, and a brother through milk

His mother, Lubaba, had a strange dream while she was pregnant. She saw that a part of the Prophet ﷺ was in her house. Not his whole body, only a part of him, and she could not say which. She came to him and asked what it meant.

He answered gently. She would give birth to a child, he said, and she would nurse another child alongside her own son. That other child was al-Husayn, the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ. So Qutham and al-Husayn grew up as brothers through nursing, raised side by side.

It is moving to think of the timing. This was the last stretch of the Prophet's life, after the move to Madinah, when his family began to gather close around him again. You can sense how much joy that gave him: to have his people near, to build a full family life in his final months, to hold the babies of his household in his arms.

There is a tender scene from those days. Lubaba was nursing al-Husayn when the Prophet ﷺ took the baby and cuddled him, and the child, being a baby, urinated on him. The Prophet ﷺ asked her to hold his son so he could clean himself. She took al-Husayn back, but then she smacked the baby and he began to cry. The Prophet ﷺ looked at her and said, gently, that she had hurt him through his son. This is how nearness to the Prophet ﷺ reached this family: in ordinary, domestic moments of love.

Qutham knew that nearness too. He once recalled playing with his brother Abdullah when the Prophet ﷺ passed by, scooped the two boys up, and carried them along with him. The same hands that received revelation reached down to gather up two playing children.

The poem of the beloved

While the Prophet ﷺ was being besieged in Madinah, his uncle Abbas was still in Makkah, and he was deeply afraid. The army gathered against the Muslims was the largest the Arabs had ever assembled, and the aim was to wipe the believers out entirely.

In his fear, Abbas would hold the little boy who looked most like the Prophet ﷺ. He would clutch Qutham close and whisper to him as if to a piece of the one he loved. The lines became a famous poem: my beloved, my beloved, the likeness of the one with the noble nose, the Prophet of the Lord of all blessings, despite the hatred of those who oppose him.

He praised the Prophet's noble nose and shamed the noses of his enemies in the dirt, holding tight to the child while he waited for news. When a servant finally came and told him that the Prophet ﷺ and the companions had survived, Abbas kissed the man on his forehead and set him free.

The last to leave the grave

Each of the sons of Abbas was woven into the final days of the Prophet ﷺ, and Qutham was given a distinction that is hard to imagine. He was the last person to come out of the grave when the Prophet ﷺ was buried.

He had the last moments down there with the body of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. He was the one who placed the last stone over him before rising from the grave. Of all the people gathered for that burial, it was the youngest son of Abbas who lingered longest beside him.

His brothers were part of these same hours. His eldest brother, al-Fadl, was the one who took the last walk with the Prophet ﷺ. The whole family was drawn close to that moment of farewell.

It is a small detail, easily lost in the pages of history. Yet it tells you something about a quiet boy in a loud family: when the most painful and most sacred task of all was at hand, he was the one who stayed until the very end.

Integrity in the years that followed

Qutham lived a long life, as most of the children of Abbas did. He grew into a man of courage and went far into the conquests, taking part in the opening of lands across Iraq and Persia and beyond. Later, when Ali went out to Iraq, he appointed Qutham as governor of Madinah.

He was known above all for his dignity. After one battle, a man tried to give him a thousand extra shares because of his bravery. Qutham refused. Put it back among the spoils, he said. Allocate it to the Muslims. I do not want it. He held his integrity higher than any reward.

It would have been the easiest thing in the world to take it. He had earned it on the battlefield, and the offer was made openly. But he understood that a reward is not only about what you deserve. It is about what it does to your heart, and he wanted nothing that might pull him toward himself and away from the cause he served.

His life reached its furthest point at the opening of Samarqand, far to the east. He carried into that battle the banner of Banu Hashim, the very same banner his eldest brother, al-Fadl, had carried beside the Prophet ﷺ when only a few stood firm with him. Qutham died there as a martyr, and was buried in that distant land. Generations later, the great scholar of hadith, Imam al-Bukhari, would be born in the same region. The young man who looked like the Prophet ﷺ had quietly helped carry Islam to the place that would one day guard the Prophet's words.

Do not only watch the superstar

Abbas had a son of dominating presence and eloquence in Abdullah, the one people called the most beautiful and the most intelligent of men. It is tempting to look only at him and stop there.

But the lesson of the sons of Abbas is the opposite. Do not only watch the superstar. Look at what was being built around him. Each of the brothers found his own way, and Allah alone knows the good they did that was never written down.

From this one family came a line that stretched across centuries. The historians counted dozens of leaders descended from the children of Abbas, an empire that endured, in one form or another, for roughly a thousand years. It began with a father of great character and a house full of sons, including a quiet youngest child who looked like the Prophet ﷺ and gave everything he had.

Qutham is a reminder that history is not only made by the famous few. It is also carried by the ones beside them: the brother who stays, the soldier who refuses the extra share, the young man who takes a banner to the far edge of the world and is buried where almost no one back home will ever see his grave.

What Qutham's life teaches us

His story is not built on one famous moment. It is built on a quiet faithfulness that stayed steady whether anyone was watching or not.

  • Your place is your own to find. Born the youngest among giants, Qutham did not compete for the spotlight. He grew into his own gift and his own service, in his own time.
  • Stay until the end of what matters. At the hardest task of all, the burial of the Prophet ﷺ, he was the last to leave the grave. Faithfulness often looks like simply not walking away.
  • Refuse what you have not earned. Offered a thousand extra shares for his courage, he sent them back to the Muslims. Dignity meant wanting nothing he did not deserve.
  • Small lives carry large things. A quiet man carried his family's banner to a distant frontier, and helped bring Islam to a land that would one day guard the Prophet's words.

Questions

Who was Qutham ibn Abbas?

He was the youngest son of Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and a cousin of the Prophet. He is remembered as the family member who most resembled the Prophet in appearance, and as the last person to rise from his grave at the burial.

Why is Qutham said to resemble the Prophet ﷺ?

Several of the Prophet's relatives were known to look like him. Among them, some scholars held that Qutham was the most identical to the Prophet ﷺ in appearance. As he was only a child in the Prophet's lifetime, the likeness showed itself fully once he grew into a man.

What is the famous poem connected to Qutham?

During the siege of Madinah, his father Abbas, anxious in Makkah, would hold the young Qutham close because he looked like the Prophet ﷺ. The lines he whispered, praising the beloved likeness of the Prophet, became a well-known poem.

What can we learn from the life of Qutham?

That a quiet person in a famous family can find a place of his own, that staying faithful to the end matters more than standing in the spotlight, and that real dignity refuses what it has not earned.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us know what it is to feel small beside others: a sibling, a colleague, a name that fills the room before we enter it. Qutham's life is a gentle answer to that feeling. He did not chase the front of the family. He grew into the gift he was given, stayed beside the Prophet ﷺ until the last stone was placed, and refused a reward he felt he had not earned.

Carry one piece of that into your own day. Be the one who stays until the work is truly finished, who wants nothing that is not honestly theirs, who trusts that a quiet life given fully to Allah is never lost, even if no one writes it down.
