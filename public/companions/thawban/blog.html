The Companions

Thawban ibn Bujdud

The One Who Could Not Bear to Be Apart

He came into the Prophet's life as a prisoner of war, with no name anyone noted and no future anyone expected. He left it as one of the most quoted narrators in Islam, the man scholars still call al-Nabawi: the one who belonged to the Prophet ﷺ.

This is the story of Thawban (may Allah be pleased with him): a freed slave from Yemen whose whole existence became defined by his attachment to the Messenger of Allah, and whose love was so pure that a verse of the Qur'an was revealed to set his heart at rest.

Thawban ibn Bujdud

The One Who Could Not Bear to Be Apart

A captive who was offered everything

Thawban was originally from Yemen. We do not know which battle brought him into the Muslim camp, only that he arrived as a prisoner of war: not a leader, not an enemy of the faith, simply a man whose life had been swept up by events larger than him.

When the Prophet ﷺ saw him among the captives, he went straight up to him. He told him plainly that he was free. If he wished to return to his people, he could go, with no ransom and no chains. There was nothing holding him.

Then came the part Thawban never expected. The Prophet ﷺ offered him something else: if he wished to stay, he would be counted among the people of his household, his own family. A man who had walked in as a prisoner was being offered a place beside the Messenger of Allah. He did not hesitate. He stayed.

The shift no one had seen before

What happened to Thawban tells you something about the world the Prophet ﷺ was building. A freed slave was no longer simply someone to be fed and clothed kindly. He could sit in the circle of knowledge, carry the words of the Prophet ﷺ, and one day teach the greatest scholars of the next generation.

And that is exactly what Thawban became. He left no children to narrate from him, yet he narrated over a hundred and twenty hadith, and the leading scholars of the tabi'in came to learn at his side: men like Shaddad ibn Aws, Jubair ibn Nufayr, Abu Idris al-Khawlani, and Khalid ibn Ma'dan.

He did not stay close to the Prophet ﷺ as a servant who only did chores. He sat with him, asked him questions, and listened carefully to everything he said. The honour was not in the title of mawla, the freed slave. It was in what he did with the nearness he was given.

What he learned about a simple life

Thawban watched the Prophet ﷺ closely, and he saw a man whose standard of living did not soften as his power grew. If anything, it grew plainer. He once carried a message that captures it exactly.

When the Prophet ﷺ returned from a journey, his daughter Fatimah had hung a fine curtain on her door and dressed her sons in silver bracelets to welcome him. He did not come in. Worried she had done something wrong, she took it all down and sent the bracelets back to him. The Prophet ﷺ gave them away, and explained to Thawban why: these are my family, and I would hate for them to use up all their good things in this short life.

Yet Thawban also saw a man who was generous with the people he loved. He had the bracelets replaced with simpler ones so his grandchildren would still be happy, and he narrated the Prophet's teaching that among the best coins a person can spend is the one spent on his own family. Plainness was never coldness. It was a heart fixed on what lasts.

He learned, too, where real wealth is kept. When a verse came down warning those who hoard gold and silver, the companions asked which kind of wealth was best to hold. The Prophet ﷺ answered, through Thawban's narration, that the best wealth is a tongue that keeps remembering Allah, a heart that is grateful, and a believing spouse who helps a person in their faith. Everything else, he understood, is something you can lose.

The hadiths we still live by

Once you know Thawban's name, you start to see it everywhere. Many of the practices Muslims hold onto today reach us through him. The fast of six days in Shawwal is his hadith. Saying Allahumma anta as-salam after the prayer is his hadith. The way to make the two prostrations of forgetfulness in salah is his hadith.

He carried hard words too. Many of the warnings about the end of times come through Thawban: that nations would one day gather to consume this ummah like guests around a table, not because it was small but because its hearts would be filled with love of the world and a hatred of death.

And he carried a quiet piece of advice that is easy to act on tonight. A man in the mosque of Damascus once asked him for a deed that would bring him close to Paradise. Thawban was silent for a long while, then turned and said: hold onto long prostrations, for you do not make a single sujood for Allah except that He raises you a degree by it and wipes away a sin.

There is gentleness in his narrations as well as warning. He related that the Prophet ﷺ said a Muslim remains in the harvest of Paradise so long as he visits his brother for the sake of Allah. The man who once entered the camp alone, with no people of his own, had been shown a faith where simply going to see another believer is counted as standing in a garden.

A love that a verse came down to answer

The most beautiful thing about Thawban was not his memory or his fame. It was his attachment. He loved the Prophet ﷺ so intensely that he could not bear to be away from him. He would wait for him to come out each day, restless until he saw his face.

One day the Prophet ﷺ came out and found him pale, changed, clearly distressed. He asked what was wrong, was he ill, was he in pain. Thawban said there was no pain. It was this: when he did not see the Prophet ﷺ for a while, he grew anxious until he could see him again. And then a heavier thought had struck him. Even if he reached Paradise, the Prophet ﷺ would be raised high with the messengers, and he feared he would be somewhere far below, barely able to see him at all. And if he did not reach Paradise, he would never see him again.

He was not worried about reward. He was worried about separation. In answer to that pure and simple love, Allah revealed a verse that has comforted everyone who has ever loved the Prophet ﷺ since:

Whoever obeys God and the Messenger will be among those He has blessed: the messengers, the truthful, those who bear witness to the truth, and the righteous- what excellent companions these are!Quran 4:69

When the Prophet ﷺ was gone

So you can imagine what the Prophet's death did to him. Thawban had the condition of Bilal (RA): he could not stay in Madinah. Everywhere he looked, he remembered the Messenger of Allah, and he could not bear the city without him. After a few days, he left.

He asked to be kept in the expeditions, away from the place that ached. He went to Sham, and over the years he settled in Damascus, in Ramla in Palestine, in Egypt, and finally in Homs, gathering students in each place and passing on what he had heard. Wherever he went, he carried the Prophet ﷺ with him.

One of the hadiths he narrated described the Prophet's fountain on the Day of Judgement: wider than the distance between two distant cities, whiter than milk, sweeter than honey, its cups as many as the stars. And the first to drink from it, the Prophet ﷺ said, would be the poor of the believers, the dishevelled and dust-covered, the ones whose knock no door opens to. Thawban had lived that life. He knew exactly whose company he had chosen.

A dignity that never asked for pity

Near the end, Thawban fell ill in Homs. The governor there, Abdullah ibn Qurt, did not come to visit him, which surprised people given his standing. Thawban was not a nobleman by the reckoning of the Arabs. He was simply the freed slave of the Prophet ﷺ, and to him that was everything.

So he dictated a letter. From Thawban, the freed slave of the Prophet, to the Amir. If you knew that a man who had once served Musa or Isa lay in your town, he wrote, you would surely have gone to visit him. The governor read it, rose at once, and hurried to his bedside.

And even then, dying, Thawban thought of him as a student. Sit down, he said, and let me narrate to you from the Prophet ﷺ. He gave him a hadith to strive toward: that seventy thousand of this ummah would enter Paradise without reckoning and without punishment, and with every thousand, another seventy thousand. He passed away in the year 54 after the Hijrah, still pointing others toward the highest they could reach.

What Thawban's life teaches us

His story is not about a single great deed. It is about where a heart can settle, and what it holds onto when everything else is taken away.

  • Worth is measured by attachment, not by birth. Thawban arrived as a captive and left as a teacher of scholars. What raised him was not his lineage but how closely he held to what was good.
  • Love can be the deepest form of faith. He did not fear punishment so much as separation. A verse came down for that love, a reminder that whoever obeys Allah and His Messenger will be in their company.
  • Keep your standard of living simple. He watched the Prophet ﷺ grow plainer as his power grew, his heart fixed on what lasts rather than on what is used up in this short life.
  • Hold onto the small, steady acts. When asked for a path to Paradise, he named long prostrations. Every sujood raises a degree and wipes a sin, and it is available to anyone, tonight.

Questions

Who was Thawban (RA)?

He was a freed slave (mawla) of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, originally from Yemen, who came into the Muslim camp as a prisoner of war and chose to stay as part of the Prophet's household. He narrated over a hundred and twenty hadith and taught many of the leading scholars of the next generation.

Why is Thawban called al-Nabawi?

The scholar al-Dhahabi gave him the title al-Nabawi, meaning the prophetic, as high praise for how completely his life became defined by his attachment to the Prophet ﷺ. In the books of hadith he is known simply as Thawban.

What verse of the Qur'an was revealed about Thawban?

When Thawban grew distressed at the thought of being separated from the Prophet ﷺ in the next life, the verse in Surah an-Nisa (4:69) was revealed: that whoever obeys Allah and the Messenger will be in the company of the prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous.

What can we learn from the life of Thawban?

That a person's worth is measured by what their heart holds onto, that love of the Prophet ﷺ is a form of faith, and that simple, steady acts of worship like long prostration are within reach of everyone.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will not be remembered in books. We are, in our own way, ordinary people in a crowd. Thawban was too, until the day he decided that the thing he wanted most in this life and the next was simply to be near what he loved. That single attachment shaped everything that came after it.

His story asks a quiet question of our own day: what is our heart attached to, and would we miss it the way he missed the Prophet ﷺ? It is worth sitting with for a moment. And it leaves us with something we can actually do, the same advice he gave a stranger in a mosque: make your prostrations long, and let one of them, tonight, be unhurried.
