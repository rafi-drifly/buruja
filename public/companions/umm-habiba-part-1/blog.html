Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan

A Dream Come True

Imagine being the only believer in your own family, with your father the most powerful enemy of the very faith you have chosen. Then imagine losing your husband in a land far from home, with a small daughter to raise and no one to turn to.

This is the story of Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan (may Allah be pleased with her): the daughter of Abu Sufyan, who held to her faith alone, and woke one morning to a dream she could not explain.

The Companions

Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan

A Dream Come True

A daughter of the rival house

Her name was Ramla, and she was born about fourteen or fifteen years before the first revelation came to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. We know little of her early life, but we know a great deal about her family, because her family would shape the story of Islam itself.

She belonged to Banu Umayya, the rival clan of the Prophet's own Banu Hashim. It was a house of power and pride, one that resisted the new faith not over its teaching but over who carried it. To accept a prophet from a rival clan felt, to them, like ceding everything.

Her father was Abu Sufyan, whose given name, Sakhr ibn Harb, meant the rock, the son of war. He was noble, wealthy, eloquent, a respected diplomat known to foreign rulers. In time he would become the chief opponent of the Prophet ﷺ. This was the home into which Umm Habiba was born.

Her mother, Safiyya bint Abi al-As, was the paternal aunt of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him), which made Umm Habiba his cousin. Safiyya, by most accounts, passed away before Islam came, so she was never there to oppose her daughter's faith, nor to share it. Of all her wide family, Umm Habiba would be only the second person from Banu Umayya to believe, after Uthman himself.

First in her family to believe

Umm Habiba was married to Ubaydullah ibn Jahsh, a man who had left idol worship long before Islam in search of the one God. When the Prophet ﷺ declared his message, Ubaydullah was quick to accept it, and so was she. They are counted among the first fifteen or sixteen to believe.

These earliest conversions were often quiet, kept hidden from the pressure of family and clan. We have no narration of the moment Umm Habiba accepted Islam, no scene of how it happened. We only know that she did, early, when believing was dangerous and the believers were few.

Like our mother Umm Salama before her, Umm Habiba reached Islam ahead of her whole family. Her father, her stepmother Hind, and her brothers would not accept the faith until the conquest of Makkah, years later. For now she stood alone among them.

When Abu Sufyan learned that his own daughter had joined the Muslims, it wounded him. He counted himself among the staunchest enemies of the Prophet ﷺ, and he was ashamed to face Quraysh. Some scholars believe it was this quiet shame that softened his hand, so that he pressed the early Muslims less harshly than the other chiefs did.

Crossing the sea while expecting a child

The pressure on the believers grew until it could no longer be borne. Umm Habiba and Ubaydullah joined the second migration to Abyssinia, the larger group, leaving Makkah for the land across the sea ruled by the Negus, the just Christian king the Muslims called Najashi.

She made that crossing while expecting her first child. Her daughter Habiba was born in Abyssinia, in exile, far from the city of her birth. From that day Ramla became known as Umm Habiba, the mother of Habiba, and she would carry that name for the rest of her life.

It is the name by which the books of hadith record her. You will not find her narrations under Ramla. You will find them under Umm Habiba, the name a foreign land gave to a woman who had left everything familiar behind.

Only a handful of the companions carried the distinction of three migrations: the two to Abyssinia and the later one to Madinah. Umm Habiba's path was its own. She would remain in Abyssinia far longer than most, long after others had moved on, and the land that received her in fear would become the place her story turned.

Alone in a foreign land

In Abyssinia, the narrations say, Umm Habiba saw a troubling dream of her husband with a disfigured face. Soon after, she learned that Ubaydullah had turned away from his faith and to drink, and that he died in that state. Dr. Omar Suleiman notes that this account, though found in the books of biography, does not carry the strong chain of an authentic report, so we hold it gently and say: Allah knows best.

What is clear is the situation it left her in. She was now alone in a foreign country, raising a small daughter, with no relative beside her to lean on. She could not return to Makkah and remain a believer, for there her faith would be crushed harder than before, now that she had no husband to shield her.

Many of the believers had since moved on to Madinah. She had missed that window. She was stranded with a small community in exile, holding firm to a faith that had cost her everything, with no way forward that she could see.

It is worth pausing on what steadfastness looked like for her here. There was no battle to fight, no public stand to take. There was only the slow daily choice to keep her faith and keep her child, in a place that was not home, with no end in sight. She made that choice, and she held it.

The dream she could not explain

It was in that stranded, uncertain time that Umm Habiba had a second dream. In it, a caller addressed her as Umm al-Mu'minin, the mother of the believers. She woke in shock. There was only one group of women given that title: the wives of the Prophet ﷺ.

It made no sense to her. Here she was, marooned in Abyssinia with her daughter, the daughter of Abu Sufyan, with no tie to the Prophet ﷺ at all. Yet the dream pleased her, and she took it as a good sign, and she waited.

Then a knock came at her door, and the next chapter of her life began to open. Far away in Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ had heard of her hardship and the unique difficulty she was in. He had sent a letter to Najashi, asking him to propose marriage to her on his behalf, and to act as his representative if she agreed.

A wedding without the groom

The Prophet ﷺ had beloved companions and family still in Abyssinia, among them Jafar ibn Abi Talib, any of whom could have stood in for him. Yet he chose Najashi, the king, to be his sole representative. Najashi, who had once said he would carry the Prophet's shoes if he could, was honoured beyond words.

He sent a servant girl named Abraha to carry the proposal. When Umm Habiba opened the door and heard the news, she was so overcome with joy that she took off all her jewellery and gave it to the girl. Najashi then gathered the Muslims to a wedding in his palace: Jafar among them, the noble companions among them. He gave the dowry himself, four hundred gold dinars, and laid out a feast, saying it was the way of the prophets that a marriage be followed by food.

It was the largest dowry any of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ received, given freely by a king who counted it an honour. Yet the lavish setting was not what the moment was about. For a small community of believers living far from home, this was a night of pure joy: a wedding for the Prophet ﷺ, celebrated in his absence, in a land that had taken them in.

So the Prophet ﷺ was married while he was not even there, in the palace of a king he had never met, to a widow stranded across the sea. There was no timeline for when she could ever join him. It would take six long years before she finally did.

The salaam that crossed the sea

Abraha, the servant girl, returned the next day with gifts from Najashi, and a friendship grew between the two women. When Umm Habiba asked what she could give her in return, Abraha asked for one thing only. If you ever stand before the Prophet ﷺ, she said, give him my salaam, for I will likely never meet him.

Years later, when Umm Habiba at last reached the Prophet ﷺ, she told him everything: how Najashi had represented him, how the wedding had been held, and how a young servant girl in a distant land had asked only that her greeting be carried to him. The Prophet ﷺ was joyful, and he replied, and upon her be peace and the mercy of Allah and His blessings.

There is something tender in that exchange. A simple girl, thousands of miles away, who would likely never meet him, wanted nothing more than for her name to reach him. When we send our own salaam upon the Prophet ﷺ, an angel carries it to him, and he answers. Her greeting was carried by Umm Habiba, and it was answered all the same.

When Abu Sufyan heard of the marriage, he was not angry. He said of the Prophet ﷺ, he is a noble steed that cannot be rejected. Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) understood a verse of the Qur'an to speak to this very union, the marriage that would, in time, begin to turn even Abu Sufyan's heart.

God may still bring about affection between you and your present enemies- God is all powerful, God is most forgiving and merciful-Quran 60:7

What Umm Habiba's life teaches us

Her story is not about a single brave act. It is about holding a quiet line, alone, when no one was watching and nothing was easy.

  • Faith can stand without the family around it. She believed while her own father led the opposition. Conviction does not wait for the people we love to agree with us.
  • Steadfastness is tested in the empty hours. Stranded, widowed, with a child and no one to lean on, she held firm. The hardest faith is often the kind no crowd ever sees.
  • Love for the Prophet ﷺ needs no nearness. A servant girl who would never meet him wanted only to send him her salaam. Distance was no barrier to her heart, and it is none to ours.
  • Relief can arrive from a direction you never expected. She was stuck with no way out that she could see. The answer came as a knock at the door, and a dream she had not understood came true.

Questions

Who was Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan?

Her name was Ramla, the daughter of Abu Sufyan, the powerful Makkan chief who long opposed the Prophet ﷺ. She was among the earliest believers and became one of the Mothers of the Believers, the wives of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Why did she migrate to Abyssinia?

She and her husband Ubaydullah ibn Jahsh could no longer bear the persecution in Makkah, so they joined the second migration to Abyssinia. She made the crossing while expecting her daughter Habiba, who was born there.

How was she married to the Prophet while in Abyssinia?

The Prophet ﷺ heard of her hardship and sent a letter to Najashi, the king of Abyssinia, asking him to propose on his behalf and act as his representative. Najashi held the wedding in his palace and gave the dowry himself, and it would be six years before she joined the Prophet ﷺ.

What can we learn from the life of Umm Habiba?

That faith can stand alone even against one's own family, that steadfastness is tested most in the quiet and difficult hours, and that relief can arrive from a direction we never expected.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never face the sea, or exile, or a father who leads the opposition. But almost all of us know what it is to hold a quiet conviction when no one around us shares it, to keep going through an empty stretch when no help is in sight. Umm Habiba lived in that exact place, and she did not let go.

Her life is an invitation to be steady in the unseen hours: to keep your faith when it costs you, to trust that relief can come from a door you were not watching, and to love the Prophet ﷺ from wherever you stand. Hold one of those today, and her story has done its work.
