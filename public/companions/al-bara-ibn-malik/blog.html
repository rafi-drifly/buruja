The Companions

Al-Bara' ibn Malik

The Underestimated Hero

When people passed him in the street, they looked away. He was small and thin, his hair untidy, his clothes worn. He owned two garments and spoke very little. Most people decided he was a nobody.

This is the story of Al-Bara' ibn Malik (may Allah be pleased with him): a man the world overlooked, whose word with Allah was so strong that armies would call out to him for a single prayer.

The Companions

Al-Bara' ibn Malik

The Underestimated Hero

The brother nobody wrote down

We know almost nothing about Al-Bara' before Islam. We do not have the story of how he became Muslim. Unlike his younger brother Anas ibn Malik, who narrated more than a thousand hadith, Al-Bara' left behind no narrations at all. When you search for him in the old books, you find only scraps.

What we do know is his family. He was the full brother of Anas, and his mother was Umm Sulaym, the woman whose sacrifice runs through this whole series. The same spirit of giving that shaped her younger son shaped this older one too, only it came out in a different way.

His life, as it reaches us, is almost entirely battle. There are no gentle family stories, no quiet moments with the Prophet ﷺ that the books preserved. Just one campaign after another, always near the front, always ready to put himself forward first.

The man the Prophet ﷺ named

There is a famous hadith about a certain kind of person. The Prophet ﷺ described someone unkempt, covered in dust, turned away from people's doors, owning almost nothing, the sort of person nobody pays attention to. And he said that if such a person were to swear an oath by Allah, Allah would honour it.

Out of all the companions, the only one the Prophet ﷺ named as an example of this person was Al-Bara' ibn Malik. He was not eloquent. He was not handsome. He would never be the centre of an important gathering. People belittled him on sight.

But that low place in the eyes of people came with a high place in the sight of Allah. It was said that the two things Al-Bara' really knew were Allah and Jannah. One book describes him in a single line: I love Allah, and I want Paradise. The lesson the Prophet ﷺ was teaching is hard to forget. Do not measure a person by what you can see.

The one they underestimated

His appearance fooled people twice. They thought he was nobody special, and they were wrong. Then his enemies saw a small, thin man walk onto the battlefield and thought he would be easy, and they were wrong about that too.

He was, in truth, extraordinary in battle. He first appears in the records at Uhud, likely too young to have fought at Badr, and after that he is in nearly every campaign. He attended the pledge under the tree, the gathering of the companions Allah was pleased with. He was among the few who did not flee at Hunayn but stayed beside the Prophet ﷺ.

On long journeys he had a humble, tender role too. He was the one who sang to the camels carrying the men, keeping the animals moving, while Anjasha sang to the camels carrying the women. Both had beautiful voices, and the Prophet ﷺ once told Anjasha to go gently with the camels carrying the women, lest his singing make them move too fast.

His enemies kept underestimating him, and that was part of why he won. The Prophet ﷺ once told the Muslims plainly: do not underestimate this man. It was the other side who would pay the price for thinking little of him.

God was pleased with the believers when they swore allegiance to you [Prophet] under the tree: He knew what was in their hearts and so He sent tranquillity down to them and rewarded them with a speedy triumphQur'an 48:18

The garden of death

His most famous stand was at the Battle of Yamama, against Musaylimah the liar, the false prophet who murdered those who would not accept him. It was a brutal fight. Many companions were killed, so many memorisers of the Qur'an among them that it later moved the Muslims to begin gathering the Qur'an into one written copy.

Musaylimah's people held a strong fortress with a walled garden. It was nicknamed the Garden of Death, because anyone who got inside was not expected to come out. They rained arrows and stones from its walls, and no one could reach the gate.

Al-Bara' had an idea that sounded like madness. He told Khalid ibn al-Walid: I am small. Strap me to a shield and throw me over the wall with a catapult, and I will open the gate from inside. They tried to talk him out of it. He did not care. So they launched him over the wall, alone, into the garden. He landed among them, fought his way to the gate, and opened it. The army poured in.

He did not stop there. Musaylimah hid behind his strongest fighter, a giant of a man. Al-Bara' went straight for him and cut him down, which opened the way for Musaylimah to be killed. Al-Bara' came out of that battle with more than eighty wounds, arrows and sword cuts across his whole body, and somehow he lived. Khalid stayed with him for a full month, nursing him, because he credited the victory to this small, unassuming man.

A great soldier, not a general

When Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) became leader, he sent a letter to all his governors with a striking instruction: do not put Al-Bara' in command of any army. Not because of any fault in his faith or his courage, but because of how much courage he had.

Umar understood that a commander has to be a strategist, sometimes patient, sometimes slow. Al-Bara' had one speed. Put him in charge, and he might lead the whole army into destruction by expecting everyone to be as fearless as he was. Keep him as a soldier, Umar said, for there is no better one. Just do not make him the general.

There is a quiet wisdom in that. Being a sincere and beloved servant of Allah does not make a person right for every role. Knowing where someone truly belongs, and not forcing them into a place that does not suit them, is its own kind of care.

It was no insult to Al-Bara'. His worth with Allah was never in question. It was simply an honest reading of where his gift would do the most good, and where it might do harm. The companions did not hand out roles by rank or affection. They handed them out by fit.

The prayer that was answered

In battle after battle, the companions noticed something. When the fighting grew desperate, they would turn to Al-Bara' and say: swear an oath upon your Lord, ask Him for victory, for we know your prayer is answered. And he would raise his hands and ask, and the tide would turn. He never lost a battle. When he was there, the Muslims felt they could not lose.

It happened at Qadisiyyah, the hardest battle against the empires of the day, where companions died in their hundreds. It happened against the Persians in Bahrain, where he cut down their commander single-handedly. He took down so many of the enemy that the leaders sometimes had to adjust how the spoils were divided, because one man had accounted for so much. He never did any of it for the world. It was simply how Allah had made him.

His brother Anas once found him near death from his wounds, singing the songs of battle. Anas gently said: my brother, Allah has given you something better to recite in these moments, why not read Qur'an? Al-Bara' looked at him with complete certainty and said: you think I will die here, on my bed? No. By Allah, He will not deprive me of death in the battlefield. And he healed, and he went back.

His last act

At the Battle of Tustar against the Persians, the Muslims were close to losing. Three times they called out to Al-Bara' to swear an oath upon his Lord, and three times he raised his hands. By the third, something in him had shifted. He asked for victory, and then he asked for something more: let me be with Your Prophet ﷺ. He was asking for martyrdom.

The Persians had a cruel tactic. They threw burning iron hooks over the walls to catch men and drag them back to be killed. Anas was caught by one of them. And his brother Al-Bara' threw himself onto him and pulled the burning hook off with his own bare hands, until there was nothing left of the skin on them, until Anas was free. Shortly after, Al-Bara' was killed, and the Muslims won the battle. His prayer was answered in full.

Think about what that snap decision held. This was about twenty years after the Hijrah. Anas was around thirty. He would live another seventy years, and almost everything we know about the daily life of the Prophet ﷺ, more than a thousand narrations, reached us through him. In one instant, with his hands burning, Al-Bara' chose his brother's life over his own. The overlooked man who left behind no hadith helped preserve the one who carried the most.

So when we say, the Prophet ﷺ said, and the words come to us through Anas, we owe a quiet debt to a brother whose last act on earth was to save him.

What Al-Bara's life teaches us

His story has almost no words in it, his own. It is told in actions, and the actions all point the same way.

  • Do not measure people by their surface. The man the Prophet ﷺ named as beloved to Allah was the one the world would have walked past. What is visible is rarely what counts.
  • Nearness to Allah is built in private. Al-Bara' spoke little to people because he was busy speaking to his Lord. That quiet closeness was the source of everything else.
  • The right place matters more than the highest place. He was the best soldier and the wrong general, and there was no shame in that. Knowing where you truly belong is wisdom, not a demotion.
  • Love shows itself in a single moment. His last act was to pull a burning hook off his brother with bare hands. Real love is decided in the instant it costs you something.

Questions

Who was Al-Bara' ibn Malik?

He was a companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the older, full brother of Anas ibn Malik. His mother was Umm Sulaym. He was known for his courage in battle and for being a man whose prayers to Allah were answered.

Is this the same Al-Bara' found in the books of hadith?

No. The Al-Bara' who narrates many hadith is a different and more famous companion. Al-Bara' ibn Malik, the brother of Anas, narrated no hadith at all, and very little about his life was recorded.

What is Al-Bara' ibn Malik most known for?

His stand at the Battle of Yamama, where he asked to be catapulted over the wall of a fortress to open it from inside, and his death at the Battle of Tustar, where his final act was saving his brother Anas from a burning hook.

Why did Umar tell his governors not to make him a commander?

Not out of any fault. Umar judged that Al-Bara's overwhelming courage made him a danger as a general, since he might lead an army into destruction. He praised him as the finest of soldiers, just not the right leader of one.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to assume that the people who matter most are the ones who look the part: confident, polished, easy to admire. Al-Bara' was none of these. He was small, plain, and quiet, and most people decided he was nothing. They were looking at the wrong thing the whole time.

His life asks a simple question of our own day. Who have we walked past without a second look? And what is the state of our own private word with Allah, the part of us no one sees? You do not need a battlefield to live by this. You need only to stop measuring yourself and others by the surface, and to tend, quietly, to the one relationship that decides everything.
