The Companions

Omar ibn al-Khattab

Ten Quiet Virtues

We usually remember Omar for his strength. When we want to put someone in their place, we borrow his name. But the strength people borrow was the smallest part of him.

This is the story of Omar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), told the way it is most instructive: through a handful of his virtues, some shared with others, some his alone. Each one, in the end, points back to the same thing.

The Companions

Omar ibn al-Khattab

Ten Quiet Virtues

Inspired toward the truth

There was a reason the Prophet ﷺ wanted Omar to accept Islam so badly. He saw something in him. It can be said that Allah shaped this one man for the sake of His religion, and that from few people after the Prophet ﷺ has so much good flowed.

Omar made a famous supplication: show me the truth as truth and let me follow it, and show me falsehood as falsehood and let me keep away from it. He did not want to look away from what was real, even when it cost him. The more sincere a person is, the more their intuition begins to line up with what Allah intends.

This is not intuition placed above revelation. It is a heart kept so clean that its instincts run in the same direction as the truth. The Prophet ﷺ said that in the nations before us there were people the angels would speak to, and that if there were anyone like that in his community, it would be Omar. He strove against himself so hard that it was as if he could hear that guidance prompting him toward good.

His honesty could be almost uncomfortable. When the Prophet ﷺ said no one truly believes until he loves the Messenger more than his own self, Omar admitted he was not sure he was there yet. He went home and turned it over in his heart. The next day he came back and said it honestly: now you are more beloved to me than myself. The Prophet ﷺ smiled and told him, now you are there.

When revelation agreed with him

On several occasions Omar held an opinion, and then the Quran came down agreeing with it. He had felt that wine should be forbidden, though he had once loved it more than anyone, and the prohibition came in stages. He had felt a veil was needed for the wives of the Prophet ﷺ, and the verse came.

After the battle of Badr, the Prophet ﷺ inclined toward mercy for the captives. Omar argued for a firmer approach, not out of harshness but because of the danger those men still posed. Revelation came down closer to his view. When he saw the place where Ibrahim once stood, he wished aloud that it could be a place of prayer, and a verse followed.

He also pushed for something quieter and lasting. When those who had memorised the Quran began to be killed, Omar urged Abu Bakr (RA) to gather it into a single written copy, so that no one could ever claim it was lost. Abu Bakr hesitated, then said Allah opened his heart to what Omar had seen.

The dreams the Prophet ﷺ saw

No companion appeared in the dreams of the Prophet ﷺ as often as Omar, and the dreams were always about his goodness. In one, the Prophet ﷺ saw people wearing shirts of different lengths, and Omar walked past dragging a long garment behind him. He explained it as his commitment to the religion.

In another, the Prophet ﷺ drank milk until it flowed from his fingers, and Omar drank from it. That milk, he said, was knowledge. Omar was so eager to catch every word that when he could not be present, he arranged for a neighbour from the Ansar to attend and tell him everything he had missed.

In a third, the Prophet ﷺ drew water from a well and handed the bucket to Abu Bakr, who pulled what he could in his short time. Then Omar took it, and the Prophet ﷺ said he had never seen anyone draw water with such strength, until everyone had drunk their fill and even the animals knelt down to drink. Omar's justice, it was said, would one day reach even the animals.

Once the Prophet ﷺ described a beautiful palace in Paradise and assumed it was his own, until he was told it belonged to Omar. He turned away out of respect for Omar's known sense of honour. When Omar heard this, he did not boast. He wept and asked, would I ever feel jealousy toward you, Messenger of Allah?

The one Shaytan avoided

Some women of Quraysh were once speaking with the Prophet ﷺ, their voices raised above his. When they heard Omar coming, they hurried behind a curtain. The Prophet ﷺ laughed, and when Omar understood why, he gently called the women out for fearing him more than they feared the Messenger himself.

One of them answered back that he was harsher than the Prophet ﷺ. Now harshness is no compliment. These are close to the very words Allah used to describe what would have driven people away from the Prophet ﷺ, so Omar grew uneasy. But the Prophet ﷺ reassured him: Shaytan would not even walk the same path as Omar.

The meaning is gentle. Omar lived in such constant remembrance of Allah that Shaytan found no opening to whisper through. His harshness was never aimed at people from a lowly place. It was aimed at falsehood, and even there it bent the moment the truth was shown to him.

By an act of mercy from God, you [Prophet] were gentle in your dealings with them- had you been harsh, or hard-hearted, they would have dispersed and left you- so pardon them and ask forgiveness for them. Consult with them about matters, then, when you have decided on a course of action, put your trust in God: God loves those who put their trust in Him.Quran 3:159

Harder on himself than on anyone

There was no one known for night prayer like Omar. His recitation was so deep it could be heard across the street, and he said he read aloud to drive Shaytan away. He could not hide his weeping. It was said two faint channels had formed beneath his eyes from how much he cried over the Quran at night.

When he appointed a governor over Kufa, his first instruction was not about politics. Guard your prayer, he wrote, because if you lose it you will lose everything after it. His worship was the foundation he led from.

This is the lesson too often forgotten. People love to borrow Omar's strength when they want to be hard with others. But his standing at night made him harder on himself than on anyone. If you do not have the night prayer of Omar, the saying goes, do not pick up the stick of Omar.

Justice that left no one out

When Omar tried to set a limit on the dowry, a woman stood and said he could not limit what Allah had left open. He accepted it at once: the woman is right, and Omar is wrong. When Salman objected that he had taken two cloths from the public share and given everyone else one, Omar explained himself publicly until Salman was satisfied.

His justice reached past his own household. When the son of the governor of Egypt struck a Coptic man and boasted of his noble blood, Omar had the man given the whip to answer the blow, asking, since when have you enslaved people whose mothers bore them free? When his own son was guilty of a grave sin, Omar refused to let him be spared the punishment everyone else would face.

Yet people forget his mercy, and his mercy was real. He lifted the punishment for theft in a year of hunger, holding that to pardon on the basis of doubt was dearer to him than to punish in doubt. He prayed often: O Allah, I am harsh, make me gentle; I am weak, make me strong; I am stingy, make me generous.

Humble, and always accountable

Omar wanted people who would correct him. May Allah have mercy on the one who shows me my faults, he said. If someone simply told him to fear Allah, you could watch his whole frame shrink, and he would listen for whatever came next. He warned that a real man is not the one with the loudest voice, but the one who guards a trust and never wounds another person's honour.

He chose a hard, simple life when he could easily have had a soft one. He said plainly that he knew what fine food and fine living were, and could have had the best of both, but he wanted to keep his good deeds for later. He even cautioned a companion carrying home a large amount of meat: do you buy whatever you crave the moment you crave it? He taught people to deny themselves a little, not to the point of harm, so they would not take their blessings for granted.

He used to take a midday nap out in the open, under a tree, with his sandals beneath his head and no guard standing over him. A visiting ruler from Persia, expecting a palace, was told the most powerful man around was asleep on the ground. You ruled with justice, the man said, so you were safe, and so you could sleep.

Above all he held himself to account. He once asked the companion who kept the secret of the hypocrites whether his own name was among them. He said that if a caller announced everyone would enter Paradise except one person, he would fear it was him, and if a caller announced everyone would enter the Fire except one, he would hope to be that one. Hold yourselves accountable, he taught, before you are held accountable, and weigh your own deeds before they are weighed for you.

What Omar's life teaches us

His virtues look like ten different things. They are really one thing seen from ten sides: a sincerity so complete that it shaped everything he did.

  • Love the truth more than your comfort. Omar asked to see things as they really are, even when it was easier not to look. Sincerity, over time, makes a person's instincts trustworthy.
  • Be strict with yourself first. His firmness with others was earned by being harder on himself in private. Standing at night made him gentle by day.
  • Justice has to include the people closest to you. He held his own son and his governors to the same measure as everyone else, and accepted correction even from a stranger.
  • Stay accountable while you still can. A man promised Paradise still feared for himself and weighed his own deeds. He did not let assurance make him careless.

Questions

Who was Omar ibn al-Khattab?

A close companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the second caliph of Islam. He is remembered for his justice, his fear of Allah, and a string of virtues, some shared with others and some his alone.

What does it mean that the Quran agreed with Omar?

On several occasions Omar held a view, on matters such as the veil for the wives of the Prophet ﷺ, the prohibition of wine, and the captives of Badr, and revelation later came down in line with it.

Why is Omar associated with strength?

Because of his firmness and his role as a just leader. But the transcript stresses that his strength rested on worship and self-discipline: he was hardest on himself, not on others.

What can we learn from the life of Omar?

To love the truth above comfort, to be strict with oneself before others, to keep justice even close to home, and to hold oneself to account while there is still time.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to admire the loud parts of Omar: the firmness, the authority, the name we reach for when we want to be hard. But the man behind them wept in the dark, asked others to point out his faults, and feared he might be the one left out of Paradise. The strength was real because the humility was real first.

Most of us will never lead a community. We will lead a family, a team, our own selves. Omar's life asks a quiet question of that ordinary authority: are you harder on yourself than on the people around you? Carry that question into today, and his story has done its work.
