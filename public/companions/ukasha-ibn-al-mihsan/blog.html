The Companions

Ukasha ibn Mihsan

He Beat You To It

The Prophet ﷺ once described a group from his ummah who would enter Paradise with no questioning and no punishment. One man stood up at once and asked to be among them. He was told yes. A second man stood and asked the same, and the answer became a phrase Muslims have repeated ever since.

This is the story of Ukasha ibn Mihsan (may Allah be pleased with him): an early Muslim from Makkah whose name became a byword for reaching good before anyone else.

Ukasha ibn Mihsan

He Beat You To It

A name that means first

Ukasha ibn Mihsan al-Asadi was an ally of the clan of Banu Abd Shams, and one of the earliest believers in Makkah. His name is tied to a single famous moment in the life of the Prophet ﷺ, and that is exactly why his story is worth slowing down over.

Over time his name became woven into the language itself. In the poetry of the Arabs, to call someone an Ukasha was to call him a forerunner, the one who gets there first. Some scholars even noted that to see this companion in a dream was read as a sign that the dreamer would precede others to something good. That is how closely his name and the idea of being first came to sit together.

He is also counted among the believers the Prophet ﷺ gave the glad tidings of Paradise. People sometimes ask why only ten companions are remembered as the ten promised Paradise, when in truth the Prophet ﷺ gave that good news to others as well. The answer is that those ten held a particular standing with him. Ukasha was not one of that ten, yet his glad tidings were of a rare kind, as we will see.

Most of all, his reputation was not luck. It was earned, choice after choice, over a lifetime. The account this story follows is careful to make that point, and so are we.

Seventy thousand, and one request

The moment comes in Madinah, after Ukasha had already migrated there. The narration is in al-Bukhari, reported by Abu Hurayrah (RA). The Prophet ﷺ stood and said that from his ummah a group of seventy thousand would enter Paradise with faces shining like the full moon on the night it is full, with no reckoning and no punishment.

Notice how complete that promise is. Not only would they not be punished, they would not even be questioned. They would walk into Paradise with no account to answer for at all. That is the good news Ukasha heard, and it is the good news he wanted his own name written into.

He rose, lifted his cloak, and said: "O Messenger of Allah, ask Allah to make me one of them." The Prophet ﷺ answered, "You are one of them." In one wording, he prayed, "O Allah, make him one of them." Then a second man stood and asked for the same. The Prophet ﷺ replied, "Ukasha has beaten you to it."

Scholars read that gentle reply in more than one way. Perhaps this was a station reserved for Ukasha alone in that gathering, and the second man simply did not reach it. Perhaps, in his perfect manners, the Prophet ﷺ closed the door kindly on a man who was not of the same rank. Because the second man is never named, both readings are left open, and either one keeps the focus where it belongs: on what made Ukasha worthy of the first answer.

Brothers and a sister who came early

Ukasha did not walk into Islam alone. He and his siblings embraced it young, while they were still counted among the youth of Makkah, and the whole family seemed to carry the same instinct to step forward.

His sister, Umm Qays bint Mihsan (RA), was among the first to accept Islam. She lived more than a hundred years, long enough that later scholars among the Followers would sit with her and narrate hadith from her. His brother Abu Sinan ibn Mihsan (RA) is said, in one report, to have been the very first to stand before the Prophet ﷺ and give the Pledge of Ridwan beneath the tree at Hudaybiyyah, a gathering that Allah Himself declared He was pleased with.

Two more brothers, Amr and Wahb (may Allah be pleased with them), are also counted among the first Muslims, though little else about them has reached us. Notice the pattern even here. Among these siblings, more than one of them was first at something: first to believe, first to pledge, first to step into the open.

This was a household with a quiet gift for being early to good, and Ukasha grew up inside it. Some families pass down wealth or a trade. This one seemed to pass down the instinct to move before anyone else did.

The stick that became a sword

Ukasha was remembered as one of the most handsome and most generous of men, with a smile that rarely left his face. When he gave himself to something, he gave it everything, and he tended to excel at whatever he took on. Early in Islam the Prophet ﷺ placed him over a company of forty men, and he was granted much success in that command.

His most famous moment in battle came at Badr. He was already a Muhajir and now a man of Badr, among the veterans of that day. In the thick of the fighting his sword broke, and he came to the Prophet ﷺ unsure what to do, asking what he should fight with. The Prophet ﷺ picked up a stick, handed it to him, and told him simply, "Fight with this."

Ukasha did not argue. If the Prophet ﷺ had placed a stick in his hand and told him to fight, then he would fight with it. And as he swung it, the stick became a bright sword in his hand, unmistakable, unlike any other blade. He carried that very sword for the rest of his life, and the companions came to know it as the sword of aid.

It was a quiet miracle, and it left a mark. Everyone who saw that sword knew where it had come from: not from a forge, but from his Lord, given to a man who trusted an instruction that looked like nothing in his hand. He used it in the path of Allah until his final day.

The man who kept coming forward

Picture his position. He was a Muhajir, one who left everything in Makkah behind. He was a man of Badr, among the most honoured of all the companions. And he had been told, plainly, that he would enter Paradise with no punishment and no questioning at all.

Now ask yourself honestly: with news like that already in hand, would you not take the rest of your life easy? Most of us would feel we had earned the right to slow down. A guarantee that high could tempt anyone to rest.

Ukasha did the opposite. Whenever the Prophet ﷺ called, he was among the first to answer. And when the Prophet ﷺ passed away and Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) summoned the loyal companions to defend the faith, it was Ukasha who once again came forward, carrying that same sword that had once been a stick.

He died a martyr in the wars against those who turned back from Islam, in the campaign against Musaylima the liar. The man assured of Paradise spent his life as though he still had everything to earn.

There are men among the believers who honoured their pledge to God: some of them have fulfilled it by death, and some are still waiting. They have not changed in the least.Quran 33:23

Why he was worthy of it

It would be a mistake to read "Ukasha has beaten you to it" as the story of a man who got lucky in a single gathering. He had a whole life standing behind that request.

This is what the scholars call a truthful intention: a heart that asks Allah for something and then lives in a way that matches the asking. When you ask Allah for His pleasure and His Paradise, your days are meant to lean in that same direction. The asking and the living are not two separate things.

So when Ukasha lifted his cloak and asked to be among the seventy thousand, his actions had already spoken for him. He was telling the truth, and his life had been telling it for years.

Be the one to lead the way

There is a clear lesson the account draws out of his life, and it is meant for us, not only for him. When you hear of a good deed, when you read of a fine quality in the Quran, when you come across something the Prophet ﷺ taught, do not look around and wait for someone else to act on it first.

Be that person. Look at your own community and notice where there is a gap, a need no one is meeting, and step in to fill it. Notice a forgotten sunnah, something good that has quietly fallen out of practice, and be the one to bring it back to life.

That is what it means to set the pace in good. Ukasha did not invent a single one of the things he raced toward. He simply refused to be late to them. The same door is open to anyone willing to move first, and the hope at the end of his story is a generous one: that Allah would count us, too, among that shining group who enter without reckoning.

What Ukasha's life teaches us

His story turns on one small moment, but it rests on a lifetime of stepping forward. That is the part worth keeping.

  • Be the one who goes first. When you hear of a good deed or a forgotten sunnah, do not look around for someone else to lead. See the gap in your community and fill it yourself.
  • Let your life match your dua. Ukasha asked for the highest place and lived toward it. When you ask Allah for something good, move your days in that direction.
  • Assurance is not an excuse to rest. He was promised Paradise without reckoning and still kept coming forward. Mercy received is a reason to do more, not less.
  • Trust the instruction, even when it looks small. He took a plain stick because he was told to. What looks slight in our hands can carry more than we can see.

Questions

Who was Ukasha ibn Mihsan?

An early Muslim of Makkah and an ally of Banu Abd Shams. He migrated to Madinah, fought at Badr, and was among those given the glad tidings of Paradise. His name became a byword for being first to good.

What does "Ukasha has beaten you to it" mean?

When the Prophet ﷺ described seventy thousand who would enter Paradise without reckoning, Ukasha asked to be among them and was told yes. A second man asked the same, and the Prophet ﷺ said Ukasha had reached it first. The phrase has been repeated ever since.

How did Ukasha die?

He died as a martyr after the Prophet's ﷺ passing, fighting in the wars against those who abandoned Islam, in the campaign against Musaylima the liar.

What can we learn from the life of Ukasha?

To be first to good rather than wait for others, to let your actions match your prayers, and to keep striving even after you have been given good news.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us wait. We hear of a good deed and assume someone better suited will do it. We make a sincere dua and then live as though the asking and the living are separate things. Ukasha did neither. He asked for the highest place and then spent his life walking toward it, and when an opening for good appeared, he was already moving.

There is a quiet challenge in his name. When you next notice a kindness no one is offering, a prayer no one is leading, a need no one is meeting, you could be the one who beats everyone to it. Let that be today's small intention, and his story has done its work.
