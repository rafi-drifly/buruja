The poet who chanted in the front line

Preview: He was a warrior and a poet, and his worship never once slipped.

Assalamu alaikum,

In a time when poets kept far from the fighting, one companion was both poet and warrior. Abdullah ibn Rawahah (may Allah be pleased with him) chanted his verses from the front line of the battle, lifting the courage of frightened, exhausted men.

He had a famously quick mind. He could glance at a handful of coins and name their worth, and read a stranger's whole story from his face. He was not a man who could be fooled. So when he finally saw the Prophet ﷺ, and arrived from pure intelligence at the words "that is him," it carried weight.

When the Muslims dug the trench around Madinah, starving and afraid, the Prophet ﷺ raised his voice with Abdullah's lines: were it not for You we would never have been guided, nor prayed, nor given charity.

What moves me most about him is not his courage. It is his worship. He used to take a friend by the arm and say, "Come, let us believe for an hour." He meant: let us sit and remember Allah for a while. And when he read a verse warning of poets led astray, he wept, certain it might be about him. Where another might say "that is not me," he asked, "what if it is me?"

He died at the Battle of Mutah. The first two leaders were killed, and when the banner came to him, he felt himself hesitate. So he answered his own soul out loud: "Do I see you resisting Paradise? By Allah, you will enter it." Then he advanced, and was martyred.

After his death, his widow was asked what he was like at home. She remembered one thing above all: he never entered the house without praying two units, and never left without praying two. He never broke that habit.

There is a quiet lesson in him. Take the gift you have been given and put it to good use. Take a warning personally instead of passing it on. And let small, steady habits hold your heart in place.

May Allah be pleased with him, and gather us in the company of those whose worship never slipped.
