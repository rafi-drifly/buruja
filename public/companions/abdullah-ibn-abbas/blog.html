The Companions

Abdullah ibn Abbas

The Scholar of the Ummah

Imagine walking into the mosque of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in the time of his companions. You would expect the grey-haired veterans of Badr to command the room. Instead, everyone turns toward a single young man, barely twenty years old, and when he speaks, they fall silent.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him): the cousin of the Prophet ﷺ, born into the hardest years of Makkah, who grew up to be remembered as the most knowledgeable companion of his age.

Abdullah ibn Abbas

The Scholar of the Ummah

Born in the boycott

Abdullah was born about three years before the migration to Madinah, during the boycott of the Prophet's clan. Banu Hashim had been cut off from the rest of Makkah, barred from trade, and left to go hungry. Into that long siege, a baby was born, and he became one of the few sources of joy the Prophet ﷺ had in those days.

His father was al-Abbas, an uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, known for his generosity and for keeping the family together. His mother, Lubaba, was among the earliest women to accept Islam. She taught her son to pray as soon as he could remember. He would later say that he and his mother were counted among the weak ones of Makkah, the believers who could not yet make the journey to Madinah.

When Abdullah was brought to the Prophet ﷺ as a newborn, the Prophet did what he always did with an infant: he performed tahnik, softening a date and placing it gently in the child's mouth. But there were no dates to be found. So the Prophet ﷺ used his own saliva instead, and Abdullah swallowed it. Of all the family of the Prophet, he was the only one whose tahnik was done this way, because there was simply no food to be had.

A sleepover in the house of the Prophet

Abdullah was still a young boy when his family finally made their way to Madinah, among the very last to migrate. From the moment he arrived, he attached himself to the Prophet ﷺ in every way he could. He fetched the water for his ablution. He carried his sandals. He wanted to be near him for every moment that was left.

His aunt Maymunah was one of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ, so Abdullah asked if he could spend the night at her house, not to see her, but to watch the Prophet. He lay across the foot of the bed and kept his eyes open. In the middle of the night the Prophet ﷺ sat up, wiped the sleep from his eyes, recited the closing verses of Surah Ali Imran, and rose to pray. Abdullah rose with him and stood at his side.

In the prayer the Prophet ﷺ gently moved him into place, and afterward he put his hand on the boy's head and made a dua that would shape the rest of his life. He asked Allah to teach him the Book and its wisdom. On more than forty occasions, it is narrated, the Prophet ﷺ held him and prayed that Allah would grant him deep understanding of the religion.

exalted be God, the one who is truly in control. [Prophet], do not rush to recite before the revelation is fully complete but say, 'Lord, increase me in knowledge!'Quran 20:114

Words he carried for life

One day Abdullah was riding behind the Prophet ﷺ when the Prophet turned to him and said he wanted to teach him some words. It was a quiet, heart-to-heart moment, and the boy never forgot it.

"Be mindful of Allah," the Prophet ﷺ told him, "and Allah will protect you. Be mindful of Allah, and you will find Him in front of you. If you ask, ask Allah. And if you seek help, seek help from Allah. Know that if the whole nation gathered to benefit you, they could not benefit you with anything except what Allah had already written for you. And if they gathered to harm you, they could not harm you with anything except what Allah had already written against you."

The Prophet ﷺ knew this boy would one day be a star, that crowds would gather around him and praise him. And so the lesson was simple: do not lean on people. None of them can truly harm you or help you. Keep your heart turned to Allah alone, and keep your intention sincere.

Knowledge is sought, it does not come to you

Abdullah had only about three years with the Prophet ﷺ, from the age of ten to thirteen, yet he narrated more than sixteen hundred hadith. He paid attention to everything: how the Prophet ate, what he recited in the silent prayers, the small details no one else thought to record. He went to the Prophet about the verses of the Quran, asking when each was revealed and why, until he understood the Book as few others did.

When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Abdullah was only thirteen, and he grieved deeply. But he noticed something: the companions who had scattered to other lands had all returned to Madinah for the funeral. He turned to a friend and said this was their chance to learn from them all. His friend preferred to go and play. Abdullah went alone.

He began going from door to door, laying his cloak on the doorstep of one companion after another and waiting in the dust for them to come out. They were embarrassed to find the cousin of the Prophet ﷺ sleeping outside their homes, and they would say, "You should have sent for us." He refused. Knowledge, he taught, is sought. It does not come to you. You go to it.

The ocean

By the time he was a young man, the companions had begun to call him by names that said everything. He was the interpreter of the Quran, the scholar of the ummah, and, most famously, the ocean. If someone said, "I asked the ocean," everyone knew exactly who they meant.

Umar ibn al-Khattab brought this teenager into his closest councils, seating him among men decades older. When some of the elders objected, Umar tested them all with a question about a chapter of the Quran. They gave good answers. Then Abdullah explained that the chapter was a quiet announcement of the Prophet's coming death, and Umar said he was the only one who had understood it.

In the time of Umar, Abdullah established what has been called the first true university of Islam. He set aside a day for the Quran, a day for hadith, a day for jurisprudence, a day for language and poetry, and a day for history. People camped outside his house to find a place inside. His students, almost all of them freed slaves whom he had taught and lifted up, would go on to become the great scholars of the next generation.

Wisdom, not only knowledge

Abdullah understood that being a scholar was more than knowing texts. Two men once came to him on the same day with the same question: if a man kills someone, can he be forgiven? To the first he said yes. To the second he said no. When asked why, he explained that he could read each man. The first had already fallen and was looking for a way back to Allah. The second was looking for permission to kill, and he would not hand it to him.

He carried that same care into the worst crisis of his time. When the Khawarij broke away, declaring other Muslims disbelievers and spilling blood, Ali sent Abdullah to reason with thousands of them in their valley. He dressed in his finest clothes, walked in among them as they ate, and met their three objections one by one, calmly, from the Quran they claimed to follow.

When he finished, two thousand of them repented and walked back with him. He had warned, years earlier, that people who raced to memorize the Quran without understanding it would fracture into sects and turn on one another. He had lived to see it, and he gave the rest of his life to healing it where he could.

The light that did not leave him

The Prophet ﷺ had once foretold that Abdullah would lose his sight, and near the age of seventy he went blind. He was not bitter. He said that if Allah had taken the light from his eyes, there was still light in his tongue and his heart. His mind could still reason, and he was pleased with his Lord.

He spent his last years in Ta'if, away from the turmoil that had overtaken the ummah, still reciting and still teaching. He had buried the Prophet ﷺ with his own hands as a boy, and he had carried that love for a lifetime, weeping when he recited, with two faint lines worn under his eyes from his crying in prayer at night.

When he died, those who washed his body said a white dove flew into his grave and did not come out, and that they heard a verse recited from the earth without knowing where the voice came from. His whole life had been the Quran, and so it was the Quran that saw him out.

'[But] you, soul at peace: return to your Lord well pleased and well pleasing; go in among My servants; and into My Garden.'Quran 89:27-30

What the life of Ibn Abbas teaches us

His story is not built on a single famous moment. It is built on a habit, kept since childhood: turn toward what is true, and go to it yourself.

  • Knowledge is sought, not awaited. He slept in the dust outside other people's doors rather than wait to be taught. What we want to learn, we have to go and get.
  • Attach your heart to Allah, not to people. He was told as a boy that the praise of the whole world could neither harm him nor help him. He built a life on that one truth.
  • Understanding matters more than speed. He warned that reciting without understanding breeds division. Depth, not pace, is what keeps knowledge safe.
  • Real scholarship is also wisdom. He read the hearts in front of him before he answered. Knowing the text is not the same as knowing the person who needs it.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Abbas?

He was a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the son of his uncle al-Abbas. Though he was only a boy during the final years of the Prophet's life, he became one of the most knowledgeable companions, known as the interpreter of the Quran and the scholar of the ummah.

Why is Ibn Abbas called the scholar of the ummah?

The Prophet ﷺ repeatedly prayed that Allah would teach him the Book and its wisdom. He devoted himself to learning, narrated more than sixteen hundred hadith, and was sought out by the senior companions for his understanding of the Quran, earning him the title and the nickname "the ocean."

How did Ibn Abbas gain so much knowledge so young?

He shadowed the Prophet ﷺ in his final years, asking about every verse he could. After the Prophet's death he went from companion to companion, waiting at their doors to record what they remembered. He taught that knowledge must be actively sought, not passively awaited.

What can we learn from the life of Ibn Abbas?

To seek knowledge ourselves rather than wait for it, to attach our hearts to Allah rather than to the opinions of people, and to pair what we know with the wisdom to use it gently and well.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think the people who knew the most were simply born that way. Abdullah ibn Abbas was not. He was a boy who decided, while his friends went off to play, that he would rather sit in the dust outside a stranger's door and wait to learn one more thing. The knowledge came because he kept going to it.

Most of us have something we keep meaning to understand: our prayer, our Book, a question we have been avoiding. His life is a quiet invitation to go and seek it, today, before the moment passes, and to hold what we learn with a heart turned toward Allah rather than toward the praise of anyone watching.
