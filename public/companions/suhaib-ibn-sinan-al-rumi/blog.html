Suhayb ibn Sinan al-Rumi

The Profitable Trade

He was an Arab boy with fair skin and light hair, born in a Persian city, captured by Roman soldiers, and sold from one master to the next for nearly twenty years. By the time he was free, he had even forgotten his own language.

This is the story of Suhayb ibn Sinan al-Rumi (may Allah be pleased with him): a man who finally built a fortune in Makkah, then handed over every coin of it to reach the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and was told it was the most profitable trade he could have made.

The Companions

Suhayb ibn Sinan al-Rumi

The Profitable Trade

An Arab boy, then a captive

Suhayb's father, Sinan ibn Malik, was an Arab so capable that the Persian emperor appointed him to govern a city on his behalf. So Suhayb grew up the son of a governor, an Arab child living among Persians, in the lands that are now part of southern Iraq. He was a much loved child, and for a while his life was settled and secure.

His roots were already a little distant from him, since he was raised far from any Arab tribe, in a household set apart by his father's rank. Then, when he was about five, his mother took him out to the countryside. Roman soldiers raided the area and carried off a large number of captives, the small boy among them.

He was separated from his mother in the slave markets, and he never saw either parent again. Sold to one master and then another, Suhayb spent close to twenty years enslaved in Roman lands. Because he was fair and had grown up among them, people assumed he was Roman. He learned to speak their language, and over the years he forgot his Arabic entirely.

He lost not only his freedom but his sense of where he belonged. Through all of it he held on to one thing: he knew he was an Arab, and he longed to find his way back to his own people. That quiet longing is what eventually set him moving.

The road to Makkah

When the chance came, Suhayb escaped and made for the land of the Arabs. He chose Makkah, a place known both as a sanctuary and as a city where a man could start again. So an Arab born in Persia, enslaved by Rome, looking and sounding like a Roman, arrived at last in the heart of Arabia.

In Makkah he came under the protection of Abdullah ibn Jud'an, the richest man in the city and a cousin of Abu Bakr. Ibn Jud'an was known not only for his wealth but for his generosity: he sent caravans of provisions to people suffering in distant lands, and the poor of Makkah were called to take their fill from his stores. He saw something in Suhayb and taught him trade. Suhayb turned out to be sharp and shrewd in the marketplace, and in time he became wealthy in his own right.

But his Arabic never lost its foreign accent, not even on the day he died. That is why the people called him Suhayb al-Rumi, Suhayb the Roman, even among his own. His wealth was real, yet it rested on borrowed protection. He had no tribe of his own to stand behind him, only the standing of the man who had taken him in.

Two men at the same door

Suhayb was perceptive, and when the first murmurs of the Prophet's ﷺ call reached the marketplace, they landed straight in his heart. He had met the Prophet ﷺ before revelation, through the house of Ibn Jud'an or through trade, and he had seen his character up close. Having lived among Zoroastrians, Christians, and idol worshippers, Suhayb had never truly belonged to any of them.

He heard that the Prophet ﷺ was quietly teaching a small group in the house of al-Arqam. Ammar ibn Yasir, who tells the story, arrived at that same door at the very same moment. Each asked the other what he wanted there. Each admitted he had come to hear what Muhammad ﷺ had to say.

They went in together. The Prophet ﷺ presented Islam to them, and together they accepted it. Suhayb and Ammar entered as two uncertain men and walked out as believers, their lives bound together from that day on. Suhayb was among the very first to make his Islam public.

No one to stand for him

By now Abdullah ibn Jud'an had died, and Suhayb's only shelter had died with him. He had no master, no tribe, no protector. As hostility toward the Prophet ﷺ grew, Suhayb was an easy target, and a tempting one, because anyone who harmed him could also seize his wealth.

So any of the powerful men of Makkah could take him from the street and torture him for days, and no one would say a word. The narrations place him beside Ammar in this: the two of them were beaten until they no longer knew what they were saying.

He was counted among the weak of Makkah, not for any lack of wealth, but because he had no clan to defend him. He was lumped in with Bilal, Khabab, and Ammar, the people the proud men of the city looked down on. And yet the Prophet ﷺ named these same men among the forerunners of the faith, saying that Suhayb was the first among the Romans to come forward to it.

The most profitable trade

When the believers began to migrate, Quraysh locked Suhayb up and watched him day and night, determined that he would not leave. One night he pretended to be ill, asking again and again to step outside, until his guards lost interest and lost sight of him. He slipped away toward Madinah.

They caught up with him on a hill. Suhayb, a skilled archer, warned that he would empty his quiver before they reached him. Then he offered a trade: they were angry that he had grown rich in their city, so he would tell them exactly where his hidden wealth was, and in return they would let him go. They agreed. He gave up the fortune of two decades and walked on with nothing.

When he reached the Prophet ﷺ, sick and empty-handed, the Prophet ﷺ said to him: "What a profitable trade, Abu Yahya. What a profitable trade." It was the same word the people of Madinah had used when they were promised nothing but Paradise. Some of the scholars say it was about Suhayb that this verse was revealed:

But there is also a kind of man who gives his life away to please God, and God is most compassionate to His servants.Quran 2:207

The brother Umar trusted

Suhayb arrived in Madinah hungry, sick, and worn out from the road, with one eye red and swollen nearly shut. The Prophet ﷺ told him to come and eat, and Suhayb fell on the dates and bread so eagerly that he forgot himself. The Prophet ﷺ teased him gently: was he eating dates with an eye in that state? Suhayb replied that he was chewing on the other side, away from the sore eye, and the Prophet ﷺ broke into a wide smile. From these small, warm moments grew a deep friendship between Suhayb and Umar ibn al-Khattab.

In Madinah, Suhayb said there was no battle of the Prophet ﷺ in which he was not at his side, in front of him, behind him, to his left or his right. And when Umar once listed the only three things he found odd about him, Suhayb answered each one simply: he carried the name, the lineage, and the love of feeding people that the Prophet ﷺ himself had given him.

Years later, when Umar was stabbed, he appointed Suhayb to lead the prayers of the Muslims until the council could choose a new leader. In a city full of the most senior companions, the man chosen to stand before them was the one with the foreign accent and no tribe of his own. Islam had remade the old order completely.

Suhayb wept over the dying Umar, crying out, "O my brother, my brother." And it was Suhayb who climbed down into the grave to receive Umar's body and lay it beside the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr. He lived quietly after that, withdrawing from the later disputes among the Muslims, and died in relative obscurity despite his lofty place.

The hadith that fit his life

Suhayb did not narrate many sayings of the Prophet ﷺ. But among the few he passed on is one of the most beloved hadith about how a believer meets whatever comes.

The Prophet ﷺ said that the affair of the believer is wonderful, for there is good for him in everything, and this is so for no one but the believer. If something good reaches him he is grateful, and that is good for him. If hardship reaches him he is patient, and that is good for him.

It is hard to imagine a saying that fits a life more closely. Captivity, slavery, exile, the loss of his parents, the loss of his fortune: at every turn Suhayb met it, and at every turn it carried him nearer to God. He had lived the meaning of those words before he ever repeated them.

What Suhayb's life teaches us

His story is not built on a single famous deed. It is the long arc of a man who kept moving toward what was true, and let go of everything that stood in the way.

  • Let go of what holds you back. Suhayb handed over the wealth of twenty years without flinching, because he had found something he valued more. We are tested by smaller versions of that choice almost every day.
  • Where you come from does not cap who you can be. An accent, a missing tribe, a hard past: none of it lowered his standing with the Prophet ﷺ or with Umar. Character did the lifting.
  • Good and hardship are both gifts. The hadith he carried turns life itself into something a believer cannot lose: gratitude in ease, patience in difficulty, and good in both.
  • Generosity is a habit worth keeping. He loved to feed people, holding to the Prophet's ﷺ words that the best of you are those who feed others. Open hands stay open in good times and bad.

Questions

Who was Suhayb ibn Sinan al-Rumi?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He was an Arab by birth who was taken captive as a child and raised among the Romans, then escaped to Makkah, became a merchant, and was among the first to publicly accept Islam.

Why was he called al-Rumi, the Roman, if he was an Arab?

He was captured by Roman soldiers as a boy and spent about twenty years enslaved in Roman lands. He came to look and sound Roman, and kept a foreign accent in his Arabic for the rest of his life, so the name stayed with him.

What was the "profitable trade" the Prophet spoke of?

To leave Makkah, Suhayb gave Quraysh all the wealth he had earned in exchange for his freedom to migrate. When he arrived with nothing, the Prophet ﷺ told him it was a most profitable trade, because he had given up the world to keep his faith.

What can we learn from the life of Suhayb?

That a person can rise above a painful past, that letting go of wealth and status for what is right is a gain and not a loss, and that meeting both ease and hardship well is itself a form of worship.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be asked to trade a fortune for our faith on a hilltop. But we are asked, in quieter ways, to let go of things we have worked hard for: a grudge, a comfort, a plan, a piece of pride. Suhayb's life makes that letting go look less like loss and more like a trade we will one day be glad we made.

There is also comfort in him for anyone who feels like an outsider, marked by an accent, a background, or a past they did not choose. None of it lowered his worth. What he carried inside, gratitude when life was kind and patience when it was not, is open to every one of us today. Take one of those into your day, and his story has done its work.
