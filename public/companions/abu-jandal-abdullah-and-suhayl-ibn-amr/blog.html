A Family and a Long Road Home

In one family of Makkah, almost everyone believed early. The mother, the sisters, the brothers, the uncles, two sons. Only the father held out, year after year, while the people he loved were driven from the city partly because of him.

This is the story of Suhayl ibn Amr (may Allah be pleased with them) and his two sons, Abdullah and Abu Jandal: a story of a long, difficult road home, and of a redemption no one around him expected.

Abu Jandal, Abdullah, and Suhayl ibn Amr

A Family and a Long Road Home

A house already full of faith

Suhayl ibn Amr was one of the great men of Quraysh: their chief orator, their negotiator, the most eloquent voice in Makkah. He was admired across the tribes, and he had a great deal to lose if Islam took root in his city.

His family thought differently. His son Abdullah was only about sixteen when revelation came, and he believed. His younger son, Abu Jandal, still a boy, followed his older brother quietly into faith. Both of Suhayl's daughters believed, and so did his brothers and an uncle by marriage.

One by one the people closest to him accepted the message. His two daughters and their husbands, and his son Abdullah, migrated to Abyssinia to escape the persecution Suhayl himself was helping to drive. His brothers believed too. One of them, Sakran, was the first husband of Sawda, who would later become a wife of the Prophet ﷺ.

So the whole tree around Suhayl turned toward faith: his children, his siblings, his in-laws. He alone remained the proud spokesman of Quraysh, and his tribalism slowly overtook not only his faith but his loyalty to his own family, all of whom were suffering because of his insistence on the old ways.

Abdullah and the question of two hearts

When Abdullah first weighed his faith, he met Umar one night in the valleys of Makkah, long before Umar himself believed. Both were restless, out under the stars, trying to ease something heavy in their chests. Abdullah put the question that was tearing at him: can a man live with two hearts, one for his people and one for himself, following his fathers while denying what his own heart feels?

Umar gave him a hard, honest answer. If it is faith that troubles you, he said, then follow it and bear the cost. I will be your enemy, but I will respect you. But if you choose safety over what you believe to be right, you will be small in my eyes. Abdullah went home and told his father he had found the very advice he was looking for.

He faced Suhayl directly. Would you rather have a son who is two-faced, he asked, or a loyal coward, or a courageous opponent? When his father chose the courageous opponent, Abdullah told him plainly that he now followed the religion of Muhammad ﷺ. Suhayl threatened to kill him, to disinherit him, to imprison him for life. To each threat Abdullah answered that what is with Allah is better and lasting, and that no chain could imprison his heart.

The defection at Badr

For a time Abdullah was imprisoned and tortured by his own father, until he pretended to renounce his faith and was released. In secret he kept helping the Prophet ﷺ, and his father believed he had returned to the old ways.

So when Quraysh marched out to Badr, Suhayl took Abdullah with him, certain his son was on his side. On the night before the battle, when the Muslims were badly outnumbered and the fight looked like it could destroy them, Abdullah slipped away from the disbelievers and crossed over to the Prophet ﷺ. He chose the smaller army and the harder road, and fought against his own father.

Suhayl was captured that day. Umar, who could not stand the man, asked the Prophet ﷺ for permission to knock out his teeth so his famous tongue would hang useless and never incite against the Muslims again. The Prophet ﷺ refused. I will not mutilate him, he said, lest Allah mutilate me. Then he added something no one else could see: leave his mouth, for one day it may please you, and he may take a stand you would envy.

Abu Jandal at Hudaybiyyah

Years later, the younger son's faith became known too, and Suhayl locked Abu Jandal in a chamber of his own house, as he had once imprisoned Abdullah. This young man had never lived near the Prophet ﷺ, never grown up among the believers. He simply held on, alone, in a cell.

When the Muslims came to Hudaybiyyah, it was Suhayl who came out to negotiate the treaty. The terms were painful: any Muslim who fled Makkah to Madinah had to be sent back, while the disbelievers kept theirs. As the ink was drying, Abu Jandal appeared, having escaped his prison and crossed more than a hundred miles in his chains. His body was covered in wounds. He cried out to the Muslims to save him.

Suhayl had already pushed hard at the table. He refused to let the document begin with the name of the Most Merciful, and refused to let it call Muhammad the Messenger of Allah, until the Prophet ﷺ wiped that title out with his own hand. Umar could barely contain himself, asking why the believers should accept such terms when they were on the truth. Only Abu Bakr could calm him, seeing what the Prophet ﷺ saw.

Then Suhayl seized his son by the collar and demanded he be the first returned under the new treaty. The Prophet ﷺ pleaded for this one young man, but Suhayl would not yield. The Companions watched, hearts breaking, as Abu Jandal was dragged back, calling out: O Muslims, will you return me to the disbelievers when I came to you as a believer? All the Prophet ﷺ could offer him were the words he had once given the persecuted in Makkah: be patient, for Allah will make a way out for you.

A way out, on the trade road

The way out came sooner than anyone expected. Abu Jandal escaped again, this time with Abu Basir, another believer Makkah had demanded back. Bound by the treaty, the Prophet ﷺ could not keep them in Madinah, so the two settled on the road between Makkah and Syria, beyond his protection.

There they were joined by others they freed from Makkah, until they were a band of more than seventy. They began intercepting the caravans of Quraysh, until the trade route that fed Makkah was strangled. The very condition Quraysh had insisted upon was now ruining them.

In the end, Abu Sufyan sent word to the Prophet ﷺ asking him to take the whole group into Madinah and erase that clause of the treaty. So Abu Jandal, Abu Basir, and their companions came at last to live beside the Prophet ﷺ. The patience the Prophet ﷺ had counselled in chains had quietly turned the treaty inside out.

The father comes home

Still Suhayl held out. When Makkah was finally conquered, he was among the few not covered by the general amnesty, and he hid himself away, sending Abdullah to plead for him. The Prophet ﷺ granted it. Even then, his sons calling him to faith, Suhayl did not believe.

It was at the Kaaba that it broke. The Prophet ﷺ asked the people of Makkah what they expected of him, and Suhayl answered that he hoped for good from a noble man, the son of a noble man. The Prophet ﷺ replied with the words of Yusuf to the brothers who had wronged him:

but he said, 'You will hear no reproaches today. May God forgive you: He is the Most Merciful of the merciful.Quran 12:92

The mouth that pleased them

In that moment, after more than twenty years, Suhayl believed. The change in him was total. He became known for his prayer, for standing at night, for his charity, and for weeping at the Kaaba, begging Allah for forgiveness. He grieved openly that his family had reached faith long before him, and that his pride had let others race ahead of him to every good.

He went out to the edge of Makkah to learn the Quran from a teacher of the Ansar. When someone scoffed that a man of Quraysh should not learn from an outsider, Suhayl answered that he could still see the traces of ignorance in them. Our stubbornness, he said, let humbler people outrun us to every good, while Allah raised them and left us behind. Once, in Madinah, Umar arranged the line at his door by who had believed first, and put Suhayl and the late-comers at the back. Suhayl did not complain. He told his companions to worry instead about being left at the back of the gates of Paradise, and resolved to spend the rest of his life trying to catch up.

When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, some in Makkah saw a chance to turn back. It was Suhayl who stood before the Kaaba and steadied them, using almost the same words Abu Bakr had used in Madinah: whoever worshipped Muhammad, he is gone; whoever worships Allah, Allah is ever-living. He warned them not to be the last to enter Islam and the first to leave it. Makkah held. The mouth the Prophet ﷺ had protected at Badr had pleased the believers at last, exactly as he foretold.

Then the father went to fight beside the two sons he had once tortured. At the battle of Yamama, Suhayl and Abu Jandal stood together against Musaylimah, and Abdullah was killed. Father and brother wept over him. Suhayl said he hoped his martyred son would count him first among those he would intercede for. Suhayl spent his remaining years on the frontiers, an old man still in the front lines, until he died a stranger far from home, as he had prayed he would.

What this family teaches us

Three lives, three different roads to the same place, and not one of them an easy one.

  • Follow your conscience, even alone. Abdullah refused to live with two hearts. He chose the truth over the safety of his father's approval, and never looked back.
  • Patience is not passivity. Abu Jandal was dragged back in chains, and told only to wait. That patience outlasted the very treaty that bound him.
  • No one is beyond return. The Prophet ﷺ saw good in Suhayl when no one else could. A man can hold out for twenty years and still come home and become a pillar.
  • Regret can become fuel. Suhayl mourned arriving late, then spent the rest of his life trying to catch up, on his knees at night and on the frontier by day.

Questions

Who were Suhayl ibn Amr, Abdullah, and Abu Jandal?

Suhayl ibn Amr (RA) was a leading chief and orator of Quraysh in Makkah. Abdullah and Abu Jandal were his sons, both early believers, while their father held out against Islam for more than twenty years before finally accepting it.

What happened to Abu Jandal at Hudaybiyyah?

Abu Jandal (RA) escaped his father's imprisonment in chains and reached the Muslims at Hudaybiyyah just as the treaty was signed. Under its terms he had to be returned to Makkah, a moment the Companions found almost unbearable to watch.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ protect Suhayl ibn Amr at Badr?

When Umar asked to disfigure Suhayl's mouth, the Prophet ﷺ refused to mutilate a prisoner, and said that one day that same mouth would please the believers and take a stand they would envy. Years later Suhayl did exactly that.

What can we learn from this family?

That conscience is worth following even when you stand alone, that patience can outlast injustice, and that no person is ever beyond a sincere return to Allah.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to divide people into the early and the late, the steadfast and the stubborn, and to decide too quickly which someone is. This family resists that. A son who crossed an army alone, a brother who waited in chains, a father who held out for twenty years and then wept at the Kaaba: all of them ended in the same place.

Most of us carry a little of each of them. Somewhere we are being asked to follow our conscience without applause. Somewhere we are being asked to be patient when nothing is moving. And somewhere, perhaps, we are the one who has delayed too long and quietly fears it is too late. Let this be the day you trust that the door is still open, for yourself and for the people you have written off. Then walk toward it, the way Suhayl finally did.
