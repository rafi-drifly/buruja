Ali and Fatima

The First Family

She was a child when she learned to wipe the dirt from her father's back and tell him, through her own tears, that it would be alright. He was a young man with one shield to his name and not enough silver for a wedding gift.

This is the story of Fatima bint Muhammad and Ali ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with them): two people raised inside the household of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and the quiet, simple marriage that joined them.

The Companions

Ali and Fatima

The First Family

A child born into the storm

Fatima was the youngest daughter of the Prophet ﷺ and Khadijah (RA). Many historians say she was born in the first year of prophethood, around the year 610, either just before the revelation came or just after. She was a baby in the years when her mother was running up the mountain of Hira with food, and a small child through the hardest days that followed.

Khadijah named her Fatima, after Khadijah's own mother. The name carries the sense of a child carried full term and weaned, healthy and complete. Of all the Prophet's children, Fatima alone was born and raised entirely within Islam.

Her sisters had known the years of comfort, when their father was at the height of his standing in Makkah. Fatima knew none of that. Her childhood was the boycott, the hunger, and the sight of her father returning home wounded. That, some scholars say, is part of why her faith ran so deep: she grew up inside the cost of it.

The mother of her father

When the command came to warn his closest relatives, the Prophet ﷺ gathered his family and called only two of them by name. One was his aunt Safiyyah. The other was Fatima. He told each of them plainly that he loved them, and that even they had to answer to Allah by their own deeds, that his love could not stand in their place on the Day of Judgement.

After Khadijah died, the Prophet ﷺ lost the one who had steadied him at home. He was now beaten and humiliated in public, and he would come back through his own door carrying the marks of it. Fatima, around ten years old, took up the role her mother had left. She would clean his wounds and comfort him.

The people gave her a name for it. They called her Umm Abiha: the mother of her father. A young girl who carried a grown man's grief, because she could not bear to see him hurt.

The day they crushed his back

One of the cruelest scenes of those years was arranged for sport. While the Prophet ﷺ prayed alone at the Kaaba, a powerful man named Uqbah ibn Abi Mu'ayt, urged on by Abu Jahl, gathered the filth and entrails of a slaughtered camel. He waited for the Prophet to go down into prostration, then dumped the whole weight onto his back. It was so heavy his body could not rise.

Word reached the house. The only one home was Fatima, still a child. She came running to the sanctuary, weeping, and pulled the filth from her father's back with her own hands while the crowd laughed.

It was one of the very few times the Prophet ﷺ prayed against his attackers by name. But first he turned to his daughter. "Do not cry," he told her. "Allah will support your father."

In another moment of those hard years she confided that she was in pain, and that they had nothing even to eat. He answered her gently: were she not pleased to be the leader of the women of her people. With the manners of a girl raised in that house, she asked about Maryam, the mother of Jesus. He told her that Maryam was the best woman of her time, and her own mother Khadijah the best of hers. Among the women whose faith was perfected, he counted Maryam, Asiyah, Khadijah, and Fatima.

A father's love, in full view

The family migrated to Madinah, and Fatima travelled with her father. His love for her was so plain that others in the family felt it. When some of them asked outright who was most beloved to him from his household, he answered without hesitation: Fatima, his daughter.

His habit told the same story. When the Prophet ﷺ left Madinah, the last person he would visit was Fatima. When he returned, before he went anywhere else, he went first to her. Our mother Aisha (RA) said she never saw anyone who resembled the Prophet more closely than Fatima, in the way she stood, walked, spoke, and carried herself.

When Fatima entered a gathering, the Prophet ﷺ would stand for her, take her hand, kiss her forehead, and sit her in his own place. When he entered upon her, she would rise and do the very same for him. In a land where men had once buried their daughters alive, he honoured his daughter in front of everyone, and let them see exactly how a daughter should be treated.

The cousin raised in the same house

Ali ibn Abi Talib was the Prophet's young cousin. Years earlier, when his uncle Abu Talib was struggling under poverty, the Prophet ﷺ had taken Ali into his own home to raise, just as his uncle Abbas took in another of the children. Ali grew up beside Fatima inside that household, and accepted Islam as a boy, among the very first.

By the time they all reached Madinah, Ali was old enough to live on his own, and he had moved out. He was known for his knowledge, his patience, and his strength, but not for his wealth. He was poor, and he knew it.

So when Abu Bakr and then Umar came to ask for Fatima's hand and the Prophet ﷺ gently turned them away, Ali assumed the matter was settled and far beyond him. If the Prophet had declined men like those, what chance did a young man with nothing have?

The proposal he could barely speak

Encouraged by his family and by some of the companions, Ali finally gathered his courage and went to the Prophet ﷺ. He sat down, and the words would not come. He, one of the most eloquent of men, sat in silence, then stumbled over a few sounds, shaking. The Prophet asked kindly if he needed something, and still he could not answer.

At last the Prophet ﷺ smiled and said it for him: perhaps he had come to ask for Fatima's hand. Ali lowered his head and said yes. Asked what he had to give, Ali admitted he owned almost nothing. The Prophet pointed to his shield. Ali sold it, and that became the gift.

Then the Prophet ﷺ went to Fatima to ask her. She, overcome by her own modesty, could not say a word either. He understood her silence for what it was, not refusal but shyness, and counted it as her consent. Two people who could not bring themselves to speak their wish, and a father who quietly carried the marriage between them.

There was even room for warmth and laughter. Fatima once teased her father that he had married her to a man with a large stomach. He answered by naming what mattered in Ali: among the first to accept Islam, the deepest in knowledge, the greatest in patience and forbearance toward people.

The simplest of weddings

The community gathered around them with joy. Uthman (RA), who would soon be Ali's brother-in-law, met him selling the shield in the market and bought it for four hundred coins, far more than its worth, then handed it straight back as his wedding gift. The Prophet ﷺ prayed for Uthman's generosity when he heard. Bilal (RA) helped Ali buy perfume. Umm Salama and others prepared the home. Abu Bakr and Umar helped choose simple clothes.

The dowry of the woman the Prophet ﷺ called the leader of the women of Paradise was a few silver coins. Their home held one bed that was a couch by day, cushions they shaped from the clay of the valley, a water skin, and stones to grind grain. The Prophet himself helped prepare it. He said the most blessed weddings are the ones that cost the least.

On the wedding night he told them to wait for him. He came in, called for the water in their house, and made supplications for them both. Then he prayed for them: "O Allah, bless them, and bless what is between them, and bless their offspring." With that quiet prayer, the first family of his household was joined.

What this family teaches us

Their story is not a single grand event. It is a child's tenderness, a father's honour, and a marriage stripped down to what actually matters.

  • Tenderness is its own kind of strength. A ten-year-old girl wiped the dirt from her wounded father's back and told him to take heart. She could not fight his enemies, so she carried his sorrow instead.
  • How we treat our families is the real test. The Prophet ﷺ stood for his daughter, kissed her forehead, and gave her his own seat, in a society that once despised daughters. Honour shown at home was never beneath him.
  • Character outweighs wealth. Ali came with one shield and a few coins. What he was chosen for was his faith, his knowledge, and his patience, not what he owned.
  • Keep it simple, and let it be blessed. The most beloved family began with a few silver coins, clay cushions, and one shared prayer. The blessing was never in the cost.

Questions

Who were Ali and Fatima?

Fatima bint Muhammad (RA) was the youngest daughter of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and Khadijah (RA). Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA) was the Prophet's cousin, raised in his household, and among the first to accept Islam. They married in Madinah and formed the first family within the Prophet's own house.

Why was Fatima called the mother of her father?

After Khadijah died, the Prophet ﷺ was beaten and humiliated in public. Fatima, around ten years old, cared for him and comforted him at home, taking up the role her mother had left. The people called her Umm Abiha, the mother of her father, for the love and care she showed him.

What was the dowry of Fatima?

It was a few silver coins, from the sale of Ali's shield and a little more he gathered. The Prophet ﷺ taught that the most blessed marriages are the ones that cost the least, and the wedding of his beloved daughter was kept deliberately simple.

What can we learn from their story?

That tenderness toward those we love is a strength, that how we treat our families is the true measure of us, that character matters more than wealth, and that the most blessed beginnings are often the simplest ones.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to think that love for our families should wait for the right moment, the comfortable year, the time when we finally have enough. This family had none of that. A daughter loved her father most when he was most rejected. A father honoured his daughter in plain sight. Two people who could barely speak built a home out of almost nothing, and it was called blessed.

Most of us will be tested the same way, not in public but at home, with the people who depend on us. Their lives are an invitation to be gentle with those we love before circumstances are easy, to honour them openly, and to stop measuring a home by what fills it. Carry one of those into today, and their story has done its work.
