The Companions

Khalid ibn al-Walid

Becoming the Sword of Allah

There was a young man in Makkah who could outride, outshoot, and outthink every fighter of his generation, and who used all of it, for years, against the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the people who followed him.

This is the story of Khalid ibn al-Walid (may Allah be pleased with him): the finest soldier of his age, the man behind the Muslims' hardest defeat, and the one who would later be called the Sword of Allah.

Khalid ibn al-Walid

Becoming the Sword of Allah

The house he was born into

Khalid came from Banu Makhzum, one of the proudest clans of Quraysh and a long-standing rival of the Prophet's own clan, Banu Hashim. His father, al-Walid ibn al-Mughira, was the chief of that clan: the wealthiest man in Makkah, one of its most eloquent, and the elder whose opinion the others waited to hear.

His father was generous on a scale that is hard to picture. He kept homes lit and stocked for travelers, and it was said he owned every other house from Makkah toward Ta'if. But the generosity was for show. He carried the nickname al-Wahid, the one alone, because he would pay to cover the Kaaba by himself one year and let all of Quraysh share the cost the next.

When the Prophet ﷺ began to recite, al-Walid sat and listened, and admitted in private that what he heard was not the speech of a man or a spirit. Then his nephew, Abu Jahl, worked on his pride until he turned away and called it magic. The Qur'an answered him by name, verse after verse, and exposed even the secrets he had hidden.

Even on his deathbed, al-Walid was still counting. He left instructions about money owed to him and an old score to settle, and almost nothing of mercy. This was the father whose shadow Khalid grew up in: generous, gifted, and ruined by his own pride. It is worth holding that picture, because the son would turn out to be nothing like him.

A born soldier

Khalid was the youngest of al-Walid's many children, and he wanted none of his father's trade. He cared about one thing only: war. From boyhood he learned the arts of battle, and people said he resembled his cousin Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) in size and strength: tall, powerfully built, the two of them known as the strongest young men in the city.

What set Khalid apart was that he mastered everything at once. He was the best at archery, the best with the spear, the best on horseback and on camel, the best with the sword, and the sharpest strategist of them all. On caravan journeys he did not study markets. He gathered the stories of the great generals of Persia and Rome.

He was related, through his mother and his father, to almost everyone of standing in Quraysh. There was little anyone could do to disadvantage such a man. He had power, wealth, ability, and a mind built for the battlefield, and almost no reason yet to think about the new faith taking root in his city.

The day he became their hero

Khalid's reputation among the Makkans was made at the Battle of Uhud. Abu Sufyan placed him in command of one flank, facing the small hill where the Prophet ﷺ had stationed fifty archers and ordered them, plainly, not to leave it for any reason.

When most of the archers came down early to gather the spoils, Khalid was watching from the far side. He wheeled his cavalry around the hill, struck the Muslims from behind, and turned a battle that was nearly won into the worst loss the believers had yet known. He went home to a hero's welcome, the man who had won the day for Makkah.

It is worth remembering what the Prophet ﷺ was praying through that defeat. With blood on his face, he said: "O Allah, forgive my people, for they do not know." The man who had just devastated them was, in those words, already being prayed for.

A heart that would not settle

Khalid faced the Muslims again at the Trench and watched them again at Hudaybiyyah, where the Prophet ﷺ slipped past his cavalry on a side road, using Khalid's own kind of tactic against him. To a man who lived for strategy, that was not only clever. It looked like something more.

His brother, al-Walid ibn al-Walid, had been taken captive at Badr and ransomed, and on the way home he slipped back to Madinah to embrace Islam as a free man, so that no one could say he had only done it to escape his chains. From then on, for years, he wrote to Khalid, calling him gently to the faith.

In one letter he told Khalid that the Prophet ﷺ had asked after him and said, "A man like Khalid is too intelligent not to recognise the truth." That a man he had fought for years asked after him with care, rather than anger, reached something in him that no defeat ever had.

Then came a dream. Khalid saw himself in a narrow, barren, miserable land, and then walking out into a wide green meadow. He woke and knew it was not an ordinary dream. Years later Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) told him what it meant: Allah had brought him out of the tightness of his old life into the open space of Islam.

The road to Madinah

When Khalid finally decided, he wanted company on the road. He sounded out his closest friends. Safwan ibn Umayya refused. So did his cousin Ikrimah, the son of Abu Jahl, who could not follow the man he believed had killed his father. Khalid did not argue with them. He understood grief.

Word reached Abu Sufyan, who came with the elders and drew his sword. Khalid did not lie or hide. "Yes, it is true," he said. It was Ikrimah, who had refused to come himself, who stepped between them and ended it: Khalid had the right to choose. That night Khalid felt finished with his own people, and he set out alone for Madinah.

On the way he met Uthman ibn Talha, and then Amr ibn al-As, each travelling for the same reason without knowing it. The three of them walked into Madinah together. When the Prophet ﷺ saw them, he smiled and said, "Makkah has given us its very heart."

Islam erases what came before

Khalid greeted the Prophet ﷺ as a messenger of Allah, and then he asked for something. "Pray for forgiveness for me," he said, "for everything I did to turn people away from the path of Allah." He had fought this man for years. He did not pretend otherwise.

The Prophet ﷺ did not list his wrongs. He said simply, "Islam erases what came before it," and then prayed, "O Allah, forgive Khalid." Khalid said that from that day, the Prophet ﷺ never treated him as anything other than a beloved companion, as though the old enmity had never been.

There was no long settling-in. Khalid knew one thing, and he offered it at once: "O Messenger of Allah, put me to work. I am ready. I need no training." Within two weeks his moment came.

The Sword of Allah

At the Battle of Mu'tah, three thousand Muslims faced a Roman army many times their size. The Prophet ﷺ had named three commanders in turn: Zayd ibn Haritha, then Ja'far, then Abdullah ibn Rawaha. One after another, all three fell. In Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ wept as he was shown their deaths.

When the banner was offered to Khalid, he refused at first. "You have more right to it than I do," he told the senior Companion who held it. He was the newest believer there, standing among the people of Badr, and he knew what that meant. Only when they all agreed on him did he take it.

Then Khalid went to work. He spread his small force, created the illusion of a far larger army, broke nine swords in his hand, cut a way through, and brought the Muslims home alive. From the pulpit in Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ said: "Khalid has taken the banner, and he is a sword among the swords of Allah." From that day he was Sayfullah, the Sword of Allah.

One more thing tells you who he had become. There were verses of the Qur'an, in Surah al-Muddathir, revealed about his own father and his rejection of the Prophet ﷺ. Khalid went to Salim, a known reciter, and asked him to teach those verses so he could repeat them himself. His faith, he felt, would not be complete until he could. He learned the lines about his father, and said them with a trembling voice, and let them stand.

What Khalid's life teaches us

Khalid's story is not really about battles. It is about a proud, gifted man who let the truth change him, and then spent everything he had in its service. The most striking parts of his life happen not on the field, but quietly, in his heart.

  • A past does not have to be a sentence. He had fought Islam for years. When he turned, he was told plainly that Islam erases what came before, and he was never once treated as the enemy he had been.
  • Gentleness can reach where force cannot. Patient letters from a brother, and a Prophet ﷺ who asked after him with care, did what no battle ever could. Khalid was won by kindness, not defeated by it.
  • Give what you are good at. Khalid did not become a scholar or a reciter. He brought his one great skill and laid it at the service of the truth, and that was enough.
  • Humility sits well on the strong. The finest soldier of his age refused the banner because others had served longer. He only led when his brothers asked him to.

Questions

Who was Khalid ibn al-Walid?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the clan of Banu Makhzum in Makkah, and the most accomplished general in early Islamic history. He first fought against the Muslims, then embraced Islam before the conquest of Makkah, and was given the title Sayfullah, the Sword of Allah.

Why is Khalid called the Sword of Allah?

At the Battle of Mu'tah, after the three appointed commanders were killed, Khalid took the banner and brought the heavily outnumbered Muslim army home safely. The Prophet ﷺ then said, "Khalid is a sword among the swords of Allah," and the name stayed with him.

Did Khalid really fight against the Muslims first?

Yes. His strategy at the Battle of Uhud turned a near-defeat into a victory for Makkah and caused the Muslims their hardest loss. He embraced Islam years later, of his own choice, and asked the Prophet ﷺ to seek forgiveness for him.

What can we learn from the life of Khalid?

That a person's past need not define them, that gentleness can change a heart no force could reach, and that offering your own particular gift in the service of something good is a worthy life.

Why this story stays with us

It would have been easy for Khalid to stay where he was. He had everything his world valued, and a long record of standing against the very faith he came to accept. What moved him was not a defeat but a slow softening: a brother who kept writing, a man who asked after him with kindness, a dream of walking out of a narrow place into open air.

Most of us carry something we would rather not be reminded of, and most of us have one or two things we are genuinely good at. Khalid's life joins those two truths: the past can be set down, and what you do well can be given away for something larger than yourself. Carry that into today, and his story has done its work.
