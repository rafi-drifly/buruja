Khunais ibn Hudhafah

One of the First Twenty

How many people have you met named Khunais? How many times have you heard the name? Almost no one would recognise it. Yet he was among the first twenty human beings on earth to accept Islam.

This is the story of Khunais ibn Hudhafah (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the earliest believers, the man who carried his whole family to faith, and a companion whose every honour the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ later sealed with his own hands.

The Companions

Khunais ibn Hudhafah

One of the First Twenty

A name almost no one remembers

Khunais was from the clan of Banu Sahm, one of the houses of Quraysh in Makkah. He was the older brother of Abdullah ibn Hudhafah, who would one day have a remarkable story of his own. At the time of the Prophet ﷺ, Khunais was still young, in his teens, and his brothers were younger still, some of them small children.

His nickname was Abu Hudhafah. It followed an old custom: people assumed that one day he would have a son and name that son after his own father. As it turned out, that son was never born. But the name stayed with him, in the days of ignorance and in Islam.

He is barely known to Muslim memory today. There is no single dramatic scene attached to his name, no famous saying, no battle named after him. And yet his quiet record holds nearly every early honour a believer could earn.

He is also a reminder of how much good can be done by someone history almost forgets. Through his hands a whole circle of people came to faith, and from that circle would come a legacy that outlived him by far.

Among the first twenty

Khunais was a young man when he heard the call of Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). Most of those who came to Islam in the very beginning came through Abu Bakr, and most of them were young. Khunais was no exception.

What sets him apart is the timing. He embraced Islam before the believers ever gathered in the house of al-Arqam, in the first secret years, when the message was carried quietly from one trusted heart to the next. By that reckoning he is counted among perhaps the first twenty people to accept this religion.

Imagine being one of the first twenty human beings to say yes to Islam, and your name being almost completely unknown to the Muslims who came after. That is the strange weight of his story: an enormous place in the beginning, and a quiet place in our memory.

To accept the message then was not a small thing. There was no community to belong to, no protection in numbers, no certainty of how any of it would end. There was only the truth itself, and a young man willing to stake his life on it.

He did not come to faith alone

When Khunais accepted Islam, he did not keep it to himself. As the eldest of his family, he went home and brought his two younger brothers, small children at the time, to embrace the faith with him.

He did not stop at his brothers. He brought seven of his cousins, and four other members of the Banu Sahm clan. Among the Muslims he was seen as their head, their most senior figure, the one who led them in.

There is something gentle in that picture. A young man, barely out of boyhood himself, becoming the door through which his small brothers, his cousins, and his clansmen walk into faith. He did not wait to be older, or stronger, or safer. He simply shared what he had found.

Then he led them further. When persecution closed in, Khunais was among that noble group who made the hijra to Abyssinia, leaving home to protect their religion through migration. He carried his family out of harm and into safety, for the sake of what they now believed.

Chosen by a careful father

Khunais was among those who later returned from Abyssinia to Makkah. There, a certain man offered him the hand of his daughter. That man was Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), and Umar did not give his daughter lightly.

Umar could see through weak character very easily. He had a deep sense of perception and a great regard for faith and good conduct. He wanted his eldest daughter, Hafsa, to have a husband worthy of her, and he chose Khunais: one of the earliest Muslims, a man who had already shown deep faith and strong character. It is a quiet testimony in itself, that a man known for reading people would hand his daughter to Khunais.

So a young couple was formed in Makkah under the pressure of its people: Khunais, by then in his early twenties, and Hafsa (may Allah be pleased with her), about fourteen years old. When Umar made the hijra to Madinah, Khunais and Hafsa went with him, and they settled into a new life in the home of Abu Abs, a man of the Ansar.

By marriage, Khunais was now bound to one of the great figures of the early ummah, the man who would later be the second leader of the Muslims. Through Hafsa, he became the first son-in-law of Umar, and so he was drawn close to the household of the Prophet ﷺ in those early Madinan days.

The only one of his clan at Badr

When the Battle of Badr took place in the year 624, Khunais was the only member of Banu Sahm to fight in it. That detail carries a quiet weight.

Islam had done away with the evils of tribalism, but in the aftermath of a battle people still looked for the members of their own family among the ranks. Almost everyone had a relative there beside them. Khunais did not. His younger brothers were still too young to fight, so he went forward alone, the single representative of his clan among the veterans of Badr.

He returned with a rank that cannot be bought. He was now not only one of the first to believe, one of those who migrated to Abyssinia and then to Madinah, but also one of the people of Badr: the stripes a believer would most want to carry when meeting Allah.

These are not small categories. Each one is the kind of honour a believer might spend a whole life hoping for. Khunais held several at once, and still he is a name we have to be reminded of.

A wound at Uhud, and a grave beside Uthman

Khunais was also among those who fought at Uhud. His name is not always listed in the formal counts of the martyrs, but what is recorded is that he was wounded there, and that the wound led to his death some time later. By that, he is counted among the shuhada of Uhud as well.

When he died, the Prophet ﷺ himself took his body, prayed the janazah over him, and buried him. He laid Khunais to rest right beside the beloved companion Uthman ibn Maz'un, a place of real honour.

He and Hafsa were never blessed with the son he had been named for. He left this world young, having gathered firsts that most companions never reach: early faith, two migrations, Badr, and martyrdom. And Hafsa, still in her late teens, became a young widow.

From that loss began another chapter. Umar sought a righteous husband for his widowed daughter, turning first to Abu Bakr and then to Uthman. In the end it was the Prophet ﷺ himself who married Hafsa, so that the first wife of Khunais became one of the Mothers of the Believers.

The good he set in motion

There is a teaching that the one who guides another to good is like the one who does that good himself. Khunais lived inside that promise. Much of what he is owed today is not in his own deeds alone, but in the deeds of the people he brought to faith.

Among the small brothers he once led to Islam as children was Abdullah ibn Hudhafah, who would grow into a companion with a profound legacy of his own. That legacy traces back, in part, to an older brother who took a young boy by the hand and brought him to the Prophet ﷺ.

So Khunais leaves us twice over. Once in the long list of his personal firsts, and once in the lives he turned toward Allah: a brother who became a story of his own, and a wife who became a mother of this entire ummah. Few of us will ever know how far a single act of guidance can travel.

What Khunais's life teaches us

His record is long, but his fame is small. That gap is exactly where his lesson lives, and it speaks directly to lives like ours, lived mostly out of sight.

  • The one who guides to good shares in it. Khunais brought his brothers, his cousins, and his clansmen to Islam. One of those young brothers, Abdullah, would leave a great legacy of his own. Faith you pass on keeps working after you are gone.
  • You can be early without being remembered. He was among the first twenty believers, yet his name is barely known. He shows that the value of a deed is with Allah, not in how many people repeat your name.
  • Quiet faithfulness gathers real weight. No single dramatic scene, just one steady yes after another: belief, migration, Badr, Uhud. A life of small loyalties added up to almost every honour a believer could earn. We tend to wait for a grand moment to prove our faith. His life suggests the grand moment is usually just many small ones, kept up over time.
  • Sometimes you stand alone. He was the only one of his clan at Badr, with no relative beside him. When no one else from your circle is there, you can still step forward for what is right, and trust that you are not truly alone in it.

Questions

Who was Khunais ibn Hudhafah?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Banu Sahm clan of Quraysh. He was among roughly the first twenty people to accept Islam, made the migrations to Abyssinia and Madinah, fought at Badr, and was the first husband of Hafsa, the daughter of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Why is Khunais counted among the first Muslims?

He accepted Islam through the call of Abu Bakr while still very young, before the believers gathered in the house of al-Arqam. By that timing he is reckoned as one of the first twenty people to embrace the religion.

How did Khunais ibn Hudhafah die?

He was wounded at the Battle of Uhud and died from that wound some time later, so he is counted among the martyrs of Uhud. The Prophet ﷺ prayed the janazah over him and buried him beside Uthman ibn Maz'un.

What is the connection between Khunais and Hafsa?

Hafsa (may Allah be pleased with her) was the first wife of Khunais, given to him by her father Umar. After Khunais died of his wound, Hafsa was widowed young, and she later married the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, becoming one of the Mothers of the Believers.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to measure a life by how widely it is remembered. Khunais quietly undoes that idea. He was among the very first to believe, he carried his family to faith, he migrated twice and fought at Badr, and still most of us had never heard his name. None of that lessened his place. The Prophet ﷺ buried him with his own hands.

Most of us will live the way Khunais did: doing good that no crowd records, drawing the people closest to us toward what is right, standing for the truth even on the days we feel we are standing alone. His life is a gentle reminder that the record kept by Allah is the only one that lasts, and that it misses nothing. The brother he guided, the wife he loved, the battle he stood in: all of it was seen, even when his name slipped from human memory. Bring one quiet, unseen act of faith into today, and his story has done its work.
