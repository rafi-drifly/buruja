The Companions

Abdullah ibn Ja'far

The Son of the Man of Two Wings

Some lives stay in one place. This one moved through almost every great event of early Islam, and through more grief than most hearts are ever asked to hold. Few people stood so close to so much, or carried so much loss so quietly.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Ja'far (may Allah be pleased with him): born to refugees in a foreign land, son of a beloved martyr, and a man the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ took into his own care when he was still a child.

The Companions

Abdullah ibn Ja'far

The Son of the Man of Two Wings

A child born far from home

Abdullah was not born in Makkah, and he was not born in Madinah. He was born in Abyssinia, the land the early Muslims called Habasha, across the sea.

His parents were Ja'far ibn Abi Talib and Asma bint Umays. They were among the believers who had fled there to escape the persecution of Makkah, crossing the water to find safety under the Negus, the just king who gave them shelter. Some of those families stayed for years. There were children born in that land, Abyssinian babies who would only later come to Madinah, and Abdullah was one of them.

His father, Ja'far, was the one who had spoken for the Muslims before the Negus, representing them with such eloquence and wisdom that he secured their place in that kingdom. The Prophet ﷺ loved him dearly. So Abdullah was born into a household at the very heart of the believing community, yet in a country none of them had chosen.

His people were Makkans, but he had never seen Makkah. His earliest world was the land that had taken his family in. It is a story that repeats in every age: a child born somewhere his parents never planned to be, because the place they loved had turned against them. That was the world Abdullah opened his eyes to.

The boat, and a father embraced

One day the family boarded a boat. Not back to Makkah, the city Abdullah had never seen, but onward to Madinah, where the believers had now built a home.

He crossed those hard waters as a young child with his parents, Ja'far and Asma. The boats from Abyssinia arrived in Madinah at the time of the victory at Khaybar. When they reached the city, Abdullah watched the Prophet ﷺ run to meet his father and embrace him. The Prophet ﷺ said he did not know which made him happier: being reunited with his cousin Ja'far, or the victory that had just been won.

Imagine seeing that as a child. The most important man in your parents' world, and in the lives of all the believers around you, runs to your father with that kind of joy.

So Abdullah did not arrive as a stranger among followers. He arrived as family. He was not simply one who followed the Prophet ﷺ; he was kin to him, and the Prophet ﷺ noticed him at once and drew him close. That is how his life in Madinah began: held, and loved, from the first moment.

The man with two wings

Then his father was sent far away, to the battle of Mutah, in the land of Jordan. There Ja'far became one of the most famous martyrs of that day. He held the banner until both his arms were cut from him on the field.

Think about how quickly the world was changing for this one boy. His life had already touched the persecution of Makkah, the migration to Abyssinia, and the unique journey to Madinah. Now his father had been martyred in the most painful way, thousands of miles from the home Abdullah had been born in, and thousands of miles from the city his family came from.

The Prophet ﷺ came to the grieving house. Abdullah's mother was weeping. With tears in his own eyes, the Prophet ﷺ held the boy and comforted him, and promised that he would care for him and that Allah would keep him under His divine protection.

Soon people began to greet Abdullah with a new name. They would say: welcome to the son of the man of two wings. For the Prophet ﷺ had told them that he saw Ja'far flying in Paradise. Allah had given him, in place of the two arms taken from him, two wings by which to fly through the Garden. A grieving boy now carried a name made entirely of his father's reward.

A family marked by loss, and by love

After Ja'far, Abdullah's mother Asma married Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). On the way to the Farewell Hajj with the Prophet ﷺ, at the place of Miqat, she gave birth to Abdullah's half-brother, Muhammad ibn Abi Bakr. So his life now touched the Farewell Hajj of the Prophet ﷺ as well, the journey on which the believers made their pilgrimage at his side.

When Abu Bakr passed away, Asma washed his body, and then she married Ali (may Allah be pleased with him), who was Abdullah's own uncle. In time Abdullah himself would marry two of Ali's daughters, Zaynab and Umm Kulthum, at separate times. Ali, it is said, loved that the daughters of his house would marry into the family of Ja'far.

There is a lot of family to hold here, so it is worth holding it gently. Abdullah was the son of Ja'far and Asma. Ja'far passed, and Asma married Abu Bakr. Abu Bakr passed, and Asma married Ali. Each name carries great weight in this history, and each one was woven quietly into the story of a single man who rarely stood at the centre of any of it.

Old enough to remember

Abdullah was about ten years old when the Prophet ﷺ passed away. He was not too young to remember any of it. He was aware, and he carried it all clearly.

He remembered the crossing from Abyssinia to Madinah. He remembered the martyrdom of his father at Mutah. And he remembered the day the Prophet ﷺ left this world, the man who had stepped so gently into his life when his own father was gone.

You can imagine how hard that last loss must have been. The Prophet ﷺ had stepped into Abdullah's life after his father was gone, had held him in his grief and promised to care for him, and now he too had left this world. Few people of Abdullah's age had seen so much, or felt the absence of so many. The losses were not distant history to him. They were his own memories, kept for a lifetime.

Karbala, and a heart that kept breaking

Abdullah's wife was Zaynab al-Kubra, the daughter of Ali and the sister of Husayn (may Allah be pleased with them). So when the terrible day of Karbala came, his life was bound to it. Husayn was martyred. Some of Abdullah's own sons were lost in that day. And his wife Zaynab was taken into captivity, made to walk through that entire painful aftermath.

It is hard to imagine carrying such a thing: to be in one place while, far away, your sons are killed and your wife is taken. Later, after Zaynab had passed, Abdullah married Umm Kulthum bint Ali, and her death too was a tragedy he had to witness.

Grief returned to him again and again, in form after form. Yet through every loss, he remained one of the most respected and beloved of his people. He is remembered, in the end, not for what was taken from him, but for the way he carried it.

An ending, and a thread that holds it together

In the year 80 after the Hijra, there was a storm the people called al-Jahaf, the flood that swept everything away. It was not a wide disaster. It struck one small valley, near Makkah, during the days of Hajj, as the pilgrims were preparing for the standing at Arafah.

It came with such force that many of those pilgrims, and their belongings, were lost in it. One of them was Abdullah ibn Ja'far. So consider the shape of his life. Born to refugees in Abyssinia. Carried by boat to Madinah. His father martyred in a faraway land. The Prophet ﷺ passing in his childhood. Karbala touching his own household. And at the very end, he died as a pilgrim, on the road to worship his Lord.

It can be hard to know what to do with a life like this, so much grief gathered around one family, generation after generation. The household of the Prophet ﷺ carried sorrow upon sorrow, and Abdullah's line carried more than most. But there is a thread running through it that is worth holding onto. Sometimes Allah chooses certain families for difficulty. That was surely the case here. Yet they were not chosen for difficulty alone. They were chosen for it because they were promised reward.

The father whose arms were taken was given wings. The boy who buried so much was kept honoured and beloved until the day he died on the path of pilgrimage. The hardship and the reward came together, and one was the measure of the other. That is why a story so full of loss does not leave us in despair. It leaves us with something steadier: the trust that nothing a believer endures is wasted, and that Allah remembers what He asks of His servants.

What Abdullah's life teaches us

His story is not built on one great deed. It is built on a lifetime of loss met with patience, and a heart that stayed soft through all of it.

  • Grief does not cancel honour. Abdullah lost his father, his sons, and those he loved, again and again. He is remembered not for being spared, but for how faithfully he carried what he was given.
  • A kind word can hold a grieving heart. The Prophet ﷺ came to a weeping house and held a fatherless boy. We can each be that presence for someone in their hardest hour.
  • Belonging is something we offer. A child born far from home was made family the moment he arrived. The people who feel like outsiders around us are waiting for someone to draw them close.
  • Some are chosen for difficulty, and for reward. The family of the Prophet ﷺ was tested deeply, yet the hardship carried a promise. Trial in a believing life is never wasted.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Ja'far?

He was a companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the son of Ja'far ibn Abi Talib and Asma bint Umays. Born in Abyssinia to migrant believers, he was brought to Madinah as a child and raised within the Prophet's own family.

Why is his father called the man of two wings?

Ja'far was martyred at the battle of Mutah, where both his arms were cut off. The Prophet ﷺ said he saw Ja'far flying in Paradise, with two wings given to him in place of his arms. Abdullah was greeted as the son of the man of two wings.

How did Abdullah ibn Ja'far's life connect to Karbala?

His wife was Zaynab, the daughter of Ali and sister of Husayn. When Husayn was martyred at Karbala, some of Abdullah's own sons were lost, and his wife Zaynab was taken captive. The tragedy reached directly into his household.

How did Abdullah ibn Ja'far die?

In the year 80 after the Hijra, a violent storm known as al-Jahaf struck a small valley near Makkah during the days of Hajj. Many pilgrims were lost in the flood, and Abdullah was among them. He died as a pilgrim on the way to worship.

Why this story stays with us

Abdullah did not choose where he was born, or which losses would find him. Almost nothing about his life was in his hands. What was his to choose was how he met it: with patience that did not harden into bitterness, and a heart that stayed tender through grief after grief.

Most of us are not asked to face what he faced. But we are all asked the same quiet question, in smaller ways, on ordinary days: when something is taken, will we let it close us, or will we carry it with grace and still reach for others? Ask Allah for that kind of heart today, and let Abdullah's life show you what it looks like.
