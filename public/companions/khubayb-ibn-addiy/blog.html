Khubayb ibn Adiy

A Prisoner of Many Miracles

He was a prisoner in Makkah, alone, marked for execution, with no friend left alive to stand beside him. He could have saved himself with a single word, or a single act of cruelty. He chose neither.

This is the story of Khubayb ibn Adiy (may Allah be pleased with him): a companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ whose captivity carried more miracles than almost any other, and whose last hours became a lesson the believers would never stop repeating.

The Companions

Khubayb ibn Adiy

A Prisoner of Many Miracles

One of the early helpers

Khubayb came from the tribe of Banu Aws among the Ansar of Madinah. He belonged to those original helpers who received the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ when he arrived, and who gave him their support without holding anything back.

He was also one of the people of Badr. That alone was a lofty title among the companions. He had stayed the course with the Prophet ﷺ through the hardest days, the kind of man who was there at the beginning and did not waver.

His name itself was gentle. In Arabic it is the smaller form of an older name, almost like saying "little." There is a quiet irony in that, because the test placed on this man would be anything but small.

His life belongs to a long line of believers who were persecuted simply for standing with the truth. The same spirit that carried Bilal and Khabbab through their torture in Makkah would carry Khubayb through his. What follows is one of the most painful chapters in the whole story, and also one of the most luminous.

The ambush at al-Raji

Khubayb was sent out with a small group of companions, and at a place called al-Raji they were ambushed. It was a betrayal against every custom of the time: unsuspecting men, surrounded, with no fair fight offered to them. It is among the least remembered and most painful episodes in the life of the Prophet ﷺ.

The captors called down to the trapped men with assurances. We do not want to harm you, they said. We only want to take you to Makkah and gain something for you. Three of the companions did not trust it and chose to fight where they stood, and they were killed. Three others came down.

The moment they descended, they were seized and chained with a roughness that gave the promise away. One of the three, seeing the deception for what it was, took up his sword again and was killed for it. That left two prisoners to be carried to Makkah, Khubayb among them.

Both men had fought at Badr, and both had a price on their heads. The families who had lost their leaders that day were waiting, and they meant to buy these prisoners and take their revenge. Khubayb was sold into the hands of one such family and marked for death.

The first prisoner to ask for halal food

The captors in Makkah held a strange sense of sanctity. They would not kill in a sacred month, so they kept Khubayb a prisoner until the month of Safar, roughly a month away. He knew the date of his own death, and he knew it was coming.

The woman set to watch him was Zaynab bint al-Harith, not yet a believer. Her own father had been among those Khubayb fought at Badr, so she had no reason to think kindly of him. Yet she would later narrate his story in full, and the scholars say no companion has more miracles reported about him than Khubayb.

The first was the simplest, and the most telling. When she asked if he had any request, he said only this: do not bring me meat slaughtered in the name of your idols. He would rather starve than eat it.

Think of what that means. A man counting down the days to his own execution, in chains, at the mercy of his enemies, and the thing he guarded most carefully was his obedience to Allah. The danger to his body did not loosen his hold on his faith.

Grapes out of season

Then Zaynab saw something she could not explain. Khubayb sat in his cell with a bunch of grapes, and she said they were larger than any she had ever seen. There were no grapes growing anywhere in Makkah at that time, and she was giving him nothing of the kind.

She came back, and the grapes were there again. And again, and again. It unsettled her at first, this fruit that came from nowhere. Finally she asked him where it came from, since it was not from her hand.

He answered that his Lord was feeding him. The provision came from Allah alone, given to a man his captors were starving toward death. It is the same answer recorded long before, when the provision of another righteous servant puzzled the one who watched over her:

Her Lord graciously accepted her and made her grow in goodness, and entrusted her to the charge of Zachariah. Whenever Zachariah went in to see her in her sanctuary, he found her supplied with provisions. He said, 'Mary, how is it you have these provisions?' and she said, 'They are from God: God provides limitlessly for whoever He will.'Quran 3:37

The razor and the child

As the day drew near, Khubayb asked Zaynab for a razor. She asked why. He said he wished to groom himself for his meeting with his Lord. She gave it to him.

A while later she lost sight of her small child, and her heart dropped. She went looking and found the boy sitting on Khubayb's lap, the razor still in his hand. Here was the moment any captor would have feared, and any prisoner might have used: a hostage, a blade, a way out.

He looked at her and understood her terror. He said, in effect, you think I would kill him? We the followers of Muhammad ﷺ are not like that. He set the razor down and handed the child back. He had been betrayed and chained and condemned, and still he would not let himself become a man who wrongs the innocent.

It is the heart of who he was. A person who is wronged often feels entitled to wrong someone in return, and tells himself the injustice he suffered has earned him the right. Khubayb refused that bargain even with his life on the line. Being a Muslim, he showed, means you do not become an oppressor just because you have been oppressed.

Two units of prayer, and a steady heart

When the month of Safar came, they took him outside the sacred precinct to a place called al-Tanim, the nearest point beyond the Haram. There they prepared to put him to death in the open, as an example to anyone watching. They told him to make a last wish, and he asked to pray two units of prayer first.

He prayed quickly, then turned to them and explained why. If he had not feared they would think he was delaying out of fear of death, he said, he would have prayed much longer. He did not want to give them that satisfaction. With this he set a practice that captured believers after him would follow: two units of prayer before death.

Then they began to break him slowly. They asked whether he would rather be safe at home, with the Prophet ﷺ put in his place instead. Barely able to speak, he said he would not wish even a thorn to prick the Prophet ﷺ to spare himself all of this. Even Abu Sufyan, who was watching, admitted he had never seen anyone love another the way the companions loved the Prophet ﷺ.

As the torture went on, Khubayb began to speak in verse. He described the crowds gathered against him, the women and children brought to watch, and turned his grief to his Lord. He said he wept, but not from fear of death, for he knew he had to die in any case. What he feared was the Fire. And then the line the believers would carry after him: I do not care how I am killed, so long as I die a Muslim, and I will show my enemy no fear, for my return is to my Lord. It was, in its full sense, the first crucifixion in the history of Islam, and yet the man on the stake was not the one who seemed defeated.

A salaam with no one to carry it

As his strength left him, Khubayb realized he would not see the Prophet ﷺ again in this life. So he called out to Allah, in front of all of them, that there was no one present to carry his salaam to the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. He asked Allah Himself to deliver it.

Far away in Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ was overcome in the way he was when revelation came, and told his companions that Khubayb had sent his greeting. He returned the salaam. He grieved deeply for the companions who had been lost at al-Raji, and for a month afterward, in every prayer, he prayed for them.

Khubayb was killed at al-Tanim, where pilgrims today renew their intention to return to the sacred house. His captors tried to turn his face away from the qiblah, and each time he turned it back. When they left his body, they found it had turned toward the qiblah on its own, and they understood that they had killed no ordinary man.

Later the Prophet ﷺ sent two trusted men on a quiet mission to bring the body back. Though a long time had passed, it had not decayed. Khubayb was carried home to Madinah and buried among his people, the helper who had given everything he had.

What Khubayb's life teaches us

His story is short, and almost all of it takes place in chains. Yet it holds a kind of strength that ease rarely produces.

  • Do not let being wronged make you a wrongdoer. Khubayb was betrayed, chained, and condemned, and still he refused to harm a child to save himself. Being treated unjustly never gave him licence to be unjust.
  • Trust that provision comes from Allah. He guarded what he ate even as a starving prisoner, and Allah fed him from where no one could account for it. The heart that holds to its principles is never truly abandoned.
  • Meet hardship without giving fear the last word. He kept his final prayer short so his enemies would not mistake devotion for dread. His certainty stayed visible to the very end.
  • Love what is good more than you fear loss. He would not wish a single thorn upon the Prophet ﷺ to spare himself. His love was larger than his own pain.

Questions

Who was Khubayb ibn Adiy?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah and one of the people of Badr. He was captured in the ambush at al-Raji and held as a prisoner in Makkah, where he was eventually executed.

What happened at al-Raji?

A small group of companions was ambushed in betrayal at a place called al-Raji. Several were killed on the spot. Khubayb and another companion were taken as prisoners to Makkah, where families who had lost men at Badr sought revenge on them.

Why are there so many miracles in his story?

His captor, Zaynab bint al-Harith, later narrated what she witnessed: provisions appearing out of season, his calm and his mercy, and his body turning toward the qiblah. The scholars say no companion has more miracles reported about him than Khubayb.

What can we learn from the life of Khubayb?

That being wronged is no excuse to wrong others, that provision and protection come from Allah, and that faith can stay steady and even gentle in the hardest moment a person can face.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be tested the way Khubayb was. But the choice at the centre of his story is one we meet often, in much smaller forms. When we are treated unfairly, do we hold to what is right, or do we tell ourselves that the wrong done to us has earned us the right to do wrong back?

Khubayb answers that question with a razor set down and a child handed back. He shows that a person can be stripped of everything and still keep the one thing that matters: a heart that refuses to harm, that trusts Allah for its provision, and that meets even its last hour without letting fear speak for it. Carry a little of that steadiness into your own day, and his story has done its work.
