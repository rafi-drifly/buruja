Amr ibn al-Jamuh

No Limping in Jannah

He was an old man with a limp, a chief of his tribe, and for most of his life he loved a small idol of wood and dressed it in perfume and jewels. When faith finally reached his heart, it did not make him a scholar or a leader of armies. It made him want one thing only: to walk into Paradise.

This is the story of Amr ibn al-Jamuh (may Allah be pleased with him): the chief of Banu Salima, the last of his people to believe, and a man whose simple, burning longing for Jannah is hard to forget.

Amr ibn al-Jamuh

No Limping in Jannah

The chief who loved his idol

Amr ibn al-Jamuh was the chief of Banu Salima, one of the tribes of Madinah. By the time the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ arrived in the city, Amr was already in his sixties, one of the few elders who had survived the long wars between the tribes of Yathrib. He was known as a peacemaker, a man of wealth, and a man of generosity.

There is something tender, and a little sad, in how he held his religion. The idols of Madinah were not a business the way they often were elsewhere. For men like Amr, the devotion was sincere. He had a personal idol he called Manat, kept in his home, and when the elders gathered they would pay their respects to it.

He loved that idol the way a man loves something he has cared for all his life. He spent freely on it: perfuming it, dressing it, placing jewellery upon it. When the elders of his people came to his home, they honoured it alongside him.

When Islam came to his city, Amr had no quarrel with the Prophet ﷺ. He simply was not interested. He was happy, he said, with his religion, and at his age he saw no reason to trade it for another.

A family that believed in secret

What Amr did not know was that his own household had already moved on without him. His wife, Hind, and his sons had quietly embraced Islam, as most of the Ansar had. They kept it from him, because they knew how much it would hurt him, and they began, gently, to warm him to the idea.

His son Muadh was a close friend of Muadh ibn Jabal, and the young men of the family were close to the household of Abdullah ibn Amr, whose own story would become one of the most moving among the Ansar. Faith was all around Amr, in the people he loved most. He only had to let it in.

Hind would raise the subject, gently, again and again. Each time, Amr gave the same answer: he was content with his way, and not looking for another. He even grew wary of how much time the young men spent around the Prophet ﷺ, worried for the children of his house.

So Hind changed her approach. Rather than argue with him, she suggested something quieter. Why not simply listen for themselves, she said, and learn what this man had truly come with, before deciding anything about him at all?

How beautiful these words are

Hind asked Amr if he would like to hear what their son Muadh had learned from the Prophet ﷺ. He was wary at first, but he agreed, and his son recited some of what he had memorised. The boy chose verses from Surah Yusuf.

Amr listened. He was an old man, and he still loved his idol, but he was honest. "How beautiful," he said, "how perfect are these words." He asked whether everything this man taught was as lovely as this. "Even better than this, father," his son answered. "This is only a taste."

Something had shifted in him. His family urged him to accept the religion, as all his people had. But Amr was a chief and an old man set in his ways, and he could not simply walk away from a lifetime of devotion.

"Not until I take the opinion of Manat," he said. He wanted to ask his idol first. It is a strange and human moment: a man already half-convinced, going to a piece of wood for permission to follow the truth he had just heard with his own ears.

Gods are not found in the dust

So Amr went to his idol. He spoke to it plainly: people were leaving, he said, and he wanted none other than it, so did it have any answer to this man who said it was no god at all? The idol, of course, said nothing. Amr decided he would wait, and see if guidance came.

His sons saw their chance. By night they took the idol, dragged it down, and threw it into a pit. Amr found it in the morning, cleaned it, and set it back in its place. The next night they did it again. This time he tied a sword to it and said, "If anyone comes for you, defend yourself."

The young men came again. They took the idol, the sword and all, tied it to the carcass of a dead dog, and threw it into a well. When Amr went searching and found it there, in the dust, bound to a dead animal and unable to lift the sword left for its defence, something in him finally broke clean. "If you were truly a god," he said, "you would not be tied to a dog, thrown in a well."

A simple, complete faith

Amr gathered his people. "Are you not upon what I am upon?" he asked them, for he was their chief and they followed his lead. They said yes. So he told them plainly: he now believed in what had been revealed to Muhammad ﷺ.

You might expect a man who resisted so long to struggle with faith afterwards, to find it cold or difficult. The opposite happened. Amr tasted the sweetness of belief quickly and completely. He was old and unlettered, and his understanding of Islam was beautifully simple: believe in Allah, pray, and reach Paradise.

He did not become a scholar. He did not become a scribe. He simply turned all the devotion he had once poured onto a piece of wood toward his Lord, and he did it with his whole heart.

There is a quiet beauty in that. The same capacity for love that had been spent on something that could not hear him was now given to the One who could. That simple longing would soon carry him further than any title ever had.

My sons are keeping me from Paradise

Amr had a severe limp. He walked with a cane, and could barely use one leg. When the Battle of Badr came, his sons, the very ones who had broken his idol, kept him home. He was too old, they said, and too weak to fight. He was heartbroken. He had heard the Prophet ﷺ praise the people of Badr, and he wanted that for himself.

"You kept me from Paradise on the day of Badr," he told them. "By Allah, if I am given the chance, I will enter it." When the call to Uhud came, he was older still and weaker still, yet he volunteered at once. Again his sons sat him down and begged him to stay. Allah had excused him, they said. There was no need.

So Amr went to the Prophet ﷺ himself, in tears. "My sons are keeping me from Paradise," he said. It is hard to imagine the purity of a heart that weeps not over loss or pain, but over the fear of missing Paradise.

His children explained that they only feared for him, that he was old and would not be able to bear it. The Prophet ﷺ did not push him away. "Do not forbid him," he said. "Perhaps Allah will grant him the martyrdom he seeks."

I can see you walking in Jannah

Before the battle, Amr asked the Prophet ﷺ a question that shows exactly what he hoped for. "O Messenger of Allah, if I fight and I am killed, do you think I will be able to walk on both my feet in Paradise?" He had limped his whole life. The Prophet ﷺ told him: yes.

On the morning of Uhud, his wife Hind remembered him gathering his things and leaving the house with a prayer: "O Allah, do not turn me back." He went forward into the battle, his son Khallad fighting bravely in another part of the field, and Amr pressed on saying, "I want Paradise, I want Paradise," until he was killed.

Afterwards the Prophet ﷺ paused over his body. "It is as if I can see you," he said, "walking in Jannah on your two healthy feet, with no limp at all." In one narration he simply said that he had seen Amr walking, no longer limping at all.

Amr was buried in a single shroud beside Abdullah ibn Amr, among the martyrs of Uhud. The man once thought of as old, weak, and behind his people had walked, at the very end, straight into the one thing he had asked for.

What the life of Amr ibn al-Jamuh teaches us

His is not the story of a brilliant scholar or a famous warrior. It is the story of an old man with a limp who wanted one thing with his whole heart, and that is exactly why it stays with us.

  • It is never too late to turn. Amr was in his sixties, set in his ways, and the last of his people to believe. The door did not close on him, and it does not close on us.
  • Honesty opens the heart. When he heard the Qur'an, he did not pretend it left him cold. He admitted it was beautiful, and that small honesty was the beginning of everything.
  • Simple faith can be complete faith. He never became a scholar. He believed, he prayed, and he longed for Paradise. A sincere and simple devotion was enough to carry him.
  • Direct your devotion toward what deserves it. He had spent a lifetime caring for an idol. He took that same capacity for love and gave it to his Lord, and it transformed him.

Questions

Who was Amr ibn al-Jamuh?

He was the chief of Banu Salima, one of the tribes of Madinah, and a respected elder known for his generosity and peacemaking. He accepted Islam late in life and was martyred at the Battle of Uhud.

Why did Amr ibn al-Jamuh resist Islam at first?

He had a sincere, lifelong devotion to a personal idol he called Manat, which he cared for and adorned. He had no political quarrel with the Prophet ﷺ; he simply did not want to leave a religion he loved, until he saw the idol could not even defend itself.

What was special about how he died?

Amr had a severe limp and walked with a cane, yet he insisted on fighting at Uhud, longing for Paradise. He was killed there, and the Prophet ﷺ said he could see him walking in Jannah on two healthy feet, with no limp.

What can we learn from the life of Amr ibn al-Jamuh?

That it is never too late to turn to Allah, that honesty can open a hard heart, and that a simple, sincere faith focused on prayer and the hope of Paradise can be enough.

Why this story stays with us

Amr did not have much to offer by the world's measure. He was old, he could barely walk, and he came to faith long after everyone around him. He had no learning to give and no strength to fight with. What he had was a heart that wanted Paradise so badly that he wept when his own children tried to keep him safe at home.

Most of us will not be tested on a battlefield. We are tested in quieter ways: whether we will still turn back to Allah when we feel too old, too set, or too late; whether our love for Him is sincere enough to redirect from the things we have always clung to. Amr's longing is worth borrowing. Carry even a small share of it into your next prayer, and his story has done its work.
