Thumama ibn Uthal

The King Who Was Tied to a Pillar

He may have been the most powerful Muslim in the world on the day he said the words. Before that day, there was no face on earth he hated more than the face of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

This is the story of Thumama ibn Uthal (may Allah be pleased with him): a king who came into Madinah as a prisoner, was met with nothing but kindness, and walked out three days later a different man.

Thumama ibn Uthal

The King Who Was Tied to a Pillar

The man who surrounded Makkah and Madinah

Six years after the migration, the Prophet ﷺ made the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah with Quraysh, and a door opened. With the fighting paused, he sent letters to the kings and rulers of the world, inviting them to Islam. Some smaller leaders accepted. Most of the great kings answered with contempt.

One of those letters went to Thumama ibn Uthal. He was the chief of the tribe of Banu Hanifa, but more than that, he was Malik al-Yamama, the king of Yamama, the wide region that wrapped around Makkah and Madinah on almost every side.

His land sat across the trade routes north toward Syria, east toward Iraq, and south toward Yemen, and both Makkah and Madinah leaned on those roads to live. He could choke off the cities by simply closing them. His people were intermarried with feared desert raiders, so even outside open war he could make travel impossible. Strategically, politically, economically, he was a man you did not want to anger.

Some of the historians say the wisest policy with a man like this was to leave him be, so that Madinah could keep growing and the message could keep spreading without provoking his wrath. But the Prophet ﷺ was inviting the world to Islam, and so a letter went out to him too. It was not a hostile letter. Thumama answered it the way the harshest of the kings had answered theirs. He did not just kill the messenger. He killed the entire group sent with it, which was, in the language of the time, a declaration of war.

Captured on the way to Makkah

Some time later, the Prophet ﷺ sent out a small force of horsemen under Muhammad ibn Maslamah to clear the desert of the bandits and mercenaries who were preying on travellers. They rode far from Madinah, hunting a group known for vanishing into the sands as quickly as they struck.

In one raid they took a number of captives, along with many camels and sheep, and brought them back to the city. One of the prisoners was a tall, commanding man dressed in the robes of a king. Muhammad ibn Maslamah did not know who he had caught.

When the prisoners were led to the mosque, the Prophet ﷺ looked up and recognised the man at once. It was Thumama, the king the Muslims were at war with, captured by accident on his own way to Makkah.

Tie him to the pillar

Here was a moment that could have changed everything. To execute Thumama would have sent a chilling message to Quraysh and to every ruler in the region: we have killed the most powerful man among you, and you may be next.

The Prophet ﷺ did none of that. He said to tie Thumama to one of the pillars of the mosque. Then he gave another instruction: bring him the best food and the best milk you have. Treat him well.

So this proud king, who had just killed the Prophet's envoys, was bound inside the mosque of Madinah. The prayers went on around him. The Muslims came to him with good words and good food. There was no hostility in their faces, only care. He watched, and he wondered who these people were.

There is something quiet and deliberate in this. The mosque of the Prophet ﷺ was an open house. All kinds of people walked through it, and whoever entered was treated with the same dignity and saw the Muslims in their natural state, at worship and at ease. Thumama was not lectured or pressured. He was simply placed where he could watch faith being lived, and left to draw his own conclusions.

Three days, three questions

The Prophet ﷺ walked up to him and asked, "What do you have, Thumama?" The king answered with all his pride intact. If you kill me, he said, my blood will be avenged. If you show me kindness, you will be showing it to a grateful man. And if you want wealth, name your price and it is yours. The Prophet ﷺ simply smiled and walked away.

The next day the same question, and the same proud answer. Again the Prophet ﷺ smiled and left him. On the third day, when Thumama repeated himself, the Prophet ﷺ gave a quiet, surprising command: let him go.

No ransom. No money taken, though it would have been lawful. No revenge for the men Thumama had killed. No conditions at all. To everyone watching, it must have looked like the most dangerous decision in the world. They had simply released the king who surrounded their cities.

The most beloved face on earth

Thumama left the mosque, but he did not go far. He had already decided, while still a captive, that he wanted to be Muslim. He had only refused to say it in chains, so that no one could ever claim he had been forced. He found water near the city, washed, and came back.

He walked into the mosque and declared, in a loud voice, that there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad ﷺ is His messenger. Then he turned to the Prophet ﷺ and spoke from the heart. There was no face on earth, he said, that I hated more than yours, and now your face is the most beloved of all faces to me. There was no religion I hated more than yours, and now it is the dearest of all religions. There was no land I hated more than your land, and now Madinah is the most beloved land in the world to me.

He had come to Madinah hating it the way a person hates an occupier of his own home. He left it loving it more than anywhere on earth. His heart had turned completely, by his own account, in the space of a few hours.

Scholars later drew a careful lesson from this. A man had once sat in this same mosque for years, hearing every verse and watching every prayer, and his heart had stayed closed. Thumama needed only a few days. Guidance is not earned by the length of time spent near the truth; it is a gift Allah places in a heart when He wills, and a little sincerity can open it faster than years of distance can.

The boycott that brought Makkah to its knees

Thumama still wished to perform the pilgrimage he had set out for, so the Prophet ﷺ taught him how to do it as a believer. This made him the first Muslim to enter Makkah and call out the talbiyah of pure monotheism, in a city that had spent years twisting that very call.

Some of the young men of Makkah moved to attack him, until the elders pulled them back: this is Thumama, they said, if he cuts our trade routes we are finished. They were right to be afraid. Angered by their treatment of him, Thumama swore that not a single grain would reach them without the Prophet's permission, and then he went home and closed the roads.

Nothing grows in Makkah. The city began to starve. In the end Abu Sufyan wrote to the Prophet ﷺ, appealing to him in the name of God and their shared kinship to let the food through. And the Prophet ﷺ, the mercy to the worlds, who would not punish people who had no part in the wrong, agreed. He wrote to Thumama to open the routes, and Thumama obeyed.

Some scholars connect a verse of the Qur'an to this season of hunger and the hearts that still would not soften:

We have already afflicted them, yet they did not submit to their Lord: they will not humble themselvesQur'an 23:76

A king who chose to disappear

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, a false prophet named Musaylimah rose in Yamama, Thumama's own region, and swept much of it into rebellion. Thumama could have ridden that wave. Backing Musaylimah might have preserved his kingdom and his power. Instead he stood before his people and warned them, plainly, that this man was a liar and that following him would only end in ruin.

When they would not listen, he refused to stay among them. He gathered the believers who remained loyal and left to join the Muslim forces gathering against the rebellion, giving up the throne rather than betray what he now believed.

And then the records simply lose him. We do not know how Thumama died, or where he is buried. A man who had once held a kingdom, who could have paralysed whole regions with a word, chose to meet his Lord as an unknown servant somewhere far from any throne. There is no grave to visit. There is only the example.

He is remembered for a handful of firsts: the king tied to a pillar who was won by kindness, the first Muslim to enter Makkah calling the pilgrimage of pure monotheism, the man who placed that city under boycott and then lifted it at the Prophet's word. But the truest measure of him may be the part with no record at all. When his faith and his throne could not both survive, he kept his faith and let the throne, and even his own name in history, slip quietly away.

What Thumama's life teaches us

His story is usually told as a single scene: a king tied to a pillar, treated with kindness, who became Muslim. The whole life around that scene is what gives it weight.

  • Kindness can do what force cannot. The Prophet ﷺ had every reason to make an example of Thumama, and instead chose pardon and good treatment. A heart that hatred could not reach was won in three days.
  • Let people see you in your ordinary worship. Thumama was changed not by an argument but by watching the Muslims pray and live with sincerity. The character we keep when we think no one important is watching is the strongest invitation of all.
  • Sincerity refuses the easy escape. He would not say the words in chains, and later he gave up a kingdom rather than follow a liar. He wanted his faith to be his own, owed to no one but Allah.
  • You do not need to be remembered to be honoured. Thumama disappeared from the records by choice, content to be unknown to people if he was known to his Lord. A quiet ending can hold more faith than a famous one.

Questions

Who was Thumama ibn Uthal?

He was the chief of the tribe of Banu Hanifa and the king of Yamama, a powerful region surrounding Makkah and Madinah. He first waged war on the Muslims, then accepted Islam after being captured and treated with kindness in Madinah.

Why was Thumama tied to a pillar in the mosque?

He was brought to Madinah as a prisoner of war. The Prophet ﷺ ordered that he be tied to one of the mosque's pillars and given the best food and milk, so that he would witness the Muslims at prayer and in their daily character before any decision was made about him.

What is Thumama known for being first in?

After accepting Islam, he completed the pilgrimage he had set out to perform, which made him the first Muslim to enter Makkah calling out the talbiyah of pure monotheism. He also placed Makkah under a trade boycott until the Prophet ﷺ asked him to lift it.

What can we learn from the life of Thumama?

That kindness can soften a heart that force never could, that sincere worship is itself an invitation, and that true faith may quietly give up power and recognition rather than compromise.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to believe that hard hearts are won by being out-argued or overpowered. Thumama's story says something gentler and harder. He was not defeated into faith. He was disarmed by three days of food, good words, and the sight of people praying with sincerity, until the face he had hated most became the one he loved most.

We meet our own Thumamas more often than we think: the person who has decided to dislike us, the relative who keeps his distance, the heart that seems closed. We rarely change them by winning. Treat them well when you have every excuse not to, let them see your better self when no one is keeping score, and leave the result to Allah. That is the work this story leaves in our hands today.
