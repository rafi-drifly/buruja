Loving Husayn and the Question of Yazid

A Sunni Reflection on Love and Hate

Every Muslim who prays sends peace upon the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and upon his family. To say those words and not love Husayn ibn Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) would leave something hollow at the centre of the prayer.

This is not a story of one event. It is a reflection on how the scholars of the Sunni tradition taught us to hold two feelings at once: deep love for the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, and honest grief over how he died, without letting either one carry us past the boundaries of faith.

A Sunni Reflection on Love and Hate

Why we love him before anything else

Set aside, for a moment, how Husayn died. Imagine we only had the gentle scenes: the Prophet ﷺ lifting him, holding him, kissing him, the joy this child brought into his grandfather's life. Picture who his grandfather was, and who his parents were, Ali and Fatima (may Allah be pleased with them). That alone would be reason enough to love him.

The Prophet ﷺ taught that Allah loves those who love his family, and he asked the ummah to love them for his sake and for the sake of Allah. There is no tension between those two loves. Allah loves what the Prophet ﷺ loves, and the Prophet ﷺ loves what Allah loves. To love Husayn is simply to follow that chain of love back to its source.

This is not sentiment alone. It is woven into our worship. A Muslim who reaches the end of the prayer and sends peace upon the Prophet ﷺ and his family, and yet feels nothing for Husayn, has something deficient in the very faith that the prayer is meant to express.

So the love comes first, and it does not depend on tragedy. We would have loved Husayn if he had lived a long and quiet life and died in his bed. That he was taken instead in one of the most painful crimes in our history only deepens an attachment that was already there.

The day at Karbala

Then came Karbala, and the killing of Husayn. It is hard to read his last hours without being moved: the isolation, the sense of abandonment, the betrayal by people who had called him to them, and finally a death so brutal that the books still struggle to describe it.

When a believer reads that chapter honestly, it pulls on the heart in every direction. It is meant to. This was not a small loss, and we should not try to make it one. It was, as the scholars said, one of the greatest tragedies ever to strike this community.

Some Muslims worry that feeling the grief too deeply might carry them past the bounds of correct belief, and so they wonder whether they should dim the story down. The answer is not to mute it. It is to feel it fully and let the principles of faith hold the shape of that feeling.

Dr. Omar Suleiman, who tells this story in his series The Firsts, notes that around the month of Muharram these emotions run high. There is heat, there is tension, there are hateful words. His aim, and ours here, is the opposite of that heat: to sit with the grief calmly and ask what it is supposed to teach us.

The question of Yazid

Yazid ibn Mu'awiya was not a companion of the Prophet ﷺ. He was not a man of virtue, and he is not someone a believer can love. He carries a terrible record: complicity in the killing of Husayn, and later terror brought upon the people of Medina and Mecca. When Imam Ahmad was told that some people claimed to love Yazid, he asked plainly how anyone who loves Allah could love such a man.

So why did many Sunni scholars stop short of cursing him by name? Not out of any affection for him, and not out of any softness toward the crime. When Imam Ahmad was asked why he did not curse Yazid, he answered with a question of his own: when have you ever seen your father curse anyone? His restraint was about his own habit of speech, not about Yazid's worth.

Other scholars gave a principle of caution. The exact degree of Yazid's guilt is disputed, and a person's final state is known only to Allah. To curse a specific named individual could open a door they preferred to keep shut. Yet this was never unanimous. A great scholar of the Hanbali school, buried near Imam Ahmad in Baghdad, wrote a whole treatise arguing that cursing Yazid is permitted.

You do not have to whitewash the crime

Here is the point that gets lost in the shouting. You do not have to excuse what happened, and you do not have to curse anyone, to stand inside the mainstream of how Sunni scholars handled this.

Most of them hated the crime and refrained from naming a curse. Some did curse. But not one of them used that restraint to soften or shrink the murder of Husayn. The discussion was about where to draw a careful line around hatred, never about belittling what was done to the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ.

Dr. Suleiman shares a memory his father carried from the city of Medina. Some people came to the grave of Abu Bakr and insulted him and Umar, which is itself an ugly thing to do. Another group answered that insult by insulting the family of the Prophet ﷺ in return. Two transgressions, and nothing but loss between them. That is exactly the trap these principles were built to avoid.

Two kinds of victory

Karbala forces us to rethink what victory even means. There is the worldly kind: the day the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Medina, he found people fasting on Ashura because it was the day Allah saved Musa (peace be upon him) from Pharaoh, and he honoured that fast. A righteous man delivered, his enemy drowned. That is one shape of triumph.

Then there is the victory of Husayn, which looks like defeat and is not. He was killed, and still he won, because his death was martyrdom. The believer who dies seeking truth for the sake of Allah arrives at a better place than the one who killed him. Our dead are bound for Paradise; the wrongdoer carries his crime, unrepented, to meet Allah.

This reframing matters far beyond history. It tells us that a life can end in apparent loss and be, in the only accounting that lasts, a complete success. The world saw a man overwhelmed and killed; Allah saw a martyr received. Those are not the same picture.

Husayn teaches that, and so do the believers in every age who have lost everything except their place with Allah. It is the quiet answer to the fear that doing right will leave us defeated. By the measure that matters, it cannot.

Memory without crossing the line

How, then, should we mark a day like this? The balance the Sunni tradition reaches is between two things: keeping a tragedy alive in our historical memory, and turning a death into an annual act of worship. We hold the first and we leave the second.

Look at how the Prophet ﷺ and his companions lived after Uhud, where his uncle Hamza was killed and his body mutilated, and the Prophet ﷺ wept over him. They did not hold a yearly commemoration of that grief. Yet the memory stayed close. Years later a companion might suddenly weep at his meal, remembering one of the martyrs who had died with so little that there was barely enough cloth to cover his body.

That is the model. We do not build devotional ceremonies around the death of the Prophet ﷺ himself, or around Hamza, or around Husayn. But we keep the memory tender and present, and we let it move us when life brings something near it.

Why it still aches today

Dr. Suleiman draws the connection that so many feel right now. Look at the suffering in Gaza, at the families losing everything, and you find children who carry these very names: Hamza, Hasan, Husayn. Parents reached for those names because of the people we love and honour, and then watched their own children share something of their fate.

Remove even the names, and the echoes remain. The loneliness of Husayn on the battlefield. The sense of betrayal and abandonment. The gruesomeness of the killing. The Prophet ﷺ weeping over an uncle whose body had been torn. It is natural for a believing heart to connect what it sees today to what happened then, and to feel all of it at once.

And then we return to the principles. We let love and hate flow as they naturally do, but we keep them inside the boundaries: guarding our creed while channelling these emotions in a way that lifts us rather than corrupts us. We connect to these blessed people in their pain and in their triumph, in worldly hardship and in worldly victory, and through all of it we aim only at the elevation Allah has promised in the hereafter.

We will never reach their station. But we are meant to face toward it, to let their example pull us upward, and to carry something of their spirit of sacrifice into our own much smaller lives.

What this reflection teaches us

This is less the story of a single life than a lesson in how to feel rightly. Love and hate are powerful currents, and faith is partly about steering them well.

  • Love the people God loves. Loving the family of the Prophet ﷺ, and Husayn among them, is part of faith itself, not an optional extra. We send peace upon them in every prayer; let the heart mean it.
  • Channel grief without crossing the line. We can grieve Karbala deeply and still keep our worship and our words within bounds. Strong emotion does not need to become excess to be sincere.
  • Do not answer a wrong with a wrong. Two people insulting each other at a graveside left nothing but loss. When others transgress, the believer is not freed to transgress back.
  • Measure victory by the hereafter. Husayn was killed and still triumphed, because his death was martyrdom. A life can look like defeat in this world and be a complete success with Allah.

Questions

Why do Muslims love Husayn ibn Ali?

Because he was the grandson of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the son of Ali and Fatima (may Allah be pleased with them), and the Prophet ﷺ loved him dearly and asked the ummah to love his family. That love comes before, and does not depend on, the tragedy of his death.

Do Sunni Muslims curse Yazid?

There is a difference of opinion. Many scholars hated the crime against Husayn but refrained from naming a curse, often as a matter of caution since a person's final state is known only to Allah. Some scholars did permit cursing Yazid. None of them used that restraint to excuse or minimise the killing of Husayn.

How should the day of Ashura be remembered?

The Prophet ﷺ honoured the fast of Ashura, connected to the day Allah saved Musa from Pharaoh. Sunni Muslims keep the memory of Husayn's martyrdom alive in the heart and in history, while not turning it into an annual devotional ceremony, the same way no such ceremony is held for the Prophet ﷺ himself or for Hamza.

What does Karbala teach us about victory?

That victory is not only worldly. Husayn was killed and yet succeeded, because he died seeking truth for the sake of Allah. The believer who loses everything but keeps his place with Allah has, in the only lasting accounting, won.

Why this reflection stays with us

It is tempting, in a heated moment, to prove our love for someone by how loudly we condemn his enemies. This reflection points another way. The scholars loved Husayn without limit, grieved him honestly, and still guarded their tongues and their creed. Their feeling was deep, and it was disciplined.

Most of us will face the smaller version of this every day: a wrong that tempts us to answer with a wrong, a grief that tempts us toward bitterness, a love that tempts us toward excess. Husayn's memory invites us to feel fully and still stay within bounds, to keep our hearts soft toward the people God loves, and to measure every loss by the life that does not end. Carry that into today, and this story has done its work.
