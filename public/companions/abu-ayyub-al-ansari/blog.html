The Companions

Abu Ayyub al-Ansari

The Host of the Prophet

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ finally arrived in Madinah, every household wanted the honour of taking him in. He told the people to leave his camel alone, and let it choose. It walked through the city, paused, turned, and sat down in front of one door.

This is the story of Abu Ayyub al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him): the man whose home received the Prophet ﷺ, who loved him so much he was afraid to walk above his head, and who would one day ask to be buried at the very edge of the Muslim world.

The Companions

Abu Ayyub al-Ansari

The Host of the Prophet

A man known only for his goodness

His name was Khalid ibn Zayd ibn Kulayb. His father was Zayd ibn Kulayb and his mother was Zahra bint Sa'd. From early on he was called Abu Ayyub, the father of Ayyub, a common custom among the Arabs even before he had a son by that name.

He belonged to Banu Najjar, the tribe in Madinah counted as the maternal family of the Prophet ﷺ. The great-grandmother of the Prophet ﷺ came from them, and the very district where Abu Ayyub lived was the same ground where the Prophet's grandfather, Abdul Muttalib, had grown up.

The books say almost nothing about his life before Islam. He held no office and played no part in the politics of the city. He was simply known as a man of some wealth who was generous, who gave in charity, and who was good to his people. He was among the seventy or so from Madinah who travelled to Makkah and gave the second pledge of Aqabah, promising to receive the Prophet ﷺ in their city.

The camel that chose his door

When the Prophet ﷺ left Quba and entered Madinah, the people crowded around his camel, each calling him toward their own quarter. Whoever hosted him would carry great honour, and some thought of the influence it might bring. The Ansar were not thinking that way. They simply wanted to serve him.

He told them to let the camel go. "Leave her," he said, "for she is under command." The camel, Qaswa, made its rounds while the crowd held its breath. It walked into the quarter of Banu Najjar, the land of his relatives, paused, turned back around to be sure, and knelt down in front of the house of Abu Ayyub.

Abu Ayyub and his wife, Umm Ayyub, were overjoyed. "Welcome to the Messenger of Allah," they said, and brought him in. As Dr. Omar Suleiman notes, the Prophet ﷺ did not pick the finest house in the city. Allah chose Abu Ayyub. Out of all the Ansar, with all their love and sacrifice, this was the man whose home was selected to hold the Prophet ﷺ for months.

Afraid to be above him

The house had two floors. The Prophet ﷺ and his wife Sawda could take one, and Abu Ayyub and Umm Ayyub the other. It made sense for the Prophet ﷺ to stay on the ground floor, where he could receive the stream of guests who now came to the new leader of the city. So the host and his wife took the floor above.

It troubled them deeply. "Are we to walk above the head of the Messenger of Allah?" Abu Ayyub asked. That first night they kept to the edges of the room rather than the centre, unable to bear the thought of sleeping over him. They offered to switch. The Prophet ﷺ gently reassured them that the lower floor suited him better and there was no harm in what they were doing, but their hearts would not settle.

One night water spilled upstairs. Terrified it would seep down onto the Prophet ﷺ, the two of them took their only blanket and used it to soak the floor dry. In the morning Abu Ayyub came down distressed and pleaded with him again to move up. This time the Prophet ﷺ agreed, and they carried his belongings to the upper floor. They were so few, Abu Ayyub recalled, that moving them took almost nothing at all.

Following him in the smallest things

Abu Ayyub and Umm Ayyub sent food up to the Prophet ﷺ each day. When the dish came back, Abu Ayyub would look to see which part he had eaten from, so he could eat from exactly the same place. It was a small thing, and it tells you everything about the love in that house.

One day they sent up fresh garlic, and it came back untouched. Alarmed, Abu Ayyub asked if it was forbidden. "No," the Prophet ﷺ said, "but I dislike to eat it raw," because of the odour it left when he went to meet people and to pray. At once Abu Ayyub said, "Then I dislike what you dislike." The Prophet ﷺ smiled and told him this was only for himself: "There come to me those who do not come to you," meaning the angels. Still, Abu Ayyub wanted to follow him in everything.

In that home they also saw his miracles. A meal prepared for four fed thirty guests at the Prophet's word, then sixty, then seventy more, until a hundred and sixty people had eaten from it. Day after day they watched his manners and his patience, and they grew to love him more.

The night three men met in the dark

After the masjid was built on their land, given as an endowment, the Prophet ﷺ became Abu Ayyub's neighbour, and the house still prepared food for him each day. The Prophet ﷺ never lived like a king. At the height of his standing he ate little, and there were nights he had nothing at all.

On one such night he went out and met Abu Bakr near the masjid, and then Umar. He asked what had brought them out at that hour. "Hunger," they said. The Prophet ﷺ answered that the same thing had brought him out, and the three of them walked together to the house of Abu Ayyub.

Abu Ayyub was working among his palm trees and came running. "No one today," he said, "is more honoured by his guests than I am." He gathered his best dates, fresh water, baked bread, and slaughtered a young goat. When the food was set before him, the Prophet ﷺ prepared a portion with his own hands and sent it to his daughter Fatima, who had not seen such food in days. Then he wept, and told Abu Bakr and Umar: "By Allah, you will be asked about this blessing on the Day of Judgement." He taught them to begin with the name of Allah and to finish with praise of the One who fed them and provided for them.

Standing up for what was right

As the host of the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Ayyub came to be seen as a caretaker of the masjid. When he heard the hypocrites mocking the Prophet ﷺ inside it one day, he could not hold himself back. He lifted the man who was speaking and put him out. He was the first to remove a hypocrite from the masjid.

His finest stand was with his tongue. When the slander against our mother Aisha (RA) spread through the city, Umm Ayyub repeated to him what everyone was saying. "Yes," he said, "and it is a lie. Would you do such a thing, Umm Ayyub?" "No, by Allah," she said. "Then Aisha is far better than you," he answered, "and this is a clear slander." His response was so true that Allah preserved its meaning in the Qur'an:

When you heard the lie, why did believing men and women not think well of their own people and declare, ‘This is obviously a lie’?Quran 24:12

A life by his example

Abu Ayyub did not stay behind. He was at Badr, the highest rank a companion could hold, and at Uhud, the Trench, and the pledge under the tree. Once the Prophet ﷺ found him standing guard outside his house with a sword. "O Allah," the Prophet ﷺ prayed, "protect him as he is protecting me."

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, he kept going out in the path of Allah under Abu Bakr, Umar, and Uthman, and Ali placed him in charge of Madinah. When civil strife broke out, he would not raise his sword against fellow Muslims at Siffin or the Camel, though he joined Ali against the Khawarij. Wherever he settled, in Madinah, Syria, Egypt, or Iraq, he became the man who loved the masjid most, present for every prayer, gently insisting that things be done the way he had seen the Prophet ﷺ do them.

His love was remembered by others, too. When he came to Basra, Ibn Abbas (RA) went out to receive him and said, "Let me repay you for how you hosted the Prophet ﷺ." He gave him his own house and everything in it, and cleared a debt of twenty thousand by handing him forty thousand. The younger companions had not forgotten what his home once held.

Buried at the edge of the world

In his old age, well into his eighties, Abu Ayyub joined an army that reached the walls of Constantinople, the greatest city of its day. He knew the Prophet ﷺ had spoken of an army that would one day take it, and he wanted to be as near to it as he could.

He fell ill there. As death approached he made one request: "Carry me to the furthest point you can reach against the enemy, and bury me there, under your feet." The man who had hosted the Prophet ﷺ in his home in Madinah now lay at the farthest reach the Muslims had come.

He had one last hadith to give, one he had held back until his final hours. "I heard the Messenger of Allah ﷺ say: whoever dies without associating any partner with Allah will enter Paradise." He had kept it back so people would not grow complacent, but now, dying, he would hold nothing back. He passed away soon after, and was buried at the gates of the city. Centuries later that ground would carry a masjid and a whole district named after him, in what is now Istanbul.

What Abu Ayyub's life teaches us

His was not a story of one great moment. It was a lifetime of reverence carried into the smallest acts, and that is why it still moves us.

  • Honour is given, not grasped. He did not compete for the Prophet ﷺ or angle for advantage. He simply opened his door, and was chosen for the thing everyone wanted.
  • Love shows in the details. He ate from the same spot on the plate, gave up garlic, and dried a floor in the night. Real devotion lives in the small, unseen acts.
  • Speak the truth when it is easiest to stay silent. When a rumour was passing from mouth to mouth, he refused to carry it. One honest sentence outlasted them all.
  • Never settle into comfort while there is work to do. He could have guarded the house and stayed home. Instead he followed the call his whole life, all the way to a distant frontier.

Questions

Who was Abu Ayyub al-Ansari?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah, of the tribe of Banu Najjar. His real name was Khalid ibn Zayd, and he is best known as the host who received the Prophet ﷺ into his home when he first came to the city.

Why is he called the host of the Prophet?

When the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Madinah, he let his camel choose where he would stay. It knelt in front of Abu Ayyub's house, and he hosted the Prophet ﷺ in his two-story home for about seven months while the masjid was being built.

How does the Qur'an connect to his story?

When the slander against our mother Aisha (RA) spread, Abu Ayyub answered that it was plainly a lie and that she was better than any of them. The meaning of his response was preserved in Surah An-Nur, verse 12.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Ayyub?

That honour comes from Allah rather than from grasping for it, that love is proven in small and quiet acts, that we should speak the truth even when it is easier to stay silent, and that we should never grow too comfortable to keep serving.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never host a prophet, lead a city, or stand at the walls of a great fortress. But the heart of Abu Ayyub's life is something we can reach for today: a reverence so deep that it shaped how he slept, what he ate, and how he spoke about people who were not in the room.

His example asks a gentle question of an ordinary day. Do we carry the people and the truths we love into the small choices, the ones no one sees? To follow what is good in the little things, to refuse the rumour, to keep serving long after we could have rested: that is a life worth imitating, and it begins quietly, at home, in the heart.
