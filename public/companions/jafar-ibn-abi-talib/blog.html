The Companions

Jafar ibn Abi Talib

The One With Two Wings

He looked so much like the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ that his own mother loved him a little extra for it. When he finally returned home after twelve years away, the Prophet ﷺ kissed him between the eyes and said he did not know which had given him more joy: this homecoming, or a victory won the same week.

This is the story of Jafar ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him): the brother of Ali, the man who explained Islam to a king and made him weep, and the companion the Prophet ﷺ called the closest to him in face and in character.

The Companions

Jafar ibn Abi Talib

The One With Two Wings

A name to live up to

Jafar was a son of Abu Talib and Fatima bint Asad, and a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. His mother was the first woman of Banu Hashim to accept Islam, the one the Prophet ﷺ called his mother, the one who had helped raise him as a boy. She loved Jafar in a particular way, because of all her sons he resembled the Prophet ﷺ the most.

Abu Talib named his four sons with care, each name something to grow into. Talib meant high aspiration. Aqil meant a sharp mind. Jafar carried the sense of flowing speech and a giving heart, like a running river. Ali meant nobility. Each of them, in time, would live close to the meaning of his name.

When Abu Talib fell into hardship, his brother al-Abbas and the Prophet ﷺ each offered to take in one of his children to ease the burden on him. The Prophet ﷺ took Ali into his home. Al-Abbas took Jafar into his. So Jafar grew up cared for, and close to the Prophet ﷺ in the way of a younger brother. It taught him early that family is wider than the people under one roof.

Early to believe, early to suffer

Some reports place Jafar among the first fifteen or sixteen people to accept Islam. We do not have the exact day, but we know it was early, and we know there was no hesitation in it.

His own clan, Banu Hashim, was divided over the message. Some, like Abu Lahab, turned against it. Others, like Ali and like Jafar's own mother, accepted it without delay. Jafar stood with the second group, beside his cousin, from the very start.

He accepted Islam alongside his wife, Asma bint Umays, one of the believing sisters who gave themselves fully to this new faith. The two of them entered Islam together and would carry its weight together for the rest of his life.

Persecution did not spare the family of the Prophet ﷺ. It pressed on Jafar and Asma until it became unbearable, and they asked permission to leave for the land of Abyssinia, modern day Ethiopia, where a just Christian king ruled. They were among those who would migrate for the sake of Allah.

The young man chosen to speak for Islam

The Prophet ﷺ told the believers that the ruler of Abyssinia, the Najashi, would not tolerate injustice, and that they would find safety in his land. From the second group of migrants, which included some of the noblest of the companions, the Prophet ﷺ chose Jafar to be their leader and their spokesman. He was only in his early twenties.

This was no small trust. The Najashi could have refused them, or sent them back to Makkah, or kept them while rejecting their faith. And Quraysh did not let them go quietly. They sent two skilled men, Amr ibn al-As among them, with gifts and arguments to turn the king against the Muslims and have them handed back.

So the Najashi summoned the believers to his court, and Jafar stepped forward to speak. Standing before a king, with the future of the whole group resting on his words, he found exactly the right ones.

The speech, and the king who wept

Jafar described who they had been and who they had become. "O King, we were a people in ignorance, worshipping idols, eating dead meat, cutting the ties of kinship, and the strong among us devouring the weak. Then Allah sent us a prophet from among ourselves, whose lineage and truthfulness and trust we knew." He told the king the Prophet ﷺ had called them to worship Allah alone, to speak the truth, to keep their promises, to be kind to relatives and good to neighbours, to protect the orphan, and to guard the honour of others.

The Najashi asked if he had anything of the revelation. Jafar recited the opening of Surah Maryam: the story of Mary and the birth of Jesus, peace be upon them. When he finished, he looked up and saw the king in tears, and the bishops around him weeping too.

The Najashi took up a stick and said that what Jafar had recited and what Jesus had brought came from the same source. He refused to hand the believers over. He returned the gifts of Quraysh and sent their envoys home, and granted the Muslims refuge in his land.

Twelve years away, and a homecoming

Jafar and Asma stayed in Abyssinia for somewhere near a decade, twelve years in all. Their three sons, Abdullah, Muhammad, and Awn, were all born in that foreign land. They lived as a small community of strangers, far from the Prophet ﷺ, far from the events filling the early years of Islam.

While they were away, the great battles of Badr, Uhud, and the Trench all passed without them. They missed the migration to Madinah and a long stretch of the Prophet's life. They held onto their faith in a place where almost no one shared it.

Jafar finally reached Madinah around the time the Prophet ﷺ returned from Khaybar. The Prophet ﷺ embraced him, kissed him between the eyes, and said he did not know which joy was greater: the coming of Jafar, or the victory of Khaybar. After twelve years, the brother he loved was home.

Asma had carried just as much. When Umar once gently reminded her that those who stayed had reached the Prophet ﷺ first, she did not let the years in exile be counted as less. The Prophet ﷺ settled it in her favour. The others, he said, had made one migration. She and her family had made two, and the reward of both was theirs.

The father of the poor

In Madinah, the companions gave Jafar a name of their own: Abu al-Masakin, the father of the poor. Abu Hurayra, who lived among the poor of the mosque, said no one after the Prophet ﷺ was better than Jafar ibn Abi Talib.

He explained why. Jafar loved the poor. He would sit with them and talk with them and listen to them, and he gave away whatever he had. One day they came to him and he had nothing in the house but a jar of honey, so he broke it open and they shared what was inside. He had eloquence, closeness to the Prophet ﷺ, and a position of leadership, and he carried none of it with pride.

The Prophet ﷺ once settled a dispute over who would raise the orphaned daughter of Hamza, and he told Jafar that he had the greatest right to her. Then he said the words he repeated more than once: "You resemble me in appearance and in character." In a community that held Abu Bakr and Uthman, the quality Jafar became known for was not the largest gift, but the love of the poor.

Mu'ta, and the two wings

Soon after, Jafar marched out to the battle of Mu'ta, in the land that is Jordan today. The Prophet ﷺ named three commanders in turn: Zayd ibn Haritha first, then Jafar if Zayd fell, then Abdullah ibn Rawaha if Jafar fell. That the Prophet ﷺ named the next commander, and the next, was itself unusual, as if he sensed this battle would be unlike the others.

The small Muslim force met a Byzantine army many times its size, deep in enemy territory. Zayd was killed early, and Jafar took the banner. He had only just come home, and now he fought his way into the enemy and would not give up the standard. Both of his arms were cut from him, and he fell with more than seventy wounds. He died a martyr that day.

In Madinah the Prophet ﷺ waited, uneasy, for the news. When it came, grief crossed his face. He had just gotten this brother back, and now Jafar and Zayd were both gone. He gathered Jafar's children close, pressed his face into their hair, and wept. Then he gave an instruction the family of every grieving household has leaned on since: "Make food for the family of Jafar, for something has come to them that keeps them busy."

Later the Prophet ﷺ told them what he had seen. He saw Jafar in Paradise, flying with the angels. From then on the companions knew him by a new name: Dhul-Janahayn, the one with two wings.

What Jafar's life teaches us

Jafar is remembered for one famous speech, but the speech was only the surface. Underneath it was a man who carried himself the same way whether he stood before a king or sat beside the poorest in the city.

  • The right words can open a closed door. Jafar did not argue or flatter. He spoke the plain truth about who they were and what they believed, and a king and his court were moved to tears.
  • Greatness keeps company with the overlooked. He could have leaned on his eloquence and his closeness to the Prophet ﷺ. Instead he sat with the poor, listened to them, and shared whatever he had down to the last jar of honey.
  • Character is what truly resembles the Prophet ﷺ. Jafar looked like the Prophet ﷺ, but he was told he resembled him in character too. The face was a gift; the character was a choice he made every day.
  • Faith holds even far from home. He kept his religion alive for twelve years as a stranger in a distant land, with almost no one beside him but his wife and his children.

Questions

Who was Jafar ibn Abi Talib?

He was a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the older brother of Ali, and an early Muslim. He led the second migration to Abyssinia and spoke for the believers before its king.

Why is Jafar called the one with two wings?

After he was martyred at the battle of Mu'ta, the Prophet ﷺ said he had seen Jafar in Paradise flying with the angels. The companions then knew him as Dhul-Janahayn, the one with two wings.

What did Jafar say to the Najashi?

He described how the believers had lived in ignorance before Islam and what the Prophet ﷺ had taught them, then recited the opening of Surah Maryam about Mary and the birth of Jesus. The king and his court wept, and he refused to hand the Muslims over to Quraysh.

What can we learn from the life of Jafar?

The power of honest speech, the dignity of loving and sitting with the poor, and a character that stayed humble through hardship, leadership, and praise.

Why this story stays with us

It would be easy to remember Jafar only for the speech before the king, the dramatic scene that ends up in the films. But the companions who knew him remembered something quieter: a man who sat on the floor with the poorest people in the city, listened to them, and gave away whatever he had. The same person who could move a court to tears was the one breaking open his last jar of honey to share it.

That is the part worth carrying into an ordinary day. Most of us will never speak before a king, but all of us decide, again and again, how we treat the people the world overlooks. Jafar was told he resembled the Prophet ﷺ in character, and character is built in small, unwatched moments. Choose one of those today: a little more patience, a little more generosity, a little more time for someone easy to ignore.
