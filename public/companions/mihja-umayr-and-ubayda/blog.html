The Martyrs of Badr

The Firsts to Fall at Badr

The best of the companions, it was said, were the companions of Badr. It was the day the truth was made clear, the day of great reward with Allah. To be counted among its martyrs was an honour beyond reckoning, and to be the very first of them is almost beyond imagining.

This is the story of three of them (may Allah be pleased with them): Mihja, the first man to fall, Ubayda ibn al-Harith, the first to be wounded, and young Umayr, who wept until he was allowed to stay. None of the three were famous in their time, and that is part of what makes them unforgettable.

The Firsts to Fall at Badr

The day everything was made clear

To understand these three men, you first have to understand the day they died on. Badr was not just a battle. It was the day the criterion was sent down, the day the line between truth and falsehood was drawn in the open for all to see.

It was said that the best of the companions were the companions of Badr, and the best of the angels were the angels of Badr. It was a day of immense reward with Allah. So imagine, then, the station of those who gave their lives on it. And imagine the very first of them to fall.

These three were among the muhajirin, the emigrants from Makkah. They were early Muslims who had already made the sacrifice of the hijrah, leaving home and security behind for the sake of their faith. Now they would make a second sacrifice, and be counted among the first of the first: the earliest believers who were also the earliest to fall at Badr.

We often remember the great battles for their famous heroes. But Badr also belonged to men like these, whose names barely survived. Their stories are short, sometimes only a sentence. And yet there is a weight in those few words that a long biography could not carry.

The first to fall

His name was Mihja, and the books tell us almost nothing about him. You would expect the first martyr of the greatest day to fill pages. Instead you can comb through the records of the early Muslims and find a line or two: that he was a slave in Makkah, that he embraced Islam, that he was persecuted for it.

Many of the persecuted slaves of Makkah were freed by Abu Bakr, who spent his wealth buying their freedom. Mihja was not one of them. The one who freed him was Umar ibn al-Khattab. From that day Mihja stayed loyal to Umar for the rest of his life, and the little we know of him is the little that matters most.

He was a worshipper. He would stand in the night in prayer alongside Umar, competing with the righteous in their devotion to Allah. This was a man whose nights were full even if his record is thin.

And there is one more honour preserved for him. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ once named the best of all black men and women as three, and Mihja was counted among them, in the company of Luqman and Bilal. A man the world almost forgot was named by the Prophet ﷺ himself.

Returning to his Lord

On the day of Badr, as the two armies faced one another, Mihja was heard repeating a single phrase. Over and over he said it, like a man already on his way home. To his Lord he was returning. To his Lord he was returning.

And then an arrow found him, and he was gone. The first martyr of the first battle. The very first soul of this ummah to be given back to its Lord on that field. What a phrase to be carrying on your lips at such a moment, and what a way to return.

We know his death far better than we know his life, and perhaps that is exactly the point. A man the world barely recorded was, in the sight of Allah, the first of the firsts on the day that mattered most. His name is small in the books of men and written large in a place we cannot see.

There is something disarming about it. We spend so much of our lives wanting to be seen, wanting our efforts noticed and our names kept. Mihja had almost none of that, and lost none of what was real. The phrase on his lips was not about being remembered. It was about going home.

The cousin who came late and gave everything

The second was Ubayda ibn al-Harith, a cousin of the Prophet ﷺ and about ten years older than him. By the time the revelation came, Ubayda was already fifty years old, with most of his life behind him. He did not weigh it or wait. He embraced the Prophet ﷺ at once and dedicated himself to him.

Because of who he was, his lineage spared him the worst of the persecution that broke the bodies of slaves like Mihja. But it did not spare him hardship. He is counted among those who suffered through the years of the boycott, when the Prophet's clan was cut off and starved in the valley, and he stayed in Makkah through all of it.

When the command came, he made the hijrah with the Prophet ﷺ. In Madinah he was trusted with responsibility early. They say he was the second to carry the banner in Islam, after Hamza, placed over sixty of the emigrants. A man who came to faith at fifty was, within a few short years, leading others into the open.

It would have been easy for someone his age to think his role was simply to believe and to watch the younger men do the work. Ubayda thought no such thing. He carried the flag, he carried the burden, and when the moment came he carried himself to the very front.

The grave that smelled of musk

At Badr, the battle opened with single combat. Three Muslims stepped forward together: Hamza, Ali, and Ubayda. Three of the enemy came out to meet them. The duels began, and of the three Muslims, Ubayda was the only one wounded in that opening exchange.

Hamza and Ali fought their opponents and overcame them. Ubayda was struck and badly hurt. He lived through the rest of the day, but the wound was too deep. As the Muslims carried him back from Badr, he died on the road, at a place called al-Safra. He was an old man by then, sixty-three or sixty-four.

He is not buried with the other martyrs of Badr. His grave lies alone, not far from the battlefield, set apart on the way home. It is just his grave, by itself, in that quiet place.

Years later, people passing al-Safra caught the scent of musk on the air, and they asked the Prophet ﷺ about it. How could it be otherwise, he answered, when Ubayda is buried there. The man who came to faith last among his cousins was honoured in a way none of them could see.

The boy who would not be sent away

The third was Umayr, the younger brother of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas. He had embraced Islam very young, and when the day of Badr came he wanted nothing in the world so much as to be among those who fought.

The Prophet ﷺ looked at him and sent him away. He was too young, still a teenager, and there was no place for a child on that field. For most boys that would have been a relief. For Umayr it was unbearable.

So he wept. He begged to be allowed to stay. He did not want to miss this, of all things, and he showed such deep emotion that the Prophet ﷺ, seeing it, let him remain.

Umayr fought at Badr, and at Badr he was martyred, still a boy. He had cried his way not into safety but into the company of the martyrs.

What they were reaching for

Picture them together on that one day. An old man of sixty, stepping forward to trade blows in the opening duel. A freed slave with a thin biography and a full heart, whispering his return to his Lord. A boy in tears, afraid of nothing on that field except being left off it.

Umayr did not weep for glory, or for a name that would be remembered, or for anything he might gain. He wept because he did not want to miss Paradise. The whole occasion meant more to him than anything he could possibly achieve in a long life lived elsewhere.

That is the thread that ties the three of them together. Each reached, in his own way, for the same thing. Not for victory. Not for status. For nearness to their Lord, on a day when that nearness was held out, and they refused to let it pass them by.

They were not the famous names of their people. When Makkah counted its great men, none of these three were on the list. But on the day Allah gathered the first of His martyrs, all three of them were there.

What these three lives teach us

Three very different men, on one morning, leaving us three quiet lessons that outlast the battle itself.

  • Worth is measured by Allah, not by records. Mihja left barely a line in the books of men, yet he was the first martyr of Badr and was named by the Prophet ﷺ among the best of people. The eyes that truly matter were never the eyes of the world, and a quiet life can be a full one.
  • It is never too late to begin. Ubayda was already fifty when he believed and sixty-three when he died for it. He did not feel that his best years were behind him. He gave the whole of his late life to the message and asked for nothing in return.
  • Long for the right things. Umayr did not weep to be left behind out of pride. His fear was missing Paradise, not missing fame. He measured the day by what would last forever, not by what would pass, and his longing was answered.
  • The hidden life can hold the highest honour. None of these three were famous, and none of them were trying to be. All three were carried into the company of the martyrs of Badr. What we do when no one is watching is often the truest thing about us.

Questions

Who was the first martyr in the Battle of Badr?

Mihja (may Allah be pleased with him), a freed slave who had been persecuted in Makkah. He was struck by an arrow as the armies met and became the first Muslim to be martyred on the day of Badr.

Who was Ubayda ibn al-Harith?

A cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, about ten years older than him, who accepted Islam at around fifty. He was the first Muslim wounded at Badr, in the opening single combat, and died of his wound on the journey home, at a place called al-Safra.

Why was young Umayr almost not allowed to fight at Badr?

Umayr, the younger brother of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, was still a teenager, and the Prophet ﷺ first sent him away for being too young. When Umayr wept and begged to stay, he was allowed to fight, and he was martyred there.

What can we learn from the martyrs of Badr?

That worth is measured by Allah and not by reputation, that it is never too late to give yourself to what is good, and that the truest longing is for nearness to Allah rather than for status or fame.

Why this story stays with us

We tend to imagine that the people who matter most are the ones the world writes about. These three lives say otherwise. A man almost unrecorded, an old convert, and a weeping boy were each carried into one of the most honoured gatherings in all of history, and the world barely noticed any of them at the time.

Most of our own lives are lived quietly too, in devotion no one sees and choices no one applauds. Their story asks a gentle question of an ordinary day: are we reaching for what truly lasts, or only for what gets remembered? Let your prayer tonight reach, like Umayr's tears, for the thing that cannot be taken away.
