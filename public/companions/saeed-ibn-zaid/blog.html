Saeed ibn Zaid

An Answer to His Father's Prayer

A man once spent his whole life searching for the one true God, rejecting the idols of his people and the cruelty done in their name. He died on the road home, before the message he longed for had reached Makkah. On that road, he made one last prayer: that his son might be given what he himself had been denied.

This is the story of Saeed ibn Zaid (may Allah be pleased with him): the answer to that prayer, one of the ten promised Paradise, and a man who spent his life trying to be no one at all.

Saeed ibn Zaid

An Answer to His Father's Prayer

A father's last prayer

Saeed's father was Zaid ibn Amr ibn Nufayl, a man who had searched the world for the religion of Ibrahim. He refused to worship idols, refused the animals sacrificed to them, and stood against the cruel practices done in their name. When baby girls were carried out to be buried alive on the outskirts of Makkah, he would take them and raise them himself, because he knew it was the right thing to do.

Zaid died on his way back to Makkah, before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ received revelation. On that final journey he turned to Allah with a prayer for his son. If You have chosen to forbid me the companionship of this Prophet, he said, then do not forbid my son Saeed from having it.

Allah answered that prayer. The father who had given his whole life to monotheism would not see the message arrive. The son would not only see it, he would be among the very first to accept it.

The boy raised without idols

Saeed grew up in one of the very few homes in Makkah that had no idol in it at all. He watched his father stand for truth and justice when the whole city stood against him. A child raised in that house grew up close to monotheism in a way almost no one else of his generation did.

He was tall and dark, with a full head of hair. But the quality that would define him, in the days of ignorance and then in Islam, was his humility. He was so gentle and unassuming that he made no enemies. People held him in high regard precisely because he never sought to be held in regard at all.

So when he first heard of a Prophet calling to the religion of Ibrahim, he did not hesitate. According to the narrations, it was Abu Bakr who first told him, and Saeed accepted Islam at once. His whole upbringing had prepared him for that single moment.

Two fathers, one faith

Saeed married Fatima bint al-Khattab, the sister of Umar ibn al-Khattab. There is something striking in this. Umar's father, al-Khattab, was the man who used to beat Saeed's father, Zaid, and make it impossible for him to stand near the Kaaba and call to the one God.

So one father had fought for monotheism in Makkah before Islam, and the other had fought to crush it. And yet their children, Saeed and Fatima, would both be among the earliest to believe. They accepted Islam together and kept it hidden, for they feared the anger of Umar, who had sworn to deal harshly with anyone in his tribe who followed the new religion.

It was their quiet faith that would, in time, reach Umar himself.

The house where Umar's heart turned

Khabbab ibn al-Aratt, one of the persecuted and weakest of the believers in Makkah, used to come to the home of Saeed and Fatima to teach them the Quran. Picture that room: a small gathering, learning to read the words of revelation in secret.

Umar was on his way to harm the Prophet ﷺ when he was told to look first to his own family, for they had already accepted Islam. He arrived at the door in a rage. Khabbab hid. Umar struck Saeed, just as al-Khattab had once struck Saeed's father for the very same faith.

In the struggle Umar hurt his sister Fatima. When he saw the mark he had left on her, he stopped. Something in him broke open. He asked to read what they had been reading, and the verses softened a heart no force in Makkah had been able to touch. The man who came to that house to destroy the believers left it as one of them.

The soldier who wished to be unknown

We do not have many stories of Saeed, and the reason is part of who he was. He lived quietly, devoted to the worship of Allah, and he wanted nothing more than to be an unknown soldier in the path of his Lord. He once said that nothing was more beloved to him than to go out and strive, his face covered in dust, fighting while the people did not even know his name.

He was honoured with writing down the revelation, a trust the Prophet ﷺ gave to few. He missed the battle of Badr only because he and Talha had been sent to scout the caravan, and the army had already left by the time they returned. It grieved him deeply. The Prophet ﷺ assigned them both a share of the spoils, not for the wealth, but to count them among the people of Badr.

Later he fought with great courage at Yarmuk and at Damascus, always in the front lines. Yet so determined was he to stay in the background that others would look at him in battle and see only another brave, anonymous fighter. He was a worshipper, an ascetic, and a warrior, all at once, and he wanted the credit for none of it.

He disliked authority just as much. When Umar appointed him governor of Damascus, he found himself living in the palace of Caesar, surrounded by glory and comfort, and he could not settle in it. He would stand on the balconies, look out over all that grandeur, and feel that this was not what he had signed up for. So he wrote to Umar asking to be relieved and to send someone better suited, so that he could spend the rest of his life as an ordinary soldier seeking only the pleasure of Allah.

The cry of the wronged

Long after the Prophet ﷺ had passed, a neighbour went to the governor and claimed that Saeed had taken part of her land. Humble people are often taken advantage of, and Saeed was not a man who raised his voice in his private life. What wounded him was not the land but the accusation, that he, of all people, would steal what was not his.

He said he had heard the Prophet ﷺ himself warn that whoever wrongfully takes a span of land will have seven earths hung around his neck on the Day of Judgment. Knowing that, how could he ever take what belonged to another? So he said: let her have it. Then he turned to Allah and prayed that, if she had lied, He would take her sight and make that very land her grave.

The Prophet ﷺ taught us to fear the prayer of the wronged, for there is no veil between it and Allah. The woman lost her sight, and one day, walking on the land she had falsely claimed, she fell into a well on it and died. Saeed would not fight for this world, but he knew he could place his cause in the hands of the One who never turns the wronged away.

A quiet ending

Years before, when his father, Zaid, had passed away without Islam, it was Umar and Saeed together who came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked if they could seek forgiveness for him. The Prophet ﷺ told them yes, and that Zaid would be raised on the Day of Judgment as a nation all by himself. Saeed's whole life was, in a sense, the fruit of his father's seeking, and the answer to a father's prayer.

When the trials and civil strife began among the Muslims, Saeed wanted no part of them. He had not embraced Islam to fight his own brothers and sisters. So he moved out of Madinah into a valley and lived out his days far from the conflict, in worship and stillness.

He lived a long life, into his seventies, around fifty years after the Hijra. He outlived nearly all of the ten promised Paradise. Out of his humility, when he narrated the hadith naming those ten, he would list only nine, leaving out his own name. We know he was the tenth only because other narrations name him.

One morning he prayed Fajr, went home, lay down, and passed away with the words of the shahada on his lips. Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas and Abdullah ibn Umar washed his body and carried it back to Madinah, where he was buried in al-Baqi. When they came to apply musk, as is the practice, they found a beautiful scent already rising from him.

What Saeed's life teaches us

His was not a life of famous moments. It was a life that deliberately stayed out of view, and that is exactly what makes it worth looking at.

  • Humility is a strength, not a weakness. Saeed made no enemies and sought no rank, yet he was honoured with writing revelation and counted among the ten promised Paradise. He let his deeds be known to Allah and content to be unknown to people.
  • A parent's prayer reaches further than they can see. Zaid died on the road without ever meeting the Prophet ﷺ. His prayer for his son outlived him and shaped a life he never witnessed.
  • Courage and gentleness can live in the same heart. He was fearless in the front lines and tender and unassuming at home. Strength did not make him loud, and humility did not make him weak.
  • Leave your wrongs with the One who hears the wronged. Saeed gave up his land rather than fight over it, and entrusted his cause to Allah. There is no veil between the cry of the wronged and their Lord.

Questions

Who was Saeed ibn Zaid?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and one of the ten promised Paradise. He was the son of Zaid ibn Amr, a seeker of monotheism, and the husband of Fatima bint al-Khattab, the sister of Umar.

Why is he called an answer to his father's prayer?

His father, Zaid ibn Amr, died before Islam reached Makkah. On his final journey he prayed that his son Saeed might be given the companionship of the Prophet ﷺ that he himself was denied. Saeed became one of the earliest Muslims, and that prayer was answered.

What role did his home play in Umar's conversion?

Saeed and his wife Fatima were learning the Quran in their home when Umar arrived in anger, intending harm. The verses he heard there softened his heart, and he accepted Islam after that encounter.

What can we learn from the life of Saeed ibn Zaid?

The quiet strength of humility, the courage to stand firm without seeking attention, and the trust that places one's wrongs in the hands of Allah rather than fighting over this world.

Why this story stays with us

Saeed had every reason to be known. He was among the first to believe, a scribe of revelation, one of the ten given glad tidings of Paradise. And he spent his life trying to disappear into the background, content that Allah knew what the people did not. In an age that prizes being seen, his life asks a quieter question: would you still do the good if no one was watching, and no one ever found out?

Most of us will never lead an army or write a verse. But we can choose to be gentle when we could be harsh, to seek no credit when we could claim it, and to hand our hurts to Allah instead of nursing them. Carry one of those into your day, and his story has done its work.
