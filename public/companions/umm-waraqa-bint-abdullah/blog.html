The Companions

Umm Waraqa bint Abdullah

The Martyred Hafidha

There is a question that comes up again and again, often from women: among the great reciters who preserved the Quran in those first years, were there any women? Had any woman gathered the whole book in her heart? There was a name to give. She lived quietly in Madinah, taught the women their verses, and prayed so long into the night that her neighbours knew her voice.

This is the story of Umm Waraqa bint Abdullah (may Allah be pleased with her): one of the first women to memorise the Quran, a teacher of its recitation, and the companion the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ called the martyr while she was still alive. Her life is not all gentle. Like several of the early believers, her story carries a hard, unexpected turn. But it begins in something very calm: a woman, her book, and her time.

A rare thing: a woman who could read

Reading and writing were uncommon among the Arabs of that time. If literacy was low across the society as a whole, it was lower still among women. The few who could write often became scribes for the Prophet ﷺ, and the number of women among them was smaller again.

Umm Waraqa was one of those few. She was one of the literate women of Madinah, one of the literate people of any kind, in a place where most could not read at all. That single skill opened a door that stayed closed to almost everyone around her.

She was also a woman of noble standing who had inherited a large amount of wealth. She did not have to worry about her daily needs or her livelihood. And she had stayed outside the politics and the wars that had divided Madinah before Islam, taking no part in the feuds of the city.

So when the message came, she was free in a way few people ever are: free of need, free of distraction, free of the quarrels that consumed others. Her time was entirely her own, and what she did with it is the whole of her story.

She gave that time to the Quran

When the message of Islam reached Madinah, Umm Waraqa embraced it early, before the Prophet ﷺ himself arrived in the city. And then she did something with her freedom that most people never manage: she gave it away on purpose.

She dedicated herself, almost completely, to the Quran. While others were occupied with survival, or with the rivalries of the city, she sat with the revelation. She memorised it, and she learned to recite it with care, until she was counted among the most proficient.

Her circumstances made it possible, but they did not make it happen. Many people in Madinah had ease of one kind or another. Most did not turn it toward the book of Allah. The literacy she happened to have, the wealth she happened to inherit, the calm she happened to be left in: she gathered all of it and pointed it at one thing.

This is what set her apart. She could have spent her years in comfort and been forgotten, as the comfortable usually are. Instead she spent them on the one thing that would outlive her, and her share among the mothers of the believers and the women of knowledge was earned in those long, quiet hours.

By the time the Prophet ﷺ settled in Madinah, she had become one of the women known for genuine skill in recitation, named among the female scholars of her day. She had not inherited that place. She had built it, verse by verse, with the time she chose not to waste.

The teacher the city relied on

Umm Waraqa became a teacher of the Quran to the women of Madinah, one of its leading authorities among the women in the recitation of the book. Students came to her home and recited to her so that she could correct them, the way reciters have always been corrected by those who hold the text most carefully.

The Prophet ﷺ once told her to lead the people of her household in prayer. Some today reach for that report to claim more than it can carry. But it is a slight and ambiguous narration, and it cannot overturn what was settled and agreed. Her home held others who depended on her: it could have been children, it could have been servants, in a large and wealthy house. The narration could mean many things.

What the record makes plain is simpler and steadier. She was a distinguished reciter, she taught the women of the city, and she recited among the most proficient of the reciters of her time. That is the place she actually held.

She became known for more than her teaching. She fasted often, and she prayed long into the night. People who walked past her home in the dark could hear her reciting the Quran. Her worship was not announced to anyone. It was simply overheard, and that detail would matter at the very end of her life.

The request at Badr

When the Battle of Badr drew near, Umm Waraqa came to the Prophet ﷺ with a request of her own. O Messenger of Allah, she said, let me go out and be part of the battle with you. Even if only to tend the wounded, she added, she wanted to be there on the field.

Then she named the thing she truly wanted. She asked Allah to grant her shahada, to die a martyr. This was the spirit of the Ansar at its purest: the longing to stand beside the Prophet ﷺ in his hardest moments, and to give the most a person can give.

It echoed the man who told the Prophet ﷺ, as the fighting began, that he wished to be struck by an arrow in a certain place and to fall there. Be truthful with Allah, the Prophet ﷺ answered him, and Allah will be truthful with you. Umm Waraqa was asking with that same honest heart.

And there is something striking in the request itself. She was a woman of standing and wealth, with every reason to stay home and be comfortable. She did not ask for safety. She asked to be spent. The thing she wanted from Allah was not ease but to give her life in His path, and she said so plainly, to His Messenger's face.

He did not send her to the battlefield. Stay in your home, he told her, and trust that Allah will grant you the martyrdom you have asked for. She would be a shahidah, he promised, though not in the place she expected.

The one they called the martyr

From that day, Umm Waraqa carried a quiet, astonishing name. Among the Prophet ﷺ and his companions, she was the shahidah, the martyr. The Prophet ﷺ used to say, let us go and visit the martyr, and people would go to see her.

Imagine walking among the companions, year after year, knowing the Prophet ﷺ had already told you how your life would end. It could have made a person fearful, or proud. It did neither to her. She kept teaching, kept fasting, kept praying through the nights, and grew old in the city she had served.

She lived on into the time of Umar (may Allah be pleased with him), an elderly woman by then, perhaps in her sixties or seventies. That she was already old in his day suggests she had been old, or close to it, when Islam first reached her: which means she had not given the Quran her youth so much as the years she had left, all of them.

The silence one morning

Umar used to make his rounds through Madinah at night, watching over the city. One morning he said something simple and heavy: I did not hear the recitation of my aunt last night. He called her his aunt as a title of honour. That was how reliable her voice had become. Its absence was itself a piece of news.

When her home was checked, the worst had happened. A male servant and a female servant in her wealthy household had plotted against her. They killed her in her own home and took her wealth, and then they were gone. The teacher of the Quran, the woman whose nights of prayer the neighbours knew by heart, had been murdered for what she owned.

Umar himself found the two who had done it, and they were executed for the crime. And the words the Prophet ﷺ had spoken years before were shown to be true. When he had said, let us go and visit the martyr, he had spoken the truth about her end.

So she is remembered as the martyred hafidha of the women companions, the shahidah he had promised she would be. Not the martyrdom she had pictured on a battlefield, but the one Allah had written for her, in the very home where she had given her life to His book.

What Umm Waraqa's life teaches us

Her story is not built on a single famous day. It is built on what she did with ordinary time, again and again, until it shaped a whole life.

  • Use what you are given. She had freedom and means that others lacked, and she spent them on the Quran instead of on comfort. Whatever room our own lives leave us, that room is the test.
  • Worship that no one watches is still seen. Her nights of prayer were overheard by accident, not performed for an audience. The quiet acts, done only for Allah, are the ones that hold a life together.
  • Knowledge is meant to be passed on. She did not keep the Quran to herself. She taught the women of Madinah and let them recite to her so she could correct them. What we learn, we are meant to give.
  • Sincerity outlasts how a life ends. She longed to die for the truth on a battlefield and instead met a cruel, hidden death at home. Allah honoured the intention, and called her a martyr all the same.

Questions

Who was Umm Waraqa bint Abdullah?

A noble woman of Madinah and one of the few women of her time who had memorised the entire Quran. She was a teacher of recitation and is remembered as the martyred hafidha among the women companions of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Why was Umm Waraqa called the martyr?

She asked the Prophet ﷺ to let her join the army at Badr and to grant her martyrdom. He told her to stay home and promised she would be a martyr. From then on he and his companions called her the shahidah, the martyr, while she was still alive.

How did Umm Waraqa die?

She lived into the time of Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) as an elderly woman. Two of her servants plotted against her, killed her in her home, and stole her wealth. Umar found them and they were executed, and the Prophet's words about her martyrdom came true.

What can we learn from the life of Umm Waraqa?

To use the time and means we are given for something lasting, to keep up the worship no one sees, to pass on what we know, and to trust that Allah honours a sincere intention even when life does not end the way we hoped.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think a meaningful life needs a great public moment: a battle joined, a stand taken in front of a crowd. Umm Waraqa wanted exactly that, and Allah gave her something quieter and longer instead. She filled her years with verses learned, students corrected, and prayers no one was meant to hear, and that turned out to be the life worth remembering.

Most of our days look more like her nights than like a battlefield. The real test is usually small and unwatched: what we do with an ordinary evening, what we keep memorising, who we patiently teach, how we pray when no one is listening. She shows that none of it is too quiet to count.

So take one thing of hers into your own day. A few verses held close. A little knowledge passed to someone who needs it. A prayer offered only for Allah, with no one nearby to see. Do that much, and trust the rest to Him, the way she trusted Him with an ending she never got to choose.
