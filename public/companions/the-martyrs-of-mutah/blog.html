The Martyrs of Mu'tah

The Three Who Carried the Banner

Three thousand men stood on a plain in the north and looked across at an army they could not count. Behind them was the city of the Prophet ﷺ. In front of them was a force many times their size. Some of them wanted to turn back. One of them said the words that decided it: what you fear is exactly why you came.

This is the story of the martyrs of Mu'tah (may Allah be pleased with them): Zayd ibn Harithah, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib, and Abdullah ibn Rawahah, three men the Prophet ﷺ loved, who took the banner one after another and would not put it down.

Why an army was sent

After the treaty of Hudaybiyyah and the opening of Khaybar, the Muslims finally had some peace and some strength. The Prophet ﷺ began sending messengers out across the world, carrying letters that called kings and emperors to Islam.

The answers varied. The emperor of Persia tore up the letter and threatened the Prophet ﷺ, and when the Prophet ﷺ heard of it, he prayed that Allah would tear that kingdom apart, as in time it was. But for all his anger, even the emperor of Persia did not harm the man who carried the letter.

One of the messengers was sent toward the north, toward Busra in the lands of Sham. On the way he was stopped in the territory of the Ghassanid Arabs, Christian tribes allied to the Roman Empire and a standing threat to the Muslims from the north. Their chief asked him where he was going, and whether he carried the message of Muhammad ﷺ. When he said yes, the chief had him killed.

This had never happened before. To kill a messenger was to break a law the whole world agreed on, that an ambassador is protected no matter the quarrel. He was the only one of the Prophet's messengers ever to be murdered. In answer, the Prophet ﷺ gathered an army of three thousand and sent it to a place called Mu'tah.

The orders before they left

Before the army marched, the Prophet ﷺ named its leaders in advance, in an order no one would forget. Zayd ibn Harithah would carry the banner first. If Zayd was killed, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib would take it. If Ja'far was killed, Abdullah ibn Rawahah would take it after him.

When the three thousand reached Mu'tah, they saw what they had not expected. Facing them was a force of perhaps a hundred thousand Ghassanid fighters, with tens of thousands more trained soldiers behind them, possibly two hundred thousand in all. The companions had never seen odds so wide.

They paused. Some said they should send word back to Madinah and wait for new instructions. Abdullah ibn Rawahah refused. He told them that the thing they were afraid of, martyrdom in the path of Allah, was the very reason they had come. They went forward.

Zayd, the beloved one

Zayd ibn Harithah had been a boy stolen from his family and sold in the market of Makkah. He passed from hand to hand until he came into the household of the Prophet ﷺ, years before Islam. His father never stopped searching for him, and for years he wept lines of poetry into the silence, not knowing whether his son was alive to be hoped for or already dead.

Then the father found him, with Muhammad ﷺ. He offered to take him home. Zayd was given the choice freely, between his own family and the man who had cared for him, and he chose to stay. He said he saw something in this man he could not bring himself to leave. His father accepted it.

The Prophet ﷺ loved him deeply. He raised him, kept his company in the years when almost no one else stood with him, and trusted him with command after command. Zayd was the first to carry the banner at Mu'tah, and the first of the three to fall.

In Madinah, far from the battlefield, the Prophet ﷺ was given sight of what was happening. His companions heard his voice shake as he spoke. "Zayd has been killed," he said, naming the loss before any news could reach the city.

Ja'far, who would not let it fall

Ja'far ibn Abi Talib was the Prophet's cousin, ten years younger than him and dearly loved. As a young man he had stood before the king of Abyssinia and given so beautiful an account of Islam that the king embraced it and gave the Muslims a safe place to live. Ja'far stayed and led that community for years, eloquent and gentle, so devoted to the poor that they called him the father of the poor.

He had only just returned to Madinah when the army was sent. The Prophet ﷺ had embraced him on his arrival and said he did not know which made him happier, the opening of Khaybar or the coming of Ja'far. He told him he resembled the Prophet ﷺ himself in his looks and in his character. They had barely had time together before Mu'tah took him.

When Zayd fell, Ja'far took up the banner. The narrations describe what followed. He carried it in his right hand until his right arm was cut, so he caught it with his left. When his left arm was cut, he held the banner against his chest with what remained of his arms. He was struck more than fifty times before he died, and he never let it touch the ground.

The Prophet ﷺ later said that Allah had given Ja'far two wings in place of his arms, and that he was flying with the angels in the Garden. His young sons would be greeted as the children of the one with two wings.

Abdullah, who spoke to his own soul

Abdullah ibn Rawahah was a poet and a worshipper. He was among the first to meet the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah, fought beside him in every battle, and stood next to him defending him with verses. It was said that his words rained down on the enemy harder than arrows. On the hottest days, the companions said, he was often the only one fasting.

He was the one who had urged the army forward when they hesitated. Yet before the battle he was seen weeping, and when they asked him why, he said he had heard the verse that every soul will pass over the Fire, a promise binding upon the Lord. A man who had been given glad tidings of the Garden, who had been close to the Prophet ﷺ in his hardest moments, still feared for himself.

When the banner came to him and he felt his own heart pull back, he spoke to his soul out loud. He asked it why it disliked the Garden, and told it to go forward whether it wished to or not. Then he drove deep into the army until he too was killed.

The sword that was not commanded

With all three appointed leaders gone, the army had no commander. The companions turned to a man who had accepted Islam only months before, whose first battle this was. Once he had been the one who devised the plan that defeated them at Uhud. Now they handed him the banner, and Khalid ibn al-Walid became the sword of Allah.

Khalid did not try to win a battle of three thousand against two hundred thousand by force. He used a plan. He had the men tie things to their camels and ride in from different directions through the night, so the dust rose and the enemy believed a far larger army had arrived. Then he struck at the center.

The Muslims withdrew without being destroyed, and in that they saw a victory. Khalid said he broke nine swords that day. Three thousand had stood against an army many times their number and come home. The Prophet ﷺ grieved for the three he had loved, and the companions returned with their faith renewed.

Bodies here, souls in the Garden

The Prophet ﷺ carried the news himself to Ja'far's home. His wife saw him come in with grief on his face. He gathered Ja'far's children to him, held them, and wept. Then he told her plainly: Ja'far had been martyred.

These three were not the only ones who fell at Mu'tah, but they are the ones the story remembers first. Their graves lie in that land in the north. The companions understood that the graves held only bodies, and that the men themselves were somewhere far better.

That is the promise the Prophet ﷺ kept repeating, and the one the Quran had already given. The killed in the path of Allah are not dead. They are alive, with their Lord, provided for, in gardens far more beautiful than anything left behind. The companions did not mourn them as the lost. They envied them their place.

What the martyrs of Mu'tah teach us

Three men, three different lives, and one banner that passed from hand to hand. What stays with us is not the size of the army but the steadiness of the men who faced it.

  • Loyalty is shown when it costs. Zayd chose the Prophet ﷺ when he could have walked home to his own family, and stood by him in the years when almost no one else did.
  • Hold on to what is right, even when it is heavy. Ja'far kept the banner against his chest when both arms were gone. Some things are worth carrying past the point where carrying is easy.
  • Courage is not the absence of fear. Abdullah wept before the battle, then spoke to his own reluctant soul and went forward anyway. Brave people feel the fear and move.
  • Do not measure by numbers alone. Three thousand faced an army many times their size and did not break. Outcomes are not decided only by what can be counted.

Questions

Who were the martyrs of Mu'tah?

They were the companions who fell at the Battle of Mu'tah, remembered above all for its three appointed commanders: Zayd ibn Harithah, Ja'far ibn Abi Talib, and Abdullah ibn Rawahah (may Allah be pleased with them), three men the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ loved.

Why was the Battle of Mu'tah fought?

The Prophet ﷺ had sent a messenger toward the north carrying a letter calling people to Islam. He was stopped and killed by Christian Arab tribes allied to Rome, breaking the law that protected messengers. An army of three thousand was sent in answer.

How did the three commanders die?

The Prophet ﷺ had named them in order. Zayd carried the banner first and fell. Ja'far took it, was wounded again and again, and held it against his chest after both arms were cut, until he was killed. Abdullah took it third and was killed driving into the enemy.

What can we learn from the martyrs of Mu'tah?

Loyalty that holds when it costs, the strength to keep carrying what is right even when it grows heavy, courage that moves forward despite fear, and the truth that outcomes are not decided by numbers alone.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never face an army. But we know the moment at Mu'tah from the inside: the pause before something difficult, the quiet voice that asks us to turn back, the choice to go forward anyway. The three men did not feel no fear. Zayd had every reason to choose comfort and chose loyalty. Ja'far kept holding the banner long after his arms were gone. Abdullah wept, and went. Each of them felt the pull to stop, and moved through it with his heart still trembling.

Carry one of them into today. Be loyal where it costs you something. Keep holding what is good when it would be easier to set it down. And when your own soul pulls back from what you know you should do, speak to it gently, the way Abdullah spoke to his, and go forward. That is the courage this story asks of an ordinary life, and it is within reach of any of us.
