Mughira ibn Shu'ba

The Prophet's Bodyguard

Some people come to faith with clean hands. This man did not. He arrived with stolen wealth, blood behind him, and a reputation for being the most cunning man of his tribe.

This is the story of Mughira ibn Shu'ba (may Allah be pleased with him): the man from Ta'if with a heavy past who walked up to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, appointed himself his guard, and let Islam make him new.

The Companions

Mughira ibn Shu'ba

The Prophet's Bodyguard

A man with a difficult past

Mughira came from Thaqif, the tribe of Ta'if. His people were known for their sharpness, and he was sharper than most. He was clever, quick to read a room, and quick to read a person. Before Islam, he turned that intelligence toward the worst ends, and by his own account he was not a good man.

He was hard to miss. He was tall, broad in the shoulders, heavily built, with thick hair he wore tied in four braids. Strength and cunning together: that was Mughira before he believed.

His story is not hidden or softened in the records. It is told plainly, because it shows something the Prophet ﷺ wanted people to see: that Islam can wash away whatever came before it.

The thirteen men of Egypt

He once told the story himself. He set out with thirteen men of Banu Malik toward the ruler of Egypt, who was known for his generosity to visitors. He was not truly one of their circle, only travelling alongside them, and his uncle had warned him plainly: you are not close to these people, he said, and if anyone is left out, it will be you. It is dangerous for you to go. Mughira went anyway.

His uncle was right. The ruler received them, seated the man he already knew at his side, and gave gifts to the whole group. Mughira alone was left with nothing. He was not a Muslim then, and he was angry. He knew he could not overpower thirteen men on the road home, so he used the only weapon he trusted: his cleverness.

He carried good wine, told them he had a headache, and offered to serve while they drank. He poured for them until they no longer knew where they were. Then he killed them, took everything, tied up the camels, and decided he was set for life. This is who he was before Allah reached him.

I want to clean my hands

When Islam reached him, he wanted it. He travelled to Madinah with all that wealth still in his possession. The Prophet ﷺ was sitting with Abu Bakr (RA), who recognised the big man arriving with his camels and his obvious means, and asked, pointedly, whether he had come from Egypt, and what had become of the men of Banu Malik.

It was an awkward question, because it already knew its own answer. Mughira did not hide anything. He admitted what he had done, said that was simply how people lived before Islam, when men cheated and exploited one another, and then said the thing that revealed his heart. He no longer wanted the wealth. He wanted to give all of it to the Prophet ﷺ to use for the sake of Allah. He wanted to be forgiven. He wanted clean hands.

The answer he received drew a clear line. The Prophet ﷺ told him: I accept your Islam, but I have nothing to do with that wealth, because it is treachery. Mughira grew anxious, asking whether he was truly forgiven. He was assured that everything before Islam could be forgiven, but that stolen money was not the Prophet's to take. He would have to find a way to undo the harm he had caused, returning what was owed and making peace with those he had wronged.

The self-appointed guard

Once he was Muslim, Mughira stayed in Madinah and gave himself a role no one had asked him to fill. He stood at the Prophet's door. He moved when the Prophet ﷺ moved. He set himself, armoured, beside the messenger of Allah to protect him at all costs. He said he kept himself as close as Abu Bakr (RA).

His past followed him even there. One day his own uncle reached out to touch the Prophet's beard, and Mughira struck his hand away and warned him not to do it again. His uncle answered with weary irony: I am still cleaning up the mess you made, he said, still paying the blood money for those men you killed, and now here you are, knocking my hand away as you stand beside the Prophet ﷺ. It was the cost of the old life arriving in the middle of the new one. Mughira's reply was simple. I am different now.

He was among those who later gave the pledge to the Prophet ﷺ under the tree, the believers the Qur'an describes Allah being pleased with. His repentance was sincere. His faith was sincere. But the past was real, and he carried it honestly.

God was pleased with the believers when they swore allegiance to you [Prophet] under the tree: He knew what was in their hearts and so He sent tranquillity down to them and rewarded them with a speedy triumphQur'an 48:18

Let them watch us pray

The story comes full circle through Ta'if. The Prophet ﷺ had once been besieged and driven out of that city, and he had prayed that its people would one day come back to him as Muslims. After his envoy to Thaqif was killed, the people of Ta'if grew afraid and sent a delegation of thirteen men to Madinah to make peace.

Imagine the Prophet ﷺ in that moment. These were people who had stoned him, who had spat at him, who had nearly killed him, and who had now killed the very chief he had sent back to them as a Muslim. They were arriving in his own city, where everyone would have understood revenge. He chose something else. He was about to enter Ramadan, and when the delegation arrived, he gave one instruction: pitch a tent for them in the corner of the mosque.

Let them hear us recite the Qur'an, he said. Let them watch us pray, so that their hearts can soften. Mughira asked to be the one to receive his own cousins, partly to make peace with the very people he had wronged years before. Slowly, watching the believers at prayer through a month of Ramadan, Islam began to enter their hearts.

You are afraid of stone

The delegation argued hard. They tried to keep their old sins as conditions, and the Prophet ﷺ refused each one with patience: what is forbidden stays forbidden. They did not want the five prayers, and he let them begin by praying twice while he waited for their hearts to catch up, telling Umar (RA) he knew these people and knew they would come to pray, give, and stand with the believers in time.

The hardest condition was their idol. They were terrified to destroy it themselves, certain it would harm them. Mughira could not hold himself back: you built that idol with your own hands, he told them, and you are afraid of stone. He offered to go and break it himself.

He climbed it, raised his hand, and pretended to collapse, clutching his back. The people cried out that the idol had struck him down. Then he stood up, laughing at their fear, and beat the idol to pieces to show them that it had never held any power at all. And so Ta'if, at last, tasted the sweetness of faith.

The man who could not be outwitted

Mughira lived a long life in the service of Islam. He narrated much of what we know about the Prophet's habits, the way he travelled and carried himself, because he was always at his side. He served as a governor over Bahrain, over Kufa, and over Basra, trusted with cities under Umar (RA) and later Muawiyah (RA) because he was so capable, even when some found him too tough. In one famous test, men tried to frame him for taking a bribe, paying one of their own to claim Mughira had bought his silence. Mughira saw through it instantly, pressed the man until he broke and confessed the whole scheme was a lie, and explained afterward that he wanted them to learn that lying would never benefit them.

People said only one man ever outwitted him. Mughira was about to marry a woman, and a friend told him he had seen her kiss a man, so he let the marriage go. The next day he saw her married to that same man, who told him: I saw her kiss her father. You are the only person, Mughira admitted, who ever tricked me.

His most beautiful trick was his last act of love. When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, the family washed and buried him, and Mughira, who had guarded that body his whole Muslim life, could no longer stand at the door. So as the Prophet ﷺ was lowered into the grave, Mughira let his ring fall in, then climbed down to retrieve it, so that he could say he was the last person ever to touch the body of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

What Mughira's life teaches us

His story is not about a spotless past. It is about what a person can become when they bring everything to Allah and ask, honestly, to be made clean.

  • Islam washes away what came before. Mughira arrived with blood and stolen wealth behind him, and his faith was still accepted. No past is too heavy to bring to Allah.
  • Repentance means undoing the harm. The Prophet ﷺ accepted his Islam but refused the stolen money. Real return is not just regret; it is making things right where you can.
  • Soft hearts win more than force. Facing people who had nearly killed him, the Prophet ﷺ chose a tent by the mosque over revenge, and let prayer do the work.
  • Give your gifts to something higher. Mughira's cleverness once served only himself. In Islam, that same mind served the Prophet ﷺ and the people he was trusted to lead.

Questions

Who was Mughira ibn Shu'ba?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the tribe of Thaqif in Ta'if. Known for his intelligence and strength, he appointed himself as the Prophet's bodyguard and later served as a governor over several cities.

Why is he called the Prophet's bodyguard?

After accepting Islam, Mughira stayed close to the Prophet ﷺ at all times, standing at his door and moving with him, taking it upon himself to protect him. No one assigned him this role; he chose it out of love.

What happened to the wealth he brought to Madinah?

Mughira had taken it after killing thirteen travelling companions before Islam. He offered it all to the Prophet ﷺ, who accepted his Islam but refused the money because it was stolen, telling him he had to undo the harm he had caused.

What can we learn from the life of Mughira?

That Islam can wash away a heavy past, that true repentance means making things right, and that a soft heart and a sincere return can change the whole course of a life.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think faith is for people who were always good, and that a heavy past quietly closes the door. Mughira's life says the opposite. He came with the worst behind him, hid none of it, asked plainly to be forgiven, and was not turned away. The man who once poured wine to deceive thirteen travellers spent the rest of his life guarding the door of the Prophet ﷺ, and slipped his ring into the grave so he could be the last to touch him.

Most of us carry something we wish we had not done, some corner of the past we would rather not name. His story is an invitation to bring it honestly to Allah, to make right what we still can rather than only regret it, and to give whatever gift we have, our cleverness, our strength, our time, to something higher than ourselves. No one is too far gone to turn, and the door does not close on a heart that means it. We can begin again today.
