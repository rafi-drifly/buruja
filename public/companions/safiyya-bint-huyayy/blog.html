Safiyya bint Huyayy

A Heart of Gold

She was the daughter of one of the Prophet's most committed enemies, and she would become one of his wives. She carried a name that meant the chosen one, a lineage that reached back to prophets, and a quiet weight that followed her for the rest of her life: the feeling of never being fully trusted.

This is the story of Safiyya bint Huyayy (may Allah be pleased with her): the only wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from a Jewish background, and a woman remembered above all for the softness of her heart.

The Companions

Safiyya bint Huyayy

A Heart of Gold

A child adored, a father at war

Safiyya was the daughter of Huyayy ibn Akhtab, chief of Banu Nadir, the most powerful Jewish tribe in Madinah. Her mother came from the nobility of Banu Qurayza. Through her line she was a descendant of the Prophet Harun, and her people had settled in that region long ago, waiting for a prophet they believed would arise among them.

She used to say that of all the children she was the most beloved to her father and to her uncle. If they saw her among her siblings, their eyes went to her first. She spoke of her father with warmth, even knowing how he would treat the Prophet ﷺ. People can be one thing inside their home and another in the world.

When the Prophet ﷺ arrived at Quba at the very start of the migration, her father and uncle went out early to see if this was the awaited prophet. They came back after sunset, slumped and beaten down, and for once they did not even look at her. She heard her uncle ask, "Is it really him?" Her father answered, "By Allah, it is." And then, "What is in your heart toward him?" Her father said, "His enmity, as long as I live."

Two losses at Khaybar

Her father became one of the chief instigators against the Muslims, working from within to harm the Prophet ﷺ even during the long siege of the trench at Khandaq. Years of treaties did not hold him back. When the plots finally unraveled, the road led to Khaybar, seven years after the migration.

Safiyya had been married before, twice. Her last husband, Kinanah ibn Abi al-Huqayq, was by her own account a violent man. Her final memory of him was a blow to the face. She had told him a dream: a moon rising over Khaybar and settling into her lap. He struck her and said, "You desire the king of Yathrib." The bruise left a faint green mark near her eye.

At Khaybar her father was killed, and her husband was killed. She was taken among the captives, a noblewoman of two tribes who had lost everyone she belonged to.

The choice she was given

After the battle, Bilal (RA) led some of the women away, and his path passed close to where the fallen lay. A woman beside Safiyya broke down screaming. The Prophet ﷺ was pained by it and said, "Has mercy been taken from you, Bilal, that you would let these women see their slain men?" Even after all that had happened, he did not want the grieving to look upon their dead.

When it was clear who Safiyya was, the Prophet ﷺ called her forward. He spoke to her plainly about her father, that he had been the most hostile of men until Allah took him, as if to say he had never wanted any of this. Then he offered her a choice: accept Islam and remain with him, or keep her religion and return to her people, free.

Her answer surprised him. She told him she had longed for Islam and had believed in his prophethood before he ever invited her. She had no father left, no brother, no one. "Allah and His Messenger," she said, "are more beloved to me than my freedom." He freed her, and that freedom became her dowry, and he married her.

A wife under suspicion

On their first night they talked until morning. He asked about the faint green mark near her eye, and she told him about the dream and the blow that followed it. She admitted, quietly, that one hard feeling remained in her: he had been the cause, in the end, of her father's death and her husband's. He did not brush it aside. He explained, gently, what her father had done, until the last of that weight lifted from her heart.

But the suspicion of others did not lift so easily. The morning after, the Prophet ﷺ found Abu Ayyub al-Ansari (RA) circling the tent with a sword, afraid she might harm him. When their camel later stumbled from exhaustion and they slipped, some murmured that she was cursed, that she was a danger. Again and again, the same wound: we do not trust her.

This became the struggle of her whole life. Her faith was questioned, her intentions were questioned. When Aisha (RA) went to look at her and called her "another Jewish woman," the Prophet ﷺ stopped her: do not say that, for she has become Muslim. Aisha never used those words against her again. When other co-wives reminded Safiyya of her origins, telling her they were closer and dearer to the Prophet ﷺ because they were his own people, he comforted her with words she never forgot: "What can they say to you, when your father is Harun, your uncle is Musa, and your husband is Muhammad?"

There was tenderness in the smaller moments too. On the farewell pilgrimage her camel bolted and she broke down in tears, and the Prophet ﷺ held the whole caravan, walked to her, and wiped her tears with his own hand until she was calm. In a time when open affection was thought shameful, that gesture told everyone watching exactly how he saw her.

She is Safiyya

One night during the last ten nights of Ramadan, the wives of the Prophet ﷺ visited him while he was in retreat in the mosque. When Safiyya rose to leave, he walked with her toward her home, because it was late and he wanted her to feel safe. Two men of the Ansar passed, saw them, and quickened their pace, eyes down.

He called them back. "It is Safiyya," he said. "This is my wife." They were startled: "How could we ever think otherwise, Messenger of Allah?" He told them that the whisper of doubt moves through a person the way blood moves through the veins, and he did not want to leave any room for it in their hearts.

From that night came a saying still repeated: she is Safiyya. It means, simply, make things clear. If what you do could be misread, do not leave it to be misread. Spare others the doubt, and spare yourself the suspicion. It is advice that fits our own loud, fast world more closely than ever.

The gentleness that defined her

Each of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ had a quality that shone. The scholar adh-Dhahabi described Safiyya's in a single phrase: a woman of forbearance and dignity. Her superpower, if you like, was a heart of gold. She was soft, nurturing, and quick to love, the same forbearance the Prophet ﷺ himself carried toward his people. She was also, by several accounts, a good cook, which gave more than one co-wife another reason to feel the sting of jealousy.

When he lay dying, she saw his pain and wept, and said, "By Allah, I wish your pain were mine instead." Some of the wives rolled their eyes, as if she were performing. From between life and death, the Prophet ﷺ told them to rinse their mouths, and said, "By Allah, she is telling the truth." In his final days, he defended her sincerity out loud.

He did not leave her unprotected. When she worried aloud that the others had family to care for them after he was gone, while she had no one, he placed her in the care of Ali (RA). She would not be cast out. She remained among his household.

Mercy that outlived the battle

Years later, in the time of Umar (RA), a servant girl tried to stir up trouble, claiming Safiyya still kept the Sabbath and still favored the Jews of Madinah. Umar came to ask her himself. She answered that she had loved no Sabbath since Allah gave her Friday in its place, and as for her relatives, they were still her family, and she would keep her ties with them, because mercy and kinship are part of this religion.

Then she called the servant girl who had slandered her and asked why she had done it. The girl blamed the whisper of Shaytan. Safiyya could have punished her. Instead she said, "Go. You are free." When the city of Uthman (RA) was under siege, she risked herself to carry food and water to him, building a plank between the rooftops when the mob barred the streets, until they cut even that off.

When she died, she left a will. She had a brother who was still Jewish, and she had offered him faith and the inheritance that came with it. He refused. So she set aside a third of her wealth for him anyway, out of love for her family. They prayed over her in Madinah, and she was buried in al-Baqi, among the Mothers of the Believers.

What Safiyya's life teaches us

Her story is not built on a single great deed. It is built on a soft heart that held its shape under years of suspicion, and never turned hard.

  • Gentleness is strength, not weakness. Safiyya was remembered for forbearance and a tender heart. When the Prophet ﷺ was dying, her first instinct was to wish his pain onto herself.
  • Do not judge a person by their family. She bore the name of a hated enemy, yet the Prophet ﷺ measured her by her own faith and her own truthfulness, and so should we measure others.
  • Answer suspicion with sincerity. She could not stop people from doubting her, but she never let their doubt make her bitter. She kept being kind, and the truth defended her in the end.
  • Keep your ties of kinship. She held on to her relatives and showed them mercy even after all that had happened, and left them a share of her wealth out of love.

Questions

Who was Safiyya bint Huyayy?

A noblewoman of the Jewish tribe Banu Nadir in Madinah, daughter of its chief Huyayy ibn Akhtab. After the Battle of Khaybar she accepted Islam, married the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and became one of the Mothers of the Believers.

Why is Safiyya's story considered unique?

She was the only wife of the Prophet ﷺ from a Jewish background, and the only one who was not of Arab origin. She also descended from the Prophet Harun, placing her in the line of the prophets.

What does the saying "she is Safiyya" mean?

When two companions saw the Prophet ﷺ walking with her at night, he called them back and clarified that she was his wife, so no doubt would settle in their hearts. The phrase became a lesson: make things clear, and do not leave room for misunderstanding.

What can we learn from the life of Safiyya?

The quiet strength of gentleness, the importance of judging people by their own character rather than their lineage, and the dignity of answering suspicion with sincerity instead of bitterness.

Why this story stays with us

Safiyya lost almost everything and was given a place she never stopped having to defend. The easy path would have been to grow hard, to keep score, to return cold looks with cold looks. She did not. She stayed soft. She wished another's pain onto herself, kept ties with the family that had nothing left to offer her, and freed the very person who tried to ruin her name.

Most of us know what it is to feel unseen, doubted, or judged for something that is not who we are. Her life is an invitation to meet that the way she did: not by hardening, but by staying gentle and letting the truth speak for itself in time. Carry that into one difficult moment today, and her story has done its work.
