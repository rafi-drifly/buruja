Those Who Came Home Late

Some of the first people to believe were also among the last to arrive. They had left Makkah at the very beginning, crossed the sea to Abyssinia, and then waited there for years while the others fought at Badr, at Uhud, and at the trench.

This is the story of a boatful of them: Anisa, Al-Numan, and Aamir (may Allah be pleased with them), and the quiet companions whose names history did not keep, who finally sailed home to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ when their long wait was over.

Those Who Came Home Late

The king who longed to see the Prophet

When the first Muslims fled the persecution of Makkah, they crossed the sea to Abyssinia, and a Christian king received them. He is remembered as the Najashi (may Allah be pleased with him). He protected them, he believed in their Prophet, and by every account he longed to meet the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in this life, though he never would.

This is a corner of the story many of us never hear. We remember those who made the crossing. We rarely picture the years that came after, the families who settled there, the children born in exile, and the quiet care of a king who kept watching the news from Madinah and wishing he could be part of it.

Once a revolt rose against the Najashi, and the believers in his land watched it with fear. If he fell, their shelter fell with him. So he gave Jafar ibn Abi Talib and his companions a ship, already furnished, with everything on it, and a secret route to safety. If things do not work out, he told them, this is your way out. Take the ship, follow the news, and go. Allah gave him victory instead, and the Muslims were as happy that day as they would be at almost nothing else.

Years later, in the seventh year after the migration, just after the conquest of Khaybar, the Najashi sent the believers two more boats. This time it was not an escape route. It was the way home. The Muslims who had stayed in Abyssinia all those years would finally sail to Madinah.

A boat from Yemen meets a boat from the past

The story is carried to us by Abu Musa al-Ashari (may Allah be pleased with him), who was not from Makkah at all. He was from Yemen. When the news reached him that the Prophet ﷺ had migrated and settled in Madinah, he wanted what so many wanted: to be near him. So he set out to join him, with his two brothers, Abu Burda and Abu Ruhm, and more than fifty men.

Their ship did not reach Madinah. It came instead to Abyssinia, and there they found Jafar and the believers who had been waiting in that land for years. Jafar told them plainly: the Prophet ﷺ sent us here and commanded us to stay, but our time to leave is coming. Stay with us, and we will all go together.

So they did. The new believers from Yemen waited beside the old believers from Makkah, strangers and veterans together, until the boats were ready. Think of those weeks: men who had only just set out sitting alongside men who had waited the better part of fifteen years, all of them facing the same sea, all of them looking toward the same city.

When the time came, they sailed for Madinah as one company.

The day the Prophet could not name his greater joy

When the boats reached Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ received the companions he had not seen in years. Among them was Jafar, his own cousin, back at last from across the sea. The Prophet ﷺ was overjoyed to see him.

He embraced Jafar and kissed him on the forehead, between his eyes. Then he said something that has stayed with the ummah ever since. The conquest of Khaybar had just been won, a victory the whole community had longed for. And still the Prophet ﷺ said he did not know which made him happier: the conquest of Khaybar, or the coming of Jafar.

It is a short line, but it tells you what a returning believer was worth. A great military victory and a single beloved face, set side by side, and he could not say which weighed more.

These were people who had missed everything. Badr, Uhud, the trench, Khaybar itself: all of it had passed while they were away. They might have arrived feeling like latecomers to their own faith. Instead they were met by a Prophet ﷺ who counted their faces among the best news he had received in years.

Anisa and Al-Numan, and the first inheritance in Islam

Among the passengers on these boats were a sister and her brother: Anisa bint Adi and Al-Numan ibn Adi (may Allah be pleased with them). Their father, Adi ibn Nadla, was one of the very first Muslims. He had crossed to Abyssinia with the rest, and he had died there, far from home, still waiting for the day he never saw.

He holds a quiet place in our history. Adi is remembered as the first person in Islam to be inherited from, the very first time the rules of inheritance were applied at all. His two children, Anisa and Al-Numan, were his heirs. It is a strange and tender detail: the opening page of the law of inheritance is written over the loss of a man who died in exile for his faith.

So when you picture this boat, picture them on it. A young woman and her brother, carrying the memory of a father who left everything for this religion and never reached its homeland, sailing toward the city he had longed for, in a sense arriving for him.

Anisa is a name still loved among Muslims today. It can be traced, among others, to her: a believer who waited, who grieved, and who in the end came home.

The one who kept the Prophet's hair, and the brother who came home

There were others on these boats whose lives are worth pausing on. One was Maamar ibn Abdullah (may Allah be pleased with him), who wanted nothing more than to be near the Prophet ﷺ. Years later, at the Farewell Hajj, it was Maamar who shaved the Prophet's head, and the Prophet ﷺ let him keep some of that hair. He treasured it for the rest of his days, and he became known as the keeper of the Prophet's hair.

There is something quietly moving in that. A man waits years in a far country simply to be close to one person, and when he finally is, he is given a part of him to hold. The longing of the exile and the closeness of the companion meet in a single keepsake.

Another was Aamir ibn Abi Waqas (may Allah be pleased with him), the brother of the famous Saad ibn Abi Waqas. The two brothers had been pulled apart by the persecution. Saad stayed behind and bore it in Makkah; Aamir escaped across the sea. Neither path was easy. On these boats, after all those years, Aamir was finally coming back to his brother and to the Prophet ﷺ.

These are not famous names. They missed every great event. And yet each of them had given the hardest, earliest years of their lives to this faith, and each was received with honour.

The long, hidden years

It is worth sitting for a moment with what these people actually lived. They were among the first to believe, which means they were among the first to be hurt for it. They left their homes and their city and crossed the sea, and then the world they had left behind became the centre of everything.

From their distance in Abyssinia, they would have heard the news arrive in pieces. The migration to Madinah. Badr. Uhud. The trench, where the armies gathered to wipe the Muslims out, and the betrayal that came with it. Then Khaybar. Each report a moment they were not part of, a chapter of their own story written without them.

We tend to imagine the early Muslims as people who were always in the thick of it. Here is a group who spent the better part of fifteen years simply holding on, raising families, burying their dead, and keeping faith in a place far from the heart of it all.

That is a kind of test that does not look heroic from the outside. There is no battle in it, no famous stand. There is only the daily decision to keep believing while life happens somewhere else. And when they finally arrived, that decision was honoured.

Every path, and one promise

This one episode holds an astonishing range of lives. Some made the migration to Abyssinia and then on to Madinah. Some came back early, thought Makkah was safe, and got trapped there. Some made both migrations and lived to tell of it. Some died in Abyssinia without ever reaching home. Some could not flee at all and died in Makkah. Some died on the road to Madinah and never saw the promised city.

And some, like these passengers, waited in Abyssinia for many long years and reached Madinah only at the very end, in time to spend just the last two or three years beside the Prophet ﷺ.

No two of these journeys look the same. They differ in length, in hardship, in how much time each person was given with him. It is tempting to rank them, to imagine the ones who stayed and fought stood higher than the ones who waited far away.

The story refuses that. Over all of these lives sits one promise: that to each one Allah has promised the best reward, each their own particular share. Their wait did not lower them. Their lateness did not lessen them. May Allah be pleased with them all.

What these passengers teach us

Their story is not about a battle won or a sermon given. It is about people who held on through years of waiting, far from where they wanted to be, and were honoured for it.

  • Faithfulness is measured by the waiting, not only the arriving. They missed every famous moment and still belonged fully to the Prophet ﷺ. The years they spent holding on counted.
  • Being late is not the same as being less. They reached Madinah at the very end, yet they were received with joy. Allah's promise of reward did not shrink because their journey was long.
  • Unknown names are not forgotten names. Most of the people on those boats are nameless to us. Each one still had a promise from Allah. The record that matters is not kept by people.
  • Grief can be carried toward what you love. Anisa and Al-Numan buried a father in exile and still sailed on to the city he had longed for. Loss did not stop the journey home.

Questions

Who were Anisa, Al-Numan, and Aamir?

Early Muslims who had migrated to Abyssinia to escape persecution. Anisa bint Adi and Al-Numan ibn Adi were the children of Adi ibn Nadla, and Aamir ibn Abi Waqas was the brother of Saad ibn Abi Waqas. They were among those who sailed from Abyssinia to Madinah on boats sent by the Najashi.

Why did they arrive in Madinah so late?

They had left Makkah at the very beginning and stayed in Abyssinia for years. They only came to Madinah in the seventh year after the migration, just after the conquest of Khaybar, when the Najashi provided boats for the believers who had remained behind.

What is the first inheritance in Islam?

Their father, Adi ibn Nadla (may Allah be pleased with him), died in Abyssinia and is remembered as the first person in Islam to be inherited from. His children Anisa and Al-Numan were his heirs, the first time the rules of inheritance were applied.

What can we learn from these companions?

That faithfulness through long waiting is honoured, that arriving late does not make a believer less, and that Allah's promise of reward reaches even those whose names history did not keep.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will not be at the centre of any great moment. We will spend our years waiting, holding on to something good while life happens elsewhere, unsure if our small steadiness is even noticed. These passengers are an answer to that fear. They missed everything visible, and they lost none of their worth.

Carry their patience into an ordinary day. The faith you keep when no one is watching, the longing you hold for years, the loyalty that outlasts a long wait: none of it is wasted. Allah has promised the best to each one, including the ones whose names are never written down.
