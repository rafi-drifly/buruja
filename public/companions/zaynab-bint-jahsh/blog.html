Zaynab bint Jahsh

The Longest Arm

After the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ passed away, his wives would sit together and quietly measure their arms against one another. He had told them that the one with the longest arm would be the first to follow him, and each of them longed to be that one.

This is the story of Zaynab bint Jahsh (may Allah be pleased with her): born into the wealth of Makkah, married from above the seven heavens, and remembered for hands that could not hold on to money for the sake of Allah.

The Companions

Zaynab bint Jahsh

The Longest Arm

Born at the top of Makkah

Zaynab was born seventeen years before the revelation, into one of the most prominent families in Makkah. Her father came from a noble, wealthy line, said to own more property in the city than almost anyone else. Her mother was the maternal aunt of the Prophet ﷺ, so nobility ran on both sides.

She had wealth, beauty, prestige, and standing. In a society shaped by class, she came from the top of the top. And yet what is striking about her family is that all of the children, Zaynab and her siblings, accepted Islam.

Her brothers were among the early believers. One was the first commander to lead a force under the Prophet ﷺ and was martyred at Uhud, buried in the same grave as his uncle Hamza. Another, Abu Ahmad, was blind, yet he refused to let anything keep him from playing his part. If there was a flag to carry, he wanted to carry it. He would later become Zaynab's guardian and would live the longest of all of them.

Her sister Hamna also carried great grief. On the day of Uhud, three of her family were lost at once: her uncle, her brother, and her husband. When the Prophet ﷺ gently broke the news to her, she bore the first two losses with patience, but the loss of her husband broke her. It is a reminder of how much sorrow this family knew, and how deeply they had given of themselves.

A name that means fragrance

Before she was Zaynab, she carried a different name, one that meant free of all sin. The Prophet ﷺ changed it, as he changed the names of several others, because no person should claim such purity for themselves, and he did not want people to speak of anyone leaving that purity behind.

He gave her the name Zaynab instead, which is the name of a fragrant plant: the kind that grows more fragrant as it grows older. It became one of the names he loved, carried by his own family and grandchildren, and it spread through the community after her.

It was a fitting name for a life that would only grow sweeter with time. The woman who began in luxury would become known, by the end, for something far more lasting than her lineage.

Married from above the heavens

Zaynab had first been married to Zayd ibn Harithah, a freed slave whom the Prophet ﷺ loved deeply. When the Prophet ﷺ came to propose on Zayd's behalf, Zaynab and her sister had expected the proposal to be for the Prophet ﷺ himself. It was, at first, a hard thing to accept. But she sent back her answer: "I believe in Allah, I believe in the Messenger. Whatever you see is fitting, I will carry it out." It was a marriage meant to break the rigid lines of class in Makkah.

Yet the two of them came from completely different worlds, and over time it simply did not work between them. When Zayd came wanting to part from her, the Prophet ﷺ told him to hold on to his wife and to be mindful of Allah. Still, the marriage came to an end.

Then Allah commanded the Prophet ﷺ to marry Zaynab. This carried a heavy weight, because Zayd had once been regarded as the Prophet's adopted son, and adoption in that society was abused to seize an orphan's wealth and even to claim a dead man's widow without her consent. Through this marriage, that whole institution was set aside, while the care of orphans itself was honoured and encouraged in a cleaner way. The Qur'an described the principle behind it:

When God and His Messenger have decided on a matter that concerns them, it is not fitting for any believing man or woman to claim freedom of choice in that matter: whoever disobeys God and His Messenger is far astray.Qur'an 33:36

The marriage she never stopped mentioning

When the news came, Zaynab fell into prostration. This was the marriage she had hoped for, and it came after years of waiting. The Prophet ﷺ celebrated it with a wedding meal of bread and meat, generous enough that food was left over.

Zaynab would say, gently and often, that her family had not married her off at all. "Allah married me from above the seven heavens," she said. There was no human guardian and no human witnesses to her marriage contract. The verses of the Qur'an themselves served as her nikah.

She counted three things as gifts from her Lord: that she was married from above the heavens, that Allah was her guardian, and that Allah was the One who gave her in marriage. To her, these were not boasts about herself. They were signs of how Allah had honoured her.

The rope in the mosque

One day the Prophet ﷺ entered the mosque and saw a rope hanging between two pillars. He asked what it was. They told him it was Zaynab's rope: she would come at night and pray until she grew tired, and then she would lean on the rope to keep standing.

He told them to take it down. A person should pray, he taught, only as long as they have the energy and the awareness to understand what they are reciting. When tiredness takes over, they should sit and rest. It was correction, but it was also a window onto how far she had come.

Here was a woman born into royalty, now leaning on a rope so she could keep praying through the night. After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, it was said that she rarely left her home, spending her days and nights between the Qur'an and her prayer, as if nothing else in the world existed.

Hands like burning charcoal

Those who knew her said that money in Zaynab's hand was like a burning piece of charcoal: whatever came to her left her just as quickly. The woman who had grown up with wealth now could not hold on to it. Anything that reached her home was given away for the sake of Allah.

When the leader of the believers sent her yearly allowance, he was told she kept none of it. He sent more, and more again, and still she gave it all away. She prayed that she would not live to see another year of that money, because she feared it was a test for her. So a thousand dirhams of his own were brought to her instead, and she gave that away too, sharing it among those in need.

She was the shelter of the widows and the orphans of Madinah. When people came to her door, they hardly had to reach it before they found her hand already extended toward them. When the leader of the believers heard what she had said about the allowance, he understood that Allah wanted good for her, and he went to her home himself to insist she keep something for her own needs. She accepted a gift from him, and then gave that away too.

By the time she died, she owned almost nothing. She had even given away her furniture. This was not asked of everyone, and the Prophet ﷺ would not let some companions give away all they had. But for Zaynab, the world had simply lost its hold.

The longest arm

The Prophet ﷺ had once told his wives that the first of them to follow him after his death would be the one with the longest arm. So after he was gone, when they gathered, they would stretch out their arms and measure them, each hoping to be the first to rejoin him.

Zaynab was in fact one of the shortest among them. Yet she was the first of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ to pass away after him. Only then did they understand what he had meant: the longest arm was not the longest in length, but the one that reached the furthest in giving. Zaynab was the most generous of them all.

As she lay dying, she asked that her burial be kept as simple as possible. If extra cloth was sent for her shroud, she said, give it away. Whatever was not strictly needed to bury her, give it away for the sake of Allah. She had prepared her own shroud, and she left this world with little more than that.

Her funeral was a great event in Madinah, for she was their first mother to die after the Prophet ﷺ. Even Aisha (RA), who had so often differed with her, wept and said a beloved and righteous woman had passed away.

What Zaynab's life teaches us

Zaynab was born with everything the world admires. Her story is what she did with it, and what she let go of.

  • Generosity is measured by reach, not by length. The shortest arm became the longest because of what it gave. What we hold loosely for the sake of others outlasts what we grip tightly for ourselves.
  • Wealth is meant to pass through your hands. Money was like burning charcoal to her: it could not stay. She treated what she had as something to move along, not something to sit on.
  • Nearness to good company changes you. A few years near the Prophet ﷺ turned a woman of royalty into one of the most devoted worshippers and givers of her time. The people we sit beside shape what we become.
  • Honour comes from God, not from status. She did not boast of her noble birth. She spoke instead of how Allah had honoured her, and she spent her life seeking to be among the honoured of the next world, not this one.

Questions

Who was Zaynab bint Jahsh?

A woman of noble Makkan birth and a wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, honoured as one of the Mothers of the Believers. She was the first of his wives to pass away after him, and she is remembered as the most generous of them.

Why is Zaynab called the one with the longest arm?

The Prophet ﷺ told his wives that the first to follow him after his death would be the one with the longest arm. After he passed away, they measured their arms, but it was Zaynab, one of the shortest, who died first. They realised he meant the one who gave the most in charity.

What was special about her marriage to the Prophet?

It was commanded by Allah and described in the Qur'an, which set aside the misuse of adoption in that society. Zaynab used to say that Allah married her from above the seven heavens, with no human guardian or witnesses, only the verses of the Qur'an.

What can we learn from the life of Zaynab?

That true generosity is measured by how far it reaches, that wealth is meant to pass through our hands to others, and that honour comes from Allah rather than from status or lineage.

Why this story stays with us

Zaynab had every reason to hold on tightly. She was born into wealth and prestige, the kind most people spend their lives chasing. Instead she opened her hands and let it all go, until money would not stay with her any longer than a coal she could not bear to hold.

Most of us will not be asked to give away our furniture. But we can ask ourselves what we are gripping, and whether we trust that what we release for the sake of others will reach further than what we keep. Her arm was the longest because she reached toward people in need. Reach toward one of them today, quietly and without keeping count, and her story has done its work.
