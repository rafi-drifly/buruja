The Companions

Anas ibn Malik

The Servant of the Beloved

He was ten years old when his mother took him by the hand, walked him to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and said she had nothing else worthy to give. Only this son. Please take him, and let him serve you for as long as you wish.

This is the story of Anas ibn Malik (may Allah be pleased with him): the boy who spent ten years at the door of the Prophet ﷺ, who never once heard a harsh word from him, and who would later teach the world what it was like to stand that close to such a man.

The Companions

Anas ibn Malik

The Servant of the Beloved

A mother's gift

When the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Madinah, the people of the city brought him gifts to honour him. Anas was a young orphan, his father gone, and his mother Umm Sulaim (may Allah be pleased with her) had little to offer. So she offered the most precious thing she had.

She came to the Prophet ﷺ and said there was not a man or woman among the Ansar who had not given him something, and she had nothing to give except this son of hers. Take him, she said, and let him serve you. Then she added a request: please, pray for him.

The Prophet ﷺ made a prayer that was as complete as a prayer can be. He asked Allah to increase Anas in wealth, to increase him in children, to give him a long life, and to forgive his sins. Anas would live to see every part of that prayer answered.

Ten years at the door

Umm Sulaim's home stood right beside the apartments of the Prophet ﷺ. Every morning Anas would step out of his own door and go straight to the door of the Messenger, ready to begin the day. For the next ten years, his life revolved entirely around that man.

He had no fortune to lose by giving these years away. People might wonder what a boy gives up by being handed to a life of service. Anas gave up nothing. He had been given the greatest opportunity of all: to wake each day to the face of the Prophet ﷺ, and to close each day having walked him home.

The Prophet ﷺ called him by tender names. Sometimes Unays, little Anas. Sometimes simply, my son. He once held the boy's two ears and called him the one with two ears, the kind of gentle teasing that meant only affection. Anas carried the name Khadim Rasul Allah, the servant of the Messenger of Allah, with quiet pride for the rest of his life.

Ten years, not one harsh word

If anyone could have been treated carelessly, it was this boy. He was an orphan, a servant, young and easy to overlook. Instead, Anas gave one of the most striking testimonies in our history. He said he had never seen a man with more beautiful character than the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

He served him for ten years, he said, and in all that time the Prophet ﷺ never hit him, never cursed him, never said a single bad word to him. He never even frowned in his face. Never once gave him a look of displeasure.

There is a famous moment Anas loved to tell. The Prophet ﷺ sent him on an errand, and on the way the boy got distracted and went off to play with other children, forgetting the task entirely. After a while he looked up and saw the Prophet ﷺ had come and was sitting nearby, watching him, and laughing. He asked gently, little Anas, did you go where I asked you to go? Anas said, yes, I am on my way. That was the whole of the rebuke.

The eyes that saw everything

Because he was beside the Prophet ﷺ every single day, Anas became one of the great narrators of his life. It is said that the home life of the Prophet ﷺ comes to us mainly through our mother Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), and his life among the people comes to us mainly through Anas, because he was the one who was with him outside the door, every day, for ten years.

He could describe the Prophet ﷺ down to the smallest detail. He counted seventeen grey hairs in all in his hair and beard. He saw how the Prophet ﷺ treated his own children with a mercy Anas had never seen in anyone. He was there, holding the moment, when the Prophet's infant son Ibrahim died in his arms, and the tears fell.

He watched the Prophet ﷺ walk into the homes of his companions and call for the children first, joke with them, place his hand on their heads, and pray for them. He saw him sit with the poor who came asking, and never turn them away empty. Over two thousand reports of this kind come from Anas, and there is not one of them that makes you think the Prophet ﷺ was anything but gentle. Every single one makes you love him more.

Learning to be like him

Anas did not only watch. He tried to live what he saw. The companions said that if you wanted to see the prayer of the Prophet ﷺ, you should look at the prayer of Anas. Abu Hurairah (may Allah be pleased with him) said he had never met anyone whose prayer resembled the Prophet's more closely. Anas prayed at night until his feet swelled, just as his teacher had done.

The Prophet ﷺ did not only give him an example to copy. He taught him the inner work behind it. He told him, blessed is the one who is busy with his own faults instead of the faults of other people. And he gave him an advice Anas never forgot: my son, if you can wake and sleep with no malice in your heart toward anyone, then do so, for that is part of my way. Whoever gives life to my way loves me, and whoever loves me will be with me in Paradise.

This was the heart of it. The Prophet ﷺ had once told him plainly how to follow this path, and the Qur'an said it in the same breath:

Say, ‘If you love God, follow me, and God will love you and forgive you your sins; God is most forgiving, most merciful.’Quran 3:31

The last smile

Anas had thought about this. He had spent his whole young life beside this man, and he worried about the day they would be parted. He once asked the Prophet ﷺ to intercede for him on the Day of Judgement, and the Prophet ﷺ promised he would. Then, with the innocence of a boy who always knew where to find his teacher, Anas asked, where will I find you? Look for me at the bridge, he said. And if I do not find you there? Then at the scales. And if not there? Then at the fountain.

When the Prophet ﷺ lay dying, it was a Monday, and Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) was leading the prayer. Suddenly the Prophet ﷺ drew back the curtain of his room, which looked out over the mosque, and stood with whatever strength he had left, gazing at the rows of his people praying. Then he smiled, and his face was so bright it looked like a page of the Qur'an. The companions almost lost their focus in prayer, turning to catch a glimpse of him. He motioned for them to complete their prayer, and let the curtain fall. It was the last time Anas saw him alive.

Anas was family enough to help bury him. He said the day the Prophet ﷺ entered Madinah, the whole city seemed to light up, and the day he died, it was as if the lights were switched off. As they laid him in the earth, the warmth seemed to leave their hearts. Fatimah (may Allah be pleased with her) turned to him in her grief and asked how he could bear to place soil over her father's face.

A whole life remembering

Anas was given a gift after that day that almost no one else has been given. The Prophet ﷺ had said that whoever sees him in a dream has truly seen him, for the devil cannot take his form. Anas lived more than eighty years after the Prophet ﷺ died, and he used to say that not a single night passed without seeing his beloved one in his sleep. When he told this, he would weep.

He spent those eighty years doing one thing above all. He sat in the mosque in Basra and told people about the Prophet ﷺ, until the greatest scholars of the next generation, Hasan al-Basri, Ibn Sirin, al-Zuhri, and others, gathered around him to listen. Many of them counted as part of that generation only because they had met Anas. He had become a living bridge back to the Messenger ﷺ.

His long life was not without pain. In his old age the tyrant al-Hajjaj ibn Yusuf summoned him to humiliate him in public for the sides he had taken in the conflicts of the time. Anas, who had once been called little Anas in love, now heard the same name said in scorn. He answered only with the words, to Allah we belong and to Allah we return. Later he said he could have spoken a word that would have ended the man, but he held it back, because he did not want blood spilled in his name. He had learned from the Messenger ﷺ never to become the oppressor.

He died past the age of a hundred, the last of the major companions to leave this world. He liked to introduce himself as the last man among them who had prayed toward both directions of prayer. At his own request, he was buried with a few belongings of the Prophet ﷺ that he had kept all his life: a small staff, a strand of hair, a piece of cloth. He carried them into the grave.

What Anas's life teaches us

His story is not built on battles or great speeches. It is built on nearness, attention, and a heart that kept faith for a hundred years.

  • Service is not a loss. Anas gave ten years to serving someone else, and gained the richest life of all. The work we think is beneath us is often where the real blessing waits.
  • Gentleness leaves the deepest mark. Ten years without a single harsh word turned a servant boy into a man who loved his teacher for life. People remember how we made them feel long after they forget what we asked of them.
  • Watch closely, then live it. Anas did not just admire the Prophet ﷺ. He copied his prayer, his patience, and his clean heart. Admiration that never becomes action changes nothing.
  • A clear heart is a daily choice. Sleep and wake with no malice toward anyone, the Prophet ﷺ told him. It is the quiet discipline of letting go of spite, renewed every single night.

Questions

Who was Anas ibn Malik?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ whose mother, Umm Sulaim, gave him to serve the Prophet when he was about ten years old. He served him for ten years in Madinah and became one of the most prolific narrators of his life and sayings.

Why is Anas ibn Malik so important to Islamic history?

Because he was with the Prophet ﷺ every day for ten years, he narrated over two thousand reports about the Prophet's character, worship, family, and daily conduct. Much of what we know about how the Prophet ﷺ lived among people comes through Anas.

How long did Anas ibn Malik live?

He lived past the age of a hundred and was the last of the major companions to die. He spent more than eighty years after the Prophet's death teaching the next generation about him in Basra.

What can we learn from the life of Anas ibn Malik?

That service is an honour rather than a loss, that gentleness leaves a lasting mark, and that loving the Prophet ﷺ means trying to live as he lived, with a clean and patient heart.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be tested on a battlefield or asked to give a great speech. We are tested in the small, daily nearness of ordinary life: how we speak to the people who serve us, how we treat the child who forgets the errand, what we carry in our hearts when we lie down at night. Anas spent ten years close enough to see all of this in the Prophet ﷺ, and what he saw was patience that never cracked.

His life is an invitation to pay attention. To soften the word we were about to say. To empty the heart of spite before sleep, and to begin again in the morning. That is the way he loved, and it is a way still open to anyone who wants it today.
