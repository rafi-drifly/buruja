The Companions

The Four Abdullahs

The Keepers of the Knowledge

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ once said that the most beloved of names to Allah are Abdullah and Abd al-Rahman. Among his companions there were more than three hundred men who carried the name Abdullah. Most of them are known only to Allah.

But four of them are remembered together, simply as the Abdullahs (may Allah be pleased with them). They were young when the Prophet ﷺ was alive, and they lived long after him, into years of turmoil, and they carried what they had learned to a new generation.

The Four Abdullahs

The Keepers of the Knowledge

A name loved above others

There was a reason so many families chose this name. The Prophet ﷺ had told them that Abdullah, the servant of Allah, was among the most beloved of names to their Lord. So you would find a single father who named more than one son Abdullah, and more than one son Abd al-Rahman. It was a name parents reached for when they wanted, above all, to mark a child as a servant of God.

The greatest Abdullah of all was Abu Bakr himself, whose given name was Abdullah ibn Uthman. And there is another Abdullah whose name, when it appears alone in a narration with nothing else attached to it, means one man: Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, one of the first to embrace Islam. He took the Quran fresh from the mouth of the Prophet ﷺ, and the Prophet ﷺ said that whoever wished to hear the Quran recited with the same freshness it was revealed should listen to Ibn Mas'ud.

But Ibn Mas'ud passed away early, before the trials that would later shake the community. He never lived through the civil wars and the painful chapters that were to come, and so we did not receive from him all that such a man could have given. When scholars speak of the Abdullahs as a set, then, they mean four others, four who lived long enough to see those trials and to guide people through them.

The four who lived long

They were Abdullah ibn Abbas, Abdullah ibn Umar, Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr, and Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As. Each of them was the son of one of the noble companions of the Prophet ﷺ, and each of them grew up close to the heart of the early community, watching and listening in the years when Islam was still young.

What set these four apart was not only what they knew, but how long they were given to share it. They lived through the most painful turns in the story of Islam: civil strife, confusion, assassinations, war between people who all loved the same Prophet ﷺ. Many companions did not see those years. These four did, and they survived them, and they kept teaching.

Because they lived so long and travelled to so many regions, their footprint in the books is enormous. In almost any branch of Islamic learning, the chain of knowledge passes through one of these four men. They are among the companions from whom the most was taken.

Four lives, four gifts

Each of the four was distinguished by something of his own. Abdullah ibn Abbas was known for his love of the Quran. He became the interpreter of the Quran, the one people turned to when they wanted to understand its meaning, and his readings would shape how generations after him heard the words of Allah.

Abdullah ibn Umar was known for his devotion to the way of the Prophet ﷺ. He followed the sunnah so closely that he would retrace the very steps the Prophet ﷺ had taken, unwilling to let even a small practice slip away. Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As was known for his worship and his detachment from the world, a man who held the life of this world loosely and gave his nights to prayer.

Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr was known for his courage. He would go on to lead, to govern, and to stand at the centre of one of the great struggles of his time. So between the four of them you have the Quran, the sunnah, devotion, and courage. It was said of two of them: we never saw a man more knowledgeable than Ibn Abbas, and we never saw a man more devout than Ibn Umar.

The few who answered the hardest questions

There were tens of thousands of companions, but not all of them were teachers of religious rulings. Only a handful were the ones people came to with their hardest questions. The scholars later counted the companions who gave the most guidance, and they named seven.

Two of the four Abdullahs, Ibn Abbas and Ibn Umar, were among those seven, and they were among the most frequent of all. It was said that if you gathered the rulings given by each of them alone, you would fill volumes. The other two Abdullahs sat in the tier just below, still among the most relied upon of an entire generation.

This is a quiet measure of trust. People do not bring their most difficult, most private questions to someone they doubt. They bring them to someone they believe has both the knowledge and the sincerity to answer honestly. These four had earned that, young as they had once been.

It is worth pausing on what that trust cost them. To be the one others come to is to carry a weight: to be careful with every answer, to say I do not know when you do not, to guide without showing off. They were not simply clever. They were trusted, and being trusted is something a person builds slowly, with a whole life.

Different roads, the same principle

The four did not all respond to the turmoil of their time in the same way. They were contemporaries. They knew each other, deliberated with each other, and weighed together how to face the wars and divisions that were tearing through the community.

And yet they reached somewhat different conclusions. One took up arms and led. Another withdrew from the fighting altogether. There was real difference of opinion among them about how a believer should act when the community fractures, and each of them followed his conscience where it led.

But not one of them sold out. Not one became a traitor. Not one undermined the community the Prophet ﷺ had left behind. Their differences were principled differences, held by sincere people doing their best in a confusing time. That, in itself, is a lesson: good people can disagree, and still all be on the side of what is right.

How they leaned on one another

There is something quietly moving in the way these four turned to each other. They did not face the storm alone, each in his own corner. They talked. They consulted. They thought aloud together about how a believer should act when assassinations, war, and confusion are tearing through the community.

Their world was not a simple one. Different regions answered to different authorities. Loyalties were tested. The questions they faced had no easy answers, and getting them wrong carried a heavy cost.

And yet, out of that hardship, they modelled something we still need. They showed that disagreement does not have to mean division, that you can hold your ground and still sit with someone who has chosen differently, and that sincerity can survive even the most bitter of times.

There is something enriching in the picture of these four, bound by a shared faith, sometimes choosing different paths, never doubting one another's hearts. It tells us that a community can hold more than one honest view at once, and be stronger for it.

What they carried to us

All four were narrators of hadith. They reported not only from the Prophet ﷺ directly, but from the older companions they had grown up around, gathering and preserving what might otherwise have been lost. They were students before they were teachers, and they never stopped being both.

Think of what their long lives meant. The generation that had seen the Prophet ﷺ was passing, one by one. These four stayed. They held the memory steady, answered the questions of people who had never met him, raised students of their own, and carried the teaching into new cities and new years.

In almost any branch of Islamic learning, you will find their names somewhere in the chain. They are among the companions from whom the most was taken, and much of what later Muslims came to know about their faith reached them through one of these four men.

They are a bridge: between the Prophet ﷺ and everyone who came after, between a lived experience and a written record, between one generation and the next. The knowledge did not survive on its own. People carried it, at real cost, with real care. These four were among the ones who carried it for us.

What the four Abdullahs teach us

Their story is not about a single dramatic moment. It is about ordinary young people who were entrusted with something precious, and who spent their long lives keeping it safe.

  • Knowledge is a trust to pass on. They did not hoard what they learned. They taught it, region after region, year after year, so that it would outlive them. What we carry is meant to be handed forward.
  • Faithful difference is still faithful. Four sincere people can read the same situation and choose different paths. Their disagreement never became betrayal. We can hold our view firmly and still respect someone who sees it differently.
  • The young are not too young. These were boys around the Prophet ﷺ who became the anchors of a generation. No one is too small to be entrusted with something that matters, and to grow into it.
  • Steadiness through hard years is its own kind of greatness. They lived through war, loss, and confusion, and kept teaching anyway. Quietly staying faithful when everything around you is shaking is a victory in itself.

Questions

Who were the four Abdullahs?

They were four companions of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ who shared the name Abdullah: Abdullah ibn Abbas, Abdullah ibn Umar, Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr, and Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with them). They are remembered together as a group, sometimes called al-Abadilah.

Why were they grouped together?

They were young companions who lived long after the Prophet ﷺ, through the hardest years of early Islamic history. Because they lived so long and taught in so many places, an enormous share of Islamic knowledge passes through them, and scholars came to speak of them as a set.

What was each one known for?

Ibn Abbas was known for his love and interpretation of the Quran, Ibn Umar for his devotion to the way of the Prophet ﷺ, Ibn Amr ibn al-As for his worship and detachment from the world, and Ibn al-Zubayr for his courage and leadership.

What can we learn from the four Abdullahs?

That knowledge is a trust meant to be passed on, that sincere people can differ without betraying one another, and that staying steady and faithful through difficult years is a quiet form of greatness.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to imagine that the faith was carried only by famous battles and great public moments. But much of it was carried by four men who simply lived a long time, stayed sincere, and kept teaching what they knew. They watched their world fall into confusion, and they refused to let the knowledge fall with it.

Most of us will not lead armies or settle the questions of an age. But each of us holds something worth passing on: a habit of prayer, a kind word, a piece of understanding, an example for a child or a friend. Their lives are an invitation to guard what we have been given, to hand it gently to the next person, and to stay steady when the times are not. Carry one of those into today, and their story has done its work.
