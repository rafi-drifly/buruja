The Companions

Abdurrahman ibn Awf

A Generous Soul

A man arrives in a new city with nothing. He has left his money, his home, and his standing behind him. A wealthy host offers to split everything he owns with the stranger: his land, his savings, even one of his two homes. The stranger thanks him, and asks for one thing only. Show me where the market is.

This is the story of Abdurrahman ibn Awf (may Allah be pleased with him): a merchant who could turn over a stone and find a way to make a living under it, and who spent his whole life afraid that wealth might come between him and his Lord.

The Companions

Abdurrahman ibn Awf

A Generous Soul

The man Makkah admired

Before Islam his name was not Abdurrahman. He was called Abd Amr, a name tied to the customs of his people. When he accepted Islam, the Prophet ﷺ gave him one of the names most beloved to Allah: Abdurrahman, the servant of the Most Merciful. His kunya, the name people used with affection, was Abu Muhammad.

He was wealthy, dignified, and strikingly handsome, the kind of man others noticed when he entered a room. He came from Banu Zuhra, the tribe of the Prophet's own mother, Amina bint Wahb. Two of the ten companions promised Paradise were related to the Prophet ﷺ through that tribe, and Abdurrahman was one of them.

He was sharp, hard-working, and self-reliant. He had always known how to take care of himself, and he never needed anyone to do it for him. None of that changed when he believed. What changed was where all of it was now pointing.

Glad tidings in Yemen

Abdurrahman was a famous merchant, and the trade of Makkah ran along two great routes: a journey south in winter and north in summer. These were the journeys the Quran itself mentions, the security that the people of that city took for granted.

On one such trip he was in Yemen, and an elder there introduced him to a monk. The monk asked where he was from. Makkah, he said. And what brought him? Trade, he answered. He had come to buy and sell. The monk leaned in. Shall I not give you glad tidings greater than any trade you could do here?

A man would rise, the monk told him, from the very place he had just left, receiving revelation from the Lord of the heavens and the earth, continuing the way of Jesus, peace be upon him. Abdurrahman returned to Makkah and went straight to his close friend Abu Bakr. Had he heard of anything? Indeed, Abu Bakr said. It is Muhammad ibn Abdullah. That same day, Abu Bakr took him to embrace Islam.

[He did this] to make the Quraysh feel secure, secure in their winter and summer journeys.Quran 106:1-2

Show me the marketplace

Abdurrahman was among the first ten or fifteen to accept Islam, and he was tested for it. He made the first migration to Abyssinia, returned, and stayed near the Prophet ﷺ until the call came to leave for Madinah. He left everything behind a second time, one of the few who migrated twice for the sake of Allah. In Madinah the Prophet ﷺ paired each migrant with a helper from the city, and Abdurrahman was paired with Sa'd ibn al-Rabi, one of the wealthiest of the Ansar.

Sa'd held nothing back. He offered half his wealth, a choice of his two gardens, even to step aside from one of his two wives so that Abdurrahman could marry after the waiting period. It was an offer of staggering generosity. Abdurrahman was moved, and a little embarrassed by it. May Allah bless you in your family and your wealth, he said. But I have no need of any of that. Then came the words people still remember him by. Just show me where the marketplace is.

The markets of Madinah were not friendly ground for a newcomer. They were controlled by established tribes, some hostile to the Muslims. He studied them anyway, a businessman looking for a way in. He began with what was easiest to acquire: yogurt, butter, dates. From there he moved to trading horses, which he had known in Makkah, and then to selling saddles. He used to say he could turn over any stone and find a way to gold beneath it.

Before long he had enough to marry, all of it by his own hand, without taking a single thing from his host. He came to the Prophet ﷺ with the scent of perfume on him, clearly happy, and told him he had married. Hold a feast, the Prophet ﷺ said, even if it is only one sheep, and may Allah bless you in your family and your wealth.

Beside the Prophet in battle and in prayer

His wealth never made him soft or distant. He fought in every battle alongside the Prophet ﷺ, never once using his riches as an excuse to stay clean and safe at home. At Uhud he held his ground and was struck more than twenty times. Some of the wounds in his leg were so deep that he limped for the rest of his life.

When the Prophet ﷺ called for someone to fund an expedition, Abdurrahman came back with two thousand coins. And what did you leave behind, the Prophet ﷺ asked. Two thousand, he said. I gave half for the sake of Allah and kept half for my family. He did this again and again, and only one man, Uthman ibn Affan, ever outgave him.

On the expedition to Tabuk, the time for prayer arrived and the Prophet ﷺ was nowhere to be found. He had gone some distance away, and the companions, unwilling to lose the window of the prayer, asked Abdurrahman to lead them, which itself tells you how they saw him. He stood and began. Partway through, the Prophet ﷺ returned, found the congregation already praying in the dark, and quietly took his place behind Abdurrahman, catching the second unit.

Abdurrahman, not knowing, completed the prayer and gave the closing salaam. Then the Prophet ﷺ rose to finish the unit he had missed, and the companions, alarmed, began to glorify Allah, certain something had gone wrong. When he finished, the Prophet ﷺ put them at ease. You did well, he said, and reminded them to pray on time. Abdurrahman ibn Awf is the only companion we know of whom the Prophet ﷺ, in full health, prayed behind. It was an honour given to no one else.

All of Madinah lived off his giving

Talha ibn Ubaydillah, himself famous for his generosity, once said that the whole of Madinah lived off Abdurrahman's charity in one way or another. He broke it down plainly. A third of the people owed debts that Abdurrahman simply paid off. A third owed their debts to him, and he forgave them. And a third received his charity outright, with no thought of return.

When his trade caravan came home, it was an event. One day the ground of Madinah seemed to rumble and clouds of dust rose, and people feared the city was under attack. It was Abdurrahman's caravan: seven hundred camels, every one of them loaded with goods. Aisha (RA) heard, and made a prayer for him that he would drink from Salsabil, a spring of Paradise.

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Abdurrahman took it upon himself to care for the Mothers of the Believers, his widows, as a son cares for his mother. Every sale he made, large or small, sent a share to them. He once sold a piece of land for forty thousand dinars and gave it all away among the Prophet's relatives, his wives, and the poor. He was the trusted one, entrusted with the Prophet's wives, and he accompanied them on Hajj himself.

Afraid of his own wealth

For all that he had, Abdurrahman was deeply uneasy with it. We were tested with hardship alongside the Prophet ﷺ, he said, and we were patient. Then we were tested with ease and prosperity, and we were not. He had learned that comfort can be harder to survive than suffering.

He remembered a warning. The Prophet ﷺ had once asked his companions what they would become if the treasures of Persia and Rome were opened to them. They said they would stay as Allah had commanded. It may not be so, the Prophet ﷺ replied. You will compete, then envy one another, then turn on one another, then hate one another. He said he did not fear poverty for them. He feared the doors of wealth.

So Abdurrahman gave, constantly, out of fear that he might betray that trust. He was once seen making tawaf around the Kaaba, and the only words on his lips were these: O Allah, protect me from the greed of my soul. When food was placed before him he sometimes wept, remembering companions like Musab, killed with nothing to be buried in but a single garment too short to cover him. Despite the great caravans, you could not pick him out in a gathering from the poorest of servants.

A name no one needed to forgive

When it fell to him to help choose the next leader of the Muslims, Abdurrahman removed his own name from the running first. Then he walked the streets and consulted people, men, women, and children, before giving his decision. Later, when one caliph wrote that Abdurrahman should be his successor, Abdurrahman went to the Prophet's resting place and prayed only this: O Allah, if this responsibility is coming to me, take me before it does. He did not want to be tested with it.

He died at about seventy-five. Before death he freed many in bondage and willed away much of what remained: four hundred dinars to every veteran of Badr still living, and a large share to the widows of the Prophet ﷺ. Even after all of it, his family was left well provided for.

Ali (RA) watched the crowds at his funeral and said it seemed the whole community had come. When someone passes and the people testify to their goodness, Paradise becomes due for them. Looking out at that gathering, Ali said it: Paradise has become due for him. And he added words worth carrying. May Allah have mercy on you, Abu Muhammad. You gained the good of this world, and you were never deceived by it.

What Abdurrahman's life teaches us

His story is not about how much a person can earn. It is about who stays in charge when the earning succeeds.

  • Ask for the means, not the handout. Offered half of another man's fortune, he asked only to be shown the marketplace. Dignity is wanting to carry your own weight when you are able to.
  • Wealth is meant to flow, not to settle. He paid off debts, forgave debts, and gave outright until much of a city leaned on him, and he never asked for it back.
  • Guard your heart from your own greed. His one prayer at the Kaaba was to be protected from the greed of his soul. The danger he watched most closely was inside him, not outside.
  • Ease can be a harder test than hardship. He admitted that prosperity tested him more than poverty ever had. Comfort asks quietly whether you still remember who provided it.

Questions

Who was Abdurrahman ibn Awf?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, a successful merchant from the tribe of Banu Zuhra, and one of the ten companions given glad tidings of Paradise. He was among the earliest to accept Islam and migrated twice for the sake of Allah.

Why is Abdurrahman ibn Awf known for generosity?

He repeatedly gave away half his wealth, paid off and forgave the debts of much of Madinah, provided for the widows of the Prophet ﷺ, and freed many people from bondage. It was said the whole city lived off his charity in some way.

What did he say when offered half of his host's wealth?

When Sa'd ibn al-Rabi offered to share his wealth, gardens, and home, Abdurrahman thanked him and asked only one thing: to be shown the marketplace, so he could earn his own living.

What can we learn from the life of Abdurrahman ibn Awf?

That self-reliance and generosity can live in the same person, that wealth is a trust to be passed on rather than hoarded, and that the heart must be guarded from greed even, perhaps especially, when riches arrive.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never command seven hundred camels, but all of us know the quiet pull of wanting more and holding on tighter. Abdurrahman had every reason to be deceived by his wealth, and he was not. He kept his hands open and his heart watchful, and he treated everything he owned as something passing through him on its way to someone who needed it.

His life asks a gentle question of an ordinary day. What is flowing through your hands right now, and where is it going? You can hold your money, your time, and your comfort the way he held his: gratefully, lightly, and ready to let it move. Carry that, and pray the prayer he made, that Allah protect you from the greed of your own soul.
