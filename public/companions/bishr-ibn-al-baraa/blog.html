Bishr ibn al-Baraa

The One Who Ate Beside the Prophet

His father was the first man to give his hand to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in pledge. The son was still young when the Prophet ﷺ pointed at him before his whole tribe and said, in effect, this is your real leader.

This is the story of Bishr ibn al-Baraa (may Allah be pleased with him): an archer at Badr, a fighter at Uhud, and the one companion who shared a meal of poisoned meat with the Prophet ﷺ and did not spit it out.

The Companions

Bishr ibn al-Baraa

The One Who Ate Beside the Prophet

A son raised in his father's footsteps

Bishr was the eldest son of al-Baraa ibn Marur, a chief of Banu Salima in Madinah. His father holds a quiet honour in the story of Islam: he was the first to stretch out his hand and pledge himself to the Prophet ﷺ. Bishr was there for that pledge, at his father's side.

After his father passed, the tribe began looking for someone to take his place. The Prophet ﷺ came to Banu Salima and asked, who is your leader? They named another man, then added that he was a little stingy. The Prophet ﷺ replied that there is hardly a worse trait than that.

Then he pointed to the fair, curly-haired young man among them. No, he said, this is your leader: Bishr ibn al-Baraa. Bishr never formally took charge of the tribe. But the Prophet ﷺ had lifted him up in front of everyone, honouring who his father was, what his father had intended, and what he already knew was in store for the son.

A house with an open door

When the believers of Makkah migrated to Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ paired each newcomer with a host from the city. Bishr was old enough to receive a man into his home, and he was paired in brotherhood with one of the migrants, Waqid ibn Abdullah.

Waqid was one of the earliest Muslims, a man who had accepted Islam in Makkah before the believers ever gathered in secret. Little is written about him, but he lived in Bishr's house, and the two became close as brothers.

Bishr was married to a woman named Qubaysah bint Sayfi, and they had a daughter, Aaliyah. His wife and daughter accepted Islam as well. This was a household given over, quietly and completely, to the new faith.

The argument with the tribes

Some of the tribes of Madinah had long told their neighbours that a prophet was coming, one who would arrive from among them and put an end to the idol worshippers. They had used the promise almost as a threat held over the people of Aws and Khazraj, the very tribes that would later become the Ansar.

When the Prophet ﷺ finally came, Bishr went with Muadh ibn Jabal and a small group of companions to speak with them. They put a simple question to the tribes. You used to pray for this prophet, they said. You told us, year after year, that he would come and that we were nothing without him. Now he is here, and you are the first to reject him. How does that make sense?

Among those they spoke to was a leader of Banu Nadir who answered that this man carried none of the signs they had waited for, and that his book and his message were foreign to them. So the dividing line was drawn. Some, like Abdullah ibn Sallam, accepted the truth they recognised at once. Others knew it and refused it only because the Prophet ﷺ was not one of their own. And others claimed not to know him at all and set themselves against him.

It was the same stubbornness that had met him in Makkah, only wearing different clothes. Bishr stood on the side of the truth, and the enmity that grew out of these days would follow him for the rest of his short life.

The torch they thought was a curse

Bishr's brother in faith, Waqid ibn Abdullah, went out on one of the early expeditions and took part in the killing of a man who had been fierce in his hostility toward the Prophet ﷺ. The hostile tribes seized on it and turned it into a superstition.

They played on the names. The man who was killed was called Amr, and they twisted it to mean the war had been given its years. They twisted the word for his clan to mean the war was now present. And Waqid, his name means the one who lights a fire, so they declared that the torch of war had been lit and took it as an omen that they would defeat the Prophet ﷺ.

They celebrated, certain the sign was in their favour. It turned out to be the opposite. All of it would fall back against those tribes, not against the believers. As for Waqid himself, he lived quietly all the way into the time of Umar ibn al-Khattab, and he stood beside the Prophet ﷺ in every battle. The omen they built their hopes on came to nothing.

The slumber that came down at Uhud

Bishr was a skilled archer. At the Battle of Badr the Prophet ﷺ placed him among the archers, and the archers gave the believers a real advantage that day.

At Uhud the fighting turned chaotic. Blows were coming from every direction. Then something descended on the believers that the Quran later described: a calm, a tranquility, like the edge of sleep settling over men in the middle of battle. They grew so still that some slouched behind their shields and rested for a moment.

Bishr was one of them. He said that he was holding his sword when that peace came down, and his grip loosened until the sword slipped from his hand. Then he picked it up again, gripped it firmly, and went on fighting. It is the kind of clarity that does not come from the world: a stillness placed in the heart at the hardest moment.

After sorrow, He caused calm to descend upon you, a sleep that overtook some of you.Quran 3:154

The meal at Khaybar

After Khaybar, a woman whose husband and close family had been killed in the fighting came to the Prophet ﷺ with a roasted lamb. She had asked which part he liked best, and she had laced the shoulder, the cut he favoured, with a powerful poison.

The only person eating beside him was Bishr. The Prophet ﷺ took a piece and chewed it, and Bishr took a piece too. Almost at once the Prophet ﷺ told the companions to hold back their hands, because, he said, this meat has told me it is poisoned.

For Bishr it was already too late. He later said that when the meat was in his mouth, it felt wrong, but he could not bring himself to spit out food in the presence of the Prophet ﷺ. He said he did not wish to save his own life after the Prophet's, and he thought the Prophet ﷺ would never have eaten it if something were truly wrong. So he swallowed it whole, out of pure reverence.

The Prophet ﷺ called the woman and asked her why. She said that if he were a false prophet she would be rid of him, and if he were a true prophet he would come to no harm. He was spared, and he did not have her killed for what she intended toward him.

A martyr, and a mother who never forgot

The poison was meant to kill instantly. The Prophet ﷺ lived on for years afterward, through the return to Makkah, through Tabuk, through the Farewell Pilgrimage. Yet he would mention the pain of that meal even at the end of his life, and the scholars understood this as a sign of his own wish: to carry, alongside everything else, the honour and the rank of a martyr.

Bishr did not have those years. His skin began to change, his body failed him from the neck down, and he passed away from the poison as a martyr. The biographers note that while the Prophet ﷺ forgave the woman for what she intended toward him, she was later held to account for the life of Bishr, whom the poison had killed.

Then there is his mother, Umm Bishr. She was devastated by the loss of her son, even knowing the rank he had been given: the son of the first man to pledge, and the only one to die a martyr in that setting beside the Prophet ﷺ. She came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked him a question only a grieving mother asks. Do the dead know one another? He told her that they do. The good soul, he said, is carried in the body of a green bird in Paradise, and those birds come together and know one another on the branches of its trees, martyrs from every generation meeting and speaking again.

So whenever anyone in her tribe was close to death, Umm Bishr would go to them with the same request. When you meet my son, she would say, carry my greeting to him. She did this until she herself passed away, and then her own people came to her deathbed asking the very same thing. A father who pledged first, a son who died first, and a mother who kept sending her greeting ahead of her, all of them waiting for the same reunion.

What Bishr's life teaches us

His name does not appear on many lists of the famous companions. Yet a few short scenes from his life carry a weight that is hard to put down.

  • Reverence is shown in small moments. Bishr would not spit out a piece of meat in front of the Prophet ﷺ. That instinct, learned from his father, decided how he met his own death.
  • Calm is a gift, not a performance. The peace that settled on him at Uhud came from above, in the middle of fear. We can ask for that same stillness when our own days turn chaotic.
  • A quiet life can hold a great standing. Bishr is barely mentioned in the books, and yet his place beside the Prophet ﷺ was very high. Worth is not measured by how loudly a life is remembered.
  • Love outlasts loss. Umm Bishr never stopped sending word to her son. Grief, carried with faith, can become a kind of hope that keeps reaching forward.

Questions

Who was Bishr ibn al-Baraa?

A young companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from Banu Salima in Madinah. He was the son of al-Baraa ibn Marur, the first man to pledge himself to the Prophet ﷺ. Bishr fought at Badr and Uhud and died a martyr from the poisoned meat at Khaybar.

How did Bishr ibn al-Baraa die?

After the Battle of Khaybar, a woman served the Prophet ﷺ a roasted lamb laced with poison. Bishr was eating beside him and swallowed his piece before the Prophet ﷺ warned the others. The poison took his life, and he is counted as a martyr.

Why did Bishr not spit out the poisoned meat?

He said the meat felt wrong in his mouth, but out of reverence he would not spit out food in the presence of the Prophet ﷺ. He also thought the Prophet ﷺ would never have eaten it if something were truly wrong, so he swallowed it.

What can we learn from the life of Bishr ibn al-Baraa?

That reverence shows itself in small, private moments, that calm in hardship is a gift from Allah, and that a quietly remembered life can hold a very high standing with Allah and His Prophet ﷺ.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never face a battlefield or a poisoned meal. But we are shaped, every day, by the small habits of reverence we keep or let go: how we speak of what is sacred, how we hold ourselves before what we love, what we refuse to discard even when it would be easier to. Bishr's whole life seemed to turn on an instinct like that, one formed long before the moment it was ever tested.

His mother's grief carries its own quiet lesson. She did not let loss harden into despair. She turned it into a message sent ahead, again and again, to a son she trusted she would meet. To want his gentle reverence, and her stubborn hope, is to want a heart that stays soft and faithful in an ordinary life. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
