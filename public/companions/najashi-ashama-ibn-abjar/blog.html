Najashi (Ashama ibn Abjar)

The Righteous King

A frightened group of Muslims, fleeing their own city, crossed the sea to a foreign land ruled by a Christian king they had never met. They came as strangers with nothing, and a man who owed them nothing chose to protect them.

This is the story of Najashi, Ashama ibn Abjar (may Allah be pleased with him): the just king of Abyssinia, the man who would not be bought, and the only person the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ ever prayed over from a distance.

The Companions

Najashi (Ashama ibn Abjar)

The Righteous King

A king known for justice before Islam

Najashi is a title, like Caesar or Khosrow. His name was Ashama ibn Abjar, and he ruled the empire of Abyssinia in what is modern Ethiopia. His people had embraced Christianity centuries earlier, and their land was known as an island of faith in a sea of idol worship.

He was not only a ruler. He was a learned and devout Christian, a worshipper known among his people for his character. He listened to every side of a story before he judged. He could not be swayed by gifts. He had integrity that did not bend.

The Prophet ﷺ taught that people are like precious stones: the best of them before Islam are the best of them after Islam, when they have proper knowledge. The good in a person is only polished further by the truth. Najashi was such a man, and his reputation for justice reached far beyond his borders.

He was learned as well as righteous. A well read man, he would recognise in the message of the Prophet ﷺ the same truth confirmed by Jesus and Moses before him, peace be upon them. He governed his people wisely and brought his empire real prosperity. He was loved, and he was trusted, and that trust had been earned over a long life.

The name that meant a gift

There is meaning hidden in his name. Ashama, in Arabic, carries the sense of atiyya: a gift. And in truth, that is what he turned out to be, a gift to a community that had nowhere else to go.

His story before Islam reads almost like a parable. His father was the king and Ashama was his only son. His uncle, by contrast, had twelve sons. Some of the people plotted to kill the king so the throne would pass to the uncle and his many heirs, and the kingdom would feel more secure. They carried out the plot, and the uncle took the throne while Ashama was still a boy.

He grew up in his uncle's house, the lone son of a murdered father raised among the twelve cousins of the man who had killed him. As he grew, his intelligence and strength outshone them all, and slowly he came to understand what had been done to his father, and why.

Sold, and brought back

When advisers warned the uncle that this gifted young man might one day take back the throne, the uncle would not kill him. Instead he had Ashama captured in the night, taken to the slave market, and sold for six hundred dirhams. A man bought him and set out by boat to carry him away.

That same night, the sky over Abyssinia filled with clouds but gave no rain. The people asked the uncle, their king, to pray for rain. He gathered them, stood beneath the clouds, and as he prayed a bolt of lightning struck him dead.

The throne passed to his sons, but they proved unfit to rule. The people grew unhappy and remembered the boy they had thrown away. They searched for Ashama, found him in a distant land, bought him back, and placed the crown on his head. Everything they had done to keep him from the throne ended with Allah returning him to it. When the man who had purchased him came to complain, Najashi ordered that he be repaid his six hundred dirhams in full.

A safe harbour for the persecuted

He ruled wisely and brought his people prosperity, and his name for justice travelled. When the early Muslims were being persecuted in Makkah, the Prophet ﷺ, who had never been to Abyssinia and had never met its king, told them where to go. He said it was a land ruled by a man who would not tolerate injustice, and that they should stay there until Allah relieved their distress.

So a group of the noblest companions crossed the sea, among them Jafar ibn Abi Talib, Uthman with his wife Ruqayyah the daughter of the Prophet ﷺ, Zubayr, Asma bint Abi Bakr, Umm Salama, and Abu Salama (may Allah be pleased with them all).

Umm Salama, who narrated much of what followed, said that when they arrived Najashi received them warmly. He told them they could worship in safety, that they would suffer no injustice, and that they would never be made to feel like foreigners. He knew nothing yet of their faith. He acted because he was just.

The day the king wept

Quraysh would not let them rest. They sent Amr ibn al-As and Abdullah ibn Abi Rabia with leather and gifts to bribe the king into handing the refugees back. They told him these people had abandoned their old religion and invented a new one. The king's own generals advised him to listen.

Najashi refused to surrender anyone who had sought his protection without first hearing them. He summoned the Muslims, and Jafar rose to speak. He described what they had been: a people of idols and cruelty, where the strong devoured the weak. Then he described what the Prophet ﷺ had taught them of truth, mercy, and good character. The king asked if he had any of the revelation, and Jafar recited the opening of Surah Maryam, the account of Mary and the birth of Jesus.

Najashi wept until his beard was wet with tears, and his bishops wept with him. He told the men of Quraysh to take back their gifts, and he swore he would never give the Muslims up.

Of believers who hear the revelation, the Qur'an says:

and when they listen to what has been sent down to the Messenger, you will see their eyes overflowing with tears because they recognize the Truth [in it]. They say, 'Our Lord, we believe, so count us amongst the witnesses.Quran 5:83

Not for a mountain of gold

Amr tried once more. He returned and told the king that these Muslims insulted Jesus, calling him only a servant of God. Najashi summoned them again. Jafar answered without flinching: Jesus is the servant of Allah and His messenger, His word delivered to the pure Maryam. The king picked up a stick, drew a line on the ground, and said that Jesus did not exceed what Jafar had said by the length of that line.

His generals grunted in protest. He turned to them and said he did not care if they grunted. Then he turned to Jafar and said three times that whoever cursed these people would be punished. He told them they were free in his land, and that not for a mountain of gold would he let anyone harm them.

When Quraysh's envoys still pressed, he gave the answer that was remembered for generations. He said: Allah did not take a bribe from me when He gave me back my kingdom, so how can I take a bribe for it? And Allah did not do to me what they wanted Him to do, so how can I do to others what they want me to do? The man who had once been sold for silver could not be bought at any price.

Faith carried quietly to the end

Alone with Jafar after the court emptied, Najashi took the letter the Prophet ﷺ had sent him and accepted Islam. He said that had he not carried the weight of a kingdom, he would have gone to the Prophet ﷺ himself and carried his shoes. Letters passed between them for the rest of his life, and he later acted as the Prophet's representative in the marriage to Umm Habiba (may Allah be pleased with her).

When a rebellion rose against him, he did not abandon his guests. He prepared a ship and told the Muslims that if he were overthrown they could sail to safety, but that as long as Allah kept him in his place they were free to live in peace. The armies met at the Nile, and the Muslims, who had stayed clear of the politics of the land, watched from a distance and prayed the same prayer again and again: O Allah, grant him victory. When word came that he had won, they wept with relief. Allah set him firmly back on his throne.

Still, his people grew suspicious of his beliefs. Rather than risk their stability, he kept his faith hidden in plain sight. He wrote the testimony of faith on paper, placed it against his chest beneath his armour, and when they questioned his belief about Jesus he pointed to his chest and said he believed nothing more than this. He held that quiet truth until he died.

When he passed away, the angel Jibril brought the news to the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ gathered the companions, told them a righteous man had died, and led them in a funeral prayer for a man whose body lay across the sea: the only time he ever prayed over someone absent. It is said the light at his grave never faded. Scholars later debated whether to count him a companion or among the very best of the generation that followed him. He believed in the Prophet ﷺ in his lifetime, served his mission, and even called Amr ibn al-As to Islam, yet he never once met him. He gave everything a believer could give to a faith he embraced almost entirely from a distance.

What the life of Najashi teaches us

His story is not about a throne. It is about a man who used the position Allah gave him to shelter the weak and stand by the truth, whatever it cost.

  • Justice is owed even to strangers. He protected people he did not know, whose faith he did not yet understand, simply because they had been wronged and had asked for his help.
  • Integrity cannot be bought. Gifts, pressure, and the grumbling of his own court did not move him. He remembered who had truly given him his place, and refused to sell it.
  • Your own hardship can become mercy for others. The boy who was betrayed and sold grew into a king who would not abandon the persecuted. What was done to him shaped how he treated the helpless.
  • Faith can be sincere even when it is quiet. He never met the Prophet ﷺ and lived far from the community, yet he served the truth with everything he had, and Allah honoured him for it.

Questions

Who was Najashi, Ashama ibn Abjar?

He was the just Christian king of Abyssinia, in modern Ethiopia. Najashi was his royal title and Ashama ibn Abjar was his name. He gave refuge to the early Muslims who migrated from Makkah and later accepted Islam himself.

Why did the Muslims migrate to Abyssinia?

The Prophet ﷺ sent them there to escape persecution in Makkah, telling them it was ruled by a king who would not tolerate injustice. Among the migrants were Jafar ibn Abi Talib, Uthman, Ruqayyah, Zubayr, Umm Salama, and others.

Why is the funeral prayer for Najashi significant?

When Najashi died, the Prophet ﷺ led the companions in Madinah in a funeral prayer for him even though his body was in Abyssinia. It is the only recorded time the Prophet ﷺ prayed over someone who was absent.

What can we learn from the life of Najashi?

That justice is owed even to strangers, that real integrity cannot be bought, and that faith can be sincere and pleasing to Allah even when it is held quietly and far from the community.

Why this story stays with us

Najashi held real power, and the easiest thing in the world would have been to use it for himself. Instead he used it to shield people who could give him nothing back, and he refused every bribe that would have cost him his conscience. He remembered, every day, that his position was a trust and not a possession.

Most of us hold some small authority: over a home, a team, a younger sibling, a moment when someone weaker needs us to do the right thing. His life asks a simple question of that authority. Will we use it to protect, to be fair, to stand by the truth even when the room grumbles? Carry that question into today, and his story has done its work.
