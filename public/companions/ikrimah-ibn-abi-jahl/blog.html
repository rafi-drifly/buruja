Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl

The Son Who Chose a Different Road

There was a man in Makkah whose father had done everything in his power to destroy the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the early Muslims. The son stood at that father's side for years, then fought the Muslims himself, and carried his father's hatred long after his father was gone.

This is the story of Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl (may Allah be pleased with him): a man born into the deepest hostility, who travelled a long road back, and ended his life giving for the faith everything he had once spent against it.

The Companions

Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl

The Son Who Chose a Different Road

The son of the worst enemy

Ikrimah grew up beside Abu Jahl, the chief of his clan and the man the Prophet ﷺ once called the Pharaoh of this ummah. It is hard to overstate what that meant. Dr. Omar Suleiman notes that scholars count more than eighty verses of the Qur'an revealed in answer to the cruelty of this one man.

Abu Jahl was the one who built a public torture camp for the slaves who became Muslim. He stamped on the neck of a believer for praying near the Kaaba and broke his collarbone. He led the boycott that wore down the Prophet's family, and he helped plan the attempt on the Prophet's life.

Ikrimah was raised as the heir to all of this. He was big, strong, and capable, groomed to one day lead the clan as his father had. He inherited his father's standing, and he inherited his hostility with it.

Have you seen the man who forbids [Our] servant to pray?Qur'an 96:9-10

What the Prophet ﷺ saw in this family

The Prophet ﷺ once prayed for Allah to strengthen Islam through one of two men, naming Abu Jahl as one of them. He saw something in that household: real strength, real ability, leaders who could one day carry the truth if their hearts ever turned.

Dr. Omar Suleiman points to a striking pattern. Umar ibn al-Khattab and Abu Jahl were almost the same kind of man before Islam. Each had a son who closely resembled him. Of those four men, only one would die clinging to his rejection, the father whose blind rage shut him out for good.

There is a sad detail in the name. Abu Jahl's first nickname was Abul Hakam, the father of wisdom, for he was eloquent and shrewd. He became known instead as Abu Jahl, the father of ignorance, because his rage against the truth turned all of that ability to waste. The gifts were real. The choice was the problem.

It is a quiet reminder that the door was open. The same strength that made Abu Jahl a tyrant could, in another heart, build something good. Ikrimah carried that same raw material. The only question was where he would point it.

Badr, and a hatred that turned personal

At the Battle of Badr, Abu Jahl led the Quraysh against the Muslims. He was struck down by two young men, boys really, who had heard how he abused the Prophet ﷺ and were determined to reach him. For Ikrimah, something shifted that day. What had been his father's war against Islam became his own grief and his own rage. Now he wanted the Prophet ﷺ dead for a reason he could feel in his chest.

At Uhud he fought fiercely, commanding one wing of the Quraysh army while his wife, Umm Hakim, beat the drums of war. He was not satisfied even then. As long as the Prophet ﷺ was alive, he could not rest, and he said as much: Muhammad had to die, because Muhammad had killed his father.

When his close friend and cousin Khalid ibn al-Walid told him he was thinking of accepting Islam, Ikrimah was furious. "You want me to follow the religion of the man who killed my father?" He could see the Prophet ﷺ in no other light.

The day Makkah was forgiven

When the Prophet ﷺ returned to Makkah at last, he came with the spirit of the Prophet Yusuf, who told the brothers who had wronged him that there would be no blame upon them that day. He offered a general amnesty to the city that had driven him out and killed his people. He did not require anyone to become Muslim to be spared.

This forgiveness stunned his enemies. By every law of war he had the right to take revenge, and he set it aside. The mercy softened hearts that years of fighting never could. Some of his fiercest opponents came to him in person, and he forgave them.

Ikrimah was a rare exception. He was named among the very few who were still to be stopped, so set against the Prophet ﷺ that the order was to take him even if he were found clinging to the covering of the Kaaba. As the Muslims entered, he and a small band attacked Khalid's flank, and Khalid had to fight them off. Then he fled.

He ran toward the coast and boarded a ship to cross the sea, hoping to escape beyond reach. The irony was hard to miss. He was heading for the same land that had once given the persecuted Muslims refuge, now seeking refuge there himself.

The storm at sea

On the open water, a violent storm struck the ship, and the boat pitched until everyone aboard believed they would drown. The captain called out to the passengers to pray sincerely to their Lord, because no one could save them now except Him.

Ikrimah looked around and saw every person turning to Allah alone, and something in him gave way. He said to himself that if it is only Allah who can save us on the sea, then surely it is only Allah who can save us on the land. He later traced the beginning of faith in his heart to that exact moment.

The storm drove the ship back to where it had set out, and he never crossed. But he came ashore a different man. Meanwhile his wife, who had accepted Islam, had gone out to find him, carrying a promise of safety from the Prophet ﷺ himself.

Coming home to the Prophet ﷺ

His wife reached him and told him plainly: the Prophet ﷺ is a man of forgiveness and mercy, he has granted you safety, come back and save yourself. Between the storm at sea and her words, his resistance was gone. They turned together toward Makkah.

As Ikrimah approached, still hesitant, the Prophet ﷺ welcomed him warmly: "Welcome to the migrant who rides toward me." He even told the companions not to insult Abu Jahl in his son's presence, because cursing the dead does not reach them and only wounds the living. He was protecting a heart he wanted to win.

Ikrimah lowered his head and stretched out his hand. "What should I say?" The Prophet ﷺ had him repeat the testimony of faith, then raised his hands and asked Allah to forgive him for every battle he had fought and every hostile word he had ever spoken, in his presence and in his absence. Ikrimah's face lit up, and he made an oath.

There was a tenderness in how he remembered this turn afterward. When he wanted to swear by something, he would say, "By the One who saved me on the day of Badr." Badr was the day his heart had filled with hatred, the day his father died. He understood that had Allah let him die then, he would have shared his father's end. Instead he had been spared, and brought all the way here.

Say, '[God says], My servants who have harmed yourselves by your own excess, do not despair of God's mercy. God forgives all sins: He is truly the Most Forgiving, the Most Merciful.Qur'an 39:53

Spending everything he once spent against it

"By Allah," Ikrimah swore, "I will not rest until I spend in the way of Allah everything I once spent against it, and fight for the cause of Allah as hard as I once fought against it." It was not a phrase. It became the shape of his life.

His Islam, it was said, became excellent. He was never known to commit a sin after that day. He fasted long into the day and stood long into the night, and at times he would hold the Qur'an to his face and weep, saying, "The book of my Lord, the words of my Lord." He gave away the wealth he had inherited from his father for the sake of Allah, and he went through Makkah helping to clear away the idols he had once defended.

His one regret was that he never had the years his friends had beside the Prophet ﷺ. At the Battle of Yarmuk against the Romans, when Khalid told him not to plunge too deep because the Muslims needed him, Ikrimah answered that the others had already had their moment with the Prophet ﷺ, and he had spent those years as an enemy. "Let me make up for what I did." He took more than seventy wounds that day.

As he lay dying among the wounded, water was brought to him, and he pointed it toward the next man. That man pointed it to another, and so it passed down a line of dying men, each preferring his brother, until the water reached no one in time and all of them had passed. The son of the Pharaoh of this ummah died the very opposite of his father.

What Ikrimah's life teaches us

His story is not about where a person starts. It is about how completely a heart can turn, and what it does once it has.

  • No one is beyond return. A man who fought the Prophet ﷺ in every battle was welcomed, forgiven, and made one of the faith's most devoted. Do not write off another person, and do not write off yourself.
  • Mercy opens doors that pressure cannot. The amnesty over Makkah softened hearts that years of conflict had only hardened. Gentleness reaches people that force never will.
  • Protect the dignity of those you are trying to win. The Prophet ﷺ forbade insulting Ikrimah's father, knowing it would only wound him. Kindness was the path, even toward a heart that had done great harm.
  • Turn your old energy toward good. Ikrimah did not just stop fighting the truth. He poured the same strength into serving it. A changed life is measured by what it now builds.

Questions

Who was Ikrimah ibn Abi Jahl?

He was the son of Abu Jahl, one of the leading enemies of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in Makkah. After years of hostility, Ikrimah accepted Islam at the conquest of Makkah and became a devoted believer, dying as a martyr at the Battle of Yarmuk.

Why did Ikrimah fight the Muslims for so long?

He inherited his father's hostility, and after Abu Jahl was killed at Badr the conflict became personal grief and rage. He could only see the Prophet ﷺ as the man who had killed his father, until forgiveness and a near-death experience at sea changed his heart.

How did Ikrimah finally accept Islam?

Fleeing Makkah by ship, he was caught in a storm and saw everyone aboard turn to Allah alone. He reasoned that the One who saves at sea is the One who saves on land. His wife then reached him with the Prophet's promise of safety, and he returned to accept Islam.

What can we learn from the life of Ikrimah?

That no one is beyond return, that mercy reaches hearts that pressure cannot, and that a changed life is proven by what it gives. He spent for the faith everything he had once spent against it.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to decide that some people are too far gone, and easier still to quietly decide it about ourselves, that we have argued too long, drifted too far, or done too much to come back. Ikrimah's life answers that thought directly. The son of the worst enemy was welcomed without a single reproach, and given a clean beginning.

What moves the heart is not only that he was forgiven, but what he did with the forgiveness. He took the same drive that once worked against the truth and spent it, fully, in service of it. That is the invitation in his story: to believe a heart can turn, to meet the people around us with mercy rather than scorekeeping, and to point whatever strength we have toward something good, starting with today.
