The Companions

Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr

The Last One Standing

A baby boy is held up over the streets of Madinah, and a crowd begins to call out, "Allahu Akbar." Seventy-three years later, the same man is killed at prayer beside the Kaaba, and his killers, strangely, chant the same words over his body.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him): the first Muslim child born after the migration to Madinah, a warrior of rare courage, and a worshipper whose stillness in prayer earned him a quiet nickname.

The Companions

Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr

The Last One Standing

Born into a family of sacrifice

It is hard to imagine a child born into more. His grandfather was Abu Bakr (RA), the first man to accept Islam and the closest companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. His mother was Asma bint Abi Bakr, the woman known as the one with the two waist-belts, who once stood her ground before Abu Jahl himself. He was even named after his grandfather: his name was Abdullah, but his kunya was Abu Bakr.

His father was az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, one of the earliest to believe and the first man to draw a sword for the sake of Allah. The Prophet ﷺ said that every prophet has a disciple, and that his disciple was az-Zubayr. His grandmother was Safiyya, the aunt of the Prophet ﷺ, who feared no one but Allah. His great-uncle was Hamza, one of the great martyrs of Islam.

Everyone in his family had a story of sacrifice. He would grow up to carry something of each of them, and later he would name his own children after them, one by one.

The child who broke a rumour

When the Muslims migrated to Madinah, some who opposed them spread a claim: that they had cast a spell on the believers so that no more children would be born to them. For a time, with the exhaustion of the journey and the hardship of those first months, it seemed almost true. No babies came.

His mother Asma carried him through all of it. She was heavily pregnant when she climbed the hills of Makkah alone to bring food to the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr (RA) in hiding. She was the one Abu Jahl struck so hard that her earring flew from her ear, and still she did not betray where they were. The child in her womb survived all of it.

She gave birth to him in Quba, just outside Madinah, and he became the first Muslim child born there after the migration. The rumour was broken. Abu Bakr (RA) took the infant out into the streets and lifted him up, calling, "Allahu Akbar," and the believers answered him until the whole quarter rang with it.

The first taste, the first sound

The Prophet ﷺ took the newborn into his lap. He chewed a date, and from his own blessed saliva he rubbed it gently onto the roof of the baby's mouth. His mother said that the first thing ever to enter Abdullah's stomach was the saliva of the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet ﷺ rejoiced over him, made supplication for him, and gave him his name.

And the first voice to reach his ear was the adhan, called by his grandfather Abu Bakr (RA). When Abu Bakr said those words, he meant them in a way few human beings ever could. Before the child had opened his eyes to the world, the truest faith and the most beloved touch had already reached him.

He grew up resembling his grandfather more than anyone else among the companions, in face and in bearing. And his memory was so sharp that he narrated two reports from the Prophet ﷺ that he remembered from when he was only three years old.

A boy who feared no one but Allah

His first word was not mama or baba. It was "sword." While other children shaped toys from the dirt, he fashioned little swords from wood and shadowed his father with them. The signs were there before he could walk: this was a child built for courage.

When Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA) was caliph, he was so imposing that children scattered from the road when he came. One day the young Abdullah stood his ground. Umar asked him why he had not fled like the others. The boy answered that he had done nothing wrong, so why should he run, and that the road was wide enough for them both. Umar did not scold him. He picked him up, embraced him, and said, "This is the son of his father."

That confidence was never arrogance. It was a heart that simply did not fear anyone but Allah. You see it when he is ten, and you see it, unchanged, when he is seventy.

The pigeon of the masjid

Abdullah ibn Abbas (RA) once said that if you want to follow the prayer of the Prophet ﷺ, then imitate the prayer of Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr. It was not only the form of his prayer, but the spirit of it.

When he stood in prayer, he was as still as a wooden post. Birds would come and settle on him, mistaking him for part of the ground. He earned a quiet nickname: the pigeon of the masjid, both for that stillness and for the way he would remain in the mosque long after everyone else had left, alone with his Lord.

His focus was complete. There is a true account that, while he was praying, a snake fell onto one of his children and the whole house erupted in panic. He did not move. When he finished and asked about the noise, he learned only then how close the danger had been. He divided his nights between standing, bowing, and prostration, tasting the sweetness of each, striving against himself when no one was watching.

It was the same with his fasting and his giving. He would fast for days at a time, and he was so generous that he could not decide who gave more freely, his mother or his aunt Aisha (RA): one gave the moment money arrived, the other gathered it and then released it all at once. When he could not name the more generous of the two, it was because both had taught him the same lesson.

Courage on the battlefield

He was too young to fight beside the Prophet ﷺ, but war was in his memory all the same. He remembered his grandmother Safiyya striking down an attacker who came upon the women and children. He remembered his father riding through the enemy lines at Yarmuk and back again, unharmed.

He inherited his father's sword, the same blade az-Zubayr had carried at Badr, still marked with the dents of that day, and one day it would be the sword he died holding. In one battle the Muslims, only twelve thousand strong, faced an army many times their size. Abdullah asked for thirty men, walked straight up to the enemy commander as if he were only a messenger, and struck him down. Then he cried "Allahu Akbar," his thirty echoed him, and the great army broke and fled.

When his father once asked him to throw away the blood from his cupping, the boy had drunk it instead, unwilling to let any part of the Prophet ﷺ be lost. The Prophet ﷺ told him, "Woe to you from the people, and woe to the people from you." Those who knew him said his strength was unlike anything natural, and they traced it back to that day.

The last one standing

He had stood at the side of his father when Uthman (RA) was besieged in his home, and he took twenty-six wounds that day trying to protect him. He used to look at those scars and say he hoped they would be counted among his best deeds before Allah.

In the years of upheaval that followed, Abdullah refused to surrender to tyranny, and many of the lands of Islam, from Egypt to Iraq to Yemen, came to recognise his leadership. He became the guardian of Makkah, and he kept the people safe with the same nobility his family was known for.

Then a tyrant laid siege to the city for months. Food was cut off. Starvation set in until the people had nothing left but the water of Zamzam, too weak even to lift their swords. Stones from the catapults struck the Kaaba itself. One fragment passed between his beard and his neck and he did not flinch. His clothes caught fire and he stayed in his prayer. When the time for prayer came, he would set down his sword, pray, and take it up again.

His army of ten thousand melted away until he stood almost alone. He was seventy-three years old, fighting an army by himself. He went to his mother Asma, now a hundred years old, and told her the people had abandoned him and were offering him the world if he would only give in.

Her answer has echoed ever since. "If you know that you are upon the truth," she said, "then hold to it. Your companions were killed upon it. To be struck by a sword in dignity is better than a hundred lashes in humiliation." He kissed her head, her hands, and her feet, returned to the Kaaba, and fought until they stoned him as he prayed. A sweet fragrance rose from his body, and even those who had killed him began to regret it.

What his life teaches us

His story runs from a baby lifted up in joy to an old man standing alone, and the same heart beats through all of it.

  • Fear God, and you need fear nothing else. As a boy he would not run from Umar, and as an old man he would not bow to a tyrant. The same courage, because it rested on the same single fear.
  • Let your prayer be the still centre. Stones could fall and fire could catch, and he stayed in his prayer. When everything around us is loud, the prayer is where the heart goes quiet.
  • Dignity is worth more than comfort. He was offered the whole world to give in. He chose to stand on what he knew was true. A short stand in dignity outlasts a long life in humiliation.
  • These bodies are nothing; the souls are with Allah. His mother understood it, and so did he. What is honoured before Allah cannot be disgraced by what is done to the body.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the first Muslim child born in Madinah after the migration. He was the grandson of Abu Bakr (RA), the son of Asma bint Abi Bakr and az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, and later a leader who governed much of the Muslim world from Makkah.

Why is he called the first Muslim child born in Madinah?

When the Muslims migrated, some opponents claimed they had cursed the believers so no more children would be born. Asma gave birth to Abdullah in Quba, and Abu Bakr (RA) carried him through the streets calling "Allahu Akbar," a public sign that the claim was false.

Why was he nicknamed the pigeon of the masjid?

He was so still in prayer that birds would settle on him, and he would remain in the mosque long after everyone else had gone home. Ibn Abbas (RA) said that whoever wished to pray like the Prophet ﷺ should imitate the prayer of Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr.

What can we learn from his life?

To fear no one but Allah, to keep prayer as the still centre of a turbulent life, to choose dignity over comfort, and to remember that what truly lasts is the soul, not the body.

Why this story stays with us

There is a line his teacher drew between two crowds. As a newborn he was lifted up while believers cried "Allahu Akbar" in joy, and at the end his killers cried the same words over his body. The words did not change. The hearts behind them did. It is a quiet warning that the right words on the tongue are not the same as the truth in the heart.

Most of us will not be tested by a siege. We are tested in smaller ways: when it would be easier to give in, to stay quiet, to let the prayer slip while everything else clamours for attention. His life asks us to keep one still place inside, to hold to what we know is true, and to remember that these bodies are passing things, and the soul is what we are really tending. Carry that into today, and his story has done its work.
