Waraqa ibn Nawfal

The First to Confirm Prophethood

He was a very old man, almost a century, and he had lost his sight. He had spent his whole life looking for one thing, and now it had walked through his door.

This is the story of Waraqa ibn Nawfal (may Allah be pleased with him): the lone Christian scholar of Makkah, a man who waited his entire life for a prophet, and the first to look at the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and confirm what had happened to him.

The First to Confirm Prophethood

The seekers who walked away from the idols

Long before Islam, a small number of people in Makkah refused the idols of their tribe. They were called the Hunafa, the seekers of the pure way of Ibrahim (peace be upon him). They wanted the worship of one God, and in a city built around its idols, they were willing to leave everything familiar to find it.

There were four of them. Two travelled north to Syria to study the older scriptures and deepen their knowledge of the Abrahamic way. One of those two was Zayd ibn Amr. The other was Waraqa ibn Nawfal. Of the four, three became Christian, and Waraqa was one of them. Only one of them, Zayd, did not, and he died some five years before the revelation came.

So by the time the moment arrived, Waraqa was the only one of those seekers still alive and still in Makkah. The others had died, or moved away. He remained, waiting, like a man who had kept a lamp burning for a guest who had not yet arrived. He had prepared himself for a prophet, and now there was no one else left in the city who had.

The noblest of a noble house

Waraqa was the first cousin of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with her), though the gap in their ages was so large, perhaps twenty-five or thirty years, that he was more like an uncle to her. His family was among the most respected in Makkah, and Waraqa was counted the noblest of them.

By the measure of his people, he was the natural match for Khadijah: equal in lineage, equal in character. But he turned away from that life. He chose not to marry. He gave up the wealth of his rich tribe, dressed simply, ate only once a day, and gave his days to worship and his years to study and travel.

He was learned in a way few around him were. He read Hebrew, he wrote from the scriptures, and he was literate at a time when most were not. He was also not a man who fought his people. He kept to himself, away from the marketplace and the crowds, and trusted that the truth would be made clear in its own time.

Even the idol-worshippers of Makkah came to him to have their dreams interpreted. They cared little for their idols beyond the trade they brought, but they knew Waraqa was a man of real knowledge: a man who had studied language, scripture, and the ways of the older faiths. He was an elder whose word carried weight, and that was why, on the hardest day of her life, Khadijah knew exactly whose door to knock on.

A dream, and a quiet prediction

When Khadijah was still a young woman, she had a dream that the sun descended into her home. She did not know what it meant, so she took it to Waraqa, the family elder who understood such things.

He told her it might mean that she would one day marry a man of great nobility, or even a prophet. Years later it was Waraqa who conducted her marriage to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He had no idea, when he spoke those words, how exactly they would come true.

Waraqa had always sensed that something was coming, and that it would come to this region. He was not the only one. There were signs around the Prophet ﷺ long before the cave, and they had been noticed by others on quiet roads far from home.

Signs on the road to Syria

As a young man, the Prophet ﷺ travelled north with his uncle on a trade route to Syria. On the way, a monk who watched the caravan saw something and asked who was in charge of the boy. He warned the uncle that this child was no ordinary one, and told him to take him home and guard him, for his people might try to harm him if they knew who he was.

Later, when Khadijah sent the Prophet ﷺ to Syria with her trade, her servant Maysarah came back with stranger stories still. He said a cloud seemed to shade the Prophet ﷺ as he travelled, and that when he rested beneath a tree, the branches leaned to cover him. Another monk who saw him said only a prophet rests under that tree.

The people of Syria were used to dealing with traders by swearing oaths on their idols. The Prophet ﷺ would not. He told them he had never sworn by those idols in his life. Even before any revelation, those who looked closely kept seeing the same thing, and Waraqa, with his learning, was waiting for exactly this.

The day they came to his door

After the first revelation in the cave of Hira, the Prophet ﷺ came home shaken. It was Khadijah who knew where to take him. She brought him to her old cousin, the man who had studied the scriptures and would understand what others could not.

Waraqa was in his nineties by then, and blind. He asked, gently, what the Prophet ﷺ had seen, expecting perhaps a dream to interpret. The Prophet ﷺ told him everything that had happened in the cave.

Waraqa did not hesitate. He said that the one who had come was the same angel God had sent to Musa (peace be upon him), the bearer of revelation. Then he turned to the Prophet ﷺ and said it plainly: you are the prophet of this people. After a lifetime of waiting, he knew.

I wish I were young

The first thing Waraqa said next was not a question. It was a wish. "I wish I were young," he said. "I wish I could be alive on the day your people drive you out."

The Prophet ﷺ was startled. He had just been told he was a prophet, and now this old man was telling him his own people would turn on him. The Prophet ﷺ had never harmed anyone in Makkah. He was the peacemaker, the trusted one, the man tribes called on to settle their quarrels. He asked, will they really drive me out? Waraqa answered that no man had ever come with what he had come with except that his people took him as an enemy. It was never about the man, he said. It was about the message he carried.

Then this blind man of ninety made a promise. If I live to see that day, he said, I will support you with all of my strength, with everything Allah has given me. He did not ask Allah to make the path lighter. He asked for the strength to carry it. He died only a few days later, before the call to faith had even gone public.

The first to confirm

Scholars have long discussed how to count Waraqa. He believed before anyone else, and he died before the message became public, so some called him a believer who passed away in the pause before the call, and others wrote that he was indeed the first male companion. Either way, he was the first man on earth to confirm to the Prophet ﷺ that he was a prophet.

When Khadijah later worried about her cousin, who had believed and then died so soon, the Prophet ﷺ reassured her. He said he had seen Waraqa in a good state, dressed in white, and that had Waraqa been of the people of the Fire he would not have seen him so. In other narrations he saw him in the gardens of Paradise. Once, hearing a man speak ill of Waraqa, the Prophet ﷺ told him not to, and said he had seen Waraqa with not one garden but two.

There is a quiet beauty in where he sits in the story. The last time the Quran mentions Isa (peace be upon him) is when Isa foretells a messenger to come after him, whose name would be Ahmad. And the very first person to confirm that messenger had arrived was the last follower of Isa left in Makkah. One legacy was handed gently to the next.

What Waraqa's life teaches us

His whole part in the story is short: a few words, then a death a few days later. Yet a lifetime of quiet preparation stands behind those few words.

  • Spend your life ready for the truth. Waraqa did not stumble into that moment. He had searched, studied, and purified himself for decades, so that when the truth arrived he recognised it at once.
  • Recognise the good in others without rivalry. He was expected to be the great man of his family, yet his first instinct was to lift up another and to wish he could serve him.
  • Ask for strength, not for an easier road. He did not pray for a lighter burden. He prayed to be made strong enough to carry it. That is the difference between wishing well and meaning it.
  • A sincere intention counts with Allah. Waraqa died before he could lift a finger to help, yet his promise was true, and the Prophet ﷺ gave glad tidings of his place. Allah weighs what is in the heart.

Questions

Who was Waraqa ibn Nawfal?

He was a cousin of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (RA) and one of the few people of Makkah who rejected idols and sought the pure way of Ibrahim. A learned man who became Christian and read the older scriptures, he was the first to confirm the prophethood of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ after the first revelation.

What did Waraqa say to the Prophet Muhammad?

After hearing what happened in the cave of Hira, Waraqa said the one who came was the same angel sent to Musa, that the Prophet ﷺ was the prophet of his people, and that his people would one day drive him out. He promised that if he lived to see that day, he would support him with all his strength.

Was Waraqa a companion of the Prophet?

Scholars differ. He confirmed the Prophet's ﷺ message and believed in him, but he died only a few days later, before the call to Islam became public. Some count him as a believer who passed in that pause; others wrote that he was indeed the first male companion.

What can we learn from the life of Waraqa?

To prepare the heart for the truth so we recognise it when it comes, to honour the good in others without rivalry, to ask Allah for strength rather than an easier path, and to trust that a sincere intention is never wasted.

Why this story stays with us

Waraqa spent almost a hundred years getting ready for a moment that lasted minutes. He never fasted a Ramadan, never prayed beside the Prophet ﷺ in a mosque, never saw the religion grow. His whole share was one true sentence and one sincere promise, offered at the end of a long life of seeking.

Most of us will not be asked for something dramatic either. We are asked, quietly, to keep our hearts honest and ready, so that when the truth is in front of us we do not miss it, and to mean the good we say we will do. It is easy to wait for a grand moment to prove our faith. Waraqa's life says the proof is in the long, ordinary years of seeking that come before it. His example is an invitation to live that way: prepared, glad for the good in others, asking only for the strength to carry what we are given. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
