Safiyyah bint Abdul Muttalib

The Aunt Who Stood Her Ground

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had many uncles and aunts, more than most people can name. Of all his paternal aunts, only one is known with certainty to have accepted Islam and then to have left her mark on the story that followed.

This is the story of Safiyyah bint Abdul Muttalib (may Allah be pleased with her): aunt of the Prophet ﷺ, beloved sister of Hamza, and the single mother who raised one of the bravest companions, az-Zubayr.

The Aunt Who Stood Her Ground

One aunt among many

Safiyyah was a daughter of Abdul Muttalib, the grandfather of the Prophet ﷺ. That made her his aunt by blood. The family was large: scholars count ten or more uncles and six aunts, yet only a handful are remembered well, because most of them passed away before the message came.

Of all those uncles and aunts, only three are known for certain to have embraced Islam: Hamza, al-Abbas, and Safiyyah. Some scholars add two more of the aunts, Atika and Arwa, but Safiyyah is the one whose faith is beyond doubt and whose life then ran straight through the heart of the early community.

Part of the reason was her age. She was born around 569 or 570, the same time as the Prophet ﷺ, perhaps a year before him. So although she was his aunt, she grew up beside him more like a sister, in the same household and the same generation.

It is worth pausing on this. The people closest to the Prophet ﷺ by blood were not all carried into faith by that closeness. Most of his family lived and died before the message arrived, or never accepted it. Safiyyah was an exception, and the way she lived shows why she is remembered when so many others are only names.

Sister of Hamza, wife and widow

Safiyyah was especially close to her brother Hamza, who was also near the Prophet ﷺ in age. Her life would stay tied to Hamza for as long as she lived. She washed his clothes, greeted him when he returned from a journey, and cared for him the way an older sister would.

She married twice. Her first husband was al-Harith ibn Harb, the older brother of Abu Sufyan. After he died she married al-Awwam, the brother of Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), the wife of the Prophet ﷺ. Through that marriage she was drawn even closer to him, by family on top of blood.

With al-Awwam she had three sons: az-Zubayr, al-Sa'ib, and a young boy named Abd al-Ka'bah who died early. When al-Awwam died, az-Zubayr and al-Sa'ib were still young, and Safiyyah chose not to remarry. She would raise her boys alone.

This is an experience the Prophet ﷺ knew from the inside. His own father died before he was born, and his mother passed away when he was only six. So when we read about Safiyyah raising her sons without a father in the home, we are reading about something the Prophet ﷺ understood deeply, and something that shaped the kind of community the early believers became.

A mother who refused to let him be weak

In that society, an orphan boy with no father and no older brother to stand for him was easy to overlook and easy to bully. Safiyyah was determined that this would not be az-Zubayr. She set out to make him strong, capable, and unafraid.

She was a deeply involved mother. She took him out to learn the arts of his time, and she called on his uncle Nawfal ibn Khuwaylid to make sure the boy got the same exposure as any other young man. Her discipline was firm, firmer than the Prophet ﷺ would advise a parent to be, but it was born of love and worry, not cruelty.

When Nawfal once told her she disciplined the boy as though she hated him, it stung her. She answered in verse: whoever says I hate my son has lied. I am hard on him so that one day he will stand on his own, strong and clever, and return from battle victorious. She wanted a falcon, she said, not a soft and easy fruit.

We should be honest about what this was. The roughness of those days, and of a mother trying to armour an orphan against it, went beyond what the Prophet ﷺ would later teach a parent to do. But her aim was never to harm him. It was to make sure that when the world came for him, he would be ready to stand.

The day her son chose faith

She never imagined she was raising a boy so strong that he would one day defy her, and the whole family, with a new faith. At about thirteen, az-Zubayr came home and told her he had accepted Islam and the message of Muhammad ﷺ, against every uncle and every expectation around him.

She argued with him. Not because she hated the Prophet ﷺ, who was like a brother to her, but because she loved her son and feared for him. He was already an orphan with no protection. Now he would join a group of outcasts and be treated more harshly still. Her resistance was a mother's misguided attempt to shield her child.

When she could not move him, she sent for Nawfal to pressure him, and the boy was treated cruelly for his faith. Safiyyah herself held back, for a time. Then came the day that changed her: the day Hamza stood up and declared his Islam openly. On that day, Safiyyah accepted Islam too, and gave herself to the Prophet ﷺ and his message.

Grief done the right way at Uhud

Safiyyah made the journey to Madinah and remained a woman of strength and faith. The day of Uhud tested that to the limit. When the battle went badly and the dead were counted, the women ran out to the field, and Safiyyah came forward with a weapon in her hand, rebuking the men who had fled from the side of the Prophet ﷺ.

Her brother Hamza had been killed, and his body had been terribly mistreated. The Prophet ﷺ could not bear for her to see it. He sent az-Zubayr to stop her. She kept pushing forward, past her own son, until he finally told her the order came from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ himself. At that, she stopped at once. "I hear and I obey," she said.

When az-Zubayr confirmed that Hamza was gone, she did not collapse into despair. She said that if it was so, she would be patient and seek the reward from Allah. She was allowed to come to the body, and she stood over her brother into the late night, repeating that to Allah we belong and to Him we return, asking forgiveness for him again and again.

This was the brother she had loved her whole life, the one whose clothes she washed and whose return she greeted. To lose him in that way could have broken anyone. Instead she gave us a picture of grief that is honest and faithful at the same time: she wept, and she still turned her loss over to Allah and asked for his reward.

Alone at the fortress

Her courage showed most clearly at the Battle of the Trench. While the men dug and defended against the largest army they had yet faced, the women, children, and elders were sheltered in a fortress, guarded by the poet Hassan ibn Thabit, who was not a fighter.

Two men from a tribe that had turned against the Muslims came to scout the fortress, to learn whether any armed men were inside. Hassan froze. Safiyyah did not. She took a pole, hid behind the door, and when one of the men slipped in, she struck him down and killed him by herself.

Then she did something shrewd. She threw the man's body out over the wall. Seeing it, the enemy outside assumed an army was inside and fled. One woman, alone, had protected the entire fortress. When the Prophet ﷺ heard, it made sense to him: Safiyyah was an army in herself.

The mother of az-Zubayr

This was the woman who raised az-Zubayr, and now his courage made sense. He had inherited her strength, and the Prophet ﷺ would call him the son of Safiyyah. The hardness she once poured into his upbringing had become a faith and a bravery that served the whole community.

Safiyyah went on living with her son and with Asma bint Abi Bakr (may Allah be pleased with her), who looked on her as a mother too. When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, she grieved him in poetry, describing him as a sun wrapped in darkness yet somehow still shining, a light of guidance for the world.

There is something quietly beautiful in that eulogy. The woman who once feared her son would be too soft now mourned the gentlest and greatest of men, and found the words for him as a poet, not only as a grieving relative. Her strength and her tenderness were never two different things.

She spent the rest of her days in Madinah and was buried there in al-Baqi. The one aunt of the Prophet ﷺ whose faith is beyond doubt, she gave her all in the most courageous of ways, and left behind a son, a family, and an example that still speaks.

What Safiyyah's life teaches us

Her story is not soft, and it is not loud for the sake of being loud. It is the steadiness of a woman who carried weight alone and never put it down.

  • Strength can be an act of love. She was hard on az-Zubayr because she wanted him to survive a world that would not be kind to an orphan. Her firmness was care, aimed at his future, not his moment.
  • Obedience to truth can stop us in our tracks. Charging forward in grief, she froze the instant she learned the command was from the Prophet ﷺ. When something higher than our own will speaks, we can choose to listen.
  • Grief and faith can hold hands. She loved Hamza deeply, and she still said the right words: to Allah we belong, and to Him we return. Sorrow and patience are not enemies.
  • Courage does not wait for permission. When the men around her froze, she acted. One person who refuses to be paralysed can shield many.

Questions

Who was Safiyyah bint Abdul Muttalib?

She was a daughter of Abdul Muttalib and therefore a paternal aunt of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the sister of Hamza, and the mother of the companion az-Zubayr. She is the one aunt of the Prophet ﷺ known with certainty to have embraced Islam.

How was she related to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ?

She was his aunt by blood, the sister of his father's generation. Born around the same time as him, she grew up beside him more like a sister. Through her second marriage to al-Awwam, brother of Khadijah (RA), she was also connected to him by marriage.

What did Safiyyah do at the Battle of the Trench?

While sheltering with the women, children, and elders, she killed a scout from an enemy tribe with a pole and threw his body over the wall. The attackers outside assumed an army was inside and fled. She protected the fortress single-handedly.

What can we learn from the life of Safiyyah?

That firmness can be a form of love, that grief and patience can live together, and that courage often means acting when others are frozen. Her strength was steady, not for show.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us carry something heavy that no one fully sees: a household held together alone, a worry for a child, a loss that does not leave. Safiyyah carried all of these and did not let them make her bitter or brittle. She turned hardship into strength, and she pointed that strength outward, toward the people who needed her.

Her life invites a quiet question into our own day. When fear or grief tells us to freeze, can we still act? When we are stretched thin, can we still be the one who steadies the room? Take one piece of her courage into today, and her story has done its work.
