Al-Baraa ibn Ma'roor

The First Hand in the Pledge

He never lived a single day in a Muslim Madinah. He died one month before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ arrived in the city he had helped open for him. And yet his hand was the first to reach out.

This is the story of Al-Baraa ibn Ma'roor (may Allah be pleased with him): the elderly chief of his people, the first man to pledge himself to the Prophet ﷺ, and a believer whose two most famous deeds were, in truth, two mistakes.

The First Hand in the Pledge

The first hand in the dark

In the years when almost no one in Makkah would listen to him, the Prophet ﷺ met six young men from Yathrib during the season of Hajj. They believed, and they carried his message home. A year later twelve men came to pledge themselves to him at a place called Aqaba. The year after that, more than seventy came.

On both of those nights, hands were placed in the hand of the Prophet ﷺ, one by one, in secret. And on both nights the first hand to reach out belonged to the same man: Al-Baraa ibn Ma'roor.

He was not chosen by accident. He was the oldest of them, the most respected, the representative of the great tribe of Banu Salima. When his people looked for someone to step forward first into something this serious, they looked at him.

A man people trusted before Islam

Al-Baraa was a chief and a man of deep piety. Those who described him reached for an unusual phrase: he was a jurist of the soul. He was someone who had studied his own heart so closely, and guarded it from wrong so carefully, that he seemed to be steered toward what was right almost before he was told.

There is a saying of the Prophet ﷺ that people are like buried treasure, and that the best of them in the days before Islam are the best of them in Islam, once they gain understanding. The meaning is gentle and clear: a person known for honesty, good character, and generosity does not lose those things when faith arrives. The faith simply refines them.

That was Al-Baraa. Everything recorded about him is good. He did not come to Islam to become a better man. He came already carrying the qualities that Islam would polish.

The first mistake: which way to pray

When Al-Baraa accepted Islam, he learned that the Muslims prayed. In those earliest days the prayer itself was still taking its full shape. The believers faced Jerusalem, as the Prophet ﷺ did then. But something in Al-Baraa could not settle.

He thought of the Kaaba, the house built by Ibrahim for the worship of the one God, and he could not bring himself to turn his back on it. So he made a quiet decision. While the others prayed toward Jerusalem, he prayed toward Makkah. His companions on the road told him plainly that he was wrong, that they had only ever seen the Prophet ﷺ pray toward Jerusalem. He would not change.

When they finally reached Makkah, he did not defend himself. He turned to his nephew and said, in effect: I have been doing something on the road, and I do not know if it is right. Let us go to the Prophet ﷺ and ask him.

Take me to him, that I may ask

They did not even know what the Prophet ﷺ looked like. This is how early it all was. They searched, and were pointed toward his uncle al-Abbas, a merchant whose face the people of Madinah knew from his travels. They found al-Abbas sitting beside another man, and that other man was the one they had crossed a desert to meet.

The Prophet ﷺ asked al-Abbas to introduce the two arrivals. This is the chief of his people, came the answer, this is Al-Baraa. And Al-Baraa, with no excuses, told the Prophet ﷺ exactly what he had done, and asked him to say whether it was wrong.

The answer was kind. You had been upon a valid direction, the Prophet ﷺ told him, if only you had stayed patient with it. So Al-Baraa returned to praying toward Jerusalem with everyone else. And then, sixteen or seventeen months after the Hijra, the command came from Allah to turn the whole community toward Makkah after all.

The second mistake: the gift he could not give

Al-Baraa went home to Madinah carrying something rare. He had pledged himself to the Prophet ﷺ twice, brought his elderly mother and his family to faith, and helped prepare a whole city to receive a man it had never seen.

Then he fell ill. As death drew near, he made his will, and into it he placed one more act of love. He left a third of everything he owned to the Prophet ﷺ, to use as he wished, and a third to his family. It was the gesture of a man who wanted to give the most precious thing he had to the most beloved person he knew.

But this, too, was not how it was meant to be done. When the Prophet ﷺ came to Madinah, he returned Al-Baraa's bequest to his rightful heirs. The instinct behind the gift was honoured. The gift itself was gently set right. Both of the deeds we remember him for were, in the end, corrected, and both are recorded with nothing but praise, because both came from a heart that only ever wanted the truth.

The grave the Prophet stood beside

Al-Baraa died in the month of Safar, one month before the Prophet ﷺ made his migration to Madinah. He had done as much as almost anyone to open the door of that city, and he did not live to see the one he loved walk through it.

When the Prophet ﷺ arrived and asked after him, he was told that Al-Baraa had passed away. Take me to his grave, he said. They led him there. He stood before it and prayed for him: O Allah, forgive him, and have mercy upon him, and admit him into Paradise. In one narration he added: and indeed You have done so.

So the first man to place his hand in the Prophet's ﷺ hand never got to greet him in this life. But the Prophet ﷺ came to him in the end, and stood at his grave, and asked Allah for the very thing Al-Baraa had spent his whole life reaching toward.

What Al-Baraa's life teaches us

His story is short, and most of it is two well-meant mistakes. That is exactly why it is worth sitting with.

  • A sincere heart inclines toward the truth. Before any command reached him, Al-Baraa leaned toward the Kaaba and toward generosity. Sincerity quietly points us the right way, even when we get the details wrong.
  • Ask, instead of insisting. He acted on his best judgement, then humbly brought it to be checked: I have done this, and I do not know if it is right. A good heart is willing to be corrected.
  • Mistakes made in love are not held against us. Both of his famous acts were set right, and both are remembered with honour. Allah weighs the intention behind the deed, not only the deed.
  • Your effort is not wasted by your timing. He died before he could see the fruit of his work. It counted in full anyway. We are asked to plant, not always to harvest.

Questions

Who was Al-Baraa ibn Ma'roor?

An elderly and respected chief of Madinah from the tribe of Banu Salima. He was among the first believers of his city and the first person to place his hand in the hand of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ at both pledges of Aqaba.

Why is Al-Baraa not as well known as other companions?

He died in the month of Safar, one month before the Prophet ﷺ migrated to Madinah. Because he lived so briefly within Islam, few of his words and deeds were recorded, and his name stayed quiet in the histories.

What were the two mistakes he is remembered for?

Before the qibla was changed, he insisted on praying toward the Kaaba rather than Jerusalem, and the Prophet ﷺ told him to be patient with the direction the others faced. Later he willed a third of his wealth to the Prophet ﷺ, who returned it to Al-Baraa's heirs. Both were well-intentioned and both were gently corrected.

What can we learn from the life of Al-Baraa?

That a sincere heart leans toward the truth, that humility means being willing to ask and be corrected, and that an effort offered to Allah is never wasted, even when we do not live to see its fruit.

Why this story stays with us

We tend to measure a life by what it finished. Al-Baraa finished almost nothing. He never prayed in a Muslim Madinah, never saw the city change, never met the Prophet ﷺ again after the pledge. What he had was a heart that reached for the right thing first, and the humility to be corrected when his reach went slightly wrong.

Most of us will be like him more than we are like the famous names. We will do our small part and not see where it leads. His life is a quiet permission to keep reaching anyway: to lean toward what is good, to ask when we are unsure, to give what we can, and to trust that Allah does not let any of it go to waste.
