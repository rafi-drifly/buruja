Khawla bint Hakim and Uthman ibn Madhun

The Righteous Couple

They were not from the great tribes of Makkah. They held no banner that frightened anyone. Yet when the count is made of the first to believe, both of their names sit near the very top of the list.

This is the story of Khawla bint Hakim and Uthman ibn Madhun (may Allah be pleased with them): a husband who would not let the world touch him, and a wife who quietly shaped the household of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Khawla bint Hakim and Uthman ibn Madhun

The Righteous Couple

Among the first to believe

Some scholars place Uthman ibn Madhun and Khawla bint Hakim as the fourteenth and fifteenth people to accept Islam. This is the earliest of days, when believers could still be counted on a few hands.

They came from the smaller clans of Quraysh, not the powerful houses. Being of Quraysh gave them a measure of cover, but not the heavy protection the larger tribes could throw around their own. To believe, for them, was to step into the open with little to shield them.

When Uthman accepted Islam, his household followed. His brothers Abdullah and Qudamah believed, and his sister Zaynab too. Zaynab was the wife of Umar ibn al-Khattab, which made Uthman the maternal uncle of Abdullah ibn Umar and Hafsa (may Allah be pleased with them all). One narration says a whole group of them entered Islam in a single hour, then went home and brought their families in after them. For years afterward, that small circle stayed bound to one another.

The man who would not lose his mind

Almost everything in the days of ignorance ran on wine. People drank at their festivals, at their gatherings, even in their worship and their funerals. To refuse it entirely was nearly unheard of, even among the nobles. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) would later say that the people of Makkah were so attached to it that, had it been forbidden outright at the start, they could not have borne it.

Uthman was one of the very few who simply would not touch it. When they asked him why, his answer was not about religion, for no command had yet come. It was about dignity. Why, he said, would I drink something that would take away my mind, and let those beneath me in character laugh at me while I make a fool of myself?

It is a small line, but it tells you who he was before a single verse reached him. He guarded his mind and his manners when nothing obliged him to, on the strength of his own sense of what a person should be. He was honest in trade, gentle in dealing, and loved even by people who would never share his faith.

He gave up his protection

When the persecution grew, the Prophet ﷺ chose Uthman to lead the first migration to Abyssinia, the small band of believers who left their homes to find safety in a Christian land. They came back when a rumour reached them that Makkah had accepted Islam. It had not.

Uthman was loved even by people who hated the message. Al-Walid ibn al-Mughirah, a man the Quran names for his hostility to the Prophet ﷺ, treated Uthman like a nephew and placed him under his protection. No one in Makkah would dare touch him.

But Uthman watched the poor and the weak among the believers being tortured, while he walked safe. It troubled him. He went to al-Walid and asked him to lift his protection. I want to feel what they feel, he said. I want to be with those who are being persecuted.

Soon after, in a gathering near the Kaaba, a famous poet recited that every blessing must one day fade. Uthman corrected him: not Paradise, that blessing never fades. A man struck him hard in the eye and injured it. Al-Walid offered his protection back. Uthman refused. The protection of Allah, he said, is better than yours. His only regret was that the other eye had not been struck too, for the sake of Allah.

The two greatest matches she ever made

Khawla accepted Islam the moment Uthman came home with it. She grew close to Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her) and to the Prophet ﷺ, and became a trusted presence in their home. She was known for her intelligence and her knowledge of medicine, and above all as a matchmaker: the woman who arranged marriages in Makkah, before Islam and after it. People trusted her with this delicate work because they trusted her judgement and her heart.

When Khadijah passed away, the house of the Prophet ﷺ fell into grief. Khawla kept quietly bringing food and checking in, and in time she was the one who gently told him it was time to marry again. He was hesitant, still bound to Khadijah's memory. She suggested two people. One was Sawda bint Zamaa (may Allah be pleased with her), a widow who would bring warmth and light back into a grieving home. The other was Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), whom she described as the daughter of the dearest person in the world to him, Abu Bakr.

The Prophet ﷺ did not seize the moment for himself. Though he could have married whomever he wished, he answered with humility: mention me to their families, and let us see. Khawla took the task all the way through, speaking to Sawda and her father, then to the parents of Aisha, arranging both matches with patient care.

Think of what flowed from that. Every hadith we carry from our mother Aisha, every word she preserved of the Prophet ﷺ, traces back in part to a woman who simply brought two people together. It may be that Allah writes a share of all that good for Khawla.

The verse that filled his heart

By now the couple had made three migrations for the sake of Allah: twice to Abyssinia, and finally to Madinah. In Madinah, Uthman's devotion deepened until he wanted to leave the world behind entirely: to fast every day, pray every night, and turn away from married life altogether. The Prophet ﷺ came to know of it when Khawla visited Aisha in worn old clothes, looking neglected, and explained that her husband no longer had any need of this world.

The Prophet ﷺ called Uthman in and asked, do you not have in me a good example? I fast and I break my fast, I pray and I sleep, and I keep the company of my family. Uthman answered that he was not turning from the Prophet's way, and returned to a balanced life. Soon Khawla came to Aisha again, dressed well and at ease, and said she was now treated as a woman should be.

One day Uthman was sitting with the Prophet ﷺ when he saw him raise his eyes to the sky, then look right and left, as if listening to something unseen. The Prophet ﷺ, who was always fully present with whoever sat before him, had turned to something else entirely. Jibril had come, he told Uthman, and revealed a verse. It was the verse recited from every pulpit on Friday since:

God commands justice, doing good, and generosity towards relatives and He forbids what is shameful, blameworthy, and oppressive. He teaches you, so that you may take heed.Quran 16:90

The dunya could not touch him

Uthman said that in that moment faith overwhelmed his heart, and he loved the Prophet ﷺ with a love he had not known before. This was the man who had already given up his protection, who had wished both his eyes struck for Allah, who had migrated three times. And still his certainty grew. Nearness to Allah, his life shows, has no ceiling; the heart that keeps being nourished keeps drawing closer.

He did not live long in Madinah. He was the first of those who had migrated from Makkah to die there, and the first of them to be buried in al-Baqi. The Prophet ﷺ was at his bedside as his soul left him, and when the tears came he leaned down and kissed Uthman on the forehead. From this the scholars take the gentle permissibility of kissing a loved one after death. He placed a stone to mark the grave, called it the grave of my brother, and said he would bury beside him those of his own family who died. So Uthman lies today among the family of the Prophet ﷺ in al-Baqi.

Over him the Prophet ﷺ said words that have never been forgotten: may Allah have mercy on you, Uthman; the dunya took nothing from you, and you took nothing from it. The world, the scholars explain, can take a person's character and faith from them by degrees. It never took anything from Uthman. Khawla wept for him in verse, glad tidings to a loved one now living in the pleasure of his Lord. Later a woman dreamt of a river flowing toward Uthman, and the Prophet ﷺ said: those are his good deeds.

A believing woman, named by Allah

After Uthman died, Khawla was left alone in Madinah. She offered herself in marriage to the Prophet ﷺ. He did not marry her, as he did not marry several women who came to him, yet the way the Quran refers to such a believing woman who offers herself was understood to point to Khawla.

Scholars drew a quiet honour from this: Allah described her as a believing woman, praising her faith. She lived on as a narrator of hadith and a teacher of the next generation. Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz (may Allah have mercy on him) learned from her and called her al-Imra'a al-Salihah, the righteous woman.

From her we still carry the words of refuge taught by the Prophet ﷺ: I seek refuge in the perfect words of Allah from the evil of what He has created, said when you stop at a place, so that nothing will harm you until you leave it.

What their lives teach us

Two people, one home, and two very different ways of carrying the same faith. Both are worth keeping.

  • Guard yourself before you are told to. Uthman kept his mind and his dignity intact when nobody required it of him. Good character is built in the small refusals long before the rules arrive.
  • Do not hide behind your safety. He gave up his protection because he could not bear to be comfortable while others suffered. Real solidarity costs the one who offers it.
  • Quiet good can outlast you. Khawla brought two people together and never sought the credit. Some of the most lasting good we do is the kind no one applauds.
  • Hold the world loosely. The dunya took nothing from Uthman, and he took nothing from it. A heart can stay clean if it never lets the world rearrange it.

Questions

Who were Khawla bint Hakim and Uthman ibn Madhun?

A married couple from Makkah and among the earliest people to accept Islam, some say the fourteenth and fifteenth. Uthman was a close and beloved companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and Khawla was a trusted friend of his household.

Why is Uthman ibn Madhun remembered?

He refused alcohol before Islam, gave up his tribal protection to stand with the persecuted, and was the first of the Makkan migrants to die and be buried in Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ kissed his forehead and said the world took nothing from him.

What was Khawla bint Hakim known for?

She was known for her intelligence and her knowledge of medicine, and above all as a matchmaker. She arranged the marriages of the Prophet ﷺ to Sawda and to Aisha after the death of Khadijah, and later became a narrator of hadith and a teacher.

Is this the same Khawla as al-Mujadila?

No. Khawla bint Hakim, the wife of Uthman ibn Madhun, is often confused with al-Mujadila, the woman who pleaded with the Prophet ﷺ about her husband. They are two different companions.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be tested in front of a crowd. We are tested in quieter ways: whether we will stay comfortable while someone near us struggles, whether we can do good without needing it seen, whether the world is slowly rearranging our hearts without our noticing.

Uthman shows us a heart the world could not touch. Khawla shows us a kindness that asked for nothing and outlived her by centuries. Carry one of them into today: refuse one small thing that would dull you, or do one good thing no one will ever know about. That is their example, brought home.
