The Companions

Abu Musa al-Ash'ari

A Voice Like No Other

If you had asked the companions of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ to name the one reciter they could listen to without ever growing tired, almost all of them would have pointed to the same man. When they imagined the sound of Paradise, they thought of his voice.

This is the story of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari (may Allah be pleased with him): a teenager who crossed the sea to find faith, a jurist and a judge, and a man whose whole life became the recitation of the Quran.

The Companions

Abu Musa al-Ash'ari

A Voice Like No Other

A teenager who went looking

His real name was Abdullah ibn Qays, but history knows him by his nickname, Abu Musa al-Ash'ari. He grew up in Yemen, far from Makkah, and he was still a teenager when traders passing through brought word of a man calling people to one God.

He did not wait. At around fourteen or fifteen, he joined a caravan and made the long journey to Makkah to see this Prophet for himself, to learn who he was and what he was calling to. When he met the Prophet ﷺ, he embraced Islam at once.

The Prophet ﷺ sent him home to his people in Yemen, to call them and to wait for further word. The young man succeeded. His brothers, Abu Ruhm and Abu Amir, accepted Islam. His mother, Zabiya bint Wahb, accepted it. His whole tribe, the Ash'ariyun, came into the faith. Then they waited, learning how to pray and how to live, until the command would come to move to Madinah.

The ship that went the wrong way

About seven years after the Hijrah, Abu Musa and around fifty of his people boarded a ship from the south of Yemen to make their way up to Madinah. The winds had other plans. The boat was pushed off course, again and again, until it came to rest on the far coast at Abyssinia, the land that had once given the first Muslims refuge.

There they found Ja'far and the companions who had fled Makkah years before, and who were themselves preparing to migrate. The Negus, the righteous Christian king who had secretly embraced Islam, had readied boats for them. "The Prophet ﷺ sent us here and told us to stay," Ja'far told the newcomers. "So stay with us." They did, until they all sailed for Madinah together.

Imagine the scene at the harbour: two boatloads arriving, some who had not seen the Prophet ﷺ in years, and others about to meet him for the very first time. As they neared land, the people on the boat began to sing, "Tomorrow we meet our beloved ones, Muhammad ﷺ and his companions." It was the reverse of the Hijrah, the city now waiting to receive them.

The Prophet ﷺ had told his companions in Madinah, "A people are coming tomorrow whose hearts are even softer than yours." When Ja'far arrived, the Prophet ﷺ kissed him on the forehead and said, "I do not know which gives me more joy, the opening of Khaybar or the coming of Ja'far."

The reward of two migrations

Because their ship had drifted, Abu Musa and his tribe gained something rare. They had fled with their faith not once but twice: first to Abyssinia, then to Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ said to them, "You have the reward of two migrations: the migration to the Negus, and the migration to me here in Madinah."

When the Ash'ariyun reached Madinah, the narration says this was the first time shaking hands was practised as a custom of greeting, because they were introducing themselves to the companions for the first time. The Prophet ﷺ even gave them a share of the spoils of Khaybar, though they had not been present for the battle, a way of binding them to him in his hardest moments.

Soon they came to him with a simple request: "Will you recite the Quran to us?" He recited, and they all began to weep. Years later, in the time of Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him), another group from Yemen wept the same way at their first hearing of the Quran. Abu Bakr looked at them and said the words that have stayed with people ever since: this is how we used to be, before our hearts grew hard.

The voice the Prophet loved

Abu Musa was short and slight. He had no commanding physical presence, yet when he entered a gathering, people turned toward him. What set him apart was his voice, and what he carried in it: the Quran.

One night the Prophet ﷺ stopped outside a house to listen to him recite, and called Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) to listen with him. They stood there for a while. When Abu Musa learned of it afterwards, he said, "O Prophet of Allah, if I had known you were listening, I would have made it more beautiful for you." The Prophet ﷺ knew his voices so well that he once said he could tell which houses in Madinah belonged to the Ash'ariyun simply by the sound of their Quran at night, even though he never saw where they lived by day.

On another night the Prophet ﷺ stood at the door of the mosque, listening to Abu Musa pray inside, and asked his companion whether the man might be showing off. When the answer came that Allah and His Messenger knew best, the Prophet ﷺ said, "No, he is a believer who turns to Allah," and added the words he often repeated about him: "He has been given one of the instruments of the family of David." Like Prophet David, whose voice the mountains and the birds joined in praising God, Abu Musa had been given something rare, but with the Quran.

That night, not knowing he was heard, Abu Musa was making a quiet supplication, bearing witness to the One God in the words of a short surah:

Say, 'He is God the One, God the eternal. He begot no one nor was He begotten. No one is comparable to Him.'Quran 112:1-4

A people Allah loves

His tribe became known for more than recitation. When the Ash'ariyun went out on an expedition and food ran low, they would gather what little they had and divide it equally among themselves. The Prophet ﷺ loved them for their charity, for the way they cared for one another, and for their love of the Quran.

When a verse was revealed about Allah replacing those who turn away with a people He loves and who love Him, the Prophet ﷺ said to Abu Musa, "They are your people, O Abu Musa." He said something similar to Salman al-Farsi, and so the verse reaches beyond any one group. The meaning stands for everyone: a gift not taken up will be given to others who will hold it as it deserves to be held.

It is a sobering thought and a hopeful one at the same time. The Quran was placed within their reach, and they reached for it with their whole hearts.

You who believe, if any of you go back on your faith, God will soon replace you with people He loves and who love Him, people who are humble towards the believers, hard on the disbelievers, and who strive in God's way without fearing anyone's reproach.Quran 5:54

The jurist who kept teaching

Abu Musa was a hafiz at a time when memorising the entire Quran was far less common than it is now, and he was one of those who recited it directly to the Prophet ﷺ. He was also a faqih and a judge. When the companions of knowledge were counted, his name stood among the few who gave verdicts in the mosque of the Prophet ﷺ. Ali described him as a man completely immersed in knowledge.

Yet he never thought himself too great to teach. Wherever he settled, after the noon prayer he would ask, "Is there anyone who wants to learn the Quran?" Then he would teach until the afternoon prayer, and if people remained, until sunset. The Prophet's own instruction to him when he was sent to Yemen was simple: keep teaching the people the Quran, and do not stop.

He recited everywhere, standing, sitting, even while riding. When his companion Mu'adh ibn Jabal asked how he kept up his recitation, Abu Musa described a life soaked in it. The Prophet ﷺ had warned him to guard it: the Quran slips away from a person the way a camel slips away if it is not tied. So Abu Musa kept renewing his bond with it, every day and every night.

When the Prophet ﷺ sent him back to Yemen, alongside Mu'adh, his counsel was just as gentle: "Make things easy, not hard. Give glad tidings, and do not drive people away." Abu Musa carried it. In every city he reached, he turned the place into a community of the Quran.

Leadership that left him unchanged

Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) trusted him deeply. Where Umar moved most governors after a single year, fearing power would corrupt them, he kept Abu Musa in Basra for four. And when Abu Musa left that post after years of authority, he left with only six hundred dirhams. Leadership had not made him rich, and it had not changed him.

He lived long enough to see civil war break out among the Muslims, and it grieved him. He narrated many of the warnings of the Prophet ﷺ about such trials: that whoever raises a weapon against the believers is not of them, that the best Muslim is one from whose tongue and hand others are safe, that the believers are like a single building, each part holding up the rest. When asked later to take a position again, he refused, saying he wanted nothing on his record before Allah but peace.

He fasted often. He was known to plead with the wealthy on behalf of the poor and those in debt. He visited the sick simply for the sake of Allah, and narrated that no believer visits another except that seventy thousand angels send blessings upon him. Even in old age, when his companions travelled with him, they remembered him standing to pray and pouring out the most beautiful supplications.

He died at sixty-three, the same age as the Prophet ﷺ. As he was passing, in the lap of his wife, he asked her not to wail or tear her clothes, gently keeping even his own death within what the Prophet ﷺ would have loved. Imam adh-Dhahabi wrote that Abu Musa fasted much, prayed much, carried a pure heart that held no ill will, and that the world never deceived him.

What Abu Musa's life teaches us

His greatness was not in his size or his office. It was in a voice given over to something higher, and in a heart that stayed soft.

  • Beauty is meant to be given back. Abu Musa was gifted a rare voice, and he spent it on the Quran, for Allah, not for the praise of people. Whatever we are given is worth offering back to the One who gave it.
  • Keep teaching what you know. He was a great companion, yet after every noon prayer he asked who wanted to learn. No one is too important to sit and teach another person, patiently, one word at a time.
  • Renew the bond before it slips. He was warned that the Quran escapes a person like an untied camel. The things we love most, faith among them, need returning to, again and again, or they quietly drift away.
  • Let what is good keep your heart soft. Abu Bakr wept and remembered when hearts were soft. A life close to the Quran kept Abu Musa tender, even through power, war, and old age.

Questions

Who was Abu Musa al-Ash'ari?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ whose real name was Abdullah ibn Qays. He came from Yemen as a young man, was renowned for the beauty of his Quran recitation, and became a jurist, a judge, and a governor under Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him).

Why is Abu Musa remembered for his recitation?

The Prophet ﷺ loved to listen to him and said he had been given one of the instruments of the family of David, comparing his voice to that of Prophet David. Many companions said his recitation was sweeter than any sound they had ever heard.

What does the reward of two migrations mean?

Abu Musa and his tribe migrated for their faith twice: first their ship drifted to Abyssinia, where earlier Muslims had taken refuge, and then they travelled on to Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ told them they had the reward of both migrations.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Musa?

To give our gifts back to Allah sincerely, to keep teaching what we know, to keep returning to the Quran so it does not slip away, and to let what is good keep our hearts soft.

Why this story stays with us

It would have been easy for a man with such a gift to perform for others, to let the praise become the point. Abu Musa did the opposite. He recited for Allah alone, and the Prophet ﷺ himself testified that he was not showing off. Then he turned around and gave it away, asking strangers each afternoon whether they wanted to learn.

Most of us have something small and good we could share, and most of us tell ourselves we are too busy or too ordinary. His life answers that quietly. Offer your gift back to the One who gave it, keep your heart soft by returning to what is beautiful, and teach the little you know to someone who has less. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
