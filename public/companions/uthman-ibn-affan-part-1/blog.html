Uthman ibn Affan

The Possessor of Two Lights

He was one of the richest men in Makkah, one of the most handsome, one of the most loved. He had more to lose than almost anyone. When the message of Islam reached him, he did not hesitate.

This is the story of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him): the fourth man to believe, a merchant known for his modesty, and the only person ever to marry two daughters of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

The Companions

Uthman ibn Affan

The Possessor of Two Lights

A man who already had everything

Uthman ibn Affan was born in Taif, about six years after the Prophet ﷺ, into the powerful clan of Banu Umayyah. His lineage met the Prophet's at their shared ancestor Abd Manaf. Through his grandmother, the full sister of the Prophet's own father, he was also a second cousin of the Prophet ﷺ.

His father died in the days before Islam, leaving him a large fortune. He grew it himself into something larger, trading along the great routes to Syria, Yemen, and Abyssinia. By the time the message came, he was one of the wealthiest merchants Makkah had known.

When the people described his face, they sounded like they were describing the Prophet ﷺ. He was neither short nor tall, broad in the shoulder, with a thick beard and a striking, gentle face. It was said that if your gaze landed on him, you would not want to look away.

He spoke eloquently and well. He had travelled widely as a merchant, knew the lineages and the history of the Arabs, and had seen the customs of distant lands. And whenever he returned from a journey, the people of Makkah felt his generosity. He never came back from a trade route without the city tasting some of his giving.

Modesty in a place that did not reward it

Everything around Uthman should have made him proud. He was richer than the men of his clan, more handsome, more admired. Yet the quality he was known for was the opposite of pride. He was known for his hayah: his modesty, his shyness, his sense of restraint.

Long before Islam, he kept himself apart from what his people took for granted. He never bowed to an idol. He never drank wine, saying it destroys the mind, and the mind is a gift from God. He refused the parties and the songs of Quraysh, and said he had no interest in any of it.

Later the Prophet ﷺ would say that every religion has a defining quality, and the quality of Islam is modesty. Much of what is said about Uthman could be said about the Prophet ﷺ himself: the same restraint, the same bashfulness, the same turning away from anything shameful.

Because of this, the people loved him across every line that usually divides people. The poor respected him, the rich respected him, those with a great tribe and those with none. A mother in Makkah would tell her child the highest words of love she knew: I love you the way Quraysh loves Uthman.

The man with the most to lose

Usually it is the person with the most to lose who hesitates. Uthman was different. He was returning from a trade journey to Syria when, the narrations say, he dreamed a voice calling out that a man named Ahmad had appeared in Makkah. When he reached home, he heard that the Prophet ﷺ had begun to call people to Islam.

It was Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) who came to him, as he had come to so many of the early believers. The two were of the same world: noble men who had never worshipped idols, never drunk wine, never followed the crowd. Abu Bakr knew how to speak to a man like Uthman.

Uthman went straight to the Prophet ﷺ and accepted Islam. He was, by these reports, the fourth man to believe, after Abu Bakr, Ali, and Zayd ibn Harithah. He knew exactly what it could cost him. He knew a man could go from the most loved to the most hated overnight. It did not slow him down.

The most blessed couple

When Abu Lahab turned against the Prophet ﷺ, he ordered his two sons to divorce the Prophet's daughters, Ruqayyah and Umm Kulthum, before the marriages were ever consummated. It was a wound meant to humiliate. Then Uthman came to propose to Ruqayyah, and there could not have been a kinder replacement.

The Prophet ﷺ said to his daughter on her marriage: take good care of Abu Abdullah, for he is the closest of my companions to me in character. They became, by every account, the most loved couple of their time, gentle with one another, and Uthman brought into his home the same goodness the people admired in him outside it.

When persecution came, the powerful believers were often tortured quietly by their own clans, who wanted to save face while still breaking their will. Uthman was seized by his own relative and pressed again and again to leave Islam. He answered that he would never give up his religion and never part from Muhammad ﷺ. In the end the torture stopped, because it was clear he would not bend.

His danger to Quraysh was never the danger of a warrior. It was how loved he was. The child who grew up hearing I love you the way Quraysh loves Uthman would one day learn that Uthman had become a Muslim. A man that admired, choosing this path, could draw others after him.

First to migrate with his family

Uthman had been to Abyssinia before, on trade. He knew its land, its people, and its customs. When some of the believers set out to migrate there for safety, he and Ruqayyah went among them. By one narration, they were the first family to make that migration for the sake of Allah after the Prophet ﷺ himself.

There was trust in this. The Prophet ﷺ was sending his own daughter into the journey at Uthman's side. When the believers later moved to Madinah, Uthman moved too, now with Ruqayyah and their young son Abdullah.

In Madinah his life in Islam truly unfolds. He bought a home, returned to the markets, and built up his trade again. He was known for his honesty in business, and people wanted to deal with him. The slower, honest road made him a man others trusted, and he flourished on it.

The one who ran when he was asked

Whenever the Prophet ﷺ made a call and attached to it the reward of Paradise, Uthman ran forward. The wells of Madinah were owned by others, and the believers who had migrated struggled for water. One well, the well of Ruma, was sold to them at a steep price. The Prophet ﷺ said that whoever bought it would have Paradise.

Uthman negotiated it patiently, then made his days at the well free for everyone. The owner's high price collapsed, and in the end Uthman bought the whole well and turned it into a gift for the Muslims. That endowment still gives today, watering gardens and funding charity centuries later.

Then came Tabuk, a hard and costly expedition. The Prophet ﷺ asked who would equip the army. Uthman stood and gave a hundred camels. The Prophet ﷺ asked again, and he gave a hundred more, and again, until he had given around nine hundred camels and sixty horses, then brought gold and poured it into the Prophet's lap. The Prophet ﷺ said: nothing can harm Uthman after what he has done today.

He also bought land to expand the mosque the moment the Prophet ﷺ asked for it. And from the day he heard that freeing a person from slavery is a protection from the Fire, every Friday for years he spent his wealth to buy back people's freedom. So many were freed this way that no one could count them all.

It is worth noticing that Uthman was not a man of the battlefield. He served the Prophet ﷺ in a different way, with his trade, his wealth, and his speed to respond. Not everyone is asked to give in the same manner, and his life is a quiet reminder that there is more than one way to be of use.

The possessor of two lights

When the Prophet ﷺ set out for Badr, Ruqayyah was gravely ill. Uthman, who loved his wife dearly, was given permission to stay and care for her. He watched her die before his eyes. Badr became both the day of great joy and the day of great sorrow: victory came, and on the same day the Prophet's daughter returned to her Lord. The Prophet ﷺ counted Uthman among the people of Badr, with all their reward, for his intention was true.

Grief did not leave him. His little son Abdullah died soon after. When Uthman told the Prophet ﷺ that, beyond losing Ruqayyah, he grieved the closeness to him that her death had severed, the answer was a mercy granted to no one else. The angel Jibril came with word to marry Uthman to the Prophet's next daughter, Umm Kulthum, with the same dowry and the same kindness he had shown before.

This is why he is called Dhun-Nurayn, the possessor of two lights. No one else was given the honour of marrying two daughters of the Prophet ﷺ. When Umm Kulthum too passed away in their lifetimes, the Prophet ﷺ said to him: by Allah, Uthman, if we had a third daughter, we would marry her to you as well.

What Uthman's life teaches us

His story is not one of the battlefield. It is the quieter, harder work of character kept private and generosity given the moment it was asked for.

  • Modesty is strength, not weakness. He had wealth, beauty, and standing, and was known above all for his shyness before God. The more a believer has, the more telling that restraint becomes.
  • Give before you can talk yourself out of it. When the Prophet ﷺ asked, Uthman stood up at once. Opportunities to do good are often a small window, and he kept stepping through it.
  • Generosity outlives the giver. A well he bought is still giving water and charity today. What we spend for Allah can keep working long after we are gone.
  • Bring your best self home. The kindness people admired in Uthman outside was even fuller inside his own house, with the people who depended on him.

Questions

Who was Uthman ibn Affan?

A wealthy and respected merchant of Makkah from the clan of Banu Umayyah, and a second cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He was the fourth man to accept Islam and later one of the rightly guided successors.

Why is Uthman called the possessor of two lights?

He married two daughters of the Prophet ﷺ, Ruqayyah and then Umm Kulthum. No one else was given that honour, which earned him the title Dhun-Nurayn, the possessor of two lights.

What is the well of Ruma?

A well in Madinah that was sold to the thirsty believers at a high price. Uthman bought it and made it a free endowment for the Muslims. That charity is said to continue benefiting people to this day.

What can we learn from the life of Uthman?

That modesty can sit alongside great wealth, that generosity given quickly and without counting matters, and that good done for Allah can keep giving long after we are gone.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to assume that the people with the most to lose will always hold back, and that good character is something we can afford only once we are comfortable. Uthman's life says the opposite. He was modest while he was rich, generous before he was asked twice, and gentle at home as well as in public.

Most of us are not asked for nine hundred camels. We are asked for a smaller thing, in a smaller moment, with a small window to say yes before the feeling passes. His life is an invitation to keep stepping through that window: to give a little before we reconsider, to stay soft where we could be proud, and to let what we do for Allah quietly outlast us.
