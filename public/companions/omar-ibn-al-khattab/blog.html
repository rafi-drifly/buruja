The Companions

Umar ibn al-Khattab

The Shepherd of the Ummah

He commanded almost every major city in the known world. He had no money of his own, fourteen patches in his garment, and a stomach that growled because he refused to eat what his people could not.

This is the story of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him): the first person in history to be called Commander of the Believers, and a ruler who never once felt he had arrived.

The Companions

Umar ibn al-Khattab

The Shepherd of the Ummah

The leader who never felt worthy

When Abu Bakr (RA) appointed him, Umar took on a weight that never lifted. He was the first person to carry the title Amir al-Muminin, the Commander of the Believers. From the first day to the last breath, he never felt he belonged in the position.

In his first sermon he climbed toward the place where the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ used to stand and stopped. He would not stand there. He stepped down to where Abu Bakr had stood, and would not stand there either. He went down to the lowest step and spoke to the people from there.

From that low step he made his prayer: O Allah, I am weak, so make me strong. O Allah, I am harsh, so make me gentle. O Allah, I can be stingy, so make me generous. It was the way he lowered himself, they said, that made Allah raise him.

Afraid of being asked

He once asked Salman al-Farisi (RA) whether he was a king or a caliph. Salman told him: if you take even a coin from the Muslims that is not yours, you are a king; if you guard their rights and never cheat them, you are a caliph. Umar wept and sought refuge in Allah.

His fear reached even the animals. He put his hand on an exhausted camel one day and said he was afraid Allah would question him about it on the Day of Judgment. He swore that if a sheep died on the bank of the Euphrates, he feared he would be asked why.

The verse he was heard reciting most often spoke of the one who fears standing before his Lord. He carried that standing with him everywhere. By day he walked the markets seeing to people's needs. By night he walked the streets caring for them.

He asked the people, and he listened

The Prophet ﷺ had said Umar was among those guided toward the truth, and the Quran had agreed with him more than once. A man with that record could have stopped consulting anyone. Instead his rule was built on consultation.

He gathered the first Muslims, the people of Badr, and those who lived by the Quran. Then he turned to the young. When he faced a hard matter he would call the youth around him and ask their advice, because he saw a sharpness and clarity in their thinking.

He held the family of the Prophet ﷺ especially dear. When a man brought a complaint against Ali (RA), Umar sat them both down as equals and judged in the other man's favour, the caliph himself answerable in his own court. He once said he could not bear to live in a land where Ali was not.

He also turned good ideas into lasting systems. When he called on the Muslims to give their gardens and lands for the sake of Allah, the endowments that care for travellers, the sick, the orphans, and the widows grew from a small practice into something the whole community could lean on. He gave the first of his own gardens, and others followed.

He would not eat what they could not eat

In the year of famine known as the Year of Ashes, the people feared Umar would die not from hunger but from worry over them. He took an oath to touch no milk, butter, or meat until ordinary people could afford them again. His body changed colour from what he denied himself.

When a man gave him a gift of yogurt, he gave it away in charity, saying he did not like to eat what the Muslims could not. When fruit was brought to him he would pull the grapes apart one by one and hand them out to the people, without tasting a single one. He found his son with a slice of watermelon and chased him through the house to take it, until his wife said the boy had bought it with his own money.

His own stomach once growled while he sat at home. He spoke to it plainly: rumble or do not rumble, but by Allah you will not be full until the children of the Muslims can eat. He said it himself: how can I be a shepherd of this ummah if I am not touched by what touches my flock?

The man who walked the streets at night

He became known for his night patrols. He had once followed Abu Bakr (RA) and found the caliph quietly serving an old woman, and it never left him. Years later a man followed Umar to a house on the edge of Madinah and found a blind, disabled woman inside. She did not know who her visitor was. She said he was simply a man who liked to do good: he cooked for her, cleaned her home, and left. He had been coming for years, the most powerful man in the world, and never once told her his name.

One night he found a woman alone in a tent, crying out in the pain of labour, her husband sitting helpless outside. Umar went home and woke his wife, the granddaughter of the Prophet ﷺ, calling it a chance at reward. The two of them returned, she to help the woman, he to light a fire and cook, until a child was safely born and the startled father realised who had served him.

He did not only comfort people on those walks; he learned from them. He heard a hungry child crying and a mother explaining that he paid a smaller stipend to nursing infants than to weaned ones, so she was starving her baby to wean him sooner. The next morning he wept so hard in the dawn prayer that no one could follow his recitation, and he changed the policy that very day.

He overheard a lonely wife grieving a husband long away at war, and quietly set a limit so no soldier would be kept from home too long. He found an old Jewish man begging because the protection tax had grown too heavy in his age, took him home, cared for him, and ordered that the elderly among the People of the Book never be left to beg: we took from you in your youth, he said, and failed you in your old age.

A door between the ummah and trial

Umar once asked who remembered what the Prophet ﷺ had said about the great trials to come. He was told there was a closed door between him and that turmoil, and that the door would be broken, not opened. He understood at once: he was the door, and when it broke it would not close again. He buried his face in his hands and wept.

He had two steady prayers in his final years. The first: O Allah, grant me martyrdom in Your cause, and let me die in the city of Your Prophet ﷺ. His daughter wondered how a man could be martyred in peaceful Madinah, where no battles were fought. He simply kept asking, year after year, certain that if Allah willed it, it would happen.

His second prayer was that he would not be killed by anyone who had ever prostrated to Allah. Three things that seemed impossible at once: a martyr's death, in Madinah, at the hand of someone who did not pray. In the end, each one would be answered exactly as he had asked.

The way he died

A man he had judged fairly, a captive craftsman, had quietly promised to kill him. Umar had heard the threat and chosen not to act on a suspicion. In the darkness of the Fajr prayer, leading the people and reciting Surah Yusuf with tears, he was stabbed again and again. His first words were from the Quran: the decree of Allah is a destiny fulfilled.

When he woke from unconsciousness after sunrise, his first question was whether the people had prayed. Told yes, he praised Allah and said there is no share of Islam for the one who abandons the prayer. He called for water, made wudu, and finished the Fajr he had been struck down in, before he would speak of anything else.

Then he turned to his affairs. He owed money, though he had almost none, and he asked his son to pay every debt, from his own little wealth first and from the family after. He sent that son to Aisha (RA) to ask, with no pressure at all, for a grave beside the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr, and to send greetings not from the Commander of the Believers but simply from Umar, since he no longer held the office.

When men came to praise him, he asked only whether they would testify to it before Allah, for he was afraid of being asked about his people. In his last moments he asked his son to lay his face in the dirt, so that when Allah looked upon him He might find a humble servant and show mercy. He was sixty-three. A man who had once set out to harm the Prophet ﷺ was buried at his side.

What Umar's life teaches us

His story is not mainly about conquest or power. It is about a man who held authority and was terrified of misusing it, and who measured himself by the smallest person in his care.

  • Real authority serves. He saw himself as a shepherd answerable for the flock, down to a sheep on a riverbank, and he carried that responsibility as a weight, not a privilege.
  • Stay close to the people you serve. He walked the streets at night, refused to eat what others could not, and changed his own decisions the moment he heard how they hurt someone.
  • Listen, even from the top. The Quran had agreed with him, yet he asked the first Muslims, the people of the Quran, and the young, and treated being corrected as a gift.
  • Humility is where strength comes from. From the lowest step of the pulpit to his face in the dirt at the end, he never felt he had arrived, and that is exactly why he was trusted.

Questions

Who was Umar ibn al-Khattab?

A close companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the second caliph of Islam. He was the first person to be called Amir al-Muminin, the Commander of the Believers, and led the community through a decade in which Islam spread across much of the known world.

Why is Umar remembered as a model of just leadership?

He held himself accountable as a shepherd responsible for everyone in his care. He patrolled at night to see people's needs himself, refused comforts his people lacked, and changed his own policies whenever he found they caused hardship.

How did Umar ibn al-Khattab die?

He was assassinated while leading the Fajr prayer in Madinah, stabbed by a captive craftsman. He lived briefly after, made wudu and completed the prayer, settled his affairs with care, and was buried beside the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr (RA).

What can we learn from the life of Umar?

That authority is a trust to be feared, that the powerful should stay close to the people they serve, and that lasting strength grows out of humility rather than entitlement.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never govern a city, but almost all of us are responsible for someone: a family, a team, a class, a customer, a parent who needs us. Umar's life asks one quiet question of all of it. Are we touched by what touches the people in our care, or have we drifted comfortably above them?

He kept himself low on purpose, down a step on the pulpit, down in the dirt at the end, because he believed that was where mercy is found. We can borrow a little of that today: to listen before we decide, to carry our own burden, and to fear being careless with the people who depend on us. Carry one of those into your day, and his story has done its work.
