Saad ibn Abi Waqqas

The Archer Whose Prayers Were Answered

He was a teenager who made bows and arrows for a living, and he had a gift for hitting what he aimed at. When he became Muslim, the first person to try to stop him was not an enemy in the street. It was his own mother, and she chose a weapon he could not fight back against: she stopped eating.

This is the story of Saad ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the first to believe, the first to shoot an arrow in defence of the faith, and a man whose prayers Allah promised to answer.

The Companions

Saad ibn Abi Waqqas

The Archer Whose Prayers Were Answered

The young archer of Makkah

Saad ibn Abi Waqqas came from Banu Zuhra, the tribe of the Prophet Muhammad's mother, Amina bint Wahb. Through his grandfather he was a maternal relative of the Prophet ﷺ, who would look at him and say, "This is my maternal uncle. Let any one of you show me an uncle like this." The word uncle here is only a way of naming the kinship. Saad was in fact about twenty years younger than the Prophet ﷺ, and only a teenager when revelation began.

His father had already died. Saad made his living crafting bows and arrows, and he was more than a craftsman: he was a gifted archer himself, known for his sharp eye and his skill. People described him as short, dark, and strong, with so much hair that they likened him to a lion.

He was honest, and he was not among the powerful of Makkah, though his tribe was not a lowly one. He was simply a young man with a trade, a widowed mother he lived with, and a quiet seriousness about him. His kunya was Abu Ishaq.

A dream, and a moon to follow

Like several of the earliest Muslims, Saad saw something before he ever reached the Prophet ﷺ. He later described a dream to his daughter. He found himself standing in a wide, pitch-dark plain where he could see nothing at all.

Then a full moon appeared, and he followed it. As he walked, he looked to see who had reached the moon before him, and there in its light he saw three men: Ali ibn Abi Talib, Zayd ibn Harithah, and Abu Bakr as-Siddiq. He asked them when they had arrived. "Just now," they said.

When he woke, he heard that the Prophet ﷺ was quietly calling people to Islam. He went straight to the streets of Ajyad, found Abu Bakr there, and Abu Bakr called him to the faith. Saad accepted at once. He used to say that no one became Muslim before the day he did, and that for seven days he was one of only a handful of believers on earth.

The mother who would not eat

Saad went home to tell his mother he had become Muslim. She fell into a rage and demanded to know what religion had taken him from the way of his mother and father. He would not back down, and she saw she could not argue him out of it. So she chose a different weapon, and in some ways a crueller one.

She did not call anyone to beat him or torture him, as happened to others. She turned the pain on herself. She told him she would not eat, would not drink, would not take shelter, and would not even comb her hair until he left his faith. She sat outside and let herself grow weak, and she warned him that if she died, his heart would be broken and people would blame him forever.

Saad loved his mother, and watching her starve herself caused him real pain. He kept coming to her with food and water, pleading, "O my mother, do not do this." She kept refusing. At last he said the words that settled it: that as much as he loved her, his love for Allah and His Messenger ﷺ was stronger. "Even if you had a hundred souls," he told her, "and they left you one by one, I would not leave my religion." Seeing it was hopeless, she finally gave up.

What is remarkable is how Allah answered this. He did not send down a verse condemning the mother. Instead He sent words about honouring her:

We have commanded people to be good to their parents: their mothers carried them, with strain upon strain, and it takes two years to wean them. Give thanks to Me and to your parents- all will return to Me. If they strive to make you associate with Me anything about which you have no knowledge, then do not obey them. Yet keep their company in this life according to what is right.Quran 31:14-15

The first arrow in defence of the faith

In those early days the Muslims could not pray openly near the Kaaba, so they would slip into the valleys and quiet corners of Makkah to pray where they would not be seen. Saad was with a small group when they were attacked, and he was the first to strike back and draw blood in defence of the Prophet ﷺ. He became the first person to shoot an arrow in the path of Islam.

From then on he was the archer at the Prophet's side. He made the migration to Madinah and was present at every battle. At Badr he fought bravely, and he watched his younger brother Umayr ibn Abi Waqqas fall as one of the few martyrs of that day. Umayr was so young that the Prophet ﷺ had first tried to send him home, until the boy wept and begged to stay.

At Uhud, when many were scattered, Saad was one of those who held his ground around the Prophet ﷺ. He ran beside him, shooting arrow after arrow with that same precise aim, while others guarded with swords. The companions gathered up fallen arrows to hand to him, and the Prophet ﷺ himself was picking up arrows and passing them to Saad.

Two gifts from the Prophet ﷺ

On that day at Uhud the Prophet ﷺ said something to Saad he is not known to have said to anyone else. As Saad shot, he told him, "Throw, O Saad. May my mother and father be sacrificed for you." Companions would say such words to the Prophet ﷺ; for him to say them to Saad was its own honour. Ali later said the Prophet ﷺ never joined his mother and father in a phrase like that for anyone except Saad.

The Prophet ﷺ gave him a second gift as well. He prayed, "O Allah, answer Saad when he calls upon You." From then on people knew that Saad's prayers were answered, and they were careful never to give him reason to pray against them.

Years later, when he was a governor and a man wronged him with lies, Saad made wudu, prayed, and quietly asked Allah, and the man lost his sight and his ease. When someone openly cursed Ali in front of him, Saad warned him to stop and then prayed, and the man met a strange and sudden end. He never used this for small things. He used it only when truth itself was being attacked.

A third is enough

During the conquest of Makkah, Saad fell so ill he thought he would die. He was still under forty, with great wealth and only one daughter to inherit it. He was known for his generosity, and he wanted to give most of it away. The Prophet ﷺ came to visit him.

"O Messenger of Allah," Saad asked, "may I give away two thirds of my wealth in charity?" No. "Then half?" No. "Then a third?" The Prophet ﷺ answered, "A third, and a third is much." Then he taught him the principle behind it: "It is better to leave your heirs wealthy than to leave them poor, begging from people." And he added that even the morsel a man places in his wife's mouth, seeking Allah's pleasure, is rewarded.

From one man's sickbed came a ruling Muslims still follow: that no more than a third of an estate is given away by will, so that families are provided for and not left in need. Saad did not die. The Prophet ﷺ prayed, "O Allah, cure Saad," and Allah cured him, and he went on to live one of the longest and fullest lives of any companion.

The man who wanted no part of the fight

Saad lived to lead the Muslim armies against the Persian Empire, then the largest power in the world. At the battle of Qadisiyyah, when the Persians sent armoured elephants against him, it was Saad who ordered the archers to aim for their eyes. Behind him in that campaign sat Salman al-Farsi, a Persian who had once been chained by his own father for leaving his religion, just as Saad had once been pressed by his own mother. Two men, hurt in similar ways in far corners of the earth, entering the palace of the emperor side by side.

Umar later placed Saad on the small council that would choose the next leader. He was qualified to be chosen himself. But when the years of internal strife came, Saad wanted none of it. He narrated the Prophet's warnings: that to fight a Muslim is disbelief and to insult one is corruption, and that in times of discord, the one who sits is better than the one who stands.

So he withdrew. He took his sheep into the hills, away from the people and away from the fighting. When one of his sons came urging him to claim power, Saad said he sought refuge in Allah from what his son was bringing. Then he gave him the answer he lived by: that Allah loves the servant who is God-fearing, self-sufficient, and unseen. He was the last of the ten promised Paradise to die, having chosen, at the end, to be quiet, content, and close to his Lord.

What Saad's life teaches us

His story runs from a teenager's hardest choice to an old man's quiet retreat, and the same heart holds both ends together.

  • Love for Allah can cost us, even at home. Saad's test was not a sword but his own mother's suffering. He honoured her and refused to obey her in wrong, exactly as the Quran taught him to.
  • Be good to your parents, even when they are wrong. Allah did not let Saad curse or abandon his mother. The command was to disagree without disobeying God, and to keep her company with kindness.
  • An answered prayer is a trust, not a tool. Saad's prayers were known to be answered, yet he held them back for years and used them only when truth was attacked, never for himself.
  • Sometimes the brave choice is to step back. He had fought every battle, yet when fighting his own brothers was the question, he chose his sheep, his family, and obscurity over power.

Questions

Who was Saad ibn Abi Waqqas?

A young archer of Makkah and a maternal relative of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He was among the first people to accept Islam, the first to shoot an arrow in its defence, one of the ten companions given glad tidings of Paradise, and the last of those ten to die.

Why are Saad's prayers said to be answered?

At the battle of Uhud the Prophet ﷺ prayed, "O Allah, answer Saad when he calls upon You." After that his prayers were known to be answered, and people were careful never to wrong him. He used this only in serious matters, never for small things.

What happened between Saad and his mother?

When he became Muslim, his mother refused to eat, drink, or shelter herself to pressure him to leave his faith. He pleaded with her and brought her food, but he would not give up his religion. About this, Allah revealed verses in Surah Luqman commanding kindness to parents while not obeying them in disbelief.

What can we learn from the life of Saad ibn Abi Waqqas?

That faith may be tested most by the people we love, that we honour our parents even when we cannot obey them, that a gift from Allah is a trust to be used carefully, and that stepping away from conflict can be its own kind of courage.

Why this story stays with us

Saad's hardest moment was not a battlefield. It was a doorway, with his mother growing weaker on the other side of it, asking him to choose. He held two things at once that day: a love for Allah he would not surrender, and a love for his mother he would not abandon. He kept bringing her food even as he refused her demand. That is a quiet kind of strength, and most of us will need it.

Our tests usually look like his later years too: not glory, but the pull to prove ourselves, to win an argument, to be seen. Saad answered it by becoming God-fearing, self-sufficient, and unseen. There is room in an ordinary life to want that: to do good without needing credit, to be kind to difficult people, to stay close to Allah when no one is watching. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
