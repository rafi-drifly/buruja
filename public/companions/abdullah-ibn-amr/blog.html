The Companions

Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As

The One Who Preserved the Sunnah

His grandfather had mocked the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ to his face, saying that when this man died, no one would remember him, because he had no sons. A boy was named after that grandfather. That same boy grew up to write down the words of the Prophet ﷺ by the hundreds, and to carry them through his whole life in a locked box.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him): a son of the Prophet's enemy who became one of the four young men who quietly preserved the religion for everyone who came after.

The Companions

Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As

The One Who Preserved the Sunnah

Born inside the house that fought the Prophet

Of the great companions named Abdullah who documented the faith, he is the only one raised in a home that stood against it. His father was Amr ibn al-As, for years a determined enemy of the Prophet ﷺ before Islam reached his heart. Amr would later open Egypt and much of north Africa, but in the early years he was a thorn in the side of the young community in Makkah.

His grandfather was al-As ibn Wa'il, named by some scholars as the most condemned man in the Quran. It was al-As who looked at the Prophet ﷺ, who had buried his sons, and said: leave him, when he dies no one will remember him, for he is cut off. Verse after verse came down answering that cruelty.

So the boy grew up surrounded by people fighting the very message he would one day spend his life protecting. The Prophet ﷺ had always taught his companions to keep hope in children, that a father might be lost to faith while his child is guided. Here that hope was answered in full.

A name changed, a heart already leaning

His birth name was not Abdullah. He was named al-As, after his grandfather, a name that carries the meaning of one who is disobedient and stubborn. No two people could have been further apart: the grandfather who belied the Prophet ﷺ, and the grandson who would be known for his obedience and his gentleness.

He came to the Prophet ﷺ quietly and accepted Islam, by the well known reports, a full year before his own father and mother. The Prophet ﷺ gave him a new name, Abdullah, the servant of Allah, one of the names most beloved to Allah. And he praised the household: what a blessed family, the father Amr, the mother, and Abdullah.

He was barely younger than his father, raised more as a companion at his side than a small child trailing behind him. He came into Islam already a grown man, intelligent and proud, the natural heir of his tribe. And he carried something heavy with that: a lifetime of being told he was better than everyone else.

The young man trying to catch up

When the Prophet ﷺ first saw him wearing two rich, dyed garments, he asked him gently whether his mother had told him to dress this way, and told him those were not clothes for a young man of this faith. Abdullah had a great deal to unlearn: the pride of his tribe, the comfort he was raised in, the idea that lineage made a person worth more.

He set himself to catch up with a kind of fear. He looked at the companions his age and felt how far behind he was, so he memorized the whole Quran in his first year, then sat at the feet of the most knowledgeable companions to gather everything he had missed. He learned the Quran from Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, whom he said he loved and would never stop loving.

But his zeal ran past what his body could carry. He prayed through whole nights and fasted every single day. On his wedding night, the night meant for two short units of prayer, he recited the entire Quran. He was the man who throws himself into the religion all at once, certain that anything less might not be enough.

Take it gently, the Prophet told him

His wife quietly mentioned to his father that he was a wonderful worshipper, which was her kind way of saying he was barely a husband at all. Word reached the Prophet ﷺ, who called him in. Is it true, he asked, that you fast every day and pray every night? Yes, messenger of Allah. Do not do that, he said.

Then came the counsel of a father to a son. Your body has a right over you. Your eyes have a right over you. Your wife has a right over you. Your guest has a right over you. Fast and break your fast. Pray and sleep. He brought Abdullah down, step by step, until he settled on the fast of Dawud, one day fasting and one day not, and on finishing the Quran no faster than once in three days.

Later in life Abdullah would say, with real regret, that he wished he had accepted the lighter way the Prophet ﷺ offered him, because the years made it hard to keep that early fire alive. The faith is gentle, he learned, and the one who travels it harshly does not reach the end. The Prophet ﷺ had also taught him where true hope is found:

Say, '[God says], My servants who have harmed yourselves by your own excess, do not despair of God's mercy. God forgives all sins: He is truly the Most Forgiving, the Most Merciful.Quran 39:53

The Truthful Tablet

Abdullah said to the Prophet ﷺ that he heard things from him he longed to keep, and asked whether he could write them down, not only hold them in his heart but support his memory with his hand. The Prophet ﷺ permitted it, pointing to his mouth and saying that nothing came from it except the truth.

So while others remembered, Abdullah wrote. He filled a scroll with more than seven hundred sayings he heard directly from the Prophet ﷺ, with no one in between. He called it as-Sahifah as-Sadiqah, the Truthful Tablet. He kept it in a locked box, and when people asked him a question, he would unlock it, take out the scroll, and read them the answer in the Prophet's own words.

When people once debated which city would be opened first, Constantinople or Rome, he did not argue. He unlocked the box, drew out the scroll, and read them the day he had sat with the Prophet ﷺ and heard the answer with his own ears. He guarded those pages so carefully that he refused to write in them anything he had not heard from the Prophet ﷺ directly, narrating the warning that whoever attributed to the Prophet ﷺ words he did not say had taken his seat in the Fire.

He said that if all he had left in this world were the Book of Allah, this scroll, and one garden he loved, he would not grieve over anything else he lost. Those pages were absorbed into the great collections of hadith that the ummah still relies on today. The boy whose grandfather said no one would be remembered became one of the main reasons the Prophet's words were never forgotten.

The man with the clean heart

Three times the Prophet ﷺ told the companions that a man of Paradise was about to walk in, and three times the same ordinary man entered, his beard still wet from wudu, his sandals in his hand. Abdullah could not understand it, so he asked to stay in the man's home for a few days to watch what he did.

He saw nothing remarkable. The man did not fast every day or pray through the night. Finally Abdullah asked him directly what his secret was. The man thought, then said: every night, before I sleep, I empty my heart of any malice toward any Muslim. I forgive everyone. I envy no one for what Allah has given them.

Here was the lesson Abdullah had been chasing in all the wrong places. It was not in exhausting the body. It was in a heart washed clean each night of resentment and envy. He spent the rest of his life learning that the religion lives inside a person, not only in the count of their deeds.

Caught in a storm he hated

Abdullah was a brave warrior who stood in battle after battle beside his father, and a commander who recorded the conquests even as he fought in them. He loved knowledge and he loved peace, and more than anything he came to hate fitna, the discord that turns believers against one another.

When two Muslim armies finally met, he wanted no part of it. But the Prophet ﷺ had once told him to obey his father as long as he lived, and his father went out with one side. So Abdullah went out too, and on that day, he said, he never threw an arrow or raised his sword. He sat with his hands down and refused to fight the Muslims, and afterward he wept that he had been there at all.

The wound of it lingered. He had been close to al-Husayn, the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, and al-Husayn would not speak to him for years after seeing him on that field. Abdullah longed for his forgiveness, greeting him in gatherings and waiting, until at last, through a man who reconciled them, the two embraced. He had learned how real a hurt between good people can be, and how patiently it must be healed.

He spent his final years far from the disputes of the Muslims, teaching the words he had preserved. He wished, he said, that he had died long before he was ever drawn into that strife. His scroll outlived him: passed to his son, and his grandson after him, until it reached the great collections the ummah still reads. A father teaching a son, an aunt teaching a nephew, is how this faith was quietly carried to us.

What the life of Abdullah ibn Amr teaches us

His story is the story of a man trying to catch up, and slowly learning that the race was never the point.

  • Travel the faith gently. He burned himself out trying to do everything at once, and spent his later years wishing he had accepted the lighter, steadier way the Prophet ﷺ offered him.
  • Guard the heart before the deeds. The man of Paradise was not the hardest worshipper. He was the one who forgave everyone and slept each night without envy or malice.
  • Small, faithful work outlasts you. He simply wrote down what he heard. Those quiet pages helped preserve the Prophet's words for everyone who came after.
  • Where you start does not decide where you end. Born into the household that fought the Prophet ﷺ, he became one of its most devoted servants. No upbringing is a sentence.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the son of Amr ibn al-As. He accepted Islam as a young man and became known for writing down hundreds of the Prophet's sayings, helping to preserve the Sunnah for later generations.

What was the Sahifah as-Sadiqah?

It was the scroll, whose name means the Truthful Tablet, in which Abdullah recorded more than seven hundred sayings he heard directly from the Prophet ﷺ. He kept it carefully throughout his life, and its contents passed into the major collections of hadith.

Why is he sometimes confused with Abdullah ibn Umar?

In Arabic their fathers' names look very similar in writing, so translators sometimes mix them up. He is the son of Amr ibn al-As, a distinct companion from Abdullah ibn Umar, the son of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

What can we learn from the life of Abdullah ibn Amr?

To carry faith gently rather than burn out, to keep the heart free of resentment, and to value the small, faithful work that quietly serves others long after we are gone.

Why this story stays with us

Many of us know his early mistake from the inside. We decide to change, and we try to change everything at once: every habit, every prayer, every flaw, all by next week. Then the fire fades, and we are left wondering why we could not keep it up. The Prophet ﷺ saw that pattern in a sincere young man and answered it with patience, not pressure. Take it gently. Give your body, your family, and your heart their rights. Build something you can carry for a lifetime.

And when you measure your faith, remember the man of Paradise. Before you count your deeds, look at your heart. Tonight, perhaps, you could do what he did: let go of one resentment, release one quiet envy, and try to sleep with nothing held against anyone. That is a small act, and it may be the heaviest thing on the scale.
