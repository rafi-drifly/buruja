The Companions

Ubaydullah ibn Abbas

The Generous Younger Brother

He was born into a family where one brother would shine so brightly that almost no one would remember the others. He could have spent his life resenting that. Instead he found a quieter role, and filled it so completely that the people of Madinah came to speak the two brothers' names in one breath.

This is the story of Ubaydullah ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him): the younger brother of Abdullah ibn Abbas, a merchant of Banu Hashim, and a man remembered for a single, steady quality. Generosity.

The Companions

Ubaydullah ibn Abbas

The Generous Younger Brother

The little Abdullah

Ubaydullah was born about a year after his brother Abdullah ibn Abbas, which placed his birth in the years around the boycott of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and his clan. His name carries the meaning of the younger one: the little Abdullah, born in the shadow of a brother who would become one of the great scholars of the ummah.

His father was Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ. So Ubaydullah grew up close to the household of the Prophet ﷺ, near the warmth at the centre of it. His brother Abdullah held a special place in that warmth. He was born during the boycott, in the time of hardship, and the Prophet ﷺ performed the tahnik for him with his own saliva, because in those hungry years there were no dates to use. In the middle of that darkness, the Prophet ﷺ found real joy in the children of his family.

There is a gentle scene preserved from Ubaydullah's childhood. The Prophet ﷺ would line up the young boys of the family, Abdullah, Ubaydullah, and their brother Kuthayyir, and then step back from them. He would say that whoever reached him first would be given something, and the children would race toward him and climb onto his chest and his back, and he ﷺ would hug them and kiss them. It is a small window into the family side of the Prophet ﷺ, and into a childhood spent inside that love. Ubaydullah was old enough to remember some of those moments for the rest of his life.

A father's trade, a son's gift

Al-Abbas was a wealthy man and a gifted merchant. He dealt in sweet scents and fine spices, and travellers who came to Makkah would go straight to him to buy. His wealth was so well known that when the Prophet ﷺ stood to give his farewell sermon and abolished interest, he named Al-Abbas by name: whatever interest was owed to Al-Abbas was now cancelled.

Part of the backstory of that wealth reaches back to the day of Badr. Al-Abbas was taken captive there, and like the other prisoners he had to ransom himself. He was concealing his Islam at the time, and he felt the loss of the ransom, but the Prophet ﷺ dealt with him fairly, as he dealt with all the prisoners. About the captives of that day, the Qur'an gave a promise:

Prophet, tell those you have taken captive, ‘If God knows of any good in your hearts, He will give you something better than what has been taken from you, and He will forgive you: God is forgiving and merciful.’Quran 8:70

The merchant of the family

Of the two famous brothers, the paths divided cleanly. Abdullah took the path of knowledge and held to it, and became the scholar everyone pointed to. Ubaydullah took after their father. He learned the trade, the ways of business, and became his father's successor as the merchant of the family.

Those who described him say he was vast in wealth and vast in generosity, a talented merchant and a noble man. The kind of person you could look at and know at once that he came from a noble house. He carried everything you would expect from the cream of Banu Hashim, only without the scholar's path. He took a different road, and he became known for what he gave.

If you wanted to picture what a true donor in a community should look like, you could point to him. He grew up watching his brother shine in knowledge and in preaching, and he had a choice. He could decide that his brother had taken all the good there was, or he could find his own way to serve. He chose to serve, and his wealth became a tool for it.

The bedouin and the goat

On one journey, Ubaydullah and his companions lost their way and ran out of food and water. In the middle of the desert they came upon a bedouin man who owned a single pregnant goat. Without knowing who his guests were, the man slaughtered his only goat and fed them. They pleaded with him not to, but he insisted, because hospitality was a thing he could not let go of.

When Ubaydullah returned to his wealth, he sent the bedouin five hundred dinars. Five hundred gold coins for one goat is an enormous sum. A servant said to him that five would have been generous, and five hundred was far too much.

His answer is the heart of the man. He said that the bedouin was more generous than they were. How, the servant asked. Because, he said, the bedouin gave away all of his world, and we only gave away a little of ours. It was never about the size of the gift. It was about how much of yourself the gift cost you.

There is a teaching of the Prophet ﷺ that one small gift can outweigh a hundred thousand. The one who gives away one out of two has emptied half of what he owns. The one who gives away a hundred thousand out of a vast fortune may not feel the loss at all. Ubaydullah understood this, and it kept him humble even as he gave. He measured his giving not against what others gave, but against what Allah had entrusted to him.

The man who fed his brother's students

Back in Madinah, Abdullah ibn Abbas was the pride of the family. Say the name Ibn Abbas, and people meant him. His house was a university. Students would line up outside the door from the early morning, waiting for the lesson of one of the greatest scholars the ummah would know.

Ubaydullah could have looked at all of that and felt small. He did not. He never carried the envy of the overlooked younger brother. He said, in effect, my brother is good at this, and I am good at something too. So he set out to sponsor every student his brother taught.

He would fill Abdullah's house with trays of bread and meat, so that everyone who came to learn was also fed. Picture it: you travel to Madinah to study with the great Abdullah ibn Abbas, and when you arrive, your teacher gives you knowledge and his younger brother gives you a full plate. A full scholarship, paid quietly from the side.

The people had a saying about the two of them: the most generous in knowledge was Abdullah, and the most generous in feeding the people was Ubaydullah. They said the two brothers had taken all the good of Madinah between them. Whoever wanted the food of this world went to the house of Ubaydullah, and whoever wanted the knowledge of the next went to the house of Abdullah.

The two brothers and the marketplace

His life put flesh on an old lesson. In the time of the Prophet ﷺ there were two brothers. One of them spent all his time learning beside the Prophet ﷺ, and the other had to work, doing business so he could provide for them both.

The working brother came to complain. It did not feel fair: his brother sat in the company of the Prophet ﷺ all day while he haggled in the market and carried the cost of both their lives. The Prophet ﷺ answered him gently. It may be, he said, that the only reason you are provided for at all is because you spend on your brother.

That was Ubaydullah's whole posture. He did not need to be as eloquent or as learned as Abdullah. He did not resent the brother who was. He saw his own role clearly, supported it with everything he had, and trusted that Allah was keeping the account that no one else could see.

It is a feeling many people know. You build the masjid, you fund the teaching, you carry the cost of work you never get to stand in the middle of, and a part of you wonders whether you are missing the sweetness others taste. The answer the Prophet ﷺ gave that working brother is the answer to that worry too. The provision itself may be the fruit of what you give, and every bit of it is being written down.

Governor, leader of Hajj, and a quiet end

Ubaydullah's life was not only generosity. He was appointed governor of Yemen by Ali ibn Abi Talib for a time, around the year 36 after the Hijra, and he was also made the leader of the Hajj for a year or two. Leading the pilgrims was a position of real honour, and it was something of a family tradition, for Abdullah too had a deep place in the rites of Hajj.

In the chains of hadith he is remembered for a single famous narration, on the rules of remarriage, which guards against treating marriage as a trick to be played. He also passes down, with his brother, three instructions of the Prophet ﷺ: to complete the ablution well, not to eat from the wealth of charity, and not to mate a donkey with a horse.

There is one more tender detail. The two brothers each named a son after the other. Abdullah named a son Ubaydullah, and Ubaydullah named a son Abdullah, so that in the chains of narration you find an Abdullah ibn Ubaydullah passing on the words of an Ubaydullah ibn Abdullah. Ubaydullah died in Madinah, by some reports as old as eighty-seven. A name many people never learn, honoured by the One who keeps every account.

What Ubaydullah's life teaches us

His story is not built on a single famous moment. It is built on a choice he made, again and again, about where to stand.

  • Generosity is measured by what it costs you. The bedouin gave one goat and outgave a man who could send five hundred dinars without feeling it. What counts is the share of yourself you let go of.
  • You do not have to envy another's gift. Ubaydullah's brother had the knowledge. Rather than resent it, he found his own role and poured himself into it. There is room for more than one kind of greatness.
  • Quiet support is its own contribution. He never taught a lesson, but he fed everyone who came to learn. The work that no one applauds is still being counted.
  • Keep giving at the level Allah gave you. When the doors of provision open, the measure is not what others give, but what we do with what we were entrusted.

Questions

Who was Ubaydullah ibn Abbas?

He was a companion and a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the younger brother of the great scholar Abdullah ibn Abbas and a son of Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib. He was a wealthy merchant of Banu Hashim, known above all for his generosity.

Why is Ubaydullah remembered for generosity?

Rather than envy his famous brother, he chose to sponsor all of Abdullah's students, filling the house of learning with food. The people of Madinah said the most generous in feeding them was Ubaydullah, and the most generous in knowledge was Abdullah.

What was the story of the bedouin and the goat?

When Ubaydullah's party was lost and out of provisions, a poor bedouin slaughtered his only goat to feed them. Ubaydullah later sent him five hundred dinars, saying the bedouin was the more generous of the two, because he had given away all of his world while they had given only a little of theirs.

What positions did Ubaydullah ibn Abbas hold?

He was appointed governor of Yemen by Ali ibn Abi Talib for a period around the year 36 after the Hijra, and he served as the leader of the Hajj for a year or two. He died in Madinah.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will live somewhere near a brighter light. There will be a sibling, a colleague, a friend who is more gifted, more praised, more remembered. Ubaydullah shows what it looks like to make peace with that, and then to turn it into something good. He did not try to become his brother. He fed his brother's students, and let that be his work.

There is a kind of giving that asks for no audience: the help no one sees, the spending no one applauds, the role that holds everything together from the back of the room. His life is a quiet invitation to take up that role gladly, to give in a way that actually costs us, and to trust that nothing done for Allah is ever lost, even when no one writes our name down.
