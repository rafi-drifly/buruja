Hanzala ibn Abi Amir and Jameela

Washed by the Angels

Two of the most famous men in Madinah spent years waiting for a prophet. One was an ascetic who already dressed the part. The other was the most powerful man in the city. When the Prophet ﷺ finally came, both of them turned against him.

This is the story of their children: Hanzala ibn Abi Amir and Jameela bint Abdullah (may Allah be pleased with them). A son and a daughter who walked away from their fathers' choices, married for a single night, and were honoured in a way no one on that battlefield had ever seen.

The Companions

Hanzala ibn Abi Amir and Jameela

Washed by the Angels

Two fathers who waited, and turned away

Before Islam reached Madinah, the city was called Yathrib, and one man stood on the edge of becoming its king. His name was Abdullah ibn Ubayy. His closest friend was Abu Amir, a deeply religious man who knew the scriptures of the People of the Book and was certain that a prophet of Allah would soon come to that land.

Abu Amir gave up the politics of the city for it. He wore rough, coarse clothes. He worshipped one God. He kept saying, "I am waiting for the prophet, the way the others are waiting for him. I am on the religion of Ibrahim." He had already become an ascetic. He already fit the part. If you had asked who in that city was most ready for a prophet, his name would have come up first.

Then the Prophet ﷺ rose, and he came from Makkah, from Quraysh. He was not from the tribe Abu Amir expected, not from the lineage he had imagined. The prophet had arrived, but not in the shape the waiting man had drawn for himself.

So the man who had waited his whole life rejected him. He was not even content to step aside and stay quiet. He crossed over to the other side and fought against the Prophet ﷺ at the battle of Uhud. His friend Abdullah ibn Ubayy took a different road to the same place: he stayed in Madinah and became the chief of its hypocrites, the man who undermined the Prophet ﷺ from within.

The children who chose differently

Both men had children, and those children did not follow them. Abu Amir's son was Hanzala. Abdullah ibn Ubayy had a daughter, Jameela. Two young people, born into the two households that had set themselves against the Prophet ﷺ, came to him instead and believed.

It is a quiet thing, but worth pausing over. Hanzala grew up watching his father reject the very prophet he had spent a lifetime expecting. Jameela grew up under a father who led the hypocrites from inside the city, smiling at the Prophet ﷺ in public and working against him in private. The pull of both homes was away from the truth.

Neither child gave in to it. Faith is not passed down like a name or a house. Each of them had to see the truth for what it was and walk toward it on their own, against the example set at home. And having found it, they found each other.

The two were married. The timing of their wedding is the part of the story almost no one forgets.

A wedding on the eve of battle

Their wedding night was the night before the battle of Uhud. The army was already preparing to march out. Hanzala had been a husband for a matter of hours, and the call to fight had already come.

He went to the Prophet ﷺ and asked permission to stay with his wife that one night before he left. He did not know it, but it was the only night he would ever spend with her. Permission was given, and he stayed.

When morning came, Hanzala did not linger. He rose and went straight from his wedding night to the battlefield. There is something very young and very brave in that: a new groom who could have found a reason to hold back, and did not.

Most of us, given one night of happiness and a hard task waiting at dawn, would bargain for a little more time. Hanzala did not bargain. He had asked for the one night, he had been given it, and then he answered the call as though nothing was owed to him.

The young man on the field

Hanzala was young, and at Uhud he fought bravely. In the thick of the battle he reached Abu Sufyan, one of the senior leaders of Quraysh, and knocked him from his horse. He was close, very close, to ending him.

Abu Jahl had not survived the battle of Badr. Many of the other leaders had already fallen. Abu Sufyan was in that same rank, and Hanzala almost added his name to the list. He stood over him, about to strike the blow.

Then a man from Quraysh reached Hanzala first, and Hanzala was killed before he could finish. A husband of one night, a young believer from a house that had fought the Prophet ﷺ, fell as a martyr on the field of Uhud.

It would have been easy to remember him only as the one who nearly killed Abu Sufyan. But that is not why his name is known. What happened to his body after he fell is the reason his story is still told.

The strangest sight on the battlefield

When the companions found his body, something stopped them. On a battlefield, every man is covered in dust and blood. Hanzala looked as though he had just stepped out of a bath. He was washed, clean, and there was the scent of musk on him.

It was the strangest thing they had seen all day. They were thirsty men who did not have water even to drink, and here was Hanzala soaked through, as if he had just bathed. They could not make sense of it. They came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked him what had happened.

The Prophet ﷺ told them what he had seen: that Hanzala's body had been raised toward the heavens, and that the angels had washed him on a platter of gold, between the sky and the earth. From that day his name was Hanzala, the one washed by the angels.

Pause on the picture for a moment. While the believers stood in the dust counting their dead, the heavens were quietly honouring one of them. The same young man whose father had fought the Prophet ﷺ was being bathed by angels in full view of the unseen.

A dream, and a child

They went to Jameela and asked whether anything had happened on their night together. She told them about a dream. As they had gone to sleep, she had seen Hanzala carried up through the heavens, and a gate had opened for him, and then closed behind him.

She had seen, in her sleep, the very thing the Prophet ﷺ would later describe: her husband lifted to the sky, the gates of heaven receiving him. One night was all the two of them were given, and even that night carried a sign neither of them could have understood while it was happening.

From that single night Jameela conceived. Their son, Abdullah ibn Hanzala, was born, and he grew up into a believer like his parents. Years later he too was killed as a martyr, by the troops of Yazid ibn Muawiya.

It is worth standing back to see the whole shape of it. The mother of Abu Jahl came to the Prophet ﷺ. The children of the chief hypocrite came to him. The household of the man who fought him at Uhud gave its sons, one after another, to the side of faith. Allah was drawing people toward His Prophet ﷺ from the very families that had opposed him.

The name the angels gave him

Hanzala had a father who was famous for his religion. People knew Abu Amir as a man of worship, an ascetic in coarse clothes who had spent years preparing for a prophet. By every outward measure, the father looked like the believer and the son was just a young man newly married.

Yet when the moment of truth came, it was the son who answered. The father had a lifetime of waiting and let pride keep him from bowing. The son had a single morning, and he gave it without hesitation. One of them is remembered as a man who fought the Prophet ﷺ. The other is remembered by the title heaven itself gave him.

That title, the one washed by the angels, did not depend on whose son he was, or on how religious he had looked the year before. It depended on a heart that recognised the truth and acted on it. Hanzala carried very little into that battle, and he came out of it with a name no amount of waiting could have earned.

It is a sobering thing to sit with. A man can spend years looking the part of a believer and miss the one moment that mattered. A young man with no reputation at all can meet that moment and be lifted to the heavens. Allah was not measuring their clothes or their history. He was measuring what they did when the truth stood in front of them.

What their story teaches us

This is one of the shortest stories in the series, and one of the hardest to forget. It turns on a single night and a single morning, and yet it leaves more than one thing behind for an ordinary life.

  • Faith is not inherited, and it is not blocked by family. Their fathers were the chief hypocrite and the man who fought the Prophet ﷺ. The children still found their way to the truth and held to it.
  • Knowing the truth is not the same as accepting it. Abu Amir waited his whole life for a prophet, dressed the part, and worshipped one God. When the prophet came, pride would not let him bow. Hanzala had nothing to prove, and he believed.
  • Allah honours the heart, not the lineage. On that field the angels did not ask whose son he was. They washed a young believer who answered the call when it came.
  • A small window of time can hold a whole life. Hanzala had one night and one morning. In it he became a husband, a father, and a martyr washed by the angels. We rarely know which of our days is the one that counts.

Questions

Who was Hanzala ibn Abi Amir?

A young companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and a martyr at the battle of Uhud. He is famous as Ghasil al-Malaika, the one washed by the angels, after the Prophet ﷺ said the angels had bathed his body between the heavens and the earth.

Why is he called the one washed by the angels?

He left his wedding night and went straight to Uhud, where he was martyred. When the companions found his body it looked freshly washed and scented, though there was no water on the field. The Prophet ﷺ said he had seen the angels wash Hanzala on a platter of gold.

Who was Jameela, and what was her dream?

Jameela bint Abdullah was Hanzala's wife and the daughter of Abdullah ibn Ubayy, the leader of the hypocrites of Madinah. On their one night together she dreamed that Hanzala was carried up through the heavens and that a gate opened for him and closed behind him.

What happened to their son?

From that single night Jameela conceived a son, Abdullah ibn Hanzala. He grew up to be a believer and was himself martyred years later, killed by the troops of Yazid ibn Muawiya.

Why this story stays with us

Two fathers had every reason to believe and did not. Two children had every reason to follow their fathers and did not. The difference between them was not knowledge, and it was not opportunity. It was the willingness to lower the head and accept the truth when it finally arrived.

Most of us will not be tested on a battlefield. We are tested in quieter ways: whether we will do the right thing when it is inconvenient, whether we will answer a call that does not arrive the way we wanted it to, whether our best moment might be hidden inside an ordinary day. Hanzala did not know his morning was the one that counted. Neither do we. The invitation in his story is simply to answer when the moment comes, and to leave the honouring to Allah.
