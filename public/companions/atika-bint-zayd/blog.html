Atika bint Zayd

The Wife of Many Martyrs

She buried four husbands, one after another, and each of them died on the way of Allah. By the end, people no longer pitied her. They said that if a man wished for martyrdom, he should marry her.

This is the story of Atika bint Zayd (may Allah be pleased with her): a poet, a worshipper, and a woman whose home was a passage for some of the most beloved companions of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Atika bint Zayd

The Wife of Many Martyrs

A name, and a daughter of the man who waited

Her name, Atika, was a common one among the Arabs of that time. It carries the sense of someone who wears a great deal of perfume. There were so many women named Atika in the lineage of the Prophet ﷺ that he once described himself as the son of the Awatiq, the Atikas, from the tribe of Sulaym.

To understand this Atika, you have to go back to her father. Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl was the man who lived on the way of Ibrahim before the revelation ever came. He rejected the idols, refused alcohol, and could not bear the burial of infant daughters. He would take the girls who were to be buried alive, bring them into his own home, and raise them until they could marry.

Zayd never lived to see the Prophet ﷺ proclaim the message, yet the Prophet ﷺ said he would be raised on the Day of Judgement as a nation all by himself. He left behind two children. One was Sa'id ibn Zayd, among the ten given the glad tidings of Paradise. The other was this noble woman.

Atika carried her father's hope into the next generation. He had longed for his children to walk the path he had only glimpsed from a distance, and through her and her brother, that hope took root.

Beauty, eloquence, and devotion

Atika was known for a few things at once, and it is rare for them to gather in one person. She was known for her beauty, and for being exceedingly beautiful. She was known for her eloquence: she was a poet, and her life is threaded with verses she composed in moments of love and loss.

She was also known for her worship. Ibn Kathir records that she was a woman of great devotion, given to long acts of prayer. And she was known for a complete, well-rounded character: not strong in one place and weak in another, but whole.

Put together, she was someone you could admire on every level. She had beauty and intelligence, religion and devotion, wisdom and grace. She was, in the fullest sense, everything a person might hope to be.

It matters to say this before the losses begin, because it is easy to remember her only for the husbands she buried. She was a full and gifted person in her own right, long before and all through the griefs that came to her.

A love that had to learn detachment

Her first husband was Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr, the son of the closest companion of the Prophet ﷺ. Abdullah was one of the early Muslims, and during the migration he was the one who carried news from Makkah to the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr while they hid in the cave.

Abdullah fell deeply in love with Atika. So deeply that he began to hold back from the good he used to do. He was rarely seen in the mosque, rarely seen serving alongside the Prophet ﷺ. His father, who wanted his household to be a household of service, saw an attachment that had grown too heavy, and he asked his son to part from her.

It is worth pausing here. This was a singular situation, not a rule for anyone else to copy. No parent reading this is Abu Bakr, and no son is Abdullah. Abu Bakr saw a particular, unhealthy attachment in his own child, and his aim was to teach him detachment from this world and attachment to the faith. We do not take from it that anyone may force a child to divorce.

Abdullah obeyed, and he poured his heart into verses. He vowed he would never forget her so long as the sun rose by day or a bird crossed the sky by night. He praised her character, her intelligence, her nobility, and said he could not imagine that a woman like her should ever be parted from anyone.

The first grave

Abdullah's poetry moved his father, and Abu Bakr told him to take her back. He was never asked to break his own heart, only to widen it: to serve as his family was expected to serve, and to love without disappearing from the world.

And serve he did. Later, in a siege, Abdullah was struck by an arrow. He carried the wound back to Madinah, and some time afterward it took his life. He died a martyr, one of the early believers who gave everything for the message.

Atika grieved him with the only language she fully owned. She took an oath in verse that her eyes would never stop weeping for him, and that her skin would never again touch sweet perfume. She meant to spend the rest of her days in mourning.

It is a striking vow from a woman whose very name spoke of perfume. She was setting aside the ordinary comforts of her life, choosing instead to keep his memory close. She believed, in that moment, that her grieving would have no end.

A way out of an oath

Some time after, Zayd ibn al-Khattab proposed to her. He was the brother of Umar, and there is a quiet wonder in this: their grandfather al-Khattab had once persecuted her father for calling people to the worship of one God. Now the family of Zayd ibn Amr and the family of al-Khattab were being woven together.

Atika was caught between her oath and a man she could not easily refuse. Widows in those days, especially the widows of martyrs, often married again, and it was understood to be a mercy rather than a betrayal. Still, she had bound herself in verse, and a vow is not a light thing to a poet.

So on their wedding night, Zayd gently recited her own poem back to her, changing its words: that her eye would at last find rest, that her skin would touch perfume again. He was showing her a door. The oath had been hers to make, and it was no sin to set it down. They married. Then Zayd, too, was martyred, at the battle of Yamama.

After him she married Umar ibn al-Khattab himself. He was careful that his household should never profit from his position as leader of the Muslims. He felt unease when she went out to the mosque in the evenings, yet he never forbade it, because the Prophet ﷺ had forbidden men from barring women from the mosque. So she kept going. Then Umar was killed, and she had now seen three husbands fall as martyrs, and composed poetry over each.

There is a quiet design in all of this that is worth noticing. The grandfather of Umar and Zayd had once tried to crush her father's call to one God. Yet in the end, the children of that family carried the very faith he had tried to silence, and Atika and her brother stood at the heart of that joining. What one man tried to stop, the next generation became.

The trajectory of her home

By now the question almost asks itself: who marries a woman after that? The answer was az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, another of the ten promised Paradise. In stature and temperament he resembled Umar, a large man raised in hard circumstances, and like Umar he too was among that blessed ten. He was martyred after he had left the field of battle, struck down while at his prayer.

People could have read an evil omen into all of this. A woman widowed four times, every husband dying soon after. They did not. They said instead: if a man wishes to be a martyr, let him marry Atika, because that was plainly the path Allah had written for everyone who entered her home.

Her fifth husband was al-Husayn, the grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, whose own martyrdom would become one of the most sorrowful in all of history. But she did not live to see that day. She passed before him, after about seventy-two years of life, in the caliphate of Mu'awiyah.

She was remembered to the end for the same things she had always been known for: her eloquence and her poetry, her knowledge, her worship, and her character. Five husbands, all of them martyrs, all of them counted among the most beloved companions, had passed through her life.

You can only imagine what that home held, and how precious it was. Her father had wished to see the seed of faith thrive in his children. Through Sa'id and through Atika, it did, in ways he never lived to witness.

What Atika's life teaches us

Her name appears in only a paragraph or two of the histories, yet what little is preserved carries a great deal. Here is some of what stays with us.

  • Love is meant to widen us, not shrink us. Abdullah's love was real and was never condemned, only redirected. The lesson was to love and still show up for everyone else who needed him.
  • Grief and devotion can share one heart. Atika mourned deeply and worshipped deeply. She wept for what she lost without ever losing her hold on her Lord.
  • An oath made in pain can be gently released. She had bound herself by a vow she never had to make. A kind reminder showed her the door, and she walked through it without shame.
  • What people call an omen, faith can read as honour. The world saw a woman whose husbands kept dying. The believers saw a home that Allah kept filling with martyrs.

Questions

Who was Atika bint Zayd?

She was the daughter of Zayd ibn Amr ibn Nufayl and the sister of Sa'id ibn Zayd, one of the ten companions given the glad tidings of Paradise. She was known for her beauty, her poetry, her worship, and her noble character.

Why is Atika called the wife of many martyrs?

She was married five times, and each husband was martyred or destined for martyrdom: Abdullah ibn Abi Bakr, Zayd ibn al-Khattab, Umar ibn al-Khattab, az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, and al-Husayn. She passed away before al-Husayn.

Did people see her marriages as a bad omen?

No. Rather than fear her, the companions said that if a man wished to be a martyr, he should marry her, because Allah had granted martyrdom to each husband she had.

What can we learn from the life of Atika bint Zayd?

That love should open us toward service rather than close us off, that grief and devotion can live together, and that what others read as misfortune can, in the light of faith, be seen as honour.

Why this story stays with us

It would be easy to read Atika's life as a string of losses. Four times she stood at a grave. Four times she gathered herself and kept worshipping, kept composing, kept opening her home. The world around her saw the burials. She seems to have kept her eyes on something steadier than the grief.

There is also a gentle correction in her story for how we measure a life. We tend to count what a person gains and keeps. Hers was a life defined by what she gave and lost, and yet it is remembered as a life of honour, not misfortune.

Most of us will not be tested the way she was. But all of us are asked, in smaller ways, to keep our hearts soft after they have been broken, and to keep trusting that what is taken from us is held by the One who gave it. Carry that into today: love the people in your home without clinging, grieve without despairing, and let your worship be the thing that does not move.
