Hakim ibn Hizam

When Money Stopped Mattering

He was born inside the Kaaba, into one of the richest families in Makkah, and he spent his early life the way many of us spend ours: busy, comfortable, always on the road, never quite finding the time for the question of faith.

This is the story of Hakim ibn Hizam (may Allah be pleased with him): a merchant who loved wealth, who finally heard one calm warning about it, and who changed so completely that he never asked another person for anything again.

The Companions

Hakim ibn Hizam

When Money Stopped Mattering

Born inside the Kaaba

Hakim came from one of the noble houses of Makkah. His grandfather was Khuwaylid ibn Asad, a chief of Banu Asad who once helped defend the Kaaba, and who stood among the senior, honoured men of the city. That makes Hakim the paternal nephew of our mother Khadijah (RA): her youngest brother, Hizam, was his father.

His birth was unusual. His mother went into the Kaaba with a group of women, and there, inside it, her labour began. She could not leave, and so Hakim was born within the Kaaba itself. People took it as a sign that this was a child marked for something.

He was born about thirteen years before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Because Khadijah was like a mother to him, he grew up close to the family, and he became like an older brother to the Prophet ﷺ. He was there, it seems, at the wedding, and present for the quiet moments that the books of seerah preserve.

A merchant who took care of the poor

Hakim was good at what he did. "I was a merchant," he later said. "I used to go out to Yemen and to Syria, and I made a lot of money." Then he added the part that tells you who he was: "And I would come back and spend it on the poor of my people."

It was the same spirit as his aunt Khadijah, who was famous for caring for the poor of Makkah. He was not at her level, but he carried the same instinct. He also had a sharp mind. He was allowed into Dar al-Nadwa, the council house where the men of Makkah made their decisions, when he was only fifteen, though no one else was admitted until they were forty.

It was Hakim who first brought Zaid ibn Haritha into the Prophet's life. He had found the boy in the market and gave him to Khadijah, and when she married the Prophet ﷺ and saw how he loved Zaid, she gave the boy to him. The Prophet ﷺ freed him. So much good was already passing through Hakim's hands.

And yet he was not interested in religion. Not hostile, not an enemy: simply too busy. He was barely in Makkah anyway, always on one of the long trade routes. This whole matter of faith was something he had no time for.

The man who would not fight

When Quraysh boycotted the Prophet's clan and his aunt Khadijah suffered with them, Hakim could not stay still. He did not come as a believer. He came as family. He would secretly load grain and goods onto camels at night and drive them toward the valley where they were trapped, so they would have something to eat.

He still would not become a Muslim, but he would not raise a hand against the Prophet ﷺ either. He was present at the Battle of Badr, brought out with people who had no real wish to fight, yet there is not a single report of him carrying a sword or harming anyone. Afterwards, when he wanted to swear an oath, he would say: "By the One who saved me from fighting on the day of Badr." He understood what he had been spared. Had he died that day, he would have died against the Prophet ﷺ, and Allah had quietly kept him from it.

Once he even travelled to Madinah to bring the Prophet ﷺ a gift, a fine garment from Yemen, simply out of old affection. "Muhammad ﷺ," he said, "was among the most beloved of people to me." The Prophet ﷺ would not accept it as a gift, so that nothing would look like a bribe, but he gently offered to buy it instead. Even then, the door was being held open for him.

There is a tender footnote to that garment. The Prophet ﷺ later wore it on the pulpit, and one who saw him said he had never seen anyone look more beautiful. Then the Prophet ﷺ gave it to Usama ibn Zaid, the son of a freed slave. When Hakim saw Usama wearing it and asked, with the old tribal pride, how he came to wear such a noble garment, Usama answered without arrogance: "By Allah, I am better than him, and my father is better than his father." Hakim did not yet understand that Islam had quietly set lineage aside and was now weighing people only by piety.

Shirk was beneath him

As the Prophet ﷺ came to Makkah for its conquest, he named four men of the city for whom, he said, idol worship was beneath them. They were too intelligent, too noble, for it. He could not see why they had held out so long. Hakim ibn Hizam was one of those four.

He was not being forgiven the way an enemy is forgiven. He was family who had ended up, sadly, on the wrong side of the matter, with no real reason to be there. The only real question was why he had waited. He had been so distracted by his wealth, so absorbed in his trade, that he had simply missed it.

On the day Makkah was conquered, Hakim embraced Islam. His children embraced it, and his wife embraced it. His whole household entered the faith together with him.

When money stopped mattering

Hakim still loved wealth, and he still gave a great deal of it away. After the Battle of Hunayn he asked the Prophet ﷺ for a share of the spoils, and was given it. He asked again, and was given more. He asked a third time, and again the Prophet ﷺ gave him, but this time he spoke to him plainly, as an old companion.

"Hakim," he said, "this wealth is green and sweet. Whoever takes it without greed will be blessed in it, and whoever takes it with a greedy soul will find no blessing in it, like one who eats and is never full." Then: "The upper hand is better than the lower hand. Whoever seeks to be self-sufficient, Allah will make him self-sufficient."

Hakim was over seventy years old. He had looked at money one way his entire life. That single conversation turned it over. He answered: "By the One who sent you with the truth, I will never ask anyone for anything again, as long as I live." From that day he would only be the giving hand, never the receiving one.

He kept the oath. When the spoils were later distributed, Abu Bakr called him to take his share, and he refused. Umar called him, and he refused again. Umar gathered the people to witness that he had offered Hakim his right and Hakim had turned it away. He took nothing he had not earned with his own hands for the rest of his life.

Learning how wealth is blessed

Hakim still had money, and he still had questions, so he kept asking. He asked so much about wealth that you could almost teach a whole course on how a Muslim should handle it from his narrations alone. The best charity, the Prophet ﷺ told him, is what you give after your own needs are met, and you begin with those closest to you.

He learned that an honest trade carries blessing and a dishonest one loses it. "If the two parties are truthful and make everything clear," the Prophet ﷺ taught him, "they will be blessed in their transaction. If they lie and hide, the blessing is wiped out." He learned, too, not to sell what he did not yet possess.

Once the Prophet ﷺ gave him a single coin to buy a sacrificial animal. Hakim bought a sheep for it, saw it was worth more, sold it for two, and came back with both a sheep and the original coin. The Prophet ﷺ prayed that Allah would bless him in his trade. He spent the rest of his life as careful about how he earned as he was generous in how he gave.

A long life of giving

Urwah ibn al-Zubair narrated that before Islam Hakim had freed a hundred people from slavery and given a hundred camels in charity, and after Islam he freed another hundred and gave another hundred. All of it was accepted, gathered up and preserved with his faith.

He lived to be a hundred and twenty: half of his life in ignorance, half in Islam. He became the richest man in the Madinah of the Prophet ﷺ, and the one who gave the most. Late in life he sold the famous council house, Dar al-Nadwa, and gave away every coin of the profit. When a relative protested that he had sold the pride of Quraysh, he answered simply: "All nobility has gone, except piety. I bought with it a home in Paradise."

When his cousin al-Zubair died leaving a great debt behind, Hakim came to al-Zubair's son and asked how much it was. Told it was vast, he simply said he would take half of it on himself. He used to say that he never woke to find someone in need at his door without knowing that Allah was testing him, and seeking the reward of that test.

On the day of Arafah he was once seen buying the freedom of a hundred enslaved people, then bringing forward a hundred camels and a hundred sheep, raising his hands to the sky and weeping: "O Allah, free our necks from the fire." The people watching wept with him, and prayed that the One who is more merciful than Hakim would free them too.

When he died, peacefully in his bed in Madinah, his last words were: "There is no god but Allah. I used to fear You, and today I have hope in You." He was buried in al-Baqi, near the Prophet ﷺ he had once known as a young friend.

What Hakim's life teaches us

His story turns on a single quiet conversation about money, and on a man old enough to be set in his ways who let it change him completely.

  • Wealth is meant to pass through your hands, not settle in them. The Prophet ﷺ told him the giving hand is better than the receiving one, and Hakim built the rest of his life around that one sentence.
  • It is never too late to change. He was past seventy, with a lifetime of habits, when he turned his whole relationship with money around. The years already spent did not disqualify him.
  • Good done quietly is never wasted. The kindness he showed before Islam, the food he sent in secret, the slaves he freed, was not lost. It was gathered up and kept.
  • Hold the world lightly. He sold what others called priceless and gave the money away, because he had learned that nothing truly lasts except piety.

Questions

Who was Hakim ibn Hizam?

A wealthy and respected merchant of Makkah and the paternal nephew of our mother Khadijah (RA). He accepted Islam on the day Makkah was conquered and became known for his immense generosity.

Why is it said he was born in the Kaaba?

His mother entered the Kaaba with a group of women and went into labour while she was inside, unable to leave. Hakim was born within it, which people took as a sign of the honour Allah would give him.

What did the Prophet ﷺ teach him about wealth?

That wealth is green and sweet, blessed for the one who takes it without greed and barren for the one who takes it greedily, and that the giving hand is better than the receiving hand. After hearing it, Hakim vowed never to ask anyone for anything again.

What can we learn from the life of Hakim ibn Hizam?

That it is never too late to change, that wealth is best when it keeps moving toward those in need, and that quiet good is never lost.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are not hostile to what is good. We are just busy. Like Hakim before that conversation, we mean to be more generous, more present, more serious about our faith, once things settle down. His life is a gentle warning that the days slip by while we wait, and a quiet promise that the door stays open longer than we think.

He heard one sentence about money and let it remake him in his seventies. We can take the smaller version into today: to be the hand that gives rather than the one that grasps, to send a little good quietly toward someone who needs it, and to trust that not one bit of it is ever wasted.
