Shurahbil ibn Hasana

The Scribe and Commander

Some companions are remembered for a single famous moment. This one is remembered for the work he did quietly, again and again, until whole lands had changed and his name was still barely known. We have little narrated about him, and yet the shape of his life carries lessons we can keep for a lifetime.

This is the story of Shurahbil ibn Hasana (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the early Muslims, a scribe who wrote down the revelation, and a commander whose armies opened much of what is now Jordan and parts of Palestine.

Shurahbil ibn Hasana

The Scribe and Commander

The boy named after his mother

We know very little about Shurahbil before Islam, and almost nothing certain about his father. The sources dispute even his father's name. Some mention a man called Abdullah ibn al-Muta'a, and trace the family to Banu Kinda or Banu Tamim, two well-known tribes of the time.

What is clear is that he is known by his mother. Hasana was the woman who adopted him and raised him as a child, and so he carried her name: Shurahbil ibn Hasana. She was herself an early Muslim, which makes her one of the first to believe.

Later in life she married a man from Madinah, Sufyan ibn Ma'mar (may Allah be pleased with him), who accepted Islam along with his sons. Those sons, Jabir and Junada, became Shurahbil's stepbrothers, and the whole household would soon leave Makkah together.

So the man we are about to follow begins almost anonymously. No famous father, no settled tribe to point to, no number marking him as the seventh or the tenth to believe. He begins with a mother who loved him, an early faith, and very little else. What he made of that is the whole of his story.

The migration to Abyssinia

When the persecution in Makkah grew heavy, Shurahbil made the second migration to Abyssinia. He did not go alone. He traveled with his mother, with his stepfather Sufyan ibn Ma'mar, and with his stepbrothers Jabir and Junada, a whole family moving as one to keep their faith.

This was part of the community that stayed in Abyssinia for some time. They did not return to Makkah and migrate with the Prophet ﷺ to Madinah. They settled abroad, held to their religion in a foreign land, and waited while events unfolded far away.

It is worth pausing on how ordinary and how hard that must have been. There were no battles to fight here, no dramatic stand to make. There was only the daily work of staying Muslim in a place that was not home, year after year, without knowing how the story would end.

It was a long, patient kind of faith, lived far from the centre of events. And it was in Abyssinia that the Prophet ﷺ would place in his hands a trust that reveals exactly how he was seen.

The trust he was given

Umm Habiba (may Allah be pleased with her), the daughter of Abu Sufyan, had migrated to Abyssinia and was left a widow there in a delicate situation. From Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ sent a proposal of marriage to her, asking the Negus to conduct the marriage on his behalf. The Negus arranged it and held the wedding feast there in Abyssinia.

Years later, when it was time for Umm Habiba to be reunited with the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah, it was Shurahbil who was chosen to accompany her on that journey. According to her own account, the Negus sent her with him.

It is no small thing to be entrusted with escorting the wife of the Prophet ﷺ across that distance, safely, into his presence. It shows the standing he already held: a man counted as competent and honourable, the kind of person you hand something precious to and do not worry.

Notice that the honour did not come from a speech he gave or a battle he won. It came from his character, proven over years of quiet faithfulness, until a trust like this could simply be placed in his hands.

Among the scribes of the revelation

When he reached Madinah, a greater honour was given to him. The Prophet ﷺ made him one of the scribes of the revelation, one of those entrusted to write down the words of the Qur'an as they were sent down.

He was an early Muslim. He was one of the few people at the time who could read and write. And he had already given much for the sake of this religion. All of that together made him deserving of the task.

Pause on what that means. Some of the earliest written record of the Qur'an we recite today came at the hand of a man most Muslims have never heard of. He did not witness many of the great battles alongside the Prophet ﷺ. His service took a quieter form: a pen, a careful hand, and the words of Allah.

There is a dignity in that which is easy to overlook. To write the revelation is not to be in the centre of the crowd. It is to do something precise and faithful, often unseen, and to know that what you have done will outlast you by centuries.

The quiet soldier becomes a commander

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Shurahbil's skill found a new field. In the time of Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him), he was made a commander in the wars against those who turned away from Islam, and he was second in command after Khalid ibn al-Walid at the decisive Battle of Yamama. Because of his unusual ability, he was also given special operations to run.

When the armies turned toward greater Syria, Abu Bakr sent four divisions to face the Byzantines under the overall command of Khalid ibn al-Walid. One was led by Amr ibn al-As, one by Abu Ubaydah, one by Yazid ibn Abi Sufyan, and one by Shurahbil ibn Hasana. He had a share in the fall of Busra, an important city, and he was a commander at the Battle of Ajnadayn, where the Byzantines were driven back toward Damascus.

When Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) later replaced Khalid with Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah as the head of the army, something quiet but telling happened: Abu Ubaydah kept Shurahbil exactly where he was, as his deputy. A new leader looked at this man and decided there was no one better for the role.

That detail says as much about Shurahbil as any battle. Leadership changed above him, and his place did not. He was not the kind of man a new commander felt the need to move. He was simply needed, and so he stayed, and he kept working.

The lands he opened

Where Shurahbil shines most is in the opening of what is now Jordan, along with parts of Palestine. With an army of about seven thousand men, he brought the greater part of that region into Islam.

If you read the chronicles of the campaigns against the Byzantines, you rarely turn a page without finding his battalion mentioned. He was not the famous name at the front of the story, but he was almost always there, doing the work that the famous names are remembered for.

For the people living in those lands today, this is a sobering thought. A man whose name they may never have learned spent his life so that the call to prayer could one day be heard where they now stand. They are still reaping what Allah brought about through his hands.

And he is, in this, a kind of mirror. So much of what we benefit from in our own lives, the faith we were handed, the words we were taught, came through people we will never be able to name and never get to thank.

The plague of Amwas

His life ended in the plague of Amwas, the same plague that took so many of the great companions. As people searched for a way to survive it, Shurahbil was part of a conversation that still speaks to us today.

Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him) wanted to put certain measures in place, a kind of separating of the people so the sickness would not spread among them. Shurahbil reminded him of his own long companionship with the Prophet ﷺ from the very earliest days, and Amr accepted that he had spoken the truth.

Then Shurahbil said the words he is remembered for. Of the plague he said it was the mercy of their Lord, the supplication of their Prophet ﷺ, and the death of the righteous who had come before them. He was not denying the danger. He was naming it for what it was in the sight of Allah, and refusing to meet it with fear alone.

He died on the very same day as Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah, the first and second in command of the army lost together to a single illness. It is hard to grasp how heavy that loss must have felt. He was about sixty-seven years old, his life spent at the front of one battalion after another, in the cause of Allah.

What Shurahbil's life teaches us

His name is not on most people's lists of famous companions. There was no single, dramatic moment that fixed him in memory. That is exactly what makes his life worth sitting with, because most of our lives look the same way.

  • Faithful work does not need an audience. He wrote the revelation, escorted a trust, and led armies, and history barely kept his name. He served anyway, and that was enough.
  • Be the person others can hand something to. He was trusted with the wife of the Prophet ﷺ, with the words of the Qur'an, and with command, because he had proven, quietly, that he was reliable.
  • Patience in a hard place is its own kind of struggle. He held to his faith for years in a foreign land, far from the centre of events, before he was ever given a great task.
  • Meet what you cannot control with trust in Allah. Facing the plague, he did not panic. He named it the mercy of his Lord and the death of the righteous, and he met it with a calm heart.

Questions

Who was Shurahbil ibn Hasana?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He was one of the scribes who wrote down the revelation, and later a trusted military commander in the time of Abu Bakr and Umar (may Allah be pleased with them). He is named after his mother, Hasana, who raised him.

Why is he named after his mother?

His father is barely known, and the sources even dispute his name. Hasana was the woman who adopted and raised him, and she was herself an early Muslim, so he carried her name: Shurahbil ibn Hasana.

What is Shurahbil ibn Hasana known for?

He was one of the scribes of the Qur'an, he escorted Umm Habiba (RA) from Abyssinia to the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah, and he was a commander whose armies opened much of what is now Jordan and parts of Palestine.

How did Shurahbil ibn Hasana die?

He died in the plague of Amwas, on the same day as the commander Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah (RA). He was about sixty-seven years old.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to measure a life by how much of it the world remembers. Shurahbil's life pushes back on that. He wrote down the Qur'an, he carried a sacred trust safely home, he led armies that changed the map, and still his name slipped almost out of memory. None of that made his work smaller in the sight of Allah.

Most of our days are like his: useful, faithful, and unnoticed. The dishes washed, the kindness no one saw, the prayer prayed when no one was watching, the duty done well with no one to thank you for it. His life is a gentle reminder that the One who matters most is keeping a perfect record, and that quiet, reliable service is never wasted. Carry that into your day, and let the rest stay hidden.
