The Ansar

The Helpers Who Asked for Nothing

After the worst day of his life, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ stood among strangers at the season of pilgrimage and asked, tribe after tribe, if anyone would simply listen. Almost no one did.

This is the story of the Ansar (may Allah be pleased with them): a broken, exhausted people from a town called Yathrib who took him in when his own city had cast him out, and who asked for nothing in this world in return.

The Ansar

The Helpers Who Asked for Nothing

The darkest moment, and a quiet beginning

The Prophet ﷺ had just lost Khadijah, the wife who comforted him, and Abu Talib, the uncle who protected him. He walked to Ta'if hoping its people might take him in. They cursed him, mocked him, and set their children to drive him out with stones until his shoes filled with blood.

He turned back not knowing where to go. "To whom are you leaving me?" he called out to his Lord. There was a real and human question inside that prayer: who on earth would shelter him now? His family had rejected him. His people had rejected him. The strangers were no kinder.

He had gone to Ta'if hoping it was the green place he had seen in a dream. Ta'if was a town of royals and proud leaders, much like Makkah. Yathrib, by contrast, was a scattered place of warring tribes that seemed to offer no hope at all. Yet Ta'if would be his hardship, and Yathrib his ease. Today few visit Ta'if, while the city that took him in is among the most visited places on earth.

It was in the season of Hajj that he came upon six young men resting at Mina. They were from Yathrib, a place so torn by war that others said it had nothing to offer. The advice of the world would have been not to waste his time on them. But the Prophet ﷺ never looked past people. He sat with them and asked, "Will you listen to what I have to say?"

A pledge made by the young

The six men listened. They had heard the Jewish tribes near their town speak of a prophet who was soon to come, and what this man said seemed to line up. One of them, As'ad ibn Zurarah, turned to the others: why not be the first to answer this call? They believed, and went home.

A year later, twelve returned and gave their pledge: that they would worship God alone, never steal, never take a life unjustly, never abandon what was right. The Prophet ﷺ sent Mus'ab ibn Umayr back with them to teach. Within a single year, every household in the town had heard the message and was waiting for him.

The third year, seventy-three came in secret, meeting after midnight in a valley behind the pillars. The Prophet's uncle al-Abbas was with him, and he took him aside: are you sure about these young people from a scattered town? If they were not ready, he told them, they could turn back, for his own family had him covered. They insisted. They knew exactly what taking him in would cost: old alliances broken, new enemies on every side, a whole community of refugees to feed. So they asked him plainly what they would get in return.

"You get Paradise"

He did not describe a great city, or a famous mosque, or a great airport, or a legacy that would be written in books. He told them the truth, and only the truth. "You get Paradise." That was the entire offer. There was no point mentioning a worldly reward to people who were not doing this for the world.

They answered without hesitation: a profitable transaction. Deal. We will take you in, we will follow you, we will give you our all, and we want nothing of this world in return. Something about these people made the promise of the next life enough.

Our mother Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) explained why their hearts were ready. The wars between their two tribes, the Aws and the Khazraj, had killed off their proud elders and left them wounded and tired of fighting. The pride had been beaten out of them. They were a humbled people, and a humbled heart can accept the truth.

The sweetness of faith

In his collection, Imam al-Bukhari places a quiet clue. He records the hadith on the sweetness of faith, narrated by Anas ibn Malik, himself one of the Ansar: that whoever holds three things has tasted that sweetness. That Allah and His Messenger ﷺ are more beloved to them than anything else. That they love other people only for the sake of Allah. And that they would hate to return to disbelief as they would hate to be thrown into fire.

Then, in the very next chapter, he writes: to love the Ansar is a sign of faith. The scholars say the placement is deliberate. No community matched that description like the Ansar did. They loved the Prophet ﷺ not for his eloquence or his lineage, but only because Allah had sent him. And they loved the refugees who came with him, the Muhajirun, only because the One they loved had told them to.

The Prophet ﷺ said it in the plainest terms: a sign of faith is love of the Ansar, and a sign of hypocrisy is to dislike them. Whoever loves them, he explained, loves them for their love of him.

Think of someone you love deeply sent away to a far place, and a family there who takes them in, feeds them, shelters them, and protects them from harm. You would love that family even before you met them. That is why we are asked to love the Ansar: for what they did for the most beloved of people. When Anas was asked whether they had named themselves the helpers, he said it was Allah who named them. The Prophet ﷺ likened them to the disciples of Jesus, peace be upon him, who, when he asked who would help him in the cause of God, answered, "We are the helpers of God."

Love that asked for nothing

When the believers reached Yathrib, the welcome was not staged. Strangers pulled at the newcomers: come to my house, come to mine. The migrants arrived having left parents, siblings, even spouses and children behind, the very people who had spat on them and tried to kill them, and now they were received like family. The Ansar gave their guests the better half of their homes and the better half of their food, and sometimes went hungry so a guest could eat.

When the Prophet ﷺ paired each migrant with a host, Sa'd ibn Rabi' offered Abdur Rahman ibn Awf half of everything he owned, and the better half. Abdur Rahman, a merchant by trade, thanked him and asked only to be shown the way to the marketplace. When the Prophet ﷺ proposed dividing the palm groves, the Ansar argued with him to give the migrants more. He had to temper their generosity, not encourage it.

They watched what he loved so they could give it to him. If he favoured a certain date, they saved it for him. If he liked a certain cloth, they gave it to him. They learned which house he liked to be in and timed their gifts to arrive while he was there. The Qur'an could have praised them for their generosity. Instead it praised them for their love.

Those who were already firmly established in their homes [in Medina], and firmly rooted in faith, show love for those who migrated to them for refuge and harbour no desire in their hearts for what has been given to them. They give them preference over themselves, even if they too are poor: those who are saved from their own souls' greed are truly successful.Quran 59:9

Tested, and never changed

The trials came. At Uhud, when they carried their dead, the Prophet ﷺ grieved that these people who took him in were now dying for him. At the trench of Khandaq, starving and covered in mud, with the hypocrites taunting them that he had ruined their lives, they answered by singing of their pledge to stay with him to the end.

After Makkah was won, the Ansar grew afraid. The Prophet ﷺ was giving the spoils to the new Makkan believers and nothing to them. They feared he would now go home to the city of his birth and leave them behind. They were too shy to ask, but their hearts ached, and they could not hide it.

So he gathered them alone in a tent. "Did I not come to you when you were lost, and Allah guided you through me? When you were poor, and Allah enriched you? When you were enemies, and Allah joined your hearts?" They agreed: Allah and His Messenger ﷺ have every favour upon us. Then he asked the question that broke them open: would you not be content that the others go home with sheep and spoils, while you go home with the Messenger of Allah?

The helpers in the background

The whole room wept until their beards were wet. All they had ever wanted was Paradise, and the companionship of the Prophet ﷺ, and now they had both. He raised his hands: O Allah, have mercy on the Ansar, and the children of the Ansar, and the children of the children of the Ansar.

They never changed after the victory. When he offered them governorships and new land, they asked only that the Muhajirun receive the same, and said they were content to remain helpers in the background. He told them they would face people after him who gave preference to others over them, and that they should be patient until they met him again at the Fountain.

In his final days, climbing the pulpit one last time with a cloth on his feverish head, he asked the believers to take care of the Ansar. They were, he said, his closest companions, the ones he confided in. They had fulfilled everything asked of them. As the ummah grew, he said, the Ansar would dwindle until they were like salt in a meal: barely noticed, yet holding everything together.

What the Ansar teach us

Their story is not one heroic act. It is a decade of giving, freely and without account, and that is exactly why it still moves us.

  • Welcome the one no one else will. The Prophet ﷺ went to six young men everyone else dismissed, and the Ansar opened their homes to refugees their neighbours feared. The people the world overlooks are often the ones ready to give the most.
  • Give before you are asked. They studied what the Prophet ﷺ loved so they could offer it, and argued to give the migrants more than was required. Real love does not wait to be requested; it looks for the next thing it can do.
  • A humbled heart can receive the truth. Makkah was too proud to listen; Yathrib had been emptied of pride by hardship. When life strips away what we cling to, it can also make room for what matters most.
  • Be content to serve unseen. Offered power, the Ansar chose to remain helpers in the background and asked only for Paradise. Worth is not measured by recognition.

Questions

Who were the Ansar?

The Ansar (the word means "the helpers") were the believers of Madinah, drawn from its two tribes, the Aws and the Khazraj. They gave the pledge to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, took him and the migrants from Makkah into their homes, and supported the young Muslim community.

Why are the Ansar so beloved?

The Prophet ﷺ said that to love the Ansar is a sign of faith, and that whoever loves them does so for their love of him. They sheltered him when his own people had driven him out and gave everything for a cause, asking nothing of this world in return.

Who named them the Ansar?

When Anas ibn Malik was asked whether they had chosen the name themselves, he answered that it was Allah who named them the Ansar. The Prophet ﷺ likened them to the disciples of Jesus, peace be upon him, who answered, "We are the helpers of God."

What can we learn from the Ansar?

To welcome those others overlook, to give generously before being asked, to let hardship humble rather than harden us, and to be content serving without recognition.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to give when something is coming back to us: a thank you, a favour returned, a name remembered. The Ansar gave when the only promise was Paradise, and they treated even that as more than enough. They fed guests before themselves, learned the small things a tired man loved, and asked to stay in the background while others took the credit.

Most of us will never shelter a prophet. But every day puts someone in front of us who needs more than we are owed to give: a neighbour, a guest, a stranger, someone easy to overlook. Their lives are an invitation to be the helper who keeps no ledger, who gives the better half and is glad to. Carry one act of that into today, and their story has done its work.
