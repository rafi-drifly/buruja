The Companions

Aisha bint Abu Bakr

The Teacher of a Generation

When the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ passed away in her room, Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) was about twenty years old. She would live for another fifty years. The greater part of her life happened after the man she loved was gone.

Those five decades were not an afterthought. They were how the world came to know him. This is the story of what she did with a long life and a short marriage: she spent it teaching, worshipping, and giving everything away.

The Companions

Aisha bint Abu Bakr

The Teacher of a Generation

A house with three graves

The Prophet ﷺ was buried in her room, in the home where she had waited for him to come back each evening. Now he was beside her, and he would not leave again. She lived in that house for the rest of her life, often sitting next to his grave as she recalled, out loud, the things he said and did.

Years before, she had dreamed that three moons fell into her home. Her father, Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him), reminded her of the dream after the Prophet ﷺ died. The best three people on earth, he told her, would be buried in her house, and the first of her moons had just been laid to rest there.

Abu Bakr was the second moon. When he felt death approaching, he asked her how old the Prophet ﷺ had been, on what day he died, and what he was wearing. Then he dressed in the same simple cloth and lay down to wait, so that his head would rest at the Prophet's shoulder.

Think of what it meant to live there. Every time she narrated the last moments of the Prophet ﷺ, she relived them, sitting an arm's length from where he lay. The grief of others was a journey to a grave across the city. Hers was simply the room she slept in.

The grave she gave away

There was one place left beside her father and the Prophet ﷺ, and everyone expected it to be hers. She had prepared herself for it. Then Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) was dying, and he sent his son to ask, gently, whether he might be buried there instead. Do not pressure her, he said. Make it only a request.

Aisha said yes. She had wanted that grave for herself, but she gave it up because she knew it would have pleased the Prophet ﷺ. It echoed something she had told him long before, when he asked permission to rise and pray at night: I love your closeness, but I love more what makes you happy.

So the three of them lie together: the Prophet ﷺ facing the Qibla, then Abu Bakr, then Umar. Aisha is not buried among them. Yet she became inseparable from the Prophet ﷺ in a different way, because we cannot remember him without remembering her.

She prayed the way he prayed

Of all the praise gathered around her, the line that may say the most is not about her learning. Her nephew Urwah ibn al-Zubair said that no one's prayer resembled the prayer of the Prophet ﷺ as closely as hers. He did not mean only the shape of it. He meant the stillness, the awe, the weeping.

She would begin reciting the Quran in the late part of the night and continue until after the sun had risen, pausing only for the dawn prayer. One who knew her said simply that she seemed to fast forever, so constant was she in it. Prayer, recitation, fasting: these filled the long years when there was no one left to perform them for.

Urwah described coming to ask her a question and finding her standing in prayer, repeating a single verse. He grew tired of waiting, went to the market, did his shopping, returned, and found her exactly where he had left her, reciting the same words, crying and praying, crying and praying. The Prophet ﷺ too, it is said, would sometimes stand the whole night with one verse.

The verse was this:

God has been gracious to us and saved us from the torment of intense heat-Quran 52:27

A grape, and an atom's weight of good

She was known above all for her charity. Muawiyah once sent her a hundred thousand dirhams while she was fasting. By the time she broke her fast that evening, she had given all of it away and kept nothing for herself. Another gift of the same size arrived, and she divided it among the other wives of the Prophet ﷺ.

One day a beggar came to a house that held nothing but a single cup of barley, set aside for her own meal. She gave it to him. Her companion Barirah teased her, wondering aloud where their food would now come from. That night a neighbour sent over a cooked goat, and Aisha gently repeated Barirah's words back to her: when you trust Allah more than you trust what is in your hand, He provides.

There was a precision to how she gave. When she sent food to a household in need, she would ask the one who delivered it what they had said in return. If they had made a prayer for her, she wanted to know the exact words, so that she could make the same prayer for them, and the whole reward of the charity would still rest with the giver.

Another time, all she had was one grape. She gave it to a beggar, who looked at it and asked if that was all. She answered with a verse, and with a question of her own: how many atoms of good are in this grape?

whoever has done an atom's-weight of good will see it,Quran 99:7

The orphans she raised as scholars

Charity, for her, was not only food handed out at the door. She took orphans into her home, fed them, and prepared them for life. She did something most people could not give them: she taught them to read and write, in a society where most adults could do neither.

She was exacting about it. They said she would correct an orphan over a single mistake in grammar. She taught them poetry and language until they were among the most literate people of their time, then saw the girls cared for and married before she let them go.

She also gave her freedom away. She loved to free those who were enslaved. When she once broke an oath in a moment of hurt, she did not settle the matter cheaply. Gift after gift came to her from afar, and she used them to free more than forty people, weeping at the thought of having spoken in anger at all.

His character was the Quran

Aisha asked the Prophet ﷺ about the things that puzzled her, and her questions saved the ummah a great deal of confusion. She narrated more than two thousand of his sayings. The senior companions brought her their hardest problems, including the intricate questions of inheritance, and she answered them. When Abu Hurairah and Ibn Umar disagreed, they said: let us go to Aisha.

She would also gently correct a report when she knew the fuller picture, having seen the Prophet ﷺ up close in his own home. Her nephew was amazed not by her grasp of law or poetry but by her knowledge of medicine, and asked where it came from. In his last years, she told him, people would visit the Prophet ﷺ and offer remedies when he was ill, and she listened closely and learned, so that she could care for him.

A man named Said came to her tent during Hajj, in a long line of people waiting their turn, and asked what the character of the Prophet ﷺ was like. Her answer was a single sentence. His character was the Quran. Have you read Surah al-Mu'minun, she asked. That was him.

Wherever she travelled she was like a moving university. Her home in Madinah drew dozens of women who came to learn their religion. One of the great scholars of the next generation said that if you weighed her knowledge against the knowledge of every other woman, hers would still be greater. Others said they had never heard anyone speak more clearly, not even the four caliphs who followed the Prophet ﷺ.

The night she was reunited

She stayed out of politics and taught from her home. When civil strife broke out and she rode toward Madinah seeking swift justice for the murdered Uthman, she did not intend a battle. People of corruption attacked both camps in the dark so that each thought the other had struck first. The fighting that followed grieved her for the rest of her life. I wish I had died before this, she would say.

Afterward Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) returned her home with honour and an escort of noble companions, and would punish anyone who spoke ill of her. She kept sending people to him for knowledge, saying he understood the way of the Prophet ﷺ better than most. There was no bitterness left between them.

She died on the seventeenth night of Ramadan. Near the end, Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) came to comfort her, and she feared he had come to praise her. He told her there was nothing now between her and the Prophet ﷺ except that her soul should leave her body. After fifty years of waiting, only that thin separation remained.

What Aisha's life teaches us

Her marriage to the Prophet ﷺ lasted a single decade. What she built afterward lasted centuries, and almost none of it was loud.

  • Love is shown in what we are willing to give up. She handed her own grave to Umar because it would please the Prophet ﷺ. We learn someone's heart by what they let go of, not only by what they hold.
  • Knowledge is a trust to pass on. She did not keep what she learned to herself. She answered the hardest questions and taught orphans to read, so that what she knew would outlive her.
  • Quiet worship shapes a whole life. She wept over a single verse until morning and fasted as though she never stopped. Her devotion was for an audience of One.
  • Generosity that keeps nothing back. She gave away fortunes the same day they arrived, down to a single grape, trusting Allah more than what was in her hand.

Questions

Who was Aisha bint Abu Bakr?

A wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the daughter of Abu Bakr, and one of the Mothers of the Believers. After the Prophet ﷺ passed away she became one of the foremost teachers and narrators of his life, recognised as a scholar of hadith, Quran, and law.

Why is Aisha so important to Islamic knowledge?

She narrated more than two thousand sayings of the Prophet ﷺ and spent fifty years teaching what she had witnessed in his home. Senior companions brought her their most difficult questions, and she trained a generation of students from her house in Madinah.

Where was Aisha buried?

She was buried in al-Baqi in Madinah. Though there was a place for her beside the Prophet ﷺ and her father, she gave it up for Umar, knowing it would have pleased the Prophet ﷺ.

What can we learn from the life of Aisha?

That love is measured by what we give up, that knowledge is a trust meant to be passed on, and that the steadiest devotion and the deepest generosity are usually the quietest.

Why this story stays with us

It would have been easy for her to let the hardest years pass once the Prophet ﷺ was gone, to accept comfort, to keep the gifts that came to her door. She did none of it. She kept living in the same simple way, gave away whatever arrived, and spent her days handing on what she knew so that others could love him too.

Most of us will not be remembered the way she is. But her life asks a plain question of an ordinary day: what are we doing with what we have been given? Teach what you know. Give without keeping count. Stand in prayer like it matters. None of it has to be seen by anyone to be worth everything.
