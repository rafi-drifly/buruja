Umayr ibn Wahb and Safwan ibn Umayyah

Two Cousins Who Came to Kill and Stayed to Believe

Two men sat in the courtyard of the Kaaba, grieving their dead and plotting a murder. One of them sharpened a sword, dipped it in poison, and set out for Madinah to kill the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He believed no living person could know what he had agreed to in secret.

This is the story of Umayr ibn Wahb and his cousin Safwan ibn Umayyah (may Allah be pleased with them): two proud nobles of Quraysh who came to the Prophet ﷺ as enemies, and were met with a patience that undid them.

Two Cousins Who Came to Kill and Stayed to Believe

Two cousins of Banu Jumah

Umayr and Safwan belonged to Banu Jumah, one of the smaller but more distinguished clans of Makkah. They were known for their wealth and for a particular honour: they perfumed the idols and largely ran the fragrance trade of the city. The sweet scents of Makkah passed through their hands.

Their fathers were among the fiercest enemies of the Prophet ﷺ. Safwan's father, Umayyah ibn Khalaf, was the chief of the clan and the man who tortured Bilal (RA) in the open sun. From the same family came Ubayy ibn Khalaf, the only man ever to die at the hand of the Prophet ﷺ, after swearing he would kill him. This was the household Umayr and Safwan came from: wealthy, proud, and bound up in the opposition to Islam from the start.

These two were not young men chasing their fathers' approval. They were grown, with wives and children old enough to fight. The hostility toward the Prophet ﷺ was not borrowed. It was theirs, held with conviction, and they were proud of it.

The day two armies met two armies

At Badr, Umayr was sent ahead to scout the Muslim camp. He counted close to three hundred men, every horse, every shield, and reported back with precision. But something unsettled him. He told the chiefs of Quraysh he had a bad feeling, that the faith he saw in those outnumbered men did not look right, and that they should turn back. Abu Jahl mocked him, and the army marched.

There is a verse that the Prophet's companions later understood through men like these. The Muslims at Badr saw the angels descend to help them. On the other side, Satan appeared in a familiar form, urging the disbelievers forward, promising that no one could defeat them. Then, when the two forces came into view, he fled.

Umayr would tell this story himself, years later, as a believer. While the two armies faced each other, another pair of armies was meeting unseen: the angels and the forces of Satan. When Satan saw them, he turned and ran.

Satan made their foul deeds seem fair to them, and said, ‘No one will conquer you today, for I will be right beside you,’ but when the armies came within sight of one another he turned on his heels, saying, ‘This is where I leave you: I see what you do not, and I fear God- God is severe in His punishment.’Quran 8:48

A man who woke among the dead

Badr went badly for Quraysh. Umayr was knocked from his horse and a Muslim drove a sword through him, so deep that its tip struck the stones beneath him. The man left him for dead.

Night fell. Umayr woke in the desert, surrounded by seventy of his own people who would never wake again. The sword had passed through him, yet somehow he had not died. He rose from among the bodies, made his way back to Makkah, and slowly healed.

It is hard to imagine the state of a man given back his life like that. But he did not yet read it as mercy. He read it as a reason for revenge, and he carried that bitterness home to a city drowning in grief.

The plot in the courtyard of the Kaaba

Every home in Makkah had lost someone at Badr, and the city was heavy with grief and the wish for revenge. Safwan, now among the richest men in Makkah, had watched his own father die at the hands of the very man he used to torture. He had all the wealth in the world and could not buy back a single thing he had lost. That, the historians note, frustrated him most of all.

One day Umayr and Safwan sat together near the Kaaba, walking and talking to pass the weight of those days. They spoke of the friends and fathers and uncles who had been killed. Umayr let his tongue slip. He said that were it not for a debt he could not pay and a family he would leave behind, he would ride to Madinah and kill the Prophet ﷺ himself. Safwan saw his opening. He promised to clear every debt and care for Umayr's family for the rest of their days. Now there was no excuse left.

They agreed it would stay between the two of them. Umayr took a sword, had it dipped in poison, concealed it, and set out for Madinah, certain he would die in the attempt and not caring, as long as the Prophet ﷺ died first.

The conversation no one had heard

Umayr reached the Prophet's mosque. Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA) saw him at the door, sword hanging from his neck, and knew at once that the man had come with harm in his heart. He seized Umayr by that very sword and dragged him in. The Prophet ﷺ told him to let go.

Umayr greeted him in the manner of the days of ignorance, and the Prophet ﷺ told him gently that Allah had given the believers a better greeting: peace. Then he asked why Umayr had come. Umayr offered his excuse: he had come to ransom his captured son. The Prophet ﷺ asked him twice more for the truth, and twice more Umayr repeated the same story. Then the Prophet ﷺ told it back to him. He recounted the conversation in the courtyard, word for word: the dead they had mourned, the vow Umayr had made, the promise Safwan had given in return.

There was no way anyone could have carried those words to Madinah in time, and no way anyone could have heard them at all. Umayr understood. He said there had been no one present but himself and Safwan, and that only Allah could have brought this to the Prophet ﷺ. He took the testimony of faith on the spot. The Prophet ﷺ returned his son and told him to teach his household this religion.

The cloak that brought Safwan back

Umayr asked to return to Makkah, into the same anger he had left, to call his own people to Islam. He guided a number of them quietly and, on his second journey to Madinah, arrived with a small caravan of new Muslims, reciting Quran on the back of his camel. His son grew into a companion who helped open Egypt and was martyred at Yarmuk.

Safwan was harder. He had sworn that if every last person of Quraysh accepted Islam, he would be the one who never did. When Makkah was finally conquered, he fled toward the sea, meaning to sail to Yemen. Umayr asked the Prophet ﷺ for a chance to bring him back, and the Prophet ﷺ handed him his own cloak as a sign of safety.

Umayr caught Safwan at the water's edge. Safwan did not believe him until he saw the cloak. He returned, but on his own terms, asking for time to consider the message. The Prophet ﷺ gave him longer than he asked for. He even fought alongside the Muslims at Hunayn while still a disbeliever, lending his stored weapons on the promise they would be returned. Afterward the Prophet ﷺ gave him herd after herd of camels until Safwan said no heart could be this generous except a prophet's. His own words: he was given all of that while the Prophet ﷺ was the most hated man on earth to him, and the giving continued until the Prophet ﷺ became the most beloved. Then he believed.

How completely a heart can turn

What is striking is not only that the two men believed, but how completely they changed. Umar, who had wanted to break Umayr's neck at the door, later said that there had been a time when no person on earth was more hateful to him than Umayr, and that now Umayr was more beloved to him than his own children.

Umayr never wanted to stay safe in Madinah. He asked to go back into the anger of Makkah precisely because he had once fought so hard against the faith, and he wanted to undo some of that harm by calling others to it. He spent the rest of his life striving in that path.

Safwan, once he believed, did not hold back either. He became known for a generosity so open that any hungry person in Makkah or Madinah knew they could come to his door and be fed. He had sworn he would be the last man alive to follow this religion. He ended his life feeding the poor in its name.

What these two lives teach us

Their story is not really about a foiled assassination. It is about how patiently mercy can wait for a hard heart, and how completely a heart can turn once it gives way.

  • No one is past saving. One man came carrying a poisoned sword; the other swore he would be the last holdout alive. Both became companions. It is a warning against ever writing a person off, including ourselves.
  • Mercy can disarm what force cannot. The Prophet ﷺ did not answer the plot with punishment. He answered it with the truth, gently spoken, and with a generosity that wore the hatred down.
  • Be slow to decide who is your enemy. Umar wanted to strike Umayr down. Days later they were brothers studying the Quran together. The person in front of you today is not fixed forever.
  • A second chance is meant to change you. Umayr woke among the dead and first wanted revenge. The same survival, read again, became the door to his faith. What we make of our mercies is up to us.

Questions

Who were Umayr ibn Wahb and Safwan ibn Umayyah?

Two cousins from the clan of Banu Jumah in Makkah, both early enemies of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Umayr set out to assassinate him and was guided to Islam instead. Safwan, one of the wealthiest men of Makkah, held out far longer before accepting faith.

How was the plot against the Prophet ﷺ discovered?

Umayr and Safwan had agreed in secret, with no one else present. When Umayr arrived in Madinah with a hidden poisoned sword, the Prophet ﷺ repeated their private conversation back to him word for word. Umayr understood that only Allah could have revealed it, and he accepted Islam.

Why did Safwan take so long to accept Islam?

Safwan was deeply tied to the leadership of Quraysh and had lost his father at Badr. He swore he would never follow this religion. He returned to Makkah under the Prophet's protection, asked for time, and only believed after the Prophet's repeated generosity softened his heart.

What can we learn from their story?

That no heart is beyond guidance, that patience and mercy can change an enemy where force never could, and that we should be slow to decide who is permanently against us.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to keep a private list of people we have quietly given up on. The relative who wronged us. The person whose mind we are sure will never change. Our own selves, on the days we feel too far gone. The story of these two cousins is a gentle argument against every name on that list.

One arrived with poison in his hand and left as a teacher of the faith. The other swore he would be the last man on earth to believe, and still his heart turned. Today, that asks something small and difficult of us: to meet a hard heart with patience instead of a verdict, to keep a door open one more day, and to trust that hearts belong to Allah, who can turn them when we have stopped expecting it.
