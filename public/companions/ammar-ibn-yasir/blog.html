Ammar ibn Yasir

The Son of the First Martyrs

Imagine meeting an old man in Iraq, well into his nineties, with very dark skin and a full grey beard. He is missing an ear. His chest and back carry old burns, deep scars, and wounds where the flesh never grew back. Most people who pass him do not ask. One man finally does. What happened to you?

He answers quietly: these are the marks of what Quraysh used to torture me with in the heat of Makkah. This is the story of Ammar ibn Yasir (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the first to believe, the son of the first two people ever killed for this faith, and a man who carried more than most of us could imagine without ever wearing it as a badge.

The Companions

Ammar ibn Yasir

The Son of the First Martyrs

A family that arrived from far away

Ammar's father, Yasir, came from Yemen. He was not a powerful man, and he belonged to no great tribe. He came to Makkah with his brothers searching for a brother who had disappeared there on a journey and never returned. When the others gave up and went home, Yasir stayed and tried to build a life.

He allied himself to Banu Makhzum, the clan of Abu Jahl, as a confederate: not quite a slave, not quite a full member of the tribe, but caught in the awkward space in between. He married Sumayyah, a woman born in Abyssinia who had been brought to Makkah and enslaved. Their son Ammar was born free.

When the man who had protected them died, the family was left exposed. They were broadly tied to Banu Makhzum, but they had no one of standing to defend them. So they kept their heads down, worked hard, and made sure they never drew the eye of anyone powerful in the city. You can imagine how quiet their lives had to be.

Two young men at a door

Ammar was close in age to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, a strong young man who did exactly what his parents did and exactly what they told him to do. Then he heard about the man known across Makkah as al-Amin, the trustworthy, who was now quietly calling people to one God.

He could not help himself. He went to the house of al-Arqam, where the Prophet ﷺ was teaching a small group in secret. On the way he met Suhayb, and each asked the other, carefully, what are you doing here. They realised they had come for the same reason. They went in together, the Prophet ﷺ taught them about Islam, and they walked back out of that door as believers.

His Islam was among the very earliest. He later recalled seeing the Prophet ﷺ at a time when only a handful of people stood with him: a few from among the enslaved, two women, and Abu Bakr. Ammar was one of those first few.

It was Ammar, too, who told his parents about Islam, and the first time he spoke to them they both accepted it. Take us to the Prophet ﷺ, they said, and they entered the faith quietly, the way they did everything. This matters, because of what came later: when he watched them suffer, Shaytan could have whispered that he had brought it upon them.

The hardest days in Makkah

For a while their faith stayed hidden. But word spread through Makkah one name at a time, until Abu Jahl learned that a family loyal to his own clan had embraced this religion right under his nose. He decided to make an example of the weakest people he could find: a family with no tribe to protect them and no strength to protect themselves. They were taken out into the burning midday heat, tied up, beaten, and starved, where the whole city could hear their cries.

The cruelty had a purpose. Quraysh wanted them to abandon their religion, and they wanted everyone watching to see it happen. Day after day the family was asked, where is your God now, where is your Allah. Not one of them gave up their faith. The Prophet ﷺ would pass by and weep for them and pray for them, but he could not free them or defend them. He could only say the words that have echoed ever since.

Sumayyah answered her tormentor with courage until the end. When Abu Jahl could not break her, he drove his spear into her and killed her in front of her husband and her son. She was the first martyr of Islam. Yasir, old and broken, died under the torture soon after. Ammar was left to witness it all and live.

A heart that never lost faith

Ammar was strong, so his torture lasted longer. They beat him until he no longer knew what he was saying. They set parts of his body on fire and put it out. The Prophet ﷺ would place a hand on his head and pray, as he is said to have prayed for Ibrahim: be cool and peaceful upon Ammar.

In the middle of all this, his mind reeling, Ammar repeated the words Abu Jahl demanded and spoke against the Prophet ﷺ. He was let go, and he came to the Prophet ﷺ in pieces. The Prophet ﷺ wiped the tears from his face and asked him: how did you find your heart when you said it? Fully at peace with faith, Ammar answered. I never lost faith for a moment. About people in exactly his situation, a verse was revealed:

With the exception of those who are forced to say they do not believe, although their hearts remain firm in faith, those who reject God after believing in Him and open their hearts to disbelief will have the wrath of God upon them and a grievous punishment awaiting them.Quran 16:106

Carrying double the load

Ammar escaped Makkah, first to Abyssinia, the land of his mother, and then to Madinah as soon as he learned that was where the migration would be. He had now migrated twice for the sake of Allah. He is also remembered as the only one of the migrants whose mother and father were both Muslim, and they were the first two martyrs of all.

In Madinah he helped build the first mosque, and he carried two bricks while everyone else carried one. When the Prophet ﷺ asked him why, he said: because I want double the reward. The Prophet ﷺ, who called him tenderly by his mother's name, told him he would indeed have two rewards. He is also said to have been the first to set aside a place of prayer inside his own home, a small habit that spread among the companions.

At Quba, while Ammar worked, the Prophet ﷺ once said something that would only make sense decades later: the last thing you will drink in this life will be a sip of milk. And when some feared an exhausted Ammar might die, the Prophet ﷺ said no, he will live, and he will one day be killed by a transgressing group.

The better of his two ears

Ammar witnessed Badr and every battle beside the Prophet ﷺ. At the battle of Yamama, already covered in old wounds, his ear was torn and left hanging, and he climbed onto high ground and called out to the retreating Muslims: are you running away from Paradise? Come to me. He lost most of that ear that day.

Years later a man mocked his torn ear, calling him deformed. Ammar simply replied that the man had insulted the better of his two ears, because he had lost it in the path of Allah. After everything he had endured, he saw his scars not as shame but as honour.

When Umar took charge of Kufa, he sent Ammar to lead it and Abdullah ibn Mas'ud to teach there, writing to the people that he was sending them two of the most beloved and distinguished companions of the Prophet ﷺ. Ammar lived as humbly as ever. Though he was effectively a governor, he owned a single garment and one cord to tie it, and he kept long hours of quiet worship through the night.

The criterion of truth

When the great trial came and the Muslims began to turn against one another, those who knew Ammar said no one was more sincere for the sake of Allah in those confused days. He pledged his loyalty to Ali, but only after testing where Ali stood, asking him careful questions to be sure he was standing on principle.

More than once the Prophet ﷺ had pointed to Ammar as a sign: when discord arises, look to where Ammar is killed, and you will know which side holds the truth. At the battle of Siffin, near the end, Ammar was handed a bowl of milk. He smiled, remembering the words spoken to him decades earlier, took the sip, and said that today he would be reunited with his beloved Prophet ﷺ. He was killed that day, ninety-three years old, a martyr and the son of martyrs.

There is a final note to this family. Ammar's son Muhammad became a scholar of hadith, and he too was killed, because he refused to fabricate a saying in the name of the Prophet ﷺ. The grandparents died rather than curse the Prophet ﷺ. The son was named the criterion of truth. The grandson died rather than lie about the Prophet ﷺ. Allah, it seems, chose this family for one thing across the generations: they would not bend on the truth.

What Ammar's life teaches us

Ammar's story is full of suffering, but it is not really about pain. It is about a heart that stayed clear and steady through all of it.

  • The heart is what counts. Under unbearable torture his tongue gave way, but his faith never moved. The Prophet ﷺ told him his heart was what mattered, and Allah revealed the same.
  • Carry more than you have to. He lifted two bricks for double the reward. He never decided he had suffered enough to coast. He kept reaching for more good.
  • Wear your wounds with dignity. He called his torn ear the better of the two, because he lost it for Allah. What others saw as disfigurement, he saw as honour.
  • Stand on principle, not on people. Even in the worst confusion, he tested where the truth was before he committed, and held to it without wavering.

Questions

Who was Ammar ibn Yasir?

One of the earliest people to accept Islam in Makkah and the son of Yasir and Sumayyah, the first two martyrs of Islam. He was tortured for his faith, migrated twice, fought beside the Prophet ﷺ, and was later martyred himself.

Why are Ammar's parents so important?

His mother Sumayyah (RA) was the first person ever killed for believing in Islam, struck down by Abu Jahl. His father Yasir (RA) died under torture soon after. Ammar is remembered as the only migrant whose mother and father were both Muslim.

Why is Ammar called a criterion of truth?

The Prophet ﷺ foretold that Ammar would be killed by a transgressing group, so that when discord arose, people could look to which side killed him to know where the truth lay. He was martyred at the battle of Siffin.

What can we learn from the life of Ammar?

That faith lives in the heart even when the body fails, that wounds carried for Allah are an honour, and that a believer holds to the truth no matter the cost.

Why this story stays with us

Ammar was not a chief or a man of standing. He was the strong, quiet son of a family with nowhere to hide, and he was hurt in ways most of us will never face. Yet the thing that defines him is not the torture. It is that through every burn and every blow, his heart stayed certain, and afterward he carried no bitterness, only the marks he was almost proud of.

Most of us will not be tested as he was. But all of us know what it is to be pressed, to be afraid, to want to give up on what is right because holding it costs too much. His life is a quiet reminder that the heart can stay firm even when everything else gives way, and that what we endure for the sake of God is never wasted. Hold one steady moment of faith today, the way he held a lifetime, and his story has done its work.
