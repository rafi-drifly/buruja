Umm Kulthum bint Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt

The Enemy's Daughter

Picture a man standing over the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ while he is in prostration, emptying the filth of a camel onto his back to make the elites of Makkah laugh. That man had children. He raised them on the hatred he carried.

This is the story of Umm Kulthum bint Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt (may Allah be pleased with her): the daughter of that man, who walked away from her family alone, for no reason but that she believed. Of all the houses in Makkah, guidance reached this one, and it reached her first.

Umm Kulthum bint Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt

The Enemy's Daughter

The man who made Fatimah cry

Go back to a moment in Makkah. The Prophet ﷺ was praying in front of the Kaaba, watched by his enemies, and they set a challenge among themselves. Abu Jahl asked who would take the entrails of a slaughtered camel and dump them onto the back of Muhammad ﷺ while he prostrated, to humiliate him in his prayer. One man volunteered: Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt.

He gathered all the filth of the camel and emptied it over the Prophet ﷺ in his sujood, and the weight of it collapsed him where he prayed. It was one of the most humiliating moments of those years, and the elites of Quraysh roared with laughter at it.

Word reached his young daughter Fatimah (may Allah be pleased with her) at home. This was after the death of her mother Khadijah, and she was a girl who should never have had to witness such a thing done to her father. She ran out in tears and cleaned his back with her own hands. He comforted her: do not be sad, he told her, Allah will give victory to your father. That same day he made a prayer against some of those men, who would later be killed at Badr.

This is the cruelty Uqbah was known for, a man willing to sink to the lowest of the low to prove his opposition to the Prophet ﷺ. And these are the children he was raising in the shadow of it.

Hope in the children of his enemies

When you see that scene unfold, think about the children of that man. Think about what they were raised on, the hatred of the Prophet ﷺ that they were taught from the beginning. It would have been easy to assume nothing good could ever come from such a house.

But the Prophet ﷺ carried hope for many of his enemies, and especially for the next generation, the children of those enemies. And of all people, out of all the houses in Makkah, every one of Uqbah's six children would enter Islam.

Al-Walid, who later served as a governor under Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him). Amarah. Khalid. Umm Hakim. Hind. And the one who stands out among them, the woman of this story, who would precede her brothers and sisters into the faith.

Umm Kulthum was a half-sister of Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him): they shared the same mother, Arwa. Uthman was among the earliest to believe, and now his sister would follow. She did not wait for the conquest of Makkah, when faith had become safe and many of the old enemies finally submitted. She believed early, and she believed at a cost.

The first woman to migrate alone

What sets her apart is not only that she believed early. It is how she did it. Umm Kulthum is remembered as the first woman to make hijrah by herself, from her own family's home. She was not married. No husband led her out, and no relative travelled at her side. She simply left, to join the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah.

This was around the time of Hudaybiyyah, not the earlier migration from Makkah that most people picture. The timing is what made it so dangerous. One of the terms of the treaty of Hudaybiyyah was that anyone who came from Makkah to join the Prophet ﷺ would be returned. To leave at that moment was to walk straight into a door that the treaty had agreed to shut behind her.

We know the painful scene of Abu Jandal (may Allah be pleased with him), who arrived at Hudaybiyyah only to be returned to his captors under that same condition. Now a young woman from one of the most powerful houses in Makkah escaped and crossed the desert into Madinah, knowing exactly what the treaty said and what it might cost her.

I am not like the men

Her brothers Amarah and Al-Walid, still disbelievers then, came to the Prophet ﷺ and demanded he return their sister according to the terms that had been agreed.

Umm Kulthum protested. O Messenger of Allah, she said, I am not like the men. You know what they will do to me, and they did not mean women like me when they made that condition with you. The treaty had young men in mind: the loyalties of battle and tribe, what a returning fighter meant. It was never meant for a woman alone, vulnerable, with no one to protect her if she were dragged back.

The Prophet ﷺ was in a difficult position, because he acted only upon revelation. And a verse came down for her situation. Allah commanded the believers to test the faith of the women who came to them as migrants, for He knows best about their belief, and not to return the ones found to be true believers to the disbelievers. She was allowed to stay, rescued from the very thing she feared.

You who believe, test the believing women when they come to you as emigrants- God knows best about their faith- and if you are sure of their belief, do not send them back to the disbelievers: they are not lawful wives for them, nor are the disbelievers their lawful husbands.Quran 60:10

A life she could never have imagined

Think about what it took for her to reach this point, and then think about what she was given. The daughter of Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt, who had pulled herself out of a powerful home and a powerful tribe to follow the message her father fought, was now in Madinah among the believers.

She married Zayd ibn Harithah (may Allah be pleased with him) and had two children with him, until he died as a martyr. She later married another of the great companions and had a daughter, and after they parted the Prophet ﷺ requested her hand on behalf of Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf (may Allah be pleased with him), with whom she had several children.

Consider that. She came out of that house for no reason at all except faith. No husband dragged her along, because she was not married. She left because she believed in Allah and His Messenger ﷺ, and Allah answered that faith with a place beside some of the finest men of the ummah.

Some of her children would go on to narrate hadith of the Prophet ﷺ. From the home of one of his fiercest enemies came a family that carried his words forward.

The weight of what she carried

It is worth pausing on how much faith this took. We often remember the companions who were enslaved and tortured in Makkah, and rightly so. But each one had their own kind of trial, and hers was particular.

She was not poor and powerless. She belonged to a strong family and a strong tribe, with everything that offered. To believe was to set all of it against herself: her father's name, her brothers, the only world she had ever known. And then to act on that belief, alone, in the most dangerous season to do so.

Think about the obstacles that stood naturally in front of such a person, and how much conviction it took to step past every one of them. She was not carried out by anyone. She carried herself, on nothing but the certainty that Allah and His Messenger ﷺ were telling the truth.

A long prayer over her grave

Her later years held marriage and loss. After Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf passed away, she married once more, and she died young, only a month after that final marriage. It was during the caliphate of her brother Uthman ibn Affan (may Allah be pleased with him).

It was Uthman who led her funeral prayer and saw to her burial himself. Afterward he was seen standing over her grave for a long time, making dua for her: the man of whom it was said that even the angels were shy. Imagine being the woman over whose grave such a man stood and prayed, and how blessed she must have been to be remembered that way.

Her father is remembered among people for his disbelief and his rejection of the Prophet ﷺ. We ask that she be counted among the most honoured of the believing women, for her sacrifice, for what she left behind, and for everything she crossed to reach it.

What Umm Kulthum's life teaches us

Her story is not about where she came from. It is about what she was willing to walk away from, and why.

  • Your past does not decide your faith. She was the daughter of a man who tormented the Prophet ﷺ. None of that closed the door of guidance to her, and none of it can close it to anyone.
  • Conviction sometimes means standing alone. No husband, no family, no companion on the road. She left for one reason only: she believed in Allah and His Messenger ﷺ.
  • Speak the truth of your own situation. When she feared being sent back, she did not stay silent. She explained plainly what she faced, and Allah answered her with revelation.
  • Guidance can reach the next generation. The Prophet ﷺ kept hope for the children of his enemies. All six of Uqbah's children became Muslim. Do not write anyone off.

Questions

Who was Umm Kulthum bint Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the daughter of Uqbah ibn Abi Muayt, one of his harshest enemies in Makkah. She was also a half-sister of Uthman ibn Affan (RA), and is remembered as the first woman to make hijrah by herself.

Why is she called the first woman to migrate alone?

She left her family's home in Makkah for Madinah without a husband or any relative accompanying her, purely out of faith. This took place around the time of the treaty of Hudaybiyyah.

What does the Quran say about her situation?

Her brothers demanded she be returned under the terms of Hudaybiyyah. A verse of Surah Al-Mumtahanah (60:10) was revealed instructing the believers to test the faith of migrating women and not to return those found to be sincere. She was permitted to stay in Madinah.

What can we learn from her life?

That a person's origins never limit where faith can take them, that conviction is sometimes carried alone, and that the Prophet ﷺ never gave up hope on the children of even his fiercest opponents.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think faith is shaped by where we begin: the family we were born into, the things we were taught, the people around us. Umm Kulthum's life quietly refuses that. She came from the worst possible house and still found her way out, alone, because she had decided what was true and would not unsay it.

Most of us are not asked to cross a desert. We are asked to choose what is right in the small rooms of our own lives, sometimes with no one walking beside us, sometimes against the very people we love. Her story is an invitation to believe in what is good even when it costs us, and to speak honestly about our own fears the way she did, trusting that Allah hears them.

And there is a second gift in her story. The Prophet ﷺ never gave up on the children of his enemies, and from that patience came six believers out of one bitter house. It is a reminder not to write anyone off: not the people who seem furthest from guidance, and not our own hearts on their hardest days.
