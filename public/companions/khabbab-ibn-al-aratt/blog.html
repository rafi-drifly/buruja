The Companions

Khabbab ibn al-Aratt

The First to Be Tortured

He was a young enslaved boy in the slave market of Makkah, bought to make money for someone else, with no name anyone cared to know and no future anyone could see.

This is the story of Khabbab ibn al-Aratt (may Allah be pleased with him): the swordsmith who heard the call to one God, said yes before it was safe to, and paid for it with his own flesh.

The Companions

Khabbab ibn al-Aratt

The First to Be Tortured

A boy with no future

A woman named Umm Anmar was walking through the slave market of Makkah, the same market where so many of the famous incidents of the seerah took place. She was not only looking for help with her chores. She wanted someone she could train to earn money for her.

Among the enslaved boys she saw one who looked different: signs of intelligence, signs of strength, something distinguished in his face. She pointed and said, that one. As she walked home with him she asked his name. Khabbab, he said. He was from Banu Tamim, an Arab, taken as a child when his tribe lost a war and everything they had, their livestock, their women, their children, was carried off.

All he had ever known was slavery. He had been passed from master to master, traded from place to place. Recognising that he was capable, Umm Anmar had him trained by a blacksmith to make swords.

The swordsmith of Makkah

Khabbab learned the craft and surpassed his teacher. Still only a teenager, he became a known expert: a swordsmith with his own workshop, the man you went to if you wanted a blade made or sharpened. People knew him for his skill, his integrity, and his good character.

But none of it was his. He earned a reputation and earned nothing else. His talent built weapons for the nobles of Quraysh and wealth for the woman who owned him. He had perfected something rare, and it bought him no freedom, no money, no future of his own.

Then he heard the call of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ to the oneness of God, and to the rejection of the idols and everything they stood for.

He did not wait

The call reached him while the Prophet ﷺ was still inviting people quietly, family by family. Khabbab did not wait for the nobles to debate it. He did not wait for the news to spread, or for it to become acceptable even among the weak and the poor of the city.

It simply made sense to him. He had not studied theology. He came to it through his nature: the oneness of God was clear, and what it meant, that people are not made to be owned and exploited, was clear too. He went straight to the Prophet ﷺ and said, extend your hand so I can pledge myself to you.

Some scholars count him as the sixth person to accept Islam, others the seventh or eighth. He is always among the first ten. And many say he was the first person to make his Islam public, and the first to be tortured for it. He hid nothing. A man famous for crafting the swords of Quraysh, with no protection in society, said openly that he had left the idols and followed Muhammad ﷺ.

The first to be tortured

When Umm Anmar heard, she sent her brother with a group of young men to find out if it was true and to deal with him if it was. They came to Khabbab at his work and said they had heard something unbelievable, that he had abandoned the religion of his people. He answered without flinching: I have not abandoned anything. I have believed in Allah, and rejected your idols, and I believe that Muhammad ﷺ is the servant and messenger of Allah.

There was quiet dignity in his answer, a refusal to let them decide who he was. He was a slave they had never valued. Now, suddenly, they cared what he believed. As soon as he spoke, they fell on him. They beat him unconscious and struck his head with the iron bars he used in his own work, until he lay bleeding in the street. Some scholars say this was the first time such a thing happened to a Muslim. He was between sixteen and eighteen years old.

When he woke, they asked again, and he said the same words again. So they starved him, lashed him, clamped him in metal under the burning sun. They experimented with ways to break him, and nothing worked. In their rage they laid burning coals on his back until the flesh cooked away. Umm Anmar would take a red-hot iron and rake it across his head. The agony made him faint. He endured all of it alone, with almost none of the Qur'an yet revealed, with no teacher and no friend beside him, holding on for the sake of Allah by himself.

Are you not going to call upon Allah for us?

Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) eventually bought his freedom, as he did for many of the tortured and enslaved believers. When his father asked why he kept freeing people who brought him no benefit, Abu Bakr said he was doing it only for Allah. Khabbab was freed not to make swords, but for the sake of God, and from then on he stayed beside the Prophet ﷺ.

Even free, he had no clan to shield him, so the abuse continued. One day Khabbab came to the Prophet ﷺ, who was resting in the shade of the Kaaba, and asked, are you not going to call upon Allah for us? Are you not going to ask Him to make a way out? There was the impatience of a man who had suffered more than anyone.

The Prophet ﷺ sat up, and his face reddened. He said that before them were people combed with iron until nothing of their flesh remained on their bones, and a man sawn in two, and none of it made them leave their religion. Then he said: Allah will surely complete this matter, until a traveller can journey alone fearing no one but Allah. But you are being hasty.

He lived to see it

There is a cruel patience to what Khabbab was being asked to trust. He might not live to see the victory at all, the Prophet ﷺ was telling him; he might die under the torture. But the faith he was tortured for would thrive, because Allah keeps His promise. Hold on.

And Khabbab lived to see it. He saw the men who had tortured him fall, one after another. The Prophet ﷺ even came to him after the battle of Badr to tell him that Allah had dealt with the one who had killed his mother. Umm Anmar, who had combed his head with hot iron, was struck with a sickness of the head so severe that in her pain she raked her own flesh the way she had once raked his, until she perished. Khabbab watched it happen.

He saw the day there was an assembly of the believers, no longer slaves in the desert under stones, but honoured companions, and the man who had once hunted them sitting among them as a leader. Once, asked about those early days, Khabbab lifted his shirt and showed his back. The flesh had never grown back. It had melted away and never fully healed, decades later still bearing what he had carried.

Years before, he had been teaching the Qur'an in a house when word came that Umar was at the door, on his way to kill the Prophet ﷺ. Khabbab hid behind a curtain, because nothing would have stopped Umar from killing him. From there he listened as Umar, having struck his own sister, sat and read the page she had been learning, and was changed by it. Khabbab stepped out and told him he hoped the prayer of the Prophet ﷺ had been answered through him. That man went on to the Prophet ﷺ and accepted Islam. The slave hiding behind the curtain had helped soften the heart the whole community had feared.

He never felt he was owed

There was a man who owed Khabbab money, perhaps for a blade he had made, and refused to pay unless Khabbab disbelieved in Muhammad ﷺ. Khabbab told him, I will not disbelieve until you die and are resurrected. These were people who denied any life after death, and the man scoffed: then I will have wealth and children there too, and I will pay you then. About that arrogance Allah revealed a verse:

In his last days Khabbab became wealthy and generous, and it frightened him. He wept, remembering companions who had died with nothing, their reward untouched and waiting. He feared that he had been given his good things here, in this life, and that nothing would be left for him with Allah.

Think about who was saying this. A man whose back had been burned to the bone for his faith was afraid he had not done enough. If Khabbab did not feel entitled, after all of that, then none of us are owed anything. Everything he endured had been for Allah, and he held no claim over Him for it.

Have you considered the man who rejects Our revelation, who says, 'I will certainly be given wealth and children'?Qur'an 19:77

What Khabbab's life teaches us

His story is not one heroic moment. It is a young man holding the same answer, over and over, while everything was done to make him let go.

  • The truth can reach anyone. Khabbab had no learning and no standing. The oneness of God simply made sense to his nature, and he answered before it was safe. Sincerity does not wait for permission.
  • Conviction is shown under pressure. Beaten, starved, and burned, he repeated the same words each time he woke. What we believe is proven not when it is easy, but when it costs us something.
  • Trust the timing of the outcome. The Prophet ﷺ told him relief was certain but he was being hasty. The believer is patient, watches the plan unfold, and does not demand that it arrive on his own schedule.
  • Stay humble before your own good. After all he suffered, Khabbab feared he had been given too much, not too little. He held no sense of being owed. Neither should we.

Questions

Who was Khabbab ibn al-Aratt?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He was an enslaved Arab from Banu Tamim, trained as a swordsmith in Makkah, who was among the first to accept Islam and is often described as the first to declare it openly and the first to be tortured for it.

Why is Khabbab remembered for torture?

Because he had no clan to protect him and refused to hide his faith, his owners and others abused him without restraint: beating, starvation, lashing, hot metal under the sun, and burning coals laid on his back. Decades later the scars on his back had still not healed.

What did the Prophet ﷺ say to him when he asked for relief?

He reminded Khabbab of believers before him who endured even worse and never abandoned their faith, and he promised that Allah would complete this matter until a lone traveller could journey in safety. He also told him he was being hasty.

What can we learn from the life of Khabbab?

That sincere faith can reach anyone, that conviction is proven under pressure, that we should trust the timing of God's promise rather than rush it, and that even a life of great sacrifice need not breed a sense of entitlement.

Why this story stays with us

Khabbab had every reason to feel the world owed him something, and he felt the opposite. A man whose body had been cooked for his faith worried, at the end, that he had been given too much. That is not weakness. It is a heart that never made its worship into a transaction.

Most of us will never be tested the way he was. But the impatience he felt in the shade of the Kaaba is familiar: the quiet demand that relief come now, that our patience be rewarded on our terms. His life gently asks us to hold our answer steady, to trust that the outcome is in better hands than ours, and to stop keeping a ledger with the One we are serving. Carry that into one hard moment today, and his story has done its work.
