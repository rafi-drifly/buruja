The Companions

Abu Bakr as-Siddiq

There Will Never Be Another One

There are people who give so much that no thanks could ever settle the account. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said as much about one man: every favour anyone ever did for him, he had repaid, except for the favour of Abu Bakr, which only Allah could repay.

This is the last part of his story: Abu Bakr as-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him), the closest companion, the one the Prophet ﷺ said no one could ever replace, and the first to carry the community when the Prophet was gone.

A name, and a father

When pilgrimage was finally permitted, the Prophet ﷺ sent a delegation to Makkah a year before he would make the journey himself, and he placed Abu Bakr in charge of it as the leader of that first Hajj. The next year, when the Prophet ﷺ made his own pilgrimage, Abu Bakr did not go. His wife had just given birth to his only son born in Islam, at the very place where pilgrims prepare to enter the rites. He named the boy Muhammad. A man who loved the Prophet ﷺ above himself gave his one son in Islam the dearest name he knew.

Then came the day Makkah was reopened to the Muslims, and the last person in Abu Bakr's family to accept Islam came forward: his father, Abu Quhafa. By then the old man was nearly blind, his hair and beard completely white, and he could not walk on his own. Abu Bakr led him by the hand to the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet looked at the frail, elderly man and said gently, why did you not leave him in his home? I would have gone to him. Abu Bakr answered that it was more fitting for his father to be brought to the Prophet than the other way around.

Then Abu Bakr began to weep. The companions were puzzled: this was the day his family was complete, a day of joy. He said he was not crying for himself. He had hoped the hand taking that pledge would have been the hand of the Prophet's uncle, because he knew how much the Prophet ﷺ had longed for that. Even in his own family's happiest hour, his first thought was the Prophet's heart, not his own.

Leave my companion alone

One day Abu Bakr walked into the mosque looking shaken. The Prophet ﷺ, who knew him so well, told those around him that Abu Bakr must have had an argument. Abu Bakr came and admitted it plainly: he had quarrelled with Umar, said things he should not have said, and gone to apologise, but Umar would not accept it. So he had come to the Prophet ﷺ for help.

Soon Umar arrived too, regretting the same thing. He saw that the Prophet ﷺ was visibly upset, and tried to take the blame himself. But the Prophet would not let the moment pass lightly. He reminded them: when I came to all of you, you said I was lying, and Abu Bakr said I was telling the truth. He supported me with himself and his wealth when no one else would.

Then he asked, twice, will you not leave my companion alone? The one who narrated it said no one troubled Abu Bakr after that. The point was clear to everyone present. The Prophet ﷺ was not weighing who had won the argument. He was saying that a man who never hesitated to believe in him deserved no hesitation in being forgiven.

If I were to take a closest friend

The Prophet ﷺ said that no one's wealth had ever benefited him the way Abu Bakr's had, in the earliest and hardest days, when help cost the most and was hardest to find. He used the same word for Abu Bakr's giving that he had once used for Khadijah: he gave me his all. As no one could replace Khadijah, no one could replace Abu Bakr.

Then he said something he repeated more than once. If I were to take a single closest friend from among people, it would be Abu Bakr. But that closeness belongs to Allah alone, so what holds us together is the brotherhood of faith.

Near the end of his life, sitting in the mosque, the Prophet ﷺ told the companions to close every private door that opened into the mosque, except the door of Abu Bakr. To this day, when you enter the Prophet's mosque in Madinah, you can see where that gate stood. He let one door remain, because for everything that one man had done, he could never be repaid.

Leading the prayer, and the day he passed

As his illness grew worse, the Prophet ﷺ began pointing people toward Abu Bakr. A woman came asking what she should do if she returned and did not find him. Go to Abu Bakr, he told her. When the Prophet ﷺ became too weak to lead the prayer, he instructed that Abu Bakr should lead it. Some, knowing his voice was soft and that he wept when he recited, suggested someone with a stronger voice. The Prophet insisted: tell Abu Bakr to lead the people. For the last three days and more, it was Abu Bakr who stood before the rows. Once the Prophet ﷺ found a little strength and was helped out to pray, and he sat beside him, the people following the voice of Abu Bakr, and Abu Bakr following the Prophet ﷺ.

Abu Bakr was outside the city when the Prophet ﷺ passed away. He rode back into a mosque full of weeping and panic, and he did not stop to speak to anyone. He went straight to where the Prophet ﷺ lay, uncovered his face, kissed his forehead, and wept. How pure you are, he said, in life and in death.

Then he walked out to a crowd in chaos. Umar, refusing to believe it, was threatening anyone who said the Prophet ﷺ had died. Abu Bakr stood and steadied the whole community with a few words: whoever used to worship Muhammad, know that Muhammad has died; and whoever worships Allah, know that Allah is living and never dies. I am not the best of you

For all the honours the Prophet ﷺ had given him, Abu Bakr never let them make him proud. His first words as leader were not a list of his own virtues. He told the people plainly: I have been placed in charge of you, but I am not the best of you. If I do well, support me; if I do wrong, set me straight. He told them truthfulness is a trust and lying is a betrayal, and that they owed him obedience only so long as he obeyed Allah and His Messenger. If he strayed from that, he said, they owed him nothing.

He taught the same lesson to Umar without speaking of himself at all. When the Prophet ﷺ had appointed a young man, Usama ibn Zayd, to lead an army, some of the elders wanted the decision reconsidered. Umar carried the request to him. Abu Bakr took hold of Umar's beard and asked: do you want me to undo what the Prophet ﷺ himself had ordered? Umar lowered his head, embarrassed, and apologised. He later said he hated to upset Abu Bakr.

In just over two years as caliph, Abu Bakr held the young community together and set it on the path the Prophet ﷺ had drawn. He governed not from a place of power, but from a place of trying to keep the trust. He won the people by his transparency and his plain sincerity, and of the first four caliphs he was the only one not to be killed. The humility that won their hearts was the same humility he had carried his whole life.

The same day, the same age

Two years into his leadership, Abu Bakr fell ill with a fever that would not leave him. He asked his daughter Aisha to compare the wealth he had now to the wealth he had owned before becoming caliph, and to return any difference to the treasury. He did not want a single thing to stain his name after he was gone.

He asked how old the Prophet ﷺ had been when he died. Sixty-three, she said. He smiled, for he too was sixty-three. He asked how many shrouds the Prophet had been wrapped in, and chose the same. When his daughter offered new cloth, he said the new was more useful to the living than to the dead, and kept the old.

He asked what day the Prophet ﷺ had died, and was told it was a Monday. It was Monday again. He said, then let it be today. He lay down and repeated the prayer of Yusuf, asking Allah to let him die in faith and join the righteous. As the last words left his lips, he passed: the same day, the same age, in the same house, beside the one he had never left.

What Abu Bakr's life teaches us

His greatness was never loud. It was a steady, lifelong habit of putting truth and others before himself, even when no one was watching.

  • Loyalty shows up in the small hours. Long after he was honoured as the first of the companions, Abu Bakr was still quietly serving those no one else served. Status never made him stop trying to do better.
  • Humility is the strength of a leader. His first words in charge were, I am not the best of you. Correct me when I go wrong. He won the community by transparency, not by listing his credentials.
  • Love should clarify, not cloud. When grief could have swept him away, his love for the Prophet ﷺ made him steadier, not blinder. He held the people to the truth on the hardest day of all.
  • Give without keeping score. He spent himself and his wealth so completely that the Prophet ﷺ said only Allah could repay him. He never asked to be repaid.

Questions

Who was Abu Bakr as-Siddiq?

The closest companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the first adult man to accept Islam, and the first caliph after the Prophet's death. The Prophet ﷺ said no one after the prophets and messengers was better than him.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ leave only Abu Bakr's door open to the mosque?

Near the end of his life, the Prophet ﷺ ordered every private door opening into the mosque closed, except Abu Bakr's. It was a public sign of his place, given because the Prophet felt he could never fully repay all that Abu Bakr had done for him.

How did Abu Bakr die?

He fell ill with a fever about two years into his leadership. He died at sixty-three, the same age as the Prophet ﷺ, on a Monday as the Prophet had, and was buried in the same house, beside him.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Bakr?

Quiet, lifelong loyalty; humility in leadership; love that holds to the truth instead of being swept away by it; and generosity that never keeps score.

Why this story stays with us

It would have been easy for Abu Bakr to rest on what the Prophet ﷺ had said about him. He was called the most truthful, the closest friend, the first. He believed every word of it, and it never once made him complacent. He kept getting up before dawn to serve people who could not thank him, and he kept calling himself the least of them.

Most of us will never lead a community or stand beside a prophet. But every day we are handed small chances to be loyal when it costs us, to admit we were wrong, to give without counting, to keep our heads clear when feelings run high. Abu Bakr's life is an invitation to take one of those chances today, in the quiet, where only Allah is watching.
