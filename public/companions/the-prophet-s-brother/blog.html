Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith

The Prophet's Brother

A year before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ passed away, you might have seen a man in Madinah who looked almost exactly like him. He kept his head down. He was the first into the mosque and the last to leave. His eyes were always wet with tears, and he spoke to almost no one.

If you asked the Prophet ﷺ who that was, he would tell you: that is my brother. The answer would have surprised you, because for twenty years this same man had been one of his most determined enemies. This is the story of Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith (may Allah be pleased with him).

Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith

The Prophet's Brother

As close as a brother could be

Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith was the Prophet's first cousin, a son of al-Harith, the eldest son of Abd al-Muttalib. His father had once dug at the well of Zamzam beside that same grandfather, throwing the dirt aside as they uncovered the water that pilgrims still drink today. From the very start, this family was bound together at the deepest level.

He was born around the same year as the Prophet ﷺ, and in that society a paternal cousin was as close as a brother. He was also nursed by the same woman who nursed the Prophet ﷺ, so he was a milk-brother too. Later he married into the family of Abu Talib, the uncle who raised the Prophet ﷺ like a father.

On top of all this, he looked strikingly like the Prophet ﷺ. He was counted among the small handful of people whose resemblance to him was remarkable. When he travelled to Syria, people who saw him would say at once: this must be the cousin, the brother, of that man, because the likeness was unmistakable.

Two brothers, one childhood

The two of them grew up joined at the hip. They were closest friends. They gave each other gifts, attended one another's weddings, and brought presents back from their journeys, exactly the way blood brothers would.

Abu Sufyan was known for his eloquence and his fine manners. He was a poet who wrote in praise of noble tribes and noble men. People rub off on one another, and a lifetime beside the Prophet ﷺ had left its mark on him. Anyone watching their lives in Makkah would have seen two cousins as inseparable as family can be.

So when the revelation came, the Prophet ﷺ expected that this man, of all people, would be among the first to recognise the truth and stand beside him. He had shown him nothing but good his whole life. Surely his brother would see in him what others were already beginning to see. He was waiting for his support.

The brother who turned away

Instead, when the Prophet ﷺ called his own clan, Banu Hashim, to Islam, Abu Sufyan turned his back. All that closeness hardened into open hostility. The Prophet ﷺ had thought his people might run him out, but never imagined the rejection would come from those nearest to him.

Scholars who read his poetry see envy in it, and something harder to name. The two of them had been equals their whole lives, married at the same time, raising children at the same time. Now one of them was a Prophet, and that ruptured everything. There was also a bitterness that said: everything was fine, why did you bring this and complicate our lives?

It was not a forced or half-hearted enmity. It was genuine. For twenty years he used both his tongue and his sword against the Prophet ﷺ. He became the leading poet attacking him, trading verse for verse with Hassan ibn Thabit, page after page, and he did not miss a single battle against the Muslims.

Much of the harsh poetry the books attribute to a man named Abu Sufyan in those years was in fact his, not that of the more famous Abu Sufyan ibn Harb. The likeness between this brother and the Prophet ﷺ even shaped how he could be answered. When Hassan offered to reply, the Prophet ﷺ asked how he would do it without wounding him too, since insulting Abu Sufyan's family and face meant insulting his own. Hassan promised to draw the Prophet ﷺ out of it the way a hair is drawn from dough.

This pain ran so deep that the Prophet ﷺ would not speak about him and could not bring himself to look at him for years afterward. It is part of his human example: even in pain he never wronged anyone, but he did feel the hurt of a brother turned enemy.

The day he ran out of excuses

One by one, his brothers and his sister accepted Islam. His own wife, Jumana, leaned toward it and could not understand her husband's stubbornness. By the conquest of Makkah, he was the last of them still holding out.

When the Prophet ﷺ arrived at the edge of Makkah with a great army, Abu Sufyan understood that it was over. He went to his wife and his son, unsure what to do. His wife shamed him gently: the whole world is embracing this man, and you, who should have been the first to support him, are the one still insisting on enmity. He said he was covered in shame.

He set out to find the Prophet ﷺ, wrapping his face, not knowing whether he would be killed by him or by someone else along the way. He left his camel and walked the last stretch on foot, so as not to cause any commotion. It was the posture of a man coming not to fight, but to apologise.

Forgiven, but not yet welcomed

When he uncovered his face before the Prophet ﷺ, the Prophet turned away. He tried again to meet his eyes, and again the Prophet turned from him. There was no aggression and no harm, but there was no warmth yet either. The Muslims saw it, and their anger rose. Abu Bakr (RA) told him to leave. Umar (RA) was harsher still. A young man of the Ansar stood and rebuked him for everything he had done.

He went to the Prophet's uncle, Abbas, who interceded for him. The Prophet ﷺ said he would be forgiven, but that he could not simply pretend none of it had happened: this was the cousin who had attacked his honour. Abbas counselled patience. Ali, his brother-in-law, told him the same: give it time.

Day after day the Prophet ﷺ softened a little more, yet the old closeness had not returned. Abu Sufyan was grateful only to be forgiven, but the full look of love he longed for was not there. He told himself that if it stayed this way, he would take his son into the desert and disappear until he died, rather than remain in the presence of the man he had wronged so badly.

The smile he was waiting for

The change finally came at the battle of Hunayn. When the Muslim army was ambushed and scattered, only a few remained around the Prophet ﷺ. Abu Sufyan was one of them. He took hold of the reins of the Prophet's mule on one side and began to fight, determined to have his back now the way he should have from the very beginning.

In the middle of the fighting the Prophet ﷺ asked who this man was, clinging to him and defending him. The answer came: your brother, Abu Sufyan. And there, at last, the Prophet ﷺ welcomed him, prayed for him, and embraced him.

He said he hoped Allah had given him this brother in place of Hamza, the beloved uncle and milk-brother whose death at Uhud had grieved him so deeply. It was a precious thing to say, and Abu Sufyan never forgot it.

A man who lived in the mosque

Back in Madinah, the companions who had once shunned him now honoured him, because they saw how the Prophet ﷺ honoured him. But Abu Sufyan did not coast on his lineage or his new status. He set out to make up for twenty lost years.

He fell in love with the Quran and became known for it, reciting day and night. He grew quiet, almost like a hermit. He was the first into the mosque and the last to leave, praying through the heat of the long summer days, his eyes welling with tears. When Aisha (RA) asked who he was, the Prophet ﷺ told her: that is my cousin, Abu Sufyan, the first to enter the mosque and the last to leave it, and his eyes never leave his sandals.

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Abu Sufyan kept this life unchanged. He carried the weight of knowing he had missed the greater part of those years, with so little time to stand beside the Prophet ﷺ as a Muslim. He grieved the loss in some of the most tender poetry ever written for him, saying he wept the whole night over the greatest tragedy ever to reach the earth, and calling that the saddest night in all of history.

Near the end of his own life, while still healthy enough to recover, he went to the graveyard beside the Prophet ﷺ and dug his own grave with his own hands, asking Allah to let him follow his brother. Three days later, as his family wept around him, he told them not to cry: he had not committed a single sin since the day he became a Muslim. He was buried in the section closest to the family of the Prophet ﷺ.

What Abu Sufyan's life teaches us

His story is not a clean one. It is a man who got almost everything wrong for twenty years, and then spent the rest of his life getting it right. That is exactly why it reaches us.

  • It is never too late to turn back. He came at the very end, after two decades of enmity, and he was still received, still forgiven, still loved. No history is too far gone to repair.
  • Forgiveness can be real even when it takes time. The Prophet ﷺ forgave him at once, yet the warmth returned slowly. Holding both is part of being human: we can pardon someone and still need time for the hurt to heal.
  • Repentance is shown in a changed life. He did not simply say the words. He gave the rest of his days to the mosque and the Quran, quietly trying to make up for the time he had lost.
  • A quiet, sincere ending can outshine a loud past. The man who once wrote poems against the Prophet ﷺ ended as the first into the mosque and the last to leave, his eyes lowered, his heart soft.

Questions

Who was Abu Sufyan ibn al-Harith?

He was a first cousin and milk-brother of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, raised alongside him and closely resembling him. He opposed the Prophet ﷺ for about twenty years before accepting Islam at the conquest of Makkah and becoming a devoted companion.

Is this the same Abu Sufyan who was a famous enemy of Islam?

No. He is often confused with Abu Sufyan ibn Harb, the well-known leader of Quraysh. This Abu Sufyan, son of al-Harith, was the Prophet's own cousin and the chief poet who attacked him in verse during those years.

How did the Prophet ﷺ receive him when he returned?

At first the Prophet ﷺ turned away from him and would not meet his eyes, because of the deep hurt of the past. He forgave him, but the warmth returned slowly, and was fully restored at the battle of Hunayn, where Abu Sufyan fought at his side.

What can we learn from his life?

That it is never too late to turn back, that genuine forgiveness can still take time to heal, and that true repentance shows itself in a changed life rather than in words alone.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us carry some version of his regret: years we wish we had spent differently, a relationship we let harden, a turn toward what is good that we kept putting off. His life says the door is still open. He came late, after a long enmity, and was still welcomed, still forgiven, still given a place close to the people he loved.

There is a second gift in his story for anyone who has been hurt. The Prophet ﷺ forgave him fully, and still needed time before the old warmth came back. If you have pardoned someone but your heart is not yet healed, you are not failing. Forgive, give it time, and let the slow work happen. Carry one of those two truths into today, and his story has done its work.
