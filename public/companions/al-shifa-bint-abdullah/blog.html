Al-Shifa bint Abdullah

The Healer and the Teacher

In a city where almost no one could read, a woman sat with a pen and taught others to hold one. She knew medicine, she knew letters, and she knew the careful art of writing well. People stopped calling her by her given name and called her the healer.

This is the story of Al-Shifa bint Abdullah (may Allah be pleased with her): one of the first to believe, a teacher of reading and writing, and a woman the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and later Umar both turned to for counsel.

The Companions

Al-Shifa bint Abdullah

The Healer and the Teacher

The woman they called the healer

Her given name was Leila. She was born into Quraysh, from the tribe of Banu Adi, the same clan as Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA), to whom she was related. She married Abu Hathma ibn Hudhayfah, and together they had two sons, Sulayman and Masruq.

She is one of those companions whose name many of us have never heard, even though she stood among the first. Her biography survives in only a handful of accounts. Yet within those few pages is a life worth slowing down for, the kind of quiet life that holds a community together without ever asking to be noticed.

People did not remember her by the name Leila. They called her al-Shifa, which means the healer, because she had learned medicine and used it to treat the sick. The nickname stayed with her for the rest of her life, until it became the only name most people knew. A name is usually given to you; hers was earned by what she did for others.

She is sometimes spoken of as if she came later, as if she belonged to the generation after the companions, because the role she is most famous for came after the Prophet ﷺ had passed away. But her place in Islam runs much deeper than that. Her reputation came before Islam, and she was among the very first people to accept the message when it arrived.

One of the few who could read

Makkah at that time was a city of speech, not of writing. Most people could not read at all. By the count of the biographers, only around twenty to thirty people in the whole city knew how to read and write, and al-Shifa was one of them.

She did more than read. She was a calligrapher, someone who could shape letters with care and beauty, and she taught that skill to others. So she carried three uncommon things at once: she could heal the body, she could read and write, and she had the patience to pass both on. Any one of these would have set her apart. She held all three.

She was known for her wisdom too, her hikma, the kind of judgement people come back to. We do not know the exact moment she accepted Islam, but we know it was within the first three years of the Prophet's call, and she was among the first women to make the Hijrah to Madinah. Her faith was not a late arrival. It was there at the very beginning, when believing was hard and the believers were few.

A house between the mosque and the market

In Madinah she was given a home in a place that suited her exactly: between the mosque and the marketplace. It became a well-known house in the city, a quiet meeting point with worship on one side and daily trade on the other. It was the right address for a woman who understood both prayer and the affairs of ordinary life.

The Prophet ﷺ trusted her judgement and took her counsel in many matters, especially questions of money and the marketplace, where she had a particular understanding. That is worth pausing on. He sought her advice not for small things, but for the workings of the community. He would visit her family from time to time, and she lived for those visits.

So she prepared. She kept a special wrap and blanket set aside, amenities meant only for him, ready for the times he would come, hoping quietly that he would come more often. When he did, he would put on what she had prepared and take his rest in their home. To host him, to learn from him, to be asked by him: this was the honour of her life, and she arranged her whole household around it.

What she left behind

Al-Shifa was a woman of means. She had her own standing, a livelihood she had built in Makkah, and an inheritance from her marriage. By the measure most people use, she had plenty to leave behind. When she died, none of that was counted as her treasure.

Her most precious belongings, the things she left for her sons Sulayman and Masruq to inherit, were the clothes and the blanket the Prophet ﷺ had used when he visited her home. Not the wealth she had earned. Not what she had inherited. The cloth he had rested in.

It is a small detail, but it tells you everything about what she valued. The most ordinary objects in her house had become the most precious, simply because of who had touched them. While she was alive, her heart had already done the sorting, and quietly decided that nearness to the Prophet ﷺ outweighed everything she owned.

Teaching Hafsa to read and to heal

One of her closest students was Hafsa (RA), the wife of the Prophet ﷺ and a relative of al-Shifa through the family of Umar. Al-Shifa taught Hafsa to read, to write, and to do calligraphy, passing her own rare skills into the next pair of hands.

There is an authentic narration in Abu Dawud where the Prophet ﷺ came in upon al-Shifa and Hafsa as they sat together. He said to al-Shifa: will you not teach Hafsa how to treat the sore of an ant bite, the same way you taught her to write? She was a healer who knew several kinds of treatment, and he approved of her continuing so long as it stayed grounded in what came from the Quran and the Sunnah, and not in the practices of the days before Islam.

This carries a weight that is easy to miss. The Quran was later gathered and kept in the house of Hafsa, and some scholars connect that to her literacy, since so few could read at all. The woman who taught Hafsa her letters had a quiet hand in that, and she could never have known it at the time. A teacher rarely sees how far a single lesson will travel.

Trusted by Umar, and the morning prayer

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, al-Shifa kept her home, and her counsel was still sought. When Umar (RA) became caliph, he would visit her house on his way to the marketplace, just as he had seen the Prophet ﷺ do. He consulted her often on the fiqh of trade and the affairs of the market, and it is even reported that he gave her a role overseeing it.

One morning Umar asked her why her son Sulayman, a man known for his recitation and his long nights of prayer, had not been present for the Fajr prayer in congregation. She answered that Sulayman had prayed through the night and had fallen asleep just before dawn.

Umar's reply has been remembered ever since. To pray Fajr in congregation, he said, is more beloved to me than praying the whole night alone. He was recalling the Prophet's words: whoever prays Isha in congregation, it is as if he prayed half the night, and whoever prays Fajr in congregation, it is as if he prayed the whole night.

It is striking that this measured, much-loved teaching reaches us through her. She was the one in the room, the one who answered the question about her son, and so her account preserved the moment. Her home was not only a place of rest for the Prophet ﷺ; it was a place where things worth remembering were said and kept.

A memory carried on

Her sons both served. Masruq was appointed as a governor and known for his knowledge. Through Sulayman her line continued, and he named his own two sons Abu Bakr and Uthman, who grew up to narrate hadith. That is part of why her words survived at all: a learned grandmother, and grandchildren careful enough to carry what she said.

Among the things she narrated is the famous account of how Umar came to be called Amir al-Mu'minin, the commander of the believers. Two men sent from Iraq came to Madinah and addressed him by that title at the door of the mosque. Umar said they had been guided to the right name: he was the commander, and the believers were the believers. From that day the name stayed. It reached us through a conversation between a grandmother and her grandson.

She lived long into the time of Umar and then passed away, leaving behind hadith recorded by Bukhari, Abu Dawud, and al-Nasa'i. When she died, it was Umar himself who led the funeral prayer over her, this early believer, healer, and teacher. May Allah be pleased with her.

What Al-Shifa's life teaches us

Her story is not built on a single famous moment. It is built on useful skill, given away patiently, in service of others.

  • Knowledge is meant to be shared. She did not guard her literacy or her medicine. She taught them, and what she taught outlived her by generations.
  • Small service can carry far. She taught one woman to read. Through that, she touched the keeping of the Quran itself. We rarely see where a single lesson lands.
  • Treasure what is sacred, not what is costly. Of all she owned, she left her children a worn blanket, because it had touched the Prophet ﷺ. Her heart valued the right things.
  • Quiet competence is honoured. Two of the greatest men consulted her on serious matters. She did not seek attention; her usefulness and wisdom spoke for themselves.

Questions

Who was Al-Shifa bint Abdullah?

One of the earliest Muslims, from the tribe of Banu Adi and a relative of Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA). She was a healer, one of the few literate women in Makkah, and a teacher who later advised the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and Umar on the affairs of the marketplace.

Why was she called Al-Shifa?

Her given name was Leila. Al-Shifa, meaning the healer, was a nickname she earned because she knew medicine and treated the sick. It stayed with her until it became the name people knew her by.

What did she teach Hafsa?

Al-Shifa taught Hafsa (RA), the wife of the Prophet ﷺ, how to read, write, and do calligraphy, and the Prophet ﷺ also asked her to teach Hafsa a way of treating the sore of an ant bite.

What can we learn from the life of Al-Shifa?

That knowledge is meant to be shared, that a small act of teaching can carry further than we ever see, and that quiet, useful service is honoured even when it never seeks attention.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will not be remembered for one dramatic act. We will be remembered, if at all, for what we patiently gave: a skill passed on, a person taught, a small service done again and again. Al-Shifa's whole life is a quiet argument that this is enough, and more than enough. She healed, she taught, she kept a corner of her home ready for a guest she loved, and through those ordinary acts she shaped people and preserved words that reached us across fourteen centuries.

There is something she carried that we can carry today: the willingness to teach what we know, and to value the sacred over the expensive. If there is something you understand that someone near you needs, give it, without keeping count and without waiting to be thanked. You may never see where it travels. She never did either, and look how far it went.
