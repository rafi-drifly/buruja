Trendsetters, Revivers, and Strangers

Five Ways to Be Among the First

There is a quiet danger in reading history. We can admire great people so much that we forget we are allowed to become like them. We treat their lives as a museum to walk through, not a road to walk on.

This is the opening of a long series called The Firsts (drawn from the lectures of Dr. Omar Suleiman). It does not begin with one companion. It begins with a question: what does it mean to be first, and which of those firsts can still be ours today?

Five Ways to Be Among the First

Why this series begins with a warning

If we only study history to admire people, we will never take part in making history ourselves. That single sentence sets the tone for everything that follows.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ spoke often of distinguished people on the Day of Judgment: the first to enter Paradise, the first to be dressed, the first to be brought near to their Lord. When we hear those words and feel only awe, we have read them wrong. Some of these firsts we can no longer be, and we are asked to follow in their footsteps. Others we can still become, and we are asked to be among them.

So the series does not move name by name through full biographies. When it reaches a companion, it studies him in one light only: not the whole of his life, but what it meant for him to be first. There are five ways to be among the first, and most of them are still open.

Three of them give this lesson its name: the trendsetters, the revivers, and the strangers. But they sit beside two others, the forerunners and the distinguished, and the five are best understood together. Here is each, in turn.

The forerunners: those who rush to good

The first group are as-sabiqun (may Allah be pleased with them), the forerunners. They are the ones who rush toward good the moment they hear of it. They do not wait for a crowd to move first. They do not drag their feet or look around to see who else is going.

The Quran names them twice in a row, the forerunners, the forerunners, and the scholars paused over that repetition. One reading is tender: they rushed to Allah in this life, so they will be rushed to Allah in the next. On the Day of Judgment there is no map and no app to find your place. These are the ones the angels escort first, straight into the shade of their Lord on a day when there is no other shade. The Quran sets them apart as a third group, smaller than the great crowds of the right hand and the left, brought near to their Lord.

There is a second reading too. The forerunners are of two kinds: those quick to answer a call to good, and those quick to flee from evil. Both are a race against hesitation. The Quran honours those who believed and gave before the conquest of Makkah, in the hardest and least convenient times, above those who came later, while reminding us that Allah has promised good to everyone.

And the hesitation itself is named as a fault. There were people in Makkah who heard the Prophet ﷺ and decided, tonight, that tomorrow they would go and accept, then woke and changed their minds. Because of that small delay they never became companions at all. Umar ibn al-Khattab, by contrast, heard the truth and went straight to the Prophet ﷺ. The lag time between hearing good and doing it is the thing being measured.

The trendsetters: the first hand to rise

Jabir ibn Abdullah (may Allah be pleased with them) describes a day in Madinah. A group of poor desert Arabs arrived, barely clothed, clearly hungry, unknown to anyone. The Prophet ﷺ looked at them and told the companions to hurry and give in charity. Then he saw them hesitate, and his face fell.

One man from the Ansar came forward with a purse full of silver and set it down. That was all it took. The others rose, went to their homes, and came back with their coins and their jewelry, until the sadness on the Prophet's face turned to joy.

That man was not only quick. He set the trend. The Prophet ﷺ said that whoever begins a good practice in Islam, one already part of the faith, and is followed in it, earns the reward of everyone who follows, without their own reward being lessened in any way. The person who removes something harmful from the road, the one who gives water to a thirsty animal: every time someone does the same and remembers them, the reward returns.

The same applies to guiding others to good, and to calling people to it openly. Sometimes the example is subtle, simply doing the good where others can see it. Sometimes it is spoken, when you teach what you know. You teach a verse, you share a sunnah, someone acts on it: the trend started with you, and so does a share of the reward, for as long as it is followed.

The strangers: those who hold the truth alone

The third group are al-ghuraba (may Allah be pleased with them), the strangers. The Prophet ﷺ said that Islam began as something strange and will return to being strange, so glad tidings to the strangers. They are described as the ones who rectify themselves when the people around them are slipping into corruption.

See the pattern. The forerunners rush toward good. The trendsetters start good and others follow. The strangers do the opposite of following: they refuse a bad trend even after it has become normal, and they hold to the truth when holding it makes them feel alone.

Abdullah ibn Umar narrated that the Prophet ﷺ took him by the shoulder and said, be in this world as though you are a stranger, or someone passing through. Ibn Umar would add: when evening comes, do not expect the morning; when morning comes, do not expect the evening. People follow popular wrong out of pressure, to fit in, to keep their worldly affairs smooth. The one who keeps the next life in front of them is not so easily pressured.

And the strangers are not passive. The truest of them rectify what others have corrupted. They notice what has been lost and quietly bring it back.

The revivers: those who bring a sunnah back to life

The fourth group are connected to the strangers. The Prophet ﷺ said that at the head of every century, Allah sends to this ummah one who revives for it its religion: someone who restores what has been lost of it.

Some try to name a single reviver for each century. In the Arabic the word is not limited to one person, so there may be several in a single age, each restoring a different part of what was neglected. The one figure almost everyone agrees upon is Umar ibn Abdul Aziz, who revived a sense of justice that the ummah had lost, returning to the way of his grandfather Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Revival can take more than one shape. Some revivers restore justice, as Umar ibn Abdul Aziz did. Others bring back a teaching, a practice, or an understanding that the people had let fade. There may be several at once, in different lands, each reviving a different part of what was lost.

There is also a smaller, nearer kind of revival open to all of us. If you find a forgotten sunnah in your own corner of life and you decide to bring it back, that too is a kind of revival. It can be something grand or something very simple. The point is the choice to be the one who restores it.

The distinguished: quality, not quantity

The last group are al-muhsinun (may Allah be pleased with them), the distinguished. They are not first because they led, and not because they revived something lost. They are set apart because they do good so well that they stand out in it. They always go the extra mile.

Ibn al-Qayyim ties this back to the strangers. A person becomes a stranger when they leave disbelief for Islam. They become a stranger again, even among Muslims, when they leave behind sins that have become normal. And the distinguished are strangers a third time: they long for good that others have neglected, the way the pious abstain from wrong that others excuse.

Excellence here is not about how much. The Quran says Allah tests us to see who is best in deeds, not who does the most. Umar ibn Abdul Aziz prayed only two units of prayer each night, never more. Yet his wife said those two were nothing like all of yours put together. Two rakahs, done with the whole heart, can weigh more than many.

There is a warning folded inside this. A person can excel in one act and neglect another. The companions once described a woman who prayed at night and fasted often, but who hurt her neighbors with her tongue. Excelling in worship did not cover for failing in piety. True excellence cannot skip over the basics on its way to the heights.

What these five firsts teach us

This is not a list of people we can only admire. It is a map of doors, and most of them are still open to us today.

  • Measure your hesitation. The forerunners were not stronger than us; they were faster. When good is in front of you, the question is simply how long it takes you to move.
  • You can start a good that outlives you. One hand rose at a fundraiser and the whole room followed. Begin a good practice, and every person who follows it adds to your account.
  • Being alone in the truth is not being wrong. The strangers held to good when it had become unpopular, even among their own people. Standing apart for the right reason is praised, not shameful.
  • Excellence is depth, not volume. Two units of prayer with a full heart outweighed a long night of distracted worship. Aim to do less better, not more carelessly.

Questions

What is The Firsts series about?

It studies companions and groups from early Islam in one specific light: what it meant for them to be first, and what that asks of us. This opening lesson lays out the five categories of firsts before the series begins moving through individual names.

What are the five ways to be first?

The forerunners, who rush to good and flee from evil; the trendsetters, who begin a good practice that others follow; the strangers, who hold to the truth when it becomes unpopular; the revivers, who restore a sunnah that was lost; and the distinguished, who do good with rare excellence.

Who is a reviver, or mujaddid?

The Prophet ﷺ said Allah sends at the head of every century one who revives the religion by restoring what has been lost of it. Umar ibn Abdul Aziz is the figure almost all scholars agree upon. On a smaller scale, anyone who brings a forgotten sunnah back to life is taking part in revival.

What does it mean that Islam began as strange?

The Prophet ﷺ said Islam began as something strange and will return to being strange, so glad tidings to the strangers. The strangers are those who keep to the truth and correct what others have corrupted, even when that makes them feel alone.

Carrying this forward

We tend to think the great names belong to another time, that the first row of believers closed long ago. This lesson gently disagrees. Some firsts can no longer be ours, and we make peace with that. But most of these doors are still open, today, in an ordinary life.

Notice too that the same drive runs through all five. The thing that pushes the stranger to abstain from a popular wrong is the thing that pushes the distinguished to long for a neglected good. It is wanting what pleases Allah more than wanting to fit in.

So you can be the first hand to rise. You can refuse a wrong that everyone around you accepts. You can bring back a small good that the people near you have forgotten. None of it needs a stage. It only needs a heart that does not wait, and that quietly wants to be among the first to please its Lord.
