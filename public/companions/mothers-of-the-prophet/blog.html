Amina bint Wahb and Halima al-Sa'diyya

The Women Who Carried Him

A teenage widow stood in Makkah holding out her newborn son, hoping a stranger would agree to nurse him. One after another the visiting women looked at the child, asked who he was, and walked past. He had no father and no fortune. He was the last baby left.

This is the story of the two mothers of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ: Amina bint Wahb, who carried him and lost everything, and Halima al-Sa'diyya (may Allah be pleased with them), the poorest woman in her tribe, who took the one child nobody else would take.

The Women Who Carried Him

A marriage of a few days

Amina bint Wahb was the daughter of a chief of Banu Zuhrah, one of the noble houses of Quraysh. While still a teenager she was married to Abdullah, the youngest son of Abd al-Muttalib, a young man barely past twenty. Two chiefs joining two families, two young people at the start of a life.

It did not last. A few weeks after the wedding, Abdullah left with a trade caravan toward Syria. He set out not knowing that his wife was already carrying a child, and that the child was the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Some narrations say the couple had been together as little as four days.

On the way home Abdullah fell ill near Yathrib, the city later known as Madinah. He told the caravan to go ahead so as not to slow them, expecting to recover and follow. He died there instead, in a place no one can now mark. Amina learned she was a widow while she was still waiting for him to come home.

It had been his first trade journey, so he left almost nothing behind: a few camels, a small flock of goats, and one servant, a woman named Baraka. Amina was barely twenty, alone but for a single brother, and carrying a child who would be born into the world fatherless.

Born an orphan

So the Prophet ﷺ was born into the world as an orphan, with a young mother in poverty, a single brother she could lean on, and an aging grandfather doing what little he could. Abd al-Muttalib named the baby Muhammad, a name no one in Makkah carried, because he wanted him to be praised in the heavens and on the earth.

It is hard to overstate how vulnerable an orphan was in that society. With no father to guard him, his small inheritance could be taken, and he could be worked like a servant by anyone who chose. People would glance at such a child and see no worth in him, nothing to gain. The Prophet ﷺ grew up under exactly those looks.

Later he would teach his followers to look for him among the weak and the overlooked, and to honour the orphan rather than turn away. He was speaking from memory. He had been that child, the one others did not take seriously, the one they assumed would never matter.

And the very first thing Allah would remind him of about his own life was this beginning. Not his victories, not his standing, but the place he started from:

Did He not find you an orphan and shelter you?Quran 93:6

The poorest woman in the tribe

The noble families of Makkah had a custom of sending their infants out to the desert to be nursed and raised for a time among a tribe called Banu Sa'd. There the children learned the purest Arabic, grew used to a simple life, and were kept away from the illnesses that travellers carried back into the city.

Once a year the nursing mothers of Banu Sa'd came to Makkah to choose children. They chose by two things: a wealthy family that would pay well, or a child being groomed for leadership who might one day repay the bond. The Prophet ﷺ offered neither. No money, no powerful father, not even an older brother to promise a future.

One of those women was Halima al-Sa'diyya. Her name itself carried a kind of tenderness: it comes from a root that means forbearing, gentle, patient. That year was a year of famine, and her home was the most stricken of all. She and her husband al-Harith arrived on a worn donkey and a camel too weak to give milk, hungry, lagging behind the others. She herself was too malnourished to nurse properly. She was, by her own account, the poorest of the lot.

The child nobody wanted

One by one the other women took their children and made their arrangements. Each time the visitors learned that Muhammad ﷺ was an orphan, they moved on. "What will his mother do for us?" they asked. By the end, Amina was still standing there holding him, and he was the only baby no one had taken.

No family had chosen Halima either, because she had nothing to offer them. She went back to her husband and said there was one boy left, an orphan, and she did not want to return home empty-handed. "Perhaps Allah will put some good in him," she said. She had no idea what she was saying. Al-Harith agreed, and they took him.

From that moment, by her own telling, things began to change. The dry camel filled with milk. The slow donkey carried her so quickly the others called for her to wait. When she first held the child to nurse him, the milk flowed for him and for her own son together. Her husband looked at her and said, "Halima, you have laid your hands on something blessed."

Two years, and then more

Halima brought the Prophet ﷺ back to her village, and for two years she was his mother in every daily sense. He grew up healthy and happy among her children, playing with her son Abdullah, loved by his older foster sister al-Shayma. When the term ended, she could not bear to give him back, and she asked Amina to let her keep him longer.

Amina, who had once held him out begging someone to take him, was puzzled by the request, and then moved by it. She agreed. Take him, keep the good around him. So Halima carried him home again, and he stayed until he was four or five years old, a full child in a family that loved him.

Amina never doubted there was something rare in her son. She said that while she carried him she felt no weight, and that at his birth she saw a light. She knew he was special long before she could understand how. She died not knowing the half of it.

A grave at al-Abwa

When the Prophet ﷺ was six, Amina took him to Yathrib to meet her relatives, the same city where his father had died. On the journey home she fell ill with a fever. They stopped at a small place called al-Abwa, and there she died, with her six-year-old son clinging to her, watching her take her last breath in the middle of nowhere.

He carried that grief quietly for the rest of his life. He rarely spoke of his own pain. When a grieving woman once snapped at him, "What do you know about loss?", he did not list his father, his mother, his grandfather, his own six children. He simply walked on, and took the weight to his Lord instead.

More than fifty years later, an old man now, he turned off a road outside Madinah and walked a long, hard distance in silence, his companions following without daring to ask. He stopped at a gently marked grave and wept until his beard was wet, and they wept with him, not knowing whose it was. Then he told them: this is the grave of my mother, Amina.

Everything comes back to him

The two who carried him in his earliest years had long since disappeared from his life. But near the end, after the conquest of Makkah and the battle of Hunayn, the past came back. A woman approached his camp and said, "I am the sister of your leader." When she was brought in, he asked for a sign only the two of them shared, and she showed it. It was al-Shayma, Halima's daughter, his foster sister.

He rose to his feet, spread his own cloak on the ground for her to sit on, and offered her a home with him for the rest of her life, honoured and cared for. And somewhere in those same tents was Halima herself. A companion later saw the Prophet ﷺ serving an old woman with his own hands and asked who she was. "This," came the answer, "is his mother, the one who nursed him."

The bond had never really been broken. Years earlier, when Banu Sa'd was struck by another famine, Halima had come to Makkah and found him grown and married to Khadijah. Khadijah, who loved to honour anyone who had honoured him, gave her forty sheep and a camel to carry her home. He was glad of it. For once he could repay the woman who had taken him in.

Halima had once accepted a child who promised her nothing. In the end she and her family received far more than the milk of camels and the grass that grew where it should not. They received Islam at the hands of the very orphan she had not turned away.

What their story teaches us

Two women, generations apart in nothing but circumstance, are remembered for the same reason: how they treated a child the world had written off.

  • See the one everybody overlooks. Every other woman walked past the orphan. Halima took him. The person with nothing to offer you may be exactly the one worth your kindness.
  • Small mercy is never wasted. She took him only so as not to go home empty-handed. That quiet, almost reluctant kindness became the honour of her whole life.
  • Pain can soften a heart instead of hardening it. The Prophet ﷺ knew loss from his first day. It did not make him bitter. It made him able to understand everyone else's grief.
  • Carry your sorrow to God, not to everyone around you. He rarely spoke of his own losses. He let his Lord hold them at night, and met others with mercy instead of complaint.

Questions

Who were the mothers of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ?

Amina bint Wahb was his birth mother, who died when he was six years old. Halima al-Sa'diyya was his wet nurse from the tribe of Banu Sa'd, who raised him in the desert for his earliest years. Both are remembered with love in his life story.

Why was the Prophet ﷺ sent to be nursed in the desert?

It was a custom among the noble families of Makkah. Children were sent to a desert tribe to grow up healthy away from the illnesses of the city, and to learn the purest form of the Arabic language.

Why did no one want to nurse the baby Muhammad ﷺ at first?

The visiting women chose children for wealth or for a powerful family. As an orphan with no father and no fortune, he offered neither, so he was passed over until only Halima, the poorest among them, agreed to take him.

What can we learn from the mothers of the Prophet ﷺ?

To value the people the world overlooks, to trust that small acts of mercy matter, and to let hardship deepen our compassion rather than close our hearts.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to give time and warmth to the people who can give something back. This story turns that instinct around. The mother who held him out was poor and alone. The woman who finally took him expected nothing and almost did not bother. Yet the whole of what came after rested on that one unguarded act of mercy.

We meet overlooked people every day: the child no one praises, the person with no status, the one easy to walk past. Halima's example asks us to stop and look again, and to trust that kindness shown to the smallest among us is never lost. And in a son who knew loss so early and grew gentle from it, there is an invitation to let our own pain make us softer, not harder. Carry that into today, and their story has done its work.
