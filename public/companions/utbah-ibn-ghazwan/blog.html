Utbah ibn Ghazwan

The Humble Governor

He stood among the first handful of people on earth to follow the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He fought in every major battle of the early years and conquered a city that would shape the history of Islam. And when he was made its governor, he refused to build himself a house.

This is the story of Utbah ibn Ghazwan (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the earliest believers, a quiet soldier whose whole life speaks through the battlefield, and a man who asked Allah to take power away from him.

The Companions

Utbah ibn Ghazwan

The Humble Governor

The seventh of seven

Years later, Utbah would stand before a crowd and say something that places him at the very beginning of this faith. He said he found himself the seventh of seven who had been with the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. Sit with that for a moment. When Islam was still a secret carried by a few people in Makkah, he was already one of them.

He came from Banu Mazin, a small tribe related to Quraysh but not of it. He was what the Arabs called a halif, a man who had pledged his allegiance to a larger tribe, Banu Nawfal of Quraysh. The Prophet ﷺ taught that the one who allies himself to a people is counted as one of them.

He was about ten years younger than the Prophet ﷺ, which puts him near thirty when he believed. People described him as tall, handsome, and strong, with large eyes, a face that carried light, and a great command of speech. He had what we would call an imposing presence.

Yet almost nothing is recorded of these early years. His was a life that would speak through service rather than through words, and in the first days there were no battles to fight, no missions to lead. So the man simply believed, early and quietly, and waited for the moments when he would be needed.

Three migrations for the sake of Allah

When the persecution in Makkah grew heavy, Utbah did not stay behind. He made the journey to Abyssinia, and then a second time. When the Prophet ﷺ settled in Madinah, he migrated there too. Three migrations in all: twice across the sea, once to the city that would become home.

His family came with him into faith. Both of his sisters accepted Islam. One of them would marry Uthman ibn Affan, and his youngest sister, Basra, would later marry Abu Hurayrah. So this quiet man became the brother-in-law of two companions whose names every Muslim knows.

In Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ paired the migrants with the helpers of the city. These pairings were never random. They matched men by temperament and strength, so that each pair could carry the other. Utbah was paired with Abu Dujana, one of the great warriors of the Ansar.

That single choice tells you what kind of man he was. To be set beside a famous warrior was to be recognised as one too. From here on, his story moves to the field, where it would stay for the rest of his life.

A veteran of every battle

Utbah fought bravely at Badr. He fought at Uhud. He fought at the trench of Khandaq. He was present in all of the major battles alongside the Prophet ﷺ, and he was sent on special raids when his particular skill was needed.

When the Prophet ﷺ passed away and some of the tribes turned back, Utbah stood with Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) in the wars of apostasy. Through all of it, not a single private conversation between him and the Prophet ﷺ comes down to us. He left behind no famous sayings from those years, only the steady record of a man who showed up wherever he was needed.

This is worth noticing. We often imagine that the people closest to the Prophet ﷺ must each have a dramatic story attached to their name. Utbah reminds us that some of the most trusted were simply present, again and again, asking for nothing.

The conquest of Basra

His most famous story comes under the leadership of Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him), who sent him against the Persian Empire. He was given a small force, somewhere between three hundred and two thousand men, but they were seasoned and brave. With them he made real gains against one of the great powers of the age.

He reached a city that was a strategic stronghold of the Persians and asked permission to take it. He understood what its fall would mean: a turning point in the long struggle against the empire. Umar granted permission, and Utbah conquered the place with his small battalion. Umar then appointed him its governor for six months. That city was Basra.

Basra mattered far beyond that single conquest. With Kufa, it became one of the two great cities of Iraq, and much of Islamic scholarship and civilisation would later flow out of it. Some historians said its name came from a word for vision, because from that high and central place you could survey the whole land.

So one of the two foundations of Iraq was laid by this quiet man and the small band of brave fighters beside him. Allah opened the city on his hands, and only Allah knows the full weight of what began there.

A governor in a tent

As Basra fell, the world rushed in with it. The spoils of war poured into the hands of the Muslims around him. Large homes began to rise, and wealth that none of them had ever known suddenly filled their possession. Utbah was uncomfortable with all of it. So the governor, the head commander, the man who had just taken the city, did not build a house. He did not move into one of the palaces left behind. He pitched a tent. While others gathered homes, he lived under cloth.

It is from this time that we have the one sermon recorded from him, preserved in Sahih Muslim in the book of asceticism. He told the people that he was the seventh of seven with the Prophet ﷺ, and that in those early days the only food they had were the leaves of a wild tree, until their gums were ruptured. He had lived through the hardest years, and he could not bring himself to sit comfortably now.

He remembered another scene too. There had been a time when he and Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas had only a single garment to share between them. Now Sa'd governed Kufa and he governed Basra, the two great outposts of Iraq held by two men who had once owned almost nothing. He did not let himself forget what they had come from.

Then he said a line that should stay with anyone who has ever been handed authority: "I seek refuge in Allah from being great in my own eyes and small in the sight of Allah."

The prayer to be released

Utbah never wanted to govern. He was a man of the field, used to a hard life for the sake of Allah, and he did not want to be drawn into the distribution of wealth and the comforts of office. But Umar had appointed him, and he could not simply abandon his post without permission.

So the next year he travelled to Madinah and begged Umar to relieve him. Umar refused. He told Utbah that he would not leave him as the only companion holding such a post, that none of them loved these positions, but that this was a trust from Allah. He insisted Utbah remain.

Think of how people destroy one another for power, how the pursuit of it corrupts and ruins. Here was a man pleading on the journey back. As he mounted his camel in Madinah, he raised his hands and prayed, "O Allah, do not return me to it." A man who had faced great armies and was almost always outnumbered, who could command animals and soldiers in the chaos of war, fell from his camel on the road home to Iraq and died. Allah answered his prayer.

A silent soldier for the sake of Allah

It is striking how little we know of a man who gave so much. He could have been the seventh person ever to accept Islam. He fought in every great battle of the early years. He conquered a city that would shape the faith for centuries. And his whole record fits in a few paragraphs.

We are used to measuring people by how much they are seen. Utbah measured himself by something else entirely. He was not interested in being known. He wanted to be useful to Allah and to His Messenger ﷺ, and then to step out of the light.

When we look at the goodness that came out of Basra, the scholars it produced and the civilisation it carried, we see the fruit of a man who asked for none of the credit. Allah alone knows what else he accomplished as a quiet soldier. May Allah be pleased with him, and allow us to inherit that same dedication.

What Utbah's life teaches us

His name fills only a few paragraphs, yet those paragraphs hold a kind of greatness that is rare and quiet. Here is what stays with us.

  • Humility is the test that comes with success. Hardship had not broken him. Victory could have. He met it by pitching a tent and refusing to let the world settle into his heart.
  • Fear being small before Allah. His prayer was not for status but against it: that he would not grow large in his own eyes while shrinking in the sight of his Lord.
  • Quiet service still counts. He narrated almost nothing and left few words behind. He simply showed up in every battle and every hard task, and Allah opened a great city on his hands.
  • Leadership is a trust, not a prize. He treated authority as a weight to carry honestly, then to set down. He wanted to be useful, not important.

Questions

Who was Utbah ibn Ghazwan?

One of the earliest people to accept Islam, by his own account the seventh of seven with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He was a commander who fought in the major early battles and later conquered the city of Basra, where he served as governor.

Why is Utbah ibn Ghazwan remembered as humble?

Although he governed Basra after conquering it, he refused to build a house and lived in a tent while others took the spoils of war. He also asked Umar to relieve him of the post, and prayed not to be returned to a position of power.

What was Utbah's connection to Basra?

He led the small force that conquered Basra from the Persians, and Umar appointed him its governor for six months. Basra would grow into one of the two great cities of Iraq and a centre of Islamic learning.

What can we learn from the life of Utbah ibn Ghazwan?

That humility is hardest to keep in times of success, that quiet and faithful service is precious to Allah even without fame, and that leadership is a trust to be carried honestly rather than a prize to be chased.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never command an army or govern a city. But all of us are handed small portions of power: over a team, a home, a budget, a younger person who looks up to us. The real test is not whether we can endure hardship. It is what happens to our hearts when things finally go well.

Utbah met success by staying small before Allah. He carried his responsibility honestly and longed to set it down rather than cling to it. He did not measure himself by what people could see, and he asked for none of the credit for the good that came through his hands.

His prayer is one we can quietly borrow today: that we never grow large in our own eyes while shrinking in the sight of the One who gave us everything. Whatever has been entrusted to us, however small, we can hold it the way he held a city, as a trust rather than a trophy, and keep our hearts humble in the moments others lose theirs.
