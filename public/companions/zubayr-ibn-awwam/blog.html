The Companions

Zubayr ibn al-Awwam

The Disciple

He was thirteen years old when his uncle wrapped him in a rug, lit a fire beside his face, and told him to leave his faith. The neighbours could hear him screaming through the night. It was not the screaming of a boy giving up. It was a boy shouting, again and again, that he would never disbelieve.

This is the story of Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the earliest to believe, the first to draw a sword in defence of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and the one the Prophet ﷺ called the disciple of this ummah.

Zubayr ibn al-Awwam

The Disciple

A boy raised to be a falcon

Zubayr was a son of Makkah's most noble line. His mother was Safiyyah bint Abd al-Muttalib, the aunt of the Prophet ﷺ, and his father was the brother of Khadijah (RA). That made Zubayr a cousin of the Prophet ﷺ through more than one route, and a child of a household that prized strength.

His mother raised him to be hard to break. She disciplined him severely, and people said she was too rough on him. She was not raising a soft boy. She was raising a falcon, and she wanted courage and nobility to be the first things in him.

He grew up an orphan in a society that mistreated orphans, and he carried that weight before he carried any other. When he embraced Islam, very early, perhaps among the first handful of believers, the weight only grew. He was twelve or thirteen, and he came to the faith alongside his close friend Talha ibn Ubaydillah at the hand of Abu Bakr.

"I will never disbelieve"

The uncle who came to break him was Nawfal ibn Khuwaylid, a man the Prophet ﷺ later called the Shaytan of Quraysh. He was the brother of Khadijah, and one of the fiercest enemies the Prophet ﷺ ever faced. The pain of that was never far from the surface.

His method with Zubayr was cruel. He would wrap the boy in a rug, shut him in a small room, and light a fire near his face so the smoke would suffocate him. He would step on him and beat him and order him to renounce his religion.

Every night Zubayr screamed until he passed out. But the people who walked past the house did not hear the cry of someone surrendering. They heard the same words over and over: laa akfur abada. I will never disbelieve. I will never turn my back on this. The courage his mother had wanted for him was already there, and it was already more than she had asked for.

The first sword

The torture did not slow him down. He kept attending the lessons in the house of al-Arqam, listening to the Quran as it came down, never missing his place beside the Prophet ﷺ. He, Talha, and Ali became a trio, close in age and closeness.

One day a rumour spread that the Prophet ﷺ had been attacked. Zubayr came running, sword drawn, breathing hard. When he reached the Prophet ﷺ and caught his breath, the Prophet ﷺ asked him what was wrong. He said he had heard that someone had attacked him.

The Prophet ﷺ smiled and asked what he would have done. Zubayr said he would have struck whoever attacked him with the sword. He had not stopped to count the odds or think about the consequences, which in those early days would almost certainly have been his own death. That is how Zubayr became known as the first man to draw his sword in defence of the Prophet ﷺ.

Across the water in Abyssinia

As the persecution grew, the Prophet ﷺ gave Zubayr permission to migrate to Abyssinia with his cousin Jafar. Most of those who made that journey went as couples. Zubayr went alone, a single young man among the believers, and there he grew close to the Negus himself and helped him learn the faith.

When a rebellion rose against the Negus and the two armies met by the Nile, the Muslims waited anxiously on the side, away from the fighting, unsure whether their protector would survive. Zubayr was the youngest, fit, and a strong swimmer. They made him a small float, and he crossed the river to see how the battle had gone.

When he swam back and the believers were straining for news, he called out across the water: Allahu Akbar. The Negus had won. Umm Salama (RA), who carried much of what we know of those years, said she did not think they were ever so relieved. It was the same instinct again: take the task no one else could, and bring the truth back.

Years later, when the Negus died far away, Zubayr stood in Madinah behind the Prophet ﷺ and prayed the funeral prayer over the man he had once known and helped. He had not just heard of the king; he had befriended him. That bond, made across a river in a strange land, stayed with him for the rest of his life.

The yellow turban at Badr

Zubayr married Asma bint Abi Bakr after Abyssinia. In Madinah, when rumours swirled that the believers were under a spell and would have no children, Asma and Zubayr were the first to be given a child: Abdullah ibn al-Zubayr, carried through the streets while the people cried out in joy. Even a birth had become a kind of victory.

His legacy, though, is tied above all to the battlefield. He was tall and lean and strong, near the height of Umar, and he could guide his horse with his legs alone while wielding two swords at once, something said of only one other man, Khalid ibn al-Walid. At Badr he was one of only two horsemen among a small, outmatched army.

He wore a yellow turban that day and cut through the ranks of the enemy, taking two serious wounds that did not stop him. His son Urwa later said there were holes in his father's body deep enough to put a finger in. And the Prophet ﷺ said that on that day, when Allah sent down the angels to fight beside the believers, Jibril and the angels came wearing the yellow turban of Zubayr.

Those who responded to God and the Messenger after suffering defeat, who do good and remain conscious of God, will have a great reward.Quran 3:172

The disciple of the ummah

At Uhud, the Muslims were more outnumbered still. When a giant of Quraysh on the finest horse called for a duel, and no one wanted to face him, Zubayr stepped forward on foot. His mother begged the Prophet ﷺ, certain her son would be killed. The Prophet ﷺ said it would be the other way. Zubayr knocked the man from his horse, took his sword, and brought the best horse on the field back to the Muslims.

When others fled that day, he was among the few who stayed and put their bodies between the enemy and the Prophet ﷺ. Years later Aisha (RA) told Urwa that the verse above had been revealed about his father and his grandfather, Abu Bakr, and those who did not flee.

Then came the trench. The Prophet ﷺ asked who would slip into the enemy fortress and bring back their plans. It was dangerous beyond reason, and three times he asked. Three times only one voice answered: Zubayr's. The Prophet ﷺ said, every prophet has a disciple, and my disciple is Zubayr. The disciples of Isa were praised in the Quran for one thing above all: they answered the call. That was Zubayr's whole life. When he returned, the Prophet ﷺ gave him the rarest words of honour, that he would sacrifice his own mother and father for him.

The years that followed only added to it. At Yarmuk he climbed the wall of the fortress and drove alone through the entire Byzantine army, all the way to the back, then turned and fought his way out again to the front. Umar, who knew courage when he saw it, said that Zubayr was worth a thousand men. Even the names he gave his children carried this stamp: while his friend Talha named his sons after prophets, Zubayr named his after martyrs and warriors, so that every time he called one of them he was calling a memory of sacrifice.

A wealth he never tasted

Zubayr lived a hard life by choice. Whenever wealth came to his house, he gave it away or lent it out, so that no money stayed with him overnight. In the process he built up a great debt, not from comfort but from generosity.

His death came not from an enemy outside but from the strife within the ummah. As the believers stood on the edge of fighting one another, Ali reminded him of a day long ago when the Prophet ﷺ had foretold that the two of them, who loved each other and had grown up together, would one day stand on opposite sides, and that Zubayr would be in the wrong. Zubayr wept, and walked away from the battle. He would not fight it.

He was killed treacherously a little later, while he prayed, by a man who had followed him from the field. And on that last day his only worry had been his debt. He told his son Abdullah to sell whatever was needed to repay it, and when the task felt impossible, to seek the help of his Master, Allah.

For four years Abdullah called out at Hajj for anyone owed money by his father. In the end it turned out far more people owed Zubayr than he owed anyone. He died a man of great wealth who never tasted a dirham of it in this life. And though he was that close to the Prophet ﷺ, he narrated only a handful of hadith, too afraid of misquoting him to say more. He died as he had prayed to die, in his prayer, and his sons would die the same way.

What Zubayr's life teaches us

His story is not one heroic moment but a single habit, repeated for a lifetime: when the call came, he answered before he counted the cost.

  • Answer before you calculate. Three times the Prophet ﷺ asked who would go, and three times only Zubayr spoke. The disciple is the one who does not wait to see if someone else will raise their hand first.
  • Conviction can be young. He was a boy of thirteen, suffocating in his uncle's fire, and he still shouted that he would never disbelieve. Faith does not wait for us to feel strong enough.
  • Give until nothing stays. No wealth slept in his house. He emptied his hands so often that he died in debt, and richer than almost anyone, with a treasure he never spent on himself.
  • Guard your tongue about what is sacred. He was beside the Prophet ﷺ for years, yet narrated only a few hadith, afraid to misspeak. Nearness gave him licence to say more, and reverence kept him quiet.

Questions

Who was Zubayr ibn al-Awwam?

A cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and one of the earliest people to accept Islam. He was the son of Safiyyah bint Abd al-Muttalib, the husband of Asma bint Abi Bakr, and one of the ten companions given the glad tidings of Paradise.

Why is Zubayr called the disciple?

After he volunteered to spy on the enemy at the Battle of the Trench when no one else would, the Prophet ﷺ said that every prophet has a disciple, and his disciple was Zubayr. The disciples of Isa were known in the Quran for answering the call to support their prophet, and Zubayr answered every call the same way.

What was special about Zubayr at the Battle of Badr?

He was one of only two horsemen in the small Muslim army, and he wore a yellow turban into the fight. The Prophet ﷺ said that the angels who came down to help the believers that day wore the same yellow turban of Zubayr.

What can we learn from the life of Zubayr?

To answer the call to do good without waiting to calculate the cost, to hold firm in faith even when young and tested, and to give so freely that wealth never settles in our hands.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never face a sword or a fire. But every day there is a quieter version of the question the Prophet ﷺ kept asking: who will go, who will help, who will do the thing that needs doing. Usually we wait, hoping someone else will raise their hand. Zubayr is the one who answered first, every time, without weighing what it might cost him.

His life is an invitation to be that person in small things: to volunteer before you are asked twice, to give without holding any of it back, to stay when staying is hard. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
