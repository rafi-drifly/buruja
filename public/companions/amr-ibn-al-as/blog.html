Amr ibn al-As

The Mind That Turned to Faith

He was the most well-travelled man in Makkah and, by reputation, the most cunning. For years he used every bit of that intelligence to plot against the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, in one battle after another. People said no one could escape his schemes.

This is the story of Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him): the politician of the Arabs, who spent the first part of his life fighting the truth, and the rest of it carrying it across the world.

The Companions

Amr ibn al-As

The Mind That Turned to Faith

The oldest of the young, the youngest of the old

Amr ibn al-As was a poet, a horseman, and a merchant of Quraysh. Imam al-Dhahabi records that he was called the most cunning of the Arabs, the one men measured themselves against in sharpness of mind. He combined nearly every quality his people prized.

He was older than Umar ibn al-Khattab and Khalid ibn al-Walid, but younger than the elders who first declared war on the Prophet ﷺ. That placed him between two generations: old enough to sit with the chiefs, young enough to stand with their sons. He used to say he remembered the night Umar was born.

Allah was also preparing him in a way no one could see. As a trader he had crossed into Syria, Egypt, Abyssinia, Yemen, and beyond, again and again. He knew their roads, their cities, and their leaders. Decades before he would return to those lands as a Muslim, he already knew the ground beneath his feet.

He was known for his generosity, and he inherited his father's entire fortune. Later, as a Muslim, the Prophet ﷺ would watch him give that wealth away in charity, and say of him that he had much good with Allah. The man of the world was slowly being turned toward the next.

The man sent to break the refugees

When a group of early Muslims fled the persecution of Makkah and took refuge in Abyssinia, Quraysh chose Amr to bring them back. Two of his own brothers were among those who had fled, yet he was the one most driven to drag them home in chains.

He knew the Christian king, the Najashi, from years of trade. So he came with a plan. The night before his audience, he visited each of the king's generals and bought their loyalty with gifts. The next day he told the Najashi these were simply troublemakers who had fled their own people, not worth a hearing.

The Najashi refused to act on one side of a story. "What kind of a man would I be if I did not hear their case?" he said. As the scholars note, this is what justice is: hearing both sides, and not being driven by bias.

He called the Muslims forward. Ja'far ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) described their faith plainly: a people who had worshipped idols and broken their bonds, until God sent them a prophet who commanded truth, kept promises, kindness to family, and protection of the weak. The king listened, looked at Amr, and said he saw nothing that required him to hand these people over. Amr's bribes and his cleverness had failed completely.

A door left open

Amr returned to Makkah on the first failed mission of his life, humiliated. He took his anger out on the Muslims who remained, his own brother Hisham among them. He fought the Prophet ﷺ at Badr and Uhud, and he was one of the chief planners behind the siege of Madinah.

At last the Prophet ﷺ raised his hands and prayed against Amr. He had prayed against others, and they had been ruined. But this time something different happened: Allah sent down a verse in reply, telling His Prophet that this man's fate was not his to seal.

It was a quiet act of mercy hidden inside a moment of war. The door to Amr was not being shut. It was being held open, by Allah Himself, for a day no one could yet imagine.

His brother Hisham, tortured for his faith, had once despaired of ever being forgiven for giving in. A verse of the Quran came to lift that despair too: that no one should ever lose hope in the mercy of Allah. This was a family Allah refused to give up on.

Whether God relents towards them or punishes them is not for you [Prophet] to decide: they are wrongdoers.Quran 3:128

The night faith finally entered

After the siege of Madinah failed, Amr read the signs and concluded the Prophet ﷺ would win. So he gathered some friends and proposed they return to Abyssinia and seek refuge with the Najashi, the way the Muslims once had.

There, in the king's court, Amr found a Muslim envoy from Madinah and asked permission to kill him. The Najashi struck him so hard he nearly broke his nose, then said the words that changed everything: "You ask me to hand over the messenger of the man to whom the same angel comes that came to Moses." Amr later said that in that instant, faith entered his heart.

He asked the Najashi to take his pledge to Islam right there, and so the king who had never met the Prophet ﷺ gave the testimony of faith to a future companion. Amr slipped away from his old friends in the night and rode straight for Madinah. On the road he met Khalid ibn al-Walid, bound for the same destination, for the same reason.

Together they reached the Prophet ﷺ among the last of those who would ever migrate to him. The sons of the men who had fought him hardest were now arriving not to attack, but to embrace him.

Islam wipes away what came before

When Amr reached the Prophet ﷺ, he held back his hand. He would give his pledge, he said, only on the condition that all his past sins be forgiven. The Prophet ﷺ told him gently: "Do you not know that Islam wipes away whatever came before it?"

Notice what the Prophet ﷺ did not say. He did not invent a special pardon to win a useful convert. He pointed to a mercy already built into the faith, open to anyone who enters it. Amr's record was long and dark, and all of it was lifted at once.

Soon after, the Prophet ﷺ said of him in one narration, "People became Muslim, and Amr ibn al-As truly believed," testifying that faith had genuinely settled in his heart. The most hated man had become one of the trusted few.

And he was eager to prove it. One night an alarm sounded at the edge of Madinah, and Amr grabbed his sword and rushed to guard the city. The Prophet ﷺ pointed to him and asked the others, "Would you not have done what these two believers did?" Amr would later say he loved the Prophet ﷺ so deeply that he could barely lift his eyes to look at him.

From Rafah to Egypt

Amr now turned his whole mind toward the faith he had fought. In the years after the Prophet ﷺ, he led armies into the lands he had once crossed as a merchant. Much of southern Palestine was opened under his command, clearing the way for Jerusalem to be received in peace.

Then he set his sights on Egypt. Umar ibn al-Khattab (may Allah be pleased with him) was hesitant, but Amr had a plan. He stationed himself at Rafah, in Gaza, on the very border, and from there his letters travelled back and forth to Madinah. He told the Egyptians the Prophet ﷺ had instructed the Muslims to treat them with kindness, for they were kin through Hajar, the mother of Ismail.

With only four thousand men he brought Egypt into Islam, and many of its people embraced the faith because of how fairly they were treated. He built a new city of tents called Fustat, the heart of what would later become old Cairo, and the mosque he founded there still carries his name today.

Umar said of him that it did not suit Amr ibn al-As to walk the earth as anything but a leader. The cunning Quraysh had once feared was now spent in service, and the man they thought too slippery to trust had become one of Islam's great commanders.

Three phases of a life

Amr ibn al-As died past the age of ninety. As recorded in Sahih Muslim, his son Abdullah sat with him at the end and saw his father weighed down by regret. So Amr divided his life into three phases for him.

In the first, he said, he fought the Prophet ﷺ, and had he died then he would surely have been among the people of the Fire. In the second, he embraced Islam and stood beside the Prophet ﷺ, and had he died then he was certain of Paradise. But the third phase, after the Prophet ﷺ, with all its trials and its mistakes, he could not be sure of himself.

His son tried to comfort him, reminding him how the Prophet ﷺ had loved him and trusted him with great affairs. Amr would not lean on it. He could not be certain, he said, whether that nearness was love or simply the Prophet ﷺ softening a hard heart. So he chose to lean on Allah alone.

Then he raised his finger to the sky. "O Allah, You commanded us and we left much of it. You forbade us and we fell into it. There is no god but You." He kept repeating those words, there is no god but Allah, until he passed away upon them.

What the life of Amr ibn al-As teaches us

His is the story of a man whose gifts never changed, only the direction he pointed them. That turn is the whole lesson.

  • Your strengths can be redeemed. The same cunning he once used to plot against the truth later carried Islam across continents. Talent is not the problem; what we aim it at is.
  • The door is held open longer than we think. Allah revealed a verse to keep Amr's fate undecided at the height of his enmity. No one is so far gone that mercy gives up on them.
  • Entering faith clears the slate. He feared his long record could never be forgiven. He was told plainly that Islam wipes away whatever came before it.
  • Honest fear of God is a mercy. At ninety, he still examined his own heart rather than rest on his rank. That humility kept him reaching for Allah to his last breath.

Questions

Who was Amr ibn al-As?

A poet, merchant, and famed strategist of Quraysh, known as the most cunning of the Arabs. He opposed the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ for years, then accepted Islam and became a major commander and the leader who brought Egypt into Islam.

How did Amr ibn al-As become Muslim?

He travelled to Abyssinia and met its Christian king, the Najashi, who told him plainly that Muhammad ﷺ was a true prophet and advised him to follow him. Amr said that in that moment faith entered his heart, and he took his pledge to Islam through the Najashi before riding to Madinah.

What is Amr ibn al-As known for?

He opened much of southern Palestine and then led the conquest of Egypt with only four thousand men, stationing himself at Rafah in Gaza. He founded the city of Fustat near present-day old Cairo, where the mosque named after him still stands.

What can we learn from the life of Amr ibn al-As?

That gifts can be turned from harm to good, that Allah leaves the door of mercy open even to His opponents, that entering Islam clears what came before, and that honest fear of God keeps the heart humble to the end.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to believe some people are simply written off: too clever, too proud, too far down the wrong road to ever turn around. Amr's life refuses that idea. At the very moment he was scheming hardest against the truth, Allah was quietly keeping his door open. The same mind he used to harm became the mind that carried a mercy to whole nations.

Most of us are not changing the map of the world. But each of us has gifts, a sharp tongue, a quick mind, a strong will, that can be pointed either way. His story asks a gentle question of our ordinary day: what am I aiming my best at? And it leaves us with the humility he died on, never sure of ourselves, always reaching for Allah, repeating that there is no god but Him.
