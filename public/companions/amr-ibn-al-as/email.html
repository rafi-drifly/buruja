The most cunning man in Makkah, and the verse that saved him

Preview: He spent years plotting against the Prophet ﷺ. Then a Christian king told him the truth.

Assalamu alaikum,

They called him the most cunning of the Arabs. Amr ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him) was a poet, a merchant, and the sharpest strategist of Quraysh. For years he aimed all of it at the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He fought him at Badr and Uhud. He helped plan the siege of Madinah.

At one point the Prophet ﷺ raised his hands to pray against him. He had prayed against others, and they were ruined. But this time Allah sent down a verse in reply, telling His Prophet that this man's fate was not his to decide. The door to Amr was not being shut. It was being held open.

The change came in an unlikely place. Amr travelled to Abyssinia and met its Christian king, a man he knew well from years of trade. The king told him plainly that Muhammad ﷺ was a true prophet, the same kind of messenger who came to Moses, and advised him to follow him. Amr said that in that instant, faith entered his heart.

He rode to Madinah and asked the Prophet ﷺ that all his past sins be forgiven before he gave his pledge. The Prophet ﷺ did not invent a special pardon for him. He simply said: "Do you not know that Islam wipes away whatever came before it?" A long, dark record, lifted at once.

And he was eager to live up to it. When an alarm sounded at the edge of Madinah one night, he was among the first to grab his sword and rush to guard the city. He would later say he loved the Prophet ﷺ so deeply he could barely raise his eyes to look at him.

The rest of his life pointed the other way. The same mind he once used to harm carried Islam from Rafah, in Gaza, into Egypt, where many embraced the faith because of how fairly they were treated.

There is a quiet lesson in him that is easy to miss. Our gifts are not the problem. What we aim them at is. And the door of mercy stays open far longer than we tend to believe.

May Allah be pleased with him, and turn the best of us toward the best of things.
