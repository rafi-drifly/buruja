The Companions

Hisham ibn al-As

The Better Brother

A father in Makkah once told the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ that he had no future. He had no sons to carry his name, the man said, so when he died he would simply be forgotten. He called the Prophet ﷺ abtar: the one who is cut off.

Years later that same father lay dead and swollen, his name remembered only as a warning. His two sons had become Muslim, and one of them had given his whole short life to the message his father tried to mock into silence. This is the story of that son, Hisham ibn al-As (may Allah be pleased with him).

Hisham ibn al-As

The Better Brother

A house built on mockery

Hisham was the younger son of al-As ibn Wa'il, chief of the Banu Sahm, one of the powerful clans of Quraysh. His mother was a noblewoman, the sister of Abu Jahl, which placed Hisham among the strongest families in Makkah. From the outside, it was a house of wealth and standing.

His father was something else underneath. Al-As was one of the bitterest opponents the Prophet ﷺ ever faced, and his weapon was not the sword but the sneer. The scholars connect him to verse after verse of the Quran that records the taunts of the disbelievers. He would bring a decayed bone to the Prophet ﷺ, crumble it, and ask who could ever bring it back to life.

He was rich, and still he preyed on the weak. Before Islam he once took goods from a visiting trader and refused to pay, certain no one would dare challenge a chief. The man stood at the Kaaba and shamed the whole city in verse, and out of that scandal came Hilf al-Fudul, the pact to protect anyone wronged. Al-As signed it too, then went on cheating the poor as before.

The father who paid in the next life

Al-As had a cruel trick. He would buy from a poor Muslim, then refuse the payment with a smirk: I will pay you in Paradise. He said it to Khabbab ibn al-Aratt (may Allah be pleased with him), a blacksmith who had forged him a sword, and told him plainly that he had so much wealth and so many followers that he was not worried about any reckoning.

The Quran answered him. The mockers of Makkah were not named, the scholars note, because there are people like al-As in every generation, wearing different faces. They are simply listed among the fuel of the fire.

On one of the hardest days of persecution, al-As and Abu Jahl chased the Prophet ﷺ through the streets, mocking him. The Prophet ﷺ made a short, exact prayer: that a small thorn from the thorns of the earth be given power over al-As. Soon after, al-As stepped on a thorn. His leg swelled, his body began to rot, and before his son could bring a doctor from Ta'if, he was dead.

Have you considered the man who rejects Our revelation, who says, ‘I will certainly be given wealth and children’?Quran 19:77

The son who chose the other side

While his father mocked, Hisham listened. He became Muslim so early that he was among those who gathered in the house of al-Arqam, the first quiet school of the new faith. He believed long before his older brother Amr, and long before most of Makkah.

His father took it as a personal disgrace. The elites of Makkah did not torture their own children in the open the way they tortured the slaves and the poor. They did it privately, behind their own walls. So Hisham was beaten at home, by his father and by his brother Amr, who tried everything to break his faith.

Amr later admitted that Hisham had always been the more beloved son. Their father favoured him because his mother was a noblewoman while Amr's was a slave, and because he saw in Hisham the one who would carry on the family name. That favourite son was the first of the two to walk away from everything his father stood for, which only deepened the bitterness in the old man's heart.

The Prophet ﷺ used to change harsh names into gentle ones. Al-As means the disobedient one, the name fitting the father perfectly. Hisham had taken the nickname Abu al-As after him, and the Prophet ﷺ changed it to Abu Muti: father of the obedient one. The contrast was the whole point. The disobedient father had raised an obedient son.

Two migrations and a decade in chains

When Makkah became unbearable, Hisham left it. He was among the larger group who migrated to Abyssinia for the sake of their faith. It was his own brother Amr who travelled there to demand the refugees be handed back, calling it a domestic dispute. Hisham was standing among the very people Amr was trying to drag home. Amr failed, and the believers stayed safe.

A rumour reached Abyssinia that Makkah had accepted Islam, so about forty believers set out for home. Hisham was one of them. The rumour was false. He was seized the moment he arrived, and the persecution that followed was worse than before.

He had planned to escape to Madinah with Umar ibn al-Khattab and a cousin, agreeing to meet at dawn on the city's edge. Umar made it. Hisham did not. He had been caught, and he would be held in an abandoned house turned dungeon for roughly ten years. Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) said they would beat a man until he could not sit up, then ask if their idols were gods beside Allah. Hisham missed Badr, missed Uhud, missed the Trench, abandoned in Makkah while the believers fought on without him.

The verse that brought him back

Under that beating, Hisham broke and said the words of disbelief his torturers demanded. His faith was intact in his heart, but his spirit was crushed, and he believed he could never be forgiven. Several believers carried that same despair: that words forced from them under torture had cut them off from Allah forever.

Then a verse came down, calling the servants who had wronged themselves not to despair of Allah's mercy, for He forgives all sins. Umar, one of the few who could write, copied it by hand and had it smuggled into Makkah. Hisham took it out to a quiet valley and read it again and again, asking Allah to let him understand, until he was sure: the door was still open. He could come back.

After the Battle of the Trench, the Prophet ﷺ asked who would go and rescue the believers still trapped in Makkah. It was the most dangerous of missions. Al-Walid ibn al-Walid, brother of Khalid ibn al-Walid, stood and volunteered. He slipped into the city in disguise, found the dungeon, cut the chains, and carried Hisham and his companion out, both too weak to stand, all the way to Madinah.

What he wanted, and what he got

In Madinah, Hisham finally sat in the company he had given everything for. He once came upon young men arguing over the Quran, setting verse against verse, and quietly drew back from them, sensing it was not a gathering the Prophet ﷺ would be pleased with. He was right. The Prophet ﷺ came out with anger in his face and warned that earlier nations were destroyed by exactly this: turning the Book of God into an argument. Hisham counted it among his happiest days, that he had read the moment correctly.

There is a small detail that captures the kind of man he was. For all his cruelty, al-As had left instructions to free a hundred slaves from his wealth. Hisham freed fifty on his father's behalf and asked Amr to free the rest. The Prophet ﷺ explained that such gifts only reach a person who died upon faith, and al-As had not. Yet Hisham had tried anyway, hoping to do some good in the name of a father who had done him nothing but harm.

His two former persecutors, Khalid and his own brother Amr, were now Muslim and now his commanders. Hisham served under them in the great battles against the Romans. At Ajnadayn, when the army grew exhausted, he pulled off his helmet and called out: are you running away from Paradise? Then he plunged in, and the men rallied behind him. Some accounts call it a turning point of the battle.

At Yarmuk he was killed, his body falling in such a place that stopping to recover it would have endangered the army. Amr, watching his only brother lie there in pieces, said the soul was already in Paradise and the body could wait. When the fighting was over, Amr walked the field and gathered the parts of his brother to bury in one grave. When the news reached Umar, he said only: may Allah have mercy on my brother Hisham; what a fine support he was to Islam.

Better, and at peace with it

Years later, Amr ibn al-As was making tawaf when he overheard a circle of young men debating a question: who was better, the famous Amr, or his quieter brother Hisham? Amr finished his circuits, sat down with them, and answered honestly.

The night before Yarmuk, he told them, the two brothers had prayed together to be accepted as martyrs. We offered ourselves to Allah, Amr said, and He took Hisham and left me. So Hisham is better than me. Allah accepted him, and has not yet accepted me. He said it with tears.

Hisham had not chased the glory of this world. He was not seeking to be remembered in gatherings or written into the histories. He was seeking only his name with Allah. And so the father who called the Prophet ﷺ cut off was himself forgotten, while his obedient son was counted among the early believers who gave everything and asked for nothing.

What Hisham's life teaches us

His was not a famous name. He chose the harder path early, paid for it for years, and asked for no credit. That is exactly why it is worth sitting with.

  • You are not bound by the house you came from. Hisham was raised by one of the cruellest men in Makkah, and still he chose mercy and faith. Where we begin does not decide who we become.
  • Despair is not the end of the road. He said words he hated under torture and thought he was lost forever. One verse reminded him that Allah's door does not close. It does not close on us either.
  • Quiet faithfulness outweighs fame. Hisham sought his name with Allah, not in the histories. His own brother, the famous one, called him the better of the two.
  • Mockery is weaker than it looks. His father spent a decade sneering at the message. The message outlived him, carried by the very sons he was sure would abandon it.

Questions

Who was Hisham ibn al-As?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the younger brother of the famous general Amr ibn al-As. He accepted Islam before his brother, migrated twice for his faith, endured years of torture in Makkah, and was killed at the Battle of Yarmuk.

Why was his father, al-As ibn Wa'il, so notable?

Al-As was a chief of Quraysh and one of the most determined opponents of the Prophet ﷺ. His particular weapon was mockery, and the scholars connect him to many verses of the Quran that record the taunts of the disbelievers of Makkah.

Why did Amr ibn al-As say Hisham was better than him?

The night before the Battle of Yarmuk the two brothers prayed together to be accepted as martyrs. Hisham was killed and accepted; Amr survived. Amr concluded that Allah had honoured his brother in a way he himself had not yet been honoured.

What can we learn from the life of Hisham ibn al-As?

That we are not trapped by the family we are born into, that despair is never the final word, and that quiet faithfulness, seeking only Allah's pleasure, can weigh more than any fame.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to measure a life by how widely it is known. Hisham's life resists that. He was the lesser-known brother, held in chains while others made history, and when he finally had his freedom he spent it asking to be accepted by Allah rather than remembered by people. The brother everyone knew said, plainly, that the quiet one was the better of the two.

Most of us will not be famous, and most of our hardest moments will pass with no one watching. Hisham's life is an invitation to want what he wanted: to be obedient when it is costly, to refuse despair when we have failed, and to seek our name with Allah before our name with anyone else. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
