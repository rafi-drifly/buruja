The Companions

Muadh ibn Jabal

The Most Knowledgeable and Beloved

A man once walked into the mosque in Damascus and saw a young teacher surrounded by people. Whenever anyone disagreed about something, they turned to him, and his word settled it. The visitor did not know who he was, only that he had to tell him: I love you for the sake of Allah.

This is the story of Muadh ibn Jabal (may Allah be pleased with him): the young man the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ told plainly that he loved him, the one named the most knowledgeable of the ummah about what is permitted and what is not, and a man who died at thirty-four already called a nation in himself.

The Companions

Muadh ibn Jabal

The Most Knowledgeable and Beloved

A boy noticed by everyone

Muadh was a young boy of Madinah, around eleven or twelve, when news of Islam reached the city. His father, Jabal ibn Amr, had died before Islam. When Musab ibn Umayr came from Mecca to teach the new Muslims, Muadh embraced the faith early, before the Prophet ﷺ himself arrived.

People remembered how he looked and how he carried himself. He was tall, with a bright complexion, curly hair, broad shoulders, and the most beautiful smile they had seen. When he was silent, people stared at him in quiet awe. When he spoke, they listened the same way.

He did not stay still with his faith. He was a young caller among the youth of Madinah, explaining Islam to other teenagers. He was sharp enough to debate the learned men of the city, reminding them that they had spent years describing a prophet who was coming, and now that he had come, they were turning away.

Brought close, treated as an equal

When the Prophet ﷺ reached Madinah, he saw the potential in this young man and kept him near. He could easily have been overlooked because of his age. Instead he was treated as an adult and paired in brotherhood with one of the most prominent of the Meccan migrants, Abdullah ibn Masud.

Imagine that household. Abdullah ibn Masud loved the Quran and had taken more of it fresh from the mouth of the Prophet ﷺ than anyone. The two of them, both young, prayed through the night together and fasted together, and the elder shaped the younger.

When the Battle of Badr came, Muadh was just old enough at fifteen to take part. From then on he was present at the campaigns alongside the Prophet ﷺ, learning, writing down revelation, and never far from his teacher.

The most knowledgeable in halal and haram

In a society that held Abu Bakr, Umar, and many senior companions, the Prophet ﷺ said the most knowledgeable of the ummah about what is permitted and what is forbidden was Muadh ibn Jabal. He was named among the four from whom the Quran should be learned, and he was one of the few allowed to give verdicts in the Prophet's own lifetime.

He understood quickly and deeply, and he never let his age shackle him or make him careless. The Prophet ﷺ praised him in the same breath as the greatest of his companions, and Umar later told people plainly: whoever has a question about jurisprudence, go to Muadh ibn Jabal. Once Muadh corrected Umar in public, and rather than take offence, Umar said that women no longer give birth to men like him, and that without Muadh he might have erred.

Many of his narrations reach us from the most intimate settings, riding behind the Prophet ﷺ on a donkey, the Prophet's hand resting on his chest or his knee. One day, riding together, the Prophet ﷺ told him that the right of Allah upon people is that they worship Him and associate nothing with Him, and that their reward, if they do so, is that He will not punish them. Muadh wanted to run and tell everyone the good news, but was told to let people keep striving. He held that hadith back until he was dying, fearing both that people would grow complacent and that he would be sinful for concealing it.

Learning where the line falls

Even a man of his understanding had room to grow. Muadh would pray the night prayer with the Prophet ﷺ, then return to his own people and lead them. One night he led them in long surahs, and a tired old man complained. Muadh assumed the man simply disliked the Quran.

The Prophet ﷺ corrected him gently but firmly: Are you going to be a hardship for the people, Muadh? Lead them with shorter surahs. Save the long recitation for your own night prayer. Among them are the elderly and the weak who cannot keep up.

Another time, when the Prophet ﷺ spoke of the tongue, Muadh asked whether people are really taken to account for what they say. The answer was sobering: most of those who fall into the Fire fall because of what their tongues have harvested. Muadh took that to heart. Later, when a man was being criticised in the Prophet's presence, it was Muadh who spoke up: how could you say that, when we know only good of him?

A heart given to Allah at night

A man does not understand the lawful and the forbidden better than anyone else without a heart deeply tied to Allah. Muadh loved the Quran the way the early Muslims did, standing through the night with it. When he rose to pray, he would say: O Allah, the stars have come out and the eyes have closed, and You are the Living, the Sustaining. My flight from the Fire is too slow, and my pursuit of Paradise too weak.

He was not boasting of his worship. He was accusing himself, certain he could be running harder and reaching higher. All he offered, he said, was that he bore witness there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad ﷺ is His servant and messenger.

He also kept a quiet secret, which he once shared with his friend Abu Musa. He sought reward from Allah for his sleep just as he sought it for his night prayer, because the only reason he slept was to gather strength to rise and worship again. He had learned to turn even rest into something offered to Allah.

That same heart made him careless with money, in the best way. Wealth came to him and he gave it away, until he fell into debt. His generosity became one of the things he was known for. Years later Umar tested him with four hundred dinars, and Muadh began handing it out at once, a few coins to this poor family, a few to that, keeping nothing until his wife reminded him that they too were in need.

The teacher of Mecca and Yemen

When Mecca opened to Islam, the Prophet ﷺ left this young man of Madinah behind to teach its people their religion for a full year. It broke an old assumption: that you may only learn from someone of your own tribe or region. Knowledge, not lineage, set the rank. They learned from the one who knew more.

Then he was sent further still, to Yemen. The Prophet ﷺ walked beside his camel to the edge of the city, giving him advice that reads like a blueprint for building a whole society: call people first to the worship of Allah alone, then prayer, then charity taken from the rich and given to the poor. Take the most modest amount, never the most precious of their wealth, and fear the prayer of anyone you wrong, for nothing stands between it and Allah.

He asked Muadh how he would judge. By the Book of Allah, Muadh said. And if you do not find it there? By the way of the Prophet ﷺ. And if not there? Then I will reason with my best effort and spare nothing to get it right. The Prophet ﷺ placed his hand on his chest and praised God that the messenger he was sending had answered in the way that pleased him.

Before he sent him off, the Prophet ﷺ took his hand and said: Muadh, I love you. Muadh answered that he loved him too. Because of that love, the Prophet ﷺ taught him a prayer to say after every salah: O Allah, help me to remember You, to thank You, and to worship You well.

The farewell, and a life that ended early

At the city's gates, the Prophet ﷺ told him something he had not expected: Muadh, you may not meet me again after this year. You may pass by my mosque and my grave. Muadh wept. The Prophet ﷺ comforted him, then turned back toward Madinah, and the closest people to him, he said, are the people of God-consciousness, wherever and whoever they are.

It happened as he said. The Prophet ﷺ passed away while Muadh was in Yemen, where the people had come to love him. Later he became the leading scholar of Sham, the man others turned to as they once had in the Prophet's own gatherings. Umar held him in such regard that he named him a possible successor to the caliphate.

Then the plague of Amwas spread through Sham. Muadh, who narrated that one who dies in a plague dies as a martyr, lost his two daughters, his two sons, and his wife. As his beloved son Abdur-Rahman lay dying, the boy did not complain; he recited a verse instead. His father answered with one of his own. When his own end came, Muadh prayed: O Allah, You know that my heart loves You, so take my soul. And he died.

He was thirty-four. The companion Abdullah ibn Masud, reciting the verse that calls Ibrahim a nation devoted to God, paused and said the name of his old friend instead: by Allah, Muadh was a nation in himself. A nation, he explained, is one who teaches people goodness.

What Muadh's life teaches us

He was young, and he did not wait. That is the thread running through everything: a heart that heard the call and answered it at once.

  • Knowledge is honoured above lineage. A teenager from Madinah was sent to teach Mecca and Yemen because he understood the religion best. What you know, and how you live it, matters more than where you come from.
  • Carry knowledge with humility. He gave verdicts as a young man, yet he answered hard questions with "Allah and His Messenger know best," and he accepted correction without defending his pride.
  • Guard the tongue. He learned that most people are ruined by what they say, and then he proved it by speaking up for an absent man instead of against him.
  • Do not wait to turn to Allah. He reached his rank because he answered the call early and gave himself fully. His last words were simply that his heart loved Allah.

Questions

Who was Muadh ibn Jabal?

A young companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from Madinah, known as one of the most knowledgeable of the early Muslims. The Prophet ﷺ named him the most learned of the ummah about what is permitted and what is forbidden, and sent him to teach the people of Mecca and then Yemen.

Why is Muadh called the most knowledgeable companion in halal and haram?

The Prophet ﷺ said the most knowledgeable of his ummah about the lawful and the unlawful was Muadh ibn Jabal. He was among the few permitted to give verdicts during the Prophet's lifetime, and Umar would tell people to take their questions of jurisprudence to him.

How did Muadh ibn Jabal die?

He died in the plague of Amwas in Sham, which also took his wife, his two sons, and his two daughters. He himself had narrated that whoever dies in a plague dies as a martyr. He was about thirty-four years old.

What can we learn from the life of Muadh ibn Jabal?

That knowledge is honoured above background, that real understanding comes with humility, that the tongue must be guarded, and that there is no reason to delay turning fully toward Allah.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to assume that a life of weight needs decades to build. Muadh had thirty-four years. In that time he became the man the Prophet ﷺ loved openly, the teacher trusted with two cities, and the one a great companion would call a whole nation. He did not earn that by waiting for the right age or the right moment. He heard the call and answered it.

Most of us keep a quiet list of things we will do once we are older, steadier, more ready. His life gently questions that list. Turn toward Allah now, in the prayer you are about to pray, in the words you are about to speak. Love what is good before it becomes easy, and let that be enough to begin. That was his head start, and it is still available to anyone.
