Abu Sufyan ibn Harb

The Enemy Who Was Forgiven

For nearly twenty years, when the Muslims in Madinah heard one name, it carried more weight than almost any other. He was not just an enemy. He was the man who organised the others, who funded the armies, who led the siege meant to end them all.

This is the story of Abu Sufyan ibn Harb (may Allah be pleased with him): the chief of Quraysh who fought the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ for two decades, and was then forgiven so completely that he died a believer and was buried among the companions.

Abu Sufyan ibn Harb

The Enemy Who Was Forgiven

The rock, the son of war

His given name was Sakhr ibn Harb, which means "rock, son of war," and few names have fit a life so well. He came from Banu Abd Shams, one of the great houses of Makkah, and inherited his father's place as a warlord and a leader of Quraysh.

His family ran the caravans that carried Makkah's trade to Syria and Yemen. He was the point man the merchants knew, the one with the head for commerce while others reached for the sword. He was wealthy, senior, and being groomed to lead the city. The Prophet's ﷺ own cousin Uthman ibn Affan (RA) came from the same house, which gives a sense of how much wealth and standing it held.

He was older than the Prophet ﷺ, by some accounts by twenty years, and he would outlive him by twenty more. When the Prophet ﷺ declared his message, Abu Sufyan saw at once what he stood to lose: his wealth, his standing, and his place at the head of Quraysh. So he decided, plainly, to fight. To concede that this man was a prophet would mean surrendering everything he had been raised to inherit, and that was a price he would not pay.

The schemer, not the brawler

He was not Abu Jahl. He did not pounce or rage in public. He was the cooler head, the one planning in the background, the diplomat who looked for the strategic way to undermine the Prophet ﷺ while keeping his own reputation clean.

His opposition came from a low and ordinary place. It was not lofty or principled. It was tribe and money, tribe and money, repeated for twenty years. He kept his trade routes open even as Makkah went on alert, growing his caravans while he schemed against the believers at home.

When his own daughter, Umm Habibah (RA), became one of the earliest Muslims and fled to Abyssinia, he confiscated and sold the homes of those who left, and folded their wealth into his caravans. That stolen wealth would later sit at the very heart of the road to Badr.

There was a strange honesty to his pride. When word reached him that the Prophet ﷺ had proposed marriage to his exiled daughter, he did not rage. "That is a noble stallion who should not be refused," he said. He was still trying to kill the man, and yet, tribe being tribe, he was content that his daughter had married into a noble line.

The man Allah kept sparing

Watch his life and you notice something strange. Again and again, by every measure, he should not have survived, and he did. At Badr, his job was to guard the caravan, so he slipped away from the battle that killed almost every leader of his rank.

At Uhud, which he now led, a young companion knocked him from his horse and stood over him ready to finish him, only for another man to strike the companion down first. Abu Sufyan rose from his back and lived. At the siege of Madinah, which he also led, Hudhayfah ibn al-Yaman crept into his camp at night and stood close enough to kill him, then remembered the Prophet's ﷺ order to cause no commotion, and put his weapon away.

He even survived the prayer of Khubayb ibn Adi, the companion he tortured and questioned on the cross. When Abu Sufyan asked whether Khubayb would rather the Prophet ﷺ take his place, Khubayb answered that he would not accept even a thorn pricking the Messenger of Allah in exchange for his own release. "I have never seen a man loved," Abu Sufyan said, "the way Muhammad is loved by his companions." It made no sense to him. It was beginning to unsettle everything he thought he knew.

The questions of a Roman king

After a truce was signed, Abu Sufyan returned to his trade and travelled to Syria, expecting a rest from war. Instead he was summoned to the court of Heraclius, the ruler of Rome, who had just received a letter from the Prophet ﷺ calling him to Islam.

Heraclius asked him to tell the truth on pain of death, then questioned him about this man in Arabia. Is he noble? Yes. Did anyone before him ever make such a claim? No. Do the powerful follow him, or the weak? The weak. Are his followers increasing? By Allah, they are. Does anyone leave his religion in disappointment once they have entered it? No. Did he ever lie, or break a promise? Never.

When the king weighed the answers, he told Abu Sufyan that all of them pointed to a prophet of God. Abu Sufyan walked out shaken. "The affair of Muhammad ﷺ has become so great," he told his companions, "that even the kings of Rome now fear him." From that day, he later admitted, he knew this would not end in his favour, and that one day Allah would bring him to Islam.

Isn't it time?

When the truce was broken and the Prophet ﷺ marched on Makkah, Abu Sufyan knew he had no chance. He went from person to person trying to salvage the situation, and one by one they turned away. Finally his old friend al-Abbas brought him by night to the Prophet ﷺ himself.

"Woe to you, Abu Sufyan," the Prophet ﷺ said gently. "Isn't it time you knew there is no god but Allah?" Abu Sufyan praised his kindness and admitted that yes, that part was beginning to make sense to him: had there been another god, surely it would have helped him by now. "Isn't it time," the Prophet ﷺ pressed, "that you knew I am the Messenger of Allah?" That, he confessed, still would not settle in his heart. Only with al-Abbas urging him did he finally say the words.

Then the Prophet ﷺ did something that revealed his whole strategy. Knowing that Abu Sufyan loved to be honoured, he declared as he entered the city: whoever enters the house of Abu Sufyan is safe, whoever enters the Sacred Mosque is safe, whoever shuts his own door is safe. The man who had been the enemy of Islam was suddenly named a place of refuge. It was a victory aimed not at his neck, but at his heart.

From enemy to defender

The forgiveness of that day was not a loophole, and Abu Sufyan slowly understood that. His wife Hind, who had mutilated the body of Hamza (RA), the most beloved of the Prophet's ﷺ uncles, came seeking pardon. The Prophet ﷺ received her with warmth and let the past go. There would be no settling of scores, ever.

He had to learn this slowly, because the old pride did not vanish overnight. He still bristled at the sight of Bilal (RA), once an enslaved man, calling the prayer from atop the Kaaba. Yet the Prophet ﷺ would not diminish the early believers to soothe him. He honoured the first to accept Islam and won over the last at the same time, and that balance was itself a sign of who he was.

Soon after came the battle of Hunayn. When a sudden ambush sent many fleeing, the Prophet ﷺ looked to his right and to his left and found Abu Sufyan among the few who refused to leave his side, fighting fiercely though he was nearly seventy. The enemy of two decades had become a protector.

The Qur'an describes this very turning, the way harshness met with something better can soften the hardest heart:

Good and evil cannot be equal. [Prophet], repel evil with what is better and your enemy will become as close as an old and valued friend,Quran 41:34

Two eyes, given for the sake of Allah

At the siege of Ta'if, fighting beside the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Sufyan lost an eye. He came to the Prophet ﷺ and said simply, "My eye was struck for the sake of Allah." The Prophet ﷺ offered to pray that his sight be restored, or, if he preferred, Paradise instead. "Paradise," he answered, without pause. The same man, a changed mind.

He went on to help destroy the idol al-Lat, the very kind of stone he had once shouted for on the battlefield. He led for Abu Bakr against those who abandoned the faith, and in old age he went out to Syria as a fighter. At the battle of Yarmuk, beneath his son's banner, he was struck in his remaining eye. Both of his eyes were given in the path he had once spent his life resisting.

He died near the age of one hundred, in Madinah, during the caliphate of his cousin Uthman (RA). His daughter Umm Habibah (RA), wife of the Prophet ﷺ, tended to his burial. He was prayed over in the very city he had once besieged, and laid to rest in al-Baqi among the companions of the Prophet ﷺ.

What Abu Sufyan's life teaches us

His is not a story of early devotion. It is a story of how patience, mercy, and good character can reach a heart that everyone else had written off.

  • No heart is beyond reach. The man most hated by the Muslims died among them as a believer. If his heart could turn, we should be slow to give up on anyone, including ourselves.
  • Mercy can be the strongest strategy. The Prophet ﷺ aimed not at Abu Sufyan's neck but at his heart, and won a lifelong defender where revenge would have won only a corpse.
  • Good character is the real invitation. It was the dignity of the Prophet ﷺ in war and in peace, and the love of his companions, that slowly dismantled everything Abu Sufyan thought he knew.
  • It is never too late to change. After twenty years on the wrong side, he still found a door open. The same offer of return is open to anyone willing to walk through it.

Questions

Who was Abu Sufyan ibn Harb?

A chief of Quraysh and a leader of Makkah's trade, whose given name was Sakhr ibn Harb. For about two decades he was the foremost leader of those who fought the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, before accepting Islam at the conquest of Makkah.

Why was Abu Sufyan such a feared enemy of the Muslims?

He led the opposing forces at the battle of Uhud and at the siege of Madinah, funded the campaigns against the Muslims, and was the ringleader the others rallied behind. To the early Muslims, few names carried more dread.

How did Abu Sufyan become Muslim?

On the eve of the conquest of Makkah, with no way left to resist, his old friend al-Abbas brought him to the Prophet ﷺ, who urged him gently to accept the truth. He embraced Islam, and the Prophet ﷺ honoured him by making his house a place of safety for the city.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Sufyan?

That no heart is beyond change, that mercy can achieve what revenge cannot, and that good character is itself the most powerful invitation to faith.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to sort people into the saved and the lost, and to decide quietly that some are simply too far gone. Abu Sufyan's life refuses that sorting. The man who organised the wars, who funded the siege, who tortured a believer on the cross, is the same man who later gave both his eyes in the cause he once fought, and was buried among the very people he had tried to destroy.

There is someone in each of our lives we have written off: a relative, a former friend, a version of ourselves we have stopped praying for. This story is a quiet argument against that despair. Meet harshness with something better. Keep the door open. And remember that the One who turned this heart can turn any heart, including the one beating in your own chest.
