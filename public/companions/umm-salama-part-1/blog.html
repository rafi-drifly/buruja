Umm Salama

The Separated Family

There is a woman who walked out of Makkah with only a camel and a baby, heading for a city weeks away, with no one to protect her on the road. She was not running from her family. Her family was the reason she had to go.

This is the first part of the story of Umm Salama (may Allah be pleased with her): a daughter of one of Makkah's noblest houses, the wife of a man worthy of his own biography, and a mother who held on to her faith while almost everything she loved was pulled away from her.

Umm Salama

The Separated Family

A daughter of a noble house

Her name was Hind, and she came from Banu Makhzum, one of the most powerful tribes in Makkah. It was the tribe of Abu Jahl. It was the tribe that tortured the family of Yasir. It was also a tribe that would one day give Islam some of its greatest companions, and she was among the first of them.

Her father, Abu Umayya, was not just any man of Makhzum. He was the chief of the tribe, known for his nobility and his generosity, and famous above all for feeding and hosting the pilgrims who came to Makkah. In a city where clans competed over who could be most hospitable, he was the one who paid for it. She grew up in the home of a leader, surrounded by status and comfort.

Her brothers would all become Muslim in time, and one of them accepted Islam only at the conquest of Makkah, years after her. So much of what we know about her comes through a brother narrating from his sister. But she went ahead of every one of them. She believed first.

There is one more thread worth holding. Through the milk that nursed them, she was counted as a sister of Ammar ibn Yasir, whose family Banu Makhzum would torture for their faith. So the same tribe that produced enemies of the Prophet ﷺ also raised people whose hearts would belong to him completely. Lineage decided nothing. Character decided everything.

Abu Salama, and the first migration

Her husband was Abu Salama, Abdullah ibn Abd al-Asad, also of Banu Makhzum. He was a first cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ through his mother, and a milk-brother as well, so the bond between them ran deep even before Islam.

Abu Salama was among the very earliest to believe. Some accounts place him as the eleventh person to accept Islam, before the Muslims even began gathering in secret. Umm Salama followed soon after, counted among the first handful of believers in the world.

When the persecution grew unbearable, Abu Salama and Umm Salama were among the first to leave for Abyssinia, and they are remembered as the first family to make that journey. They migrated there twice. The second time was because a rumour reached them that Makkah had embraced Islam, and when they returned and found it false, they turned back to Abyssinia again.

Almost everything we know about those years across the sea is preserved through her eyes. The stories of the king who protected the Muslims, the two armies, the messengers sent to drag them home: she carried those memories and passed them on. Their son Salama was born in that distant land, in exile. By the time they finally came back to Makkah, this was a family that had already given up home twice for the sake of their faith.

The day the family was torn apart

When word reached them that the Prophet ﷺ was preparing to migrate to Madinah, the small family packed their belongings and set out together: Abu Salama, Umm Salama, and their child. They did not get far.

Her own relatives from Banu Makhzum intercepted them. Abu Salama, they said, could go where he pleased. But Umm Salama was the daughter of their chief, the cousin of Abu Jahl, one of their own, and she would not be leaving with him. They forced the two apart and sent Abu Salama on the road to Madinah alone.

Then the family of Abu Salama heard what had happened, and their pride was wounded. The child, they argued, belonged to the father's line. They came and pulled the baby from his mother's arms, and in the struggle they wrenched his small shoulder. In a single day she lost her husband and her son, and the men who did it were not strangers but kin.

A year at the Kaaba, and a dua

What followed was not a few weeks. For an entire year she would walk out toward the Kaaba and weep. She would retell what had been done to her, the husband taken, the child taken, and she would turn to Allah and ask for a way out.

Picture the patience that takes. A year of waking each morning to an empty home, carrying the same grief to the same place, and refusing to let it harden into despair. Eventually her husband's clan returned her son to her, and in time even her own relatives softened. What harm would it do, some of them said, to let the poor woman go to her husband? At last she was told she was free.

She had been afflicted with exactly the kind of loss the Qur'an speaks of, and she met it the way the steadfast are described as meeting it:

We shall certainly test you with fear and hunger, and loss of property, lives, and crops. But [Prophet], give good news to those who are steadfast, those who say, when afflicted with a calamity, 'We belong to God and to Him we shall return.'Quran 2:155-156

The lone road to Madinah

She did not wait for an escort. She mounted her camel, set her baby in front of her, and started for Madinah by herself. It was a journey of weeks, and a dangerous one. She could have been robbed. She could have been lost. Her child could have died on the way. She told it simply: I was not thinking about any of that. I only wanted my family back.

A few miles out, at a place called Tan'im, she met a man named Uthman ibn Talha. He was not a Muslim. He was a man of high standing, the keeper of the keys of the Kaaba. He saw a woman alone with a baby on the open road and asked where she was going. To my husband in Madinah, she said. And who is with you? No one, she answered, no one but Allah and this child.

By Allah, he said, you will not be left to do this alone. What kind of man would let you? He took the camel's rein and walked. He never rode, because there was nothing for him to ride: he made the whole journey on foot. At every stop he would set the camel down, walk far away, and turn his back so she could rest in privacy. Then he would bring it close, hold it steady, and walk on. She said she had never met a more honourable man among the Arabs.

Nobility that asked for nothing

When they reached the outskirts of Madinah, Uthman pointed to the settlement where her husband was staying. Your husband is there, he said. May Allah be with you. Then he turned around and walked all the way back to Makkah, on foot, the way he had come.

He did not go to Abu Salama to be paid. He did not ask for a camel or a coin for two long journeys on his own legs. He had done it out of pure decency, for a woman who was not his relative, on behalf of a faith he did not yet hold. Set that against the men who tore the family apart out of arrogance and ego, and the contrast tells you everything.

Years later Uthman ibn Talha would accept Islam, and at the conquest of Makkah the Prophet ﷺ placed the keys of the Kaaba back in his hands and ordered that they remain with his descendants forever. The man who asked for nothing was repaid with an honour that has passed down his line to this day.

A loss, and a promise kept

In Madinah the family was whole again, and more children followed. Abu Salama fought at Badr and at Uhud, where he was wounded. He came home one day full of joy and told Umm Salama of something he had heard from the Prophet ﷺ: whoever says, at the moment of calamity, that to Allah we belong and to Him we return, and asks Allah to reward him in his loss and replace it with something better, will be given something better. He did not know he was teaching his wife the very words she would need at his own death.

His wounds from Uhud reopened, and months later he passed away. As she watched him go, she remembered the dua he had carried home to her. She said it, and then a thought came: who could possibly be better than Abu Salama? Yet she had been promised better, and Allah kept the promise. He gave her the Messenger of Allah ﷺ himself.

It did not happen at once. She was a widow with young children and a heart heavy with all she had endured. When the proposal came, she answered honestly: I am a jealous woman, I am no longer young, and I have children to raise. The Prophet ﷺ replied that he would ask Allah to ease her jealousy, that he too was advanced in age, and that her children would be his own. Her family, he said, would be his family. And so they were.

What Umm Salama's story teaches us

Part of her life is loud with loss, and part of it is quiet with patience. Both halves are worth carrying.

  • Faith can cost you the people you love. She did not lose her family to strangers but to her own kin, and she held on anyway. Conviction is sometimes tested closest to home.
  • Patience is a long road, not a moment. She wept at the Kaaba for a full year before relief came. Steadiness over months, not a single brave act, is what carried her through.
  • The dua of calamity is a gift, not a formality. To Allah we belong, and to Him we return. She said it through real grief, certain that what was taken could be replaced with something better.
  • Good character needs no audience and no reward. Uthman ibn Talha walked weeks on foot for a stranger and asked for nothing. Decency that expects no payment is its own kind of greatness.

Questions

Who was Umm Salama?

Umm Salama (may Allah be pleased with her), whose given name was Hind bint Abi Umayya, was a noblewoman of the Makhzum tribe of Makkah and one of the earliest Muslims. After the death of her husband Abu Salama, she became one of the Mothers of the Believers.

Who was Abu Salama?

Abu Salama, Abdullah ibn Abd al-Asad, was her first husband: a cousin and milk-brother of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, one of the earliest believers, a man who made the migration to Abyssinia and then to Madinah, and a veteran of Badr. He died from wounds received at Uhud.

Why was Umm Salama separated from her family?

When the family set out to migrate to Madinah, her own tribe refused to let her leave and forced her apart from Abu Salama. His clan then took their son. She was kept from both for about a year before she was finally allowed to go.

Who helped Umm Salama reach Madinah?

A man named Uthman ibn Talha, the non-Muslim keeper of the keys of the Kaaba, found her travelling alone with her child. He escorted her the entire way on foot, guarded her dignity at every stop, and returned to Makkah without asking for anything in return.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be pulled apart from our families by a hostile tribe. But most of us know what it is to carry a loss we did not choose, and to wonder whether relief will ever come. Umm Salama's answer was not to pretend the pain away. It was to weep honestly, to keep turning toward Allah, and to trust that what was taken could be returned in a better form.

Her story leaves us with two things worth wanting. The patience to wait without despairing, and the quiet decency of a man who helped a stranger and asked for nothing. Carry one of those into your day, into the next loss or the next chance to help, and her story has done its work.
