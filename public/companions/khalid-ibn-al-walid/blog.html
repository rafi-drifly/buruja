The Companions

Khalid ibn al-Walid

The Sword of Allah

He spent years fighting against the Muslims, and then one day he stood among them, marching back toward the very city he had once left to do them harm. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ called him by a name no one else carried: a sword from the swords of Allah.

This is the story of Khalid ibn al-Walid (may Allah be pleased with him): a commander who is said never to have lost a battle, and a man who spent the rest of his life learning that victory was never really his to give.

The Sword of Allah

The conquest of a heart

When the Prophet ﷺ returned to Makkah, he did not enter with one army. He divided his forces and sent them in from four directions, each under a commander. One of those banners was carried by Khalid ibn al-Walid, the same man who had once ridden out from this city again and again to fight the Muslims.

That, in itself, was a kind of conquest. The streets that had known Khalid as an enemy now watched him arrive as one of their own. The hearts had been won before the city was.

As his column entered, it was ambushed by men he knew well, including his own brother-in-law. Khalid defended his battalion and the attackers fled. At first the Prophet ﷺ was concerned, for he had wanted no fighting that day. But when he learned that Khalid had not started it, he said, "The judgment of Allah is better," and the matter was closed.

Afterward the Prophet ﷺ sent him to destroy al-Uzza, one of the idols his people had worshipped. There is something fitting in that. Years earlier, at Uhud, Khalid had stood victorious beside Abu Sufyan as he called out the name of that same idol. Now he was sent to bring it down with his own hands. History was folding back on itself, and the man who had once defended falsehood was given the task of ending it.

Learning to rein in the sword

Khalid spoke one language fluently, and it was the language of battle. Negotiation and diplomacy were not in his vocabulary. The thing most feared about him was how quickly he reached for his sword, and in his early years as a Muslim, that speed cost him.

Sent to a tribe to call them to Islam, he misread their words and their weapons and attacked people who had, in fact, accepted the faith. It did not help that this was a tribe he had once feuded with, one that had killed an uncle of his in the days before Islam. When the news reached the Prophet ﷺ, he turned toward the Kaaba and said, "O Allah, I disavow myself of what Khalid has done."

It was a serious mistake. Yet the Prophet ﷺ did not break him over it. He corrected the wrong, sent Ali to make amends, and then moved Khalid forward into a place of growth. The error became a lesson, not a verdict. Some things take time to unlearn, and an old habit of the sword was one of them.

When Khalid argued the point, the Prophet ﷺ put everything in proportion. He told the companions not to curse one another, for if one of them were to spend a mountain of gold the size of Uhud, he would not reach the rank of those who had believed early, not even by a handful. Khalid had come to Islam late, and the Prophet ﷺ was teaching him to honour the ones who had carried the message when it was hardest. The Quran had already drawn the same line:

Why should you not give for God’s cause when God alone will inherit what is in the heavens and earth? Those who gave and fought before the triumph are not like others: they are greater in rank than those who gave and fought afterwards. But God has promised a good reward to all of them: God is fully aware of all that you do.Quran 57:10

The general who was always thinking

After the Prophet ﷺ passed, it was Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) who first placed Khalid over the Muslim army. When some worried about how quick he was to his sword, Abu Bakr answered that he had heard the Prophet ﷺ call Khalid a sword of Allah, and that he would say as much to his Lord on the Day of Judgement. He had weighed it, and he believed Khalid was the right man.

Khalid never fought the same way twice. In battle after battle, against far larger armies, he used something new: feints that made his enemy march back and forth across the desert until they were exhausted, archers told to aim for the eyes, a thin line of soldiers hiding one deep flank. Military writers still study his campaigns today.

But the genius rested on something simpler. The man always had a solution. Sent to reach Syria quickly, he chose a route with no water for over a hundred miles, said, "We put our trust in Allah," and brought his army through. When his soldiers feared thirst, the rain came. He was, by every account, unfazed by anything.

A people who love death

When the Muslims turned toward the Persian empire, the most powerful of the age, Khalid sent its commander a letter offering peace, or the protection of a treaty, or war. In it he wrote a line that has been remembered ever since: I am bringing you a people who love death as much as you love life.

The Persians thought little of these desert Arabs. They sent twenty thousand fighters into the field, some of them chained leg to leg so the line could not be moved, meaning to overwhelm the small Muslim force by sheer weight. So Khalid did not meet that weight head on. He marched his army from place to place, scaring the enemy from one side and slipping away on another, until the chained soldiers were worn down by their own movement through the desert.

When at last they met, the Muslims were outnumbered but rested, and the enemy was exhausted. The battle became known as the Battle of the Chains. It would be the first of a long line of defeats Khalid dealt to an empire that had assumed these people were beneath its notice. Some of its own soldiers accepted Islam after seeing it.

I hear and I obey

While the Muslims were fighting for Damascus, Abu Bakr died and Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) became the leader. Umar sent a letter appointing Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah as commander, which meant removing Khalid. Abu Ubaidah, trustworthy as ever, held the letter quietly until the battle was won so as not to unsettle the army, then came to Khalid almost in embarrassment and read it to him.

Khalid could have torn the community apart in that moment. The man he had been in the days of ignorance would have done exactly that. Instead he said, "We hear and we obey. I will accept any position you give me." He served on, an adviser and a soldier under the very command he had once held.

Umar's reason was not a grudge, whatever later writers claimed. A dangerous idea had begun to grow: that the Muslims could not lose while Khalid was among them, that victory came from him. Umar feared the people would trust the sword more than the One who held it. He chose to protect their hearts over their winning streak, and Khalid understood.

The hair beneath the helmet

At the farewell pilgrimage, when the Prophet ﷺ had his head shaved, he gave some of his hair to Khalid. It was a quiet sign of love and honour, a way of binding this late companion close. Khalid kept it, and where he kept it tells you everything.

Years later, at the great battle of Yarmouk against the Byzantines, Khalid's helmet fell during the fighting. This careful general, who would never risk himself needlessly, threw himself into the ranks to find it, almost recklessly. Afterward, when people asked why, he explained that he kept the Prophet's hair in the front of that helmet. He had not been protecting his head. He had been protecting what the Prophet ﷺ had given him.

At Yarmouk, roughly twenty-five thousand Muslims overcame an army four times their size. It was the turning point against the Roman empire, and it is remembered as one of the most significant battles in history.

Dying on his bed

Khalid did not live long after Umar became leader, and the end did not come the way he had always imagined. In his last months, unable to fight, he turned to the Quran he had never had time to learn, reading from dawn until midday, falling in love with it in the quiet he had never known.

He died in his bed in Homs. For a man who had given everything to the battlefield, it felt almost unbearable. He showed his visitors his body, scarred from head to foot, and said there was not a hand's span on him without the mark of a sword, an arrow, or a spear, "and now I die on my bed as a camel dies." Then he said, "May the eyes of the cowards never sleep."

One of his companions reminded him of the name the Prophet ﷺ had given him. You may break every sword in battle, he said, but you are the Sword of Allah, and the Sword of Allah will not be broken. Over more than a hundred campaigns, outnumbered nearly every time, Khalid had never been defeated. Abu Bakr is said to have wept that women would no longer give birth to a man like him.

What Khalid's life teaches us

His story is not really about winning battles. It is about a strong man learning where his strength belonged, and giving it up the moment he was asked.

  • Strength has to be reined in. Khalid's gift was also his flaw. His growth was not in being braver, but in learning when not to draw the sword.
  • A mistake can be a doorway, not a verdict. The Prophet ﷺ corrected Khalid without crushing him, then moved him into a place to grow. We can do the same for the people we lead.
  • Obedience is harder than command. When he was removed, Khalid said, "We hear and we obey." The ego that wins is the one willing to step down quietly.
  • Victory was never his to give. Umar feared people would trust the sword over the One who held it. Khalid agreed. Whatever we are good at, the help comes from Allah.

Questions

Who was Khalid ibn al-Walid?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and a military commander known as Saifullah, the Sword of Allah. He led the Muslim armies in the Ridda wars and against the Persian and Roman empires, and is said never to have lost a battle.

Why was Khalid called the Sword of Allah?

The Prophet ﷺ gave him the name after the battle of Mu'tah, where Khalid took up the banner and led the Muslims out of a difficult position. Abu Bakr later repeated it as his reason for trusting Khalid with command.

Why did Umar remove Khalid from command?

Not out of any grudge. People had begun to believe victory came from Khalid himself, and Umar wanted to protect their faith by reminding them that help comes from Allah, not from any one man. Khalid accepted the decision.

What can we learn from the life of Khalid?

That strength must be disciplined, that mistakes can become lessons rather than verdicts, and that the hardest test for a capable person is to obey and step aside when asked.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to admire Khalid for the battles. The harder, truer thing to admire is the way he handled himself off the field: corrected and not broken, honoured and not spoiled, removed from command and answering only, "We hear and we obey." His sword made him famous. His humility made him a companion.

Most of us will never lead an army, but all of us know the pull of wanting credit for what we do well. Khalid's life asks a quiet question into an ordinary day: when you are good at something, can you still hold it loosely, knowing the help was never yours alone? Carry that question with you, and his story has done its work.
