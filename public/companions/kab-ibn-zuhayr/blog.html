Ka'b ibn Zuhayr

The Poet of the First Burda

He had a death sentence on his head. He had spent years writing the cruelest verses he could compose against the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the Muslims, and his poems were recited aloud in the public squares of Makkah.

This is the story of Ka'b ibn Zuhayr (may Allah be pleased with him): the greatest poet of his day, who walked unrecognised into a mosque in Madinah, placed his hand in the hand of the man he had wronged, and asked to be forgiven.

The Companions

Ka'b ibn Zuhayr

The Poet of the First Burda

Born into the house of the master poet

To understand Ka'b, you first have to understand his father. Zuhayr ibn Abi Sulma was the great poet of his age, the king poet of the Arabs. His long poem about the ending of an old war between two tribes was counted among the seven famous odes that were hung in the Kaaba, the verses that the Arabs treasured as their history, their genealogy, and the very standard of their language.

Poetry, in that world, was power. A poet was the media of his time. Zuhayr was so honoured that he amassed a fortune from his words alone, showered with wealth by the chiefs he praised. When the scholars of the seerah later asked who was the greatest of all poets, companions who loved language pointed to Zuhayr.

Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA) once asked Abdullah ibn Abbas (RA) what made Zuhayr so beloved. The answer was telling: he did not speak foolishness, he did not betray sound logic, and he never praised anyone except for what was truly in them. His verses were not only eloquent. They were honest.

A father's warning, a son's gift

Zuhayr did not want Ka'b to become a poet. He feared the corrupting world of poetry, and he feared that his son might never live up to the name he had built, that one careless line could bring the whole legacy down. He warned Ka'b plainly: if he heard him reciting poetry, he would strike him for every word.

But the boy could not stay away. He kept studying his father's poetry in private, learning its grammar and its structure, until one day he had the courage to recite his own lines to his father. Zuhayr tested him, twisted lines on him, pushed him hard, and finally gave his blessing. The gift was real. Ka'b would carry his father's craft.

Before he died, Zuhayr is said to have had a dream of a rope let down from the sky that he reached for but could not grasp. He understood it to mean that an awaited prophet would come to that land, but that he would not live to see him. He told his sons: if this chosen one comes, follow him. Then he passed away, before the revelation began.

Ka'b inherited his father's wealth, and he inherited his throne. He became the master poet of the Arabs, the natural heir, the one whose verses everyone studied.

The poet who chose the wrong side

When the message of the Prophet ﷺ began to spread, the poets had the most to lose. The Quran was beyond anything they could compose, and it exposed their craft as small. The festivals, the bribery, the position they held in society: all of it was threatened. Most of them turned against Islam, and Ka'b was among them.

It is worth pausing on that. A poet at the time was not only an artist. He was a voice that could lift a tribe or tear a reputation apart, and the powerful paid well to be praised. To accept the Quran was to watch all of that fall away. For a man who had inherited both the wealth and the throne of poetry, the cost looked impossibly high, and so he hardened himself against the very spirit of what was coming.

His tribe, Banu Muzaynah, lived outside Makkah and Madinah, so at first he could keep his distance and avoid taking a clear stand. But his hostility deepened when his younger brother, Bujayr, went to listen to the Prophet ﷺ, accepted Islam, and did not come back.

It enraged Ka'b. He felt his brother had been taken from him. He sent Bujayr biting verses, mocking him for abandoning the path of their father and their mother, accusing him of being intoxicated by the cup of Abu Bakr, of following an illiterate and brainless crowd. When the Prophet ﷺ heard the lines, he simply said that Ka'b was right about one thing: Bujayr had not found his father or mother upon this faith, and had been brave enough to follow the truth anyway.

The world closes in

Ka'b kept writing. From his safe distance he composed poem after poem against the Prophet ﷺ, against Islam, and against the Muslims, and his verses were carried into the public squares and recited there. The worst of them slandered the Muslims and made vile implications about Muslim women, the same ugly weapon that humiliates the one who wields it more than the one it targets.

Then the ground began to shift. The Prophet ﷺ was victorious. After the opening of Makkah, the world was closing in, and it grew harder and harder for Ka'b to find anywhere to recite his attacks. A death sentence now hung over him, for he was treated as one who incited war.

His brother Bujayr, who was now with the Prophet ﷺ, wrote to him. The letter, fittingly for that family, came in verse. Come to him while there is still something left in your heart, Bujayr urged. He does not kill anyone who comes to him repentant. He has forgiven people who did far worse than you. This is not about wanting your blood. Turn to Allah, and you will be saved.

Into the mosque, unrecognised

Ka'b made his decision. He slipped into Madinah quietly and stayed with an old acquaintance. Then, at the time of the dawn prayer, he wrapped his face and went to the mosque. He found the Prophet ﷺ, sat close, and placed his hand in his hand, still unrecognised.

It was a dangerous moment. Anyone who realised who he was could have struck before he finished a sentence. So he spoke carefully. He asked, as if about someone else: if Ka'b ibn Zuhayr came to you repentant, after everything he has done, would you forgive him? The Prophet ﷺ said yes.

Then Ka'b uncovered his face. "I am Ka'b ibn Zuhayr," he said, "and I bear witness that there is no god but Allah, and that you are the Messenger of Allah." One of the Ansar leapt up: we know this man, let me deal with him. The Prophet ﷺ told him to stop. Leave him. He has come repentant.

The poem that left him

Ka'b was a poet, and he had come prepared. There in the mosque he recited the ode that history remembers as the first Burda. It is sixty lines long, and it is called Banat Suad, which means "Suad has departed." It opens with the grief of being left behind, and many scholars read that loss as the days of ignorance themselves slipping away: the wealth, the wine, the wandering life that finally showed him its emptiness.

He does not even reach the Prophet ﷺ until line forty, and then the praise pours out. He admits he was told that the Messenger of Allah meant to take his life, but that pardon is always to be hoped for from such a man. He says that if an elephant stood where he was standing and saw what he saw, it would tremble with fear. He calls the Prophet ﷺ a drawn sword from whose light guidance is sought, one of the swords of Allah.

When he finished, the Prophet ﷺ stood, took off his own cloak, his burda, and placed it on Ka'b's shoulders. The man who had walked in wrapped and afraid of being killed now walked out wearing the mantle of the Prophet ﷺ, an assurance of safety and a sign of love.

There was one correction. Ka'b's first verses had praised only the Muhajirun, because an Ansari had threatened him. The Prophet ﷺ told him: you wounded the Ansar with your tongue, now praise them with it. So Ka'b composed lines in their honour too.

The mantle and the name

Ka'b never gave up that cloak. He wore it everywhere. Later, Mu'awiyah tried to buy it from him, and Ka'b refused outright. He kept it for the rest of his life, staying in Madinah, writing poetry in praise of the Prophet ﷺ and in defence of the Muslims, even long after the Prophet ﷺ had passed away.

It became a saying among the people: the greatest poet of the days of ignorance was Zuhayr, and the greatest poet of Islam was his son Ka'b. The father had been the master poet of one age, and the son became the master poet of the next. Zuhayr's old fear had proven empty. The name did not fall. It rose, and Ka'b's own sons and grandsons carried it on as poets after him.

Ka'b was not alone in this. The transcript closes with another such poet, Abdullah ibn al-Ziba'ra, who had attacked Islam from the heart of Makkah and traded verses with Hassan ibn Thabit, the Prophet's own poet. He too fled, then returned to embrace Islam, and the Prophet ﷺ told him that Islam wipes away whatever came before it. These were not minor men. They had been weapons against the faith, and the Prophet ﷺ had the heart to forgive them.

What Ka'b's life teaches us

His story turns on a single choice: to stop running from the truth and walk toward it, even when he had every reason to expect the worst.

  • It is never too late to turn back. Ka'b had spent years as an open enemy, with a sentence on his head. He still came, and he was still received with mercy.
  • Forgiveness is stronger than revenge. The Prophet ﷺ could have let the sentence stand. Instead he gave Ka'b his own cloak, and turned an enemy's tongue into a voice for good.
  • Gifts should serve what is true. Ka'b's talent had been used to wound. Once his heart changed, the same gift became praise, defence, and something he gave the rest of his life to.
  • Honest words carry further than flattery. His father was loved because he praised people only for what was truly in them. Sincerity outlasts exaggeration, in poetry and in life.

Questions

Who was Ka'b ibn Zuhayr?

He was the son of Zuhayr ibn Abi Sulma and one of the greatest poets of the Arabs. He first attacked the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the Muslims in verse, then came to Madinah, accepted Islam, and became a poet of Islam.

What is the first Burda?

It is the ode Ka'b recited to the Prophet ﷺ when he came to repent, known as Banat Suad. After he finished, the Prophet ﷺ placed his own cloak (burda) on his shoulders, which is why the poem carries that name. It is the earliest poem recorded with this meaning, distinct from later, more famous poems also called the Burda.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ forgive him?

Ka'b came to him repentant and accepted Islam. As his brother Bujayr had told him, the Prophet ﷺ did not seek the blood of those who came to him in repentance, and had already forgiven people who had done far worse.

What can we learn from Ka'b ibn Zuhayr?

That it is never too late to turn back, that forgiveness can be stronger than revenge, and that a gift once used to harm can be turned toward good.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are not at war with anyone. But almost everyone carries something they regret, a stretch of years spent on the wrong thing, words they wish they could take back, a feeling that they have gone too far to be welcomed home. Ka'b had all of that, and more. What he did was simple and very hard: he stopped running, and he came.

His story is an invitation to do the same with whatever we have been avoiding. The door is open wider than we fear. And the gift we think disqualifies us, the talent, the voice, the energy we once aimed at the wrong things, is exactly what can be turned, today, toward something true.
