Khadijah bint Khuwaylid

The First to Believe

Before there was a single mosque, before a single verse was recited in public, before anyone on earth had said the word "Muslim," there was a woman in Makkah who looked at a frightened man and told him, calmly, that God would never abandon him.

This is the story of Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with her): the most honoured woman of Makkah, the first person to accept Islam, and the steadfast support behind the first revelation.

Khadijah bint Khuwaylid

The First to Believe

The most honoured woman in Makkah

Khadijah was born around the year 555, into one of the noble houses of Quraysh. She shared the same lineage as the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ back to their common ancestor Qusayy ibn Kilab. Her father, Khuwaylid, was a chief who had once stood ready to defend the Kaaba.

Long before Islam, people called her Tahirah: the pure one. It was said of her that she never lied, never backbit, and never hurt or upset anyone. She was beautiful, literate, and sharp, and she never bowed to the idols of her people.

She was also, by any measure of her time, immensely successful. Widowed twice by the age of 25, she grew her wealth into the largest trading caravan in Makkah. Over her roof flew a green silk pavilion, and it was not a banner of wealth: it was a sign to the poor that this was a house where they would be helped.

A marriage built on character

Tired of being cheated by merchants, Khadijah looked for an honest man to take her goods to Syria. Her sister pointed her to a young shepherd known across Makkah for one thing above all: he was al-Amin, the trustworthy. She hired him, and her servant Maysarah returned with story after story of his honesty on the road.

When a friend suggested marriage, the Prophet ﷺ wondered why a woman of her standing would ever accept him. She had already turned down some of the most powerful men in Makkah. Yet when she told him why she wanted him, she did not mention his lineage or his looks. She said: "I have come to you because of your noble character and your truthful tongue."

She was about 40, he was about 25. For the next 25 years he married no one else. Years later, the Qur'an would describe the One who had quietly arranged all of this:

Did He not find you an orphan and shelter you? Did He not find you lost and guide you? Did He not find you in need and make you self-sufficient?Qur'an 93:6-8

The cave, and the words that held him

By his late thirties, the Prophet ﷺ had everything a man of Makkah could want. Then the dreams began, and a love of solitude drew him up to a cave on the mountain of Hira. The climb takes the better part of an hour. Khadijah, by then in her fifties, made that climb herself to carry him food and water so he would not have to break his retreat.

It was there that the angel Jibril came to him. He returned trembling and broke into the house saying, "Cover me, cover me." She held him until the fear left him. When he was finally able to speak, he confided the thing he feared most: that he had lost his mind.

In that moment, a single wrong word could have broken him. She chose hers perfectly. "No," she said. "Never. Rejoice. By Allah, Allah would never disgrace you." Then she told him why: he kept good relations, spoke only the truth, carried the burdens of others, and stood by those in hardship. She believed in him before he was certain of himself. Soon the command came:

You, wrapped in your cloak, arise and give warning! Proclaim the greatness of your Lord; cleanse yourself; keep away from all filth.Qur'an 74:1-5

Standing with him through the boycott

"You are the first I am calling to this," he said. "And I am the first to accept it," she answered. With that, Khadijah became the first believer in the history of Islam.

As the message spread, so did the persecution, and she spent her great fortune on the cause until little of it was left. When Quraysh boycotted the Prophet's clan, Khadijah did not have to enter the valley of hardship. She belonged to a different clan and could have stayed in the comfort of her home. She chose the valley.

A woman who had lived her whole life in elegance was reduced to eating what she could find to survive, and she never once complained. In those years the angel Jibril came with a message meant only for her: that Allah Himself sent her peace, and the glad tidings of a home in Paradise made of pearl, where there would be no noise and no weariness.

The Year of Sorrow

Khadijah died around the year 620, in what became known as the Year of Sorrow. The Prophet ﷺ buried her himself, lowering into the grave the woman whose arms had held him when he came down from the mountain. Those close to him feared his grief might take him too.

He never forgot her. Years later, our mother Aisha (RA) narrated that not a day passed without him mentioning Khadijah. When he conquered Makkah at last, returning in triumph to the city that had driven him out, he did not stay in its finest homes. He pitched his tent near her grave.

When Aisha once asked whether Allah had not given him someone better, his answer was firm: "She believed in me when no one else did. She supported me with her wealth when others held back." And in another narration, a line of pure tenderness: "I was blessed with her love."

The first of many firsts

Her place in this series is earned many times over. She was the first to believe. She was the first to pray beside the Prophet ﷺ, the first to make wudu and the first to make tawaf at his side. She was the first person in this ummah to receive salaam from Allah, carried to her by the angel Jibril.

She did not live to see the religion she believed in take its final shape. She never prayed the five daily prayers as we know them, never fasted a Ramadan of obligation, never saw Madinah. Her whole share of Islam was its hardest, earliest years, and she gave herself to them completely.

When the people of Makkah counted their great names, hers was not on the list. When Allah counted the first of the believers, hers was the first name of all.

What Khadijah's life teaches us

Her story is not a single dramatic moment. It is a quarter of a century of steadiness, and that is exactly why it endures.

  • Character is the foundation. She chose the Prophet ﷺ for his honesty and his good character, the same qualities the Qur'an would later define as righteousness.
  • Quiet support can change history. She did not preach in the streets. She held a frightened man, said the right words, and gave him the ground to stand on.
  • Real generosity asks for nothing back. She spent her wealth, her standing, and her comfort, and considered the gratitude of Allah enough.
  • Conviction outlasts hardship. She walked into the boycott by choice and never complained her way back out of it.

Questions

Who was Khadijah bint Khuwaylid?

A respected and successful merchant of Makkah and the first wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. She was the first person to accept Islam and is honoured as the first of the Mothers of the Believers.

Why is Khadijah called the first believer?

When the Prophet ﷺ returned from the cave of Hira after the first revelation and told her what had happened, she accepted his message without hesitation, before any other man, woman, or child.

How old was Khadijah when she married the Prophet?

According to the well-known narrations, she was about 40 and the Prophet about 25. They were married for about 25 years, until her death, and he did not marry anyone else in her lifetime.

What can we learn from the life of Khadijah?

The weight of good character, the strength of quiet and steadfast support, and generosity that asks for nothing in return.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to imagine that faith needs a stage: a battle, a migration, a public stand. Khadijah's life says otherwise. Her faith showed itself in a steady voice at the worst possible moment, in wealth quietly spent, in a climb up a mountain with food for a husband who needed her. None of it was loud. All of it mattered.

Most of us are tested the way she was: not in front of crowds, but in our own homes, with the people who depend on us. Her life is an invitation to be the one who steadies others, who gives without keeping count, who believes in what is right before it becomes easy. Carry one of those into today, and her story has done its work.
