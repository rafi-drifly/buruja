The Companions

Urwa ibn az-Zubayr

The First Historian of Islam

Much of what we know about the life of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ did not come from a battlefield or a famous mosque. It came from quiet conversations in one small room, between an aunt and the nephew who sat with her and wrote down everything he heard.

This is the story of Urwa ibn az-Zubayr (may Allah be pleased with him): the nephew of Aisha, one of the seven jurists of Medina, and the first man to gather and write the story of the Prophet ﷺ for the rest of the world.

Urwa ibn az-Zubayr

The First Historian of Islam

Born into a family of martyrs

Urwa was born around the year 23 after the Hijra, near the end of the caliphate of Umar and the beginning of the caliphate of Uthman. He came late to a remarkable family. His father was az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam, the cousin of the Prophet ﷺ. His mother was Asma bint Abi Bakr, and his aunt was Aisha (RA).

His father named all of his children after martyrs, the way the Prophet ﷺ named his children after prophets. So Urwa carried the name of Urwa ibn Mas'ud, the leader who was struck down by the arrows of his own people for preaching Islam.

He was not a companion of the Prophet ﷺ. He belonged to the generation after, the tabi'in. But he stood so close to that first generation, through his family and his learning, that he is sometimes spoken of as if he were one of them.

Choosing knowledge over the sword

Urwa grew up admiring his elder brother, Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr: the warrior, the leader, the hero of the family. One of his earliest memories was of Abdullah defending a man under siege, while Urwa, still a small boy, watched in fear and pride.

When the Battle of the Camel came, Urwa and his cousin were turned away for being too young to fight. He never returned to the path of battle. He chose another road. While others took up the sword, he took up knowledge, and he stayed on that road for the rest of his life.

There is a famous account of four young men sitting together and naming what they hoped to become. One said he would rule the two holy cities. One said he would govern the two Iraqs. One said he would lead it all. Urwa said only this: as for me, I will take knowledge, and seek the forgiveness of my Lord. The narrators say each of them was given what he asked for. These were people who aimed high, and did not wait to grow old before they began.

His father had warned them early. He once asked his sons why they were not seeking knowledge, and reminded them: you are the young of your people today, but one day you will be the elders. And what good, he asked, is an old man who is ignorant?

So Urwa did not wait. He sat in the front row of every circle of learning he could find, with the companions of the Prophet ﷺ, in Medina, in Egypt, wherever he travelled, collecting and writing down all that he could.

The room where the seerah was kept

Urwa had one advantage no other student had. His aunt was Aisha (RA), and her sister was his mother. While the other students of Medina had to sit behind a curtain and ask their questions through it, Urwa could simply enter her house and sit with her.

He went to her almost every day. He asked her about everything that had passed between her and the Prophet ﷺ: how he lived, how he prayed, where he slept, the small daily things that no one else thought to record. She would point to the very place the Prophet ﷺ used to pray, and describe how he lay down to rest.

Much of the seerah we read today, the intimate, human picture of the Prophet's ﷺ life, was gathered in that room, from those conversations between an aunt and her nephew. He memorised, and he wrote. He memorised, and he wrote.

He absorbed more than facts. He inherited her eloquence. When people asked how he had become so poetic, he would answer simply: do you not know who my aunt is? He met the most eloquent of the Arabs, he said, and never found anyone more eloquent than Aisha.

A verse, and a flood of tears

Urwa once came to visit his aunt and found her standing in prayer, weeping over a single verse, repeating it again and again as she made her supplication. He did not want to disturb her, so he went out to the market to finish some errands.

When he returned a long while later, he found her in the same place, reading the same verse, still weeping. It was a verse about the mercy Allah had shown, and the punishment He had spared them from.

He learned more from her than narrations. He learned how she stood before her Lord. Years later, near the end of her life, Urwa said that four years before Aisha died he had a quiet certainty: that if she passed away, he would not have missed a single one of her narrations, and her knowledge would not be lost.

The first to write the story

When the long years of civil war finally settled, the caliph Abd al-Malik sought to honour what remained of az-Zubayr's family. He returned to Urwa the sword his brother Abdullah had carried, and Urwa named the marks on it, one from Badr, two from the Battle of Yarmuk.

Urwa took no position of power. He asked for none. Instead the caliph wrote to him with questions, about the law, about the Prophet ﷺ, and Urwa answered in letters. Some of the earliest written records of the seerah are those very letters.

He became the first to compile and write the life of the Prophet ﷺ. The historians who came after him all drew from his work. He was counted as the foremost of the seven jurists of Medina, and later the teacher of Imam Malik. The great scholar az-Zuhri, who was the first commissioned to collect hadith, said of him: I found him an ocean with no shore.

He once explained how he could bear to stand aside from the wars of his time: it might be, he said, that a word of humiliation I carried with patience will give me lasting honour with Allah. So he kept to his knowledge, and spread it everywhere he could reach.

The leg, the son, and the prayer

Late in his life, Urwa travelled to Syria to visit the caliph, with his son beside him. On the way something bit his leg, and by the time he arrived it had turned, and the doctors said it would have to be amputated to save his life. There was no anaesthetic in those days. They offered him wine to dull his senses. He refused, saying there is no cure to be found in what is harmful.

He had one request. Let me pray first, he said, and take the leg while I am in prostration, in the deepest part of my focus. So they did. He did not cry out. He did not break his prayer. His leg was taken while he stood before Allah, and he prayed on. This was the family whose father and brother were both killed while praying.

On the same journey, his young son was kicked by a horse and died. They came to him gently with the news, while he was still bandaging the loss of his leg.

His answer is preserved to this day. He turned it into a supplication: O Allah, I had four limbs, You took one and left me three. I had four sons, You took one and left me three. If You have taken, You have given. If You have tested me, You have spared me. I have no complaint against You.

The well, the fast, and the last Friday

Urwa read a quarter of the Quran every day from the mushaf, and stood at night to recite that same quarter from memory. He never gave up that habit, not even on the night his leg was amputated. He fasted nearly every day of the year, breaking his fast only on the two days of Eid.

There is a story in the Quran of people who hoarded the fruit of their garden so the poor could not reach it. Urwa wanted to be their opposite. When his own fruit ripened, he opened his gardens and called the people in to take what they wished, and Allah kept on blessing what grew there.

Troubled by the politics and the injustice he saw around him, he eventually moved away and built a home in the valley of al-Aqiq, where he taught and dug a well still known for the sweetness of its water.

He died in the year 94 after the Hijra, a year so heavy with the loss of scholars that it was called the Year of the Jurists. He died on a Friday, fasting. When his family begged him to break his fast, he said he hoped to break it instead with the drink of the next life. He passed shortly before sunset, leaving behind a gift almost every Muslim has received without knowing his name.

What Urwa's life teaches us

Urwa never led an army or governed a province. His whole life was one quiet, patient devotion to knowledge, and that is exactly why it still reaches us.

  • Begin while you are young. His father told him: you are the young of your people now, but one day you will be the elders. Do not wait until you are old to start learning, praying, and reading the Quran.
  • Quiet, steady work outlasts the loud kind. While others fought famous battles, Urwa sat and memorised and wrote. Centuries later, it is his patient record that we still read.
  • Patience can be its own victory. He chose to bear a small humiliation rather than pick up a sword, trusting that lasting honour with Allah was worth more than being proven right.
  • Gratitude can survive any loss. On the day he lost his leg and his son, he counted what remained and thanked his Lord. He met testing with surrender, not complaint.

Questions

Who was Urwa ibn az-Zubayr?

He was the son of az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam and Asma bint Abi Bakr, the nephew of Aisha (RA), and the brother of Abdullah ibn az-Zubayr. He belonged to the generation after the companions and became one of the seven jurists of Medina.

Why is he called the first historian of Islam?

He was the first to gather and write down the life of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in an organised way. Much of it came from his aunt Aisha, and the historians who came after him drew from his work.

How did Urwa lose his leg?

On a journey to Syria his leg became diseased and had to be amputated. He refused wine to numb the pain and asked instead to be in prayer. His leg was removed while he was in prostration, and he did not break his prayer.

What can we learn from the life of Urwa?

To seek knowledge early, to value quiet and patient work, to choose patience over conflict, and to meet loss with gratitude rather than complaint.

Why this story stays with us

Urwa lived in a time of wars and famous men, and he chose to sit quietly and write. It would have been easy to call that the smaller life. But the loud battles of his age faded, while the careful work he did in one small room became a window the whole world looks through.

Most of our own days are quiet too. We are not asked to lead armies, only to be faithful in small, unseen work, to begin before we feel ready, to bear what stings without bitterness, and to thank God for what remains when something is taken. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
