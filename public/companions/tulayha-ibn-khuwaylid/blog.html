The Companions

Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid

From False Prophet to Martyr

Of all the people who walked into the mosque of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, there may be none harder to place than this one. He is the only man we know of who claimed to be a prophet, gathered armies, fought the Muslims, and then turned back to God.

This is the story of Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid (may Allah be pleased with him): a tribal chief from Banu Asad, a gifted poet and horseman, who fell as far as a person can fall, and then ran the rest of the way home.

Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid

From False Prophet to Martyr

A warning, and a wave of false claims

The Prophet ﷺ once said that the Hour would not come until nearly thirty liars had appeared, each one claiming to be a prophet of God, though there is no prophet after him. Across history, scholars have traced roughly that number: people who managed to gather real movements around a false claim.

The Qur'an names this as one of the gravest of all wrongs. To invent a lie against God, to say revelation has come to you when nothing has, is placed among the very worst things a person can do.

Tulayha belongs to this dark chapter. But he belongs to it in a way no one else does, because his story does not end where it begins.

Who could be more wicked than someone who invents a lie against God, or claims, 'A revelation has come to me,' when no revelation has been sent to him.Qur'an 6:93

The man before the fall

Tulayha was a chief of Banu Asad, wealthy and commanding, with land that sat closer to Madinah than most of his rivals. He was a notable horseman and, like many of the leaders of his time, a poet. Among the Arabs, poetry carried the weight of sorcery.

He opposed Islam early. For years his tribe stood with Quraysh against the Prophet ﷺ, taking part in the campaigns against Madinah. His people were among the battalions that gathered for the great siege of the city, and he kept up his agitation from the outside. He had never met the Prophet ﷺ. He was simply living inside a story Makkah had told about him: that this man was a threat who would break their idols, their tribes, and their families.

It helps to remember how little many of these tribes understood. Out in the desert, far from the daily company of the Prophet ﷺ, the ideas of one God and a messenger sent by Him were simple to grasp but easy to twist. Faith had not had the time to settle into their hearts, and a clever man could bend the concept without seeming to change much at all.

Then, in the ninth year after the Hijrah, when delegations were arriving from every direction to accept Islam, Tulayha came too. He came among the men of Banu Asad and entered the faith. For a moment, the story could have turned right there.

We did you a favour

When the men of Banu Asad reached the mosque, the one speaking before Tulayha boasted to the Prophet ﷺ. We came to you through the darkest of nights, he said, through a hard season and a hard road, and you never even sent us a messenger. He was trying to win favour, as if their Islam was a gift to the Prophet ﷺ.

An answer came in the Qur'an. They were told plainly not to count their faith as a favour they had done. If anything, the favour ran the other way: it was God who had guided them, if they were truthful.

It is a quiet warning sitting under this whole story. Faith that is worn for advantage, that keeps a ledger and waits to be thanked, has not yet settled into the heart. For Tulayha, it had not settled at all.

The claim

When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Islam outside Madinah was still fragile, and Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) had a great deal on his hands. Some tribes refused to pay the zakat, treating a pillar of the faith as a tax they could now drop. Others looked for a way to claim power for themselves by copying the very man they had once fought. Tulayha thought the same thought, and made the same move. He claimed to be a prophet.

His was not a small disturbance. He was close to Madinah, he was eloquent in a way the other claimants were not, and he was favoured by many of the surrounding tribes. Of all the false prophets of that time, perhaps only one stirred greater trouble. He began to perform tricks, small feats of deception, to make himself look like a man who received revelation.

Like every deviation, his message worked by removing what people found heavy. He sent word to Madinah, through his brother, that God did not ask you to put your face in the dirt or bend your back like a bow in prayer. Pray standing, he said, and be quick about it. He lifted the obligations people least wanted to carry, and to some, that was exactly the appeal. It is an old pattern: those who drift from the truth tend to drop whatever is most inconvenient, and there are always people glad to be told they need not carry it.

Defeat, and a long silence

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) sent his armies to set the region right. At the Battle of Buzakhah, Tulayha's forces met a much smaller Muslim army led by Khalid ibn al-Walid (may Allah be pleased with him). Two beloved companions, Ukkashah ibn Mihsan and Thabit ibn Aqram, were killed in the fighting before the battle turned.

Tulayha had gathered the larger force by far, but fighting on his own ground, Khalid struck from several directions at once and broke his army anyway. He sent Tulayha running. He fled the entire region and went to live in Syria, the same land where another false prophet had ended up before him.

Then time passed, and something began to move in him. Away from the noise, Tulayha actually read about Islam. He came to understand it, and he saw how deeply he had wronged himself. The chief who had once led armies against the believers found himself a fugitive in a foreign land, wondering whether the door was still open.

Would Abu Bakr accept me?

He had no example to follow. No false prophet had ever come back. So Tulayha wrote a letter to Abu Bakr, and in it he placed a poem. He named his crimes one by one: the men he had killed, every battle he had fought against the Muslims. And he said the greatest crime of all was his blindness to the beauty of Islam.

He confessed that he had been neither Jew nor Christian, only a disbeliever and a hypocrite, and that the devil had cast something into his heart that made him the most miserable of men. Now he bore witness to the truth, and he had nowhere left to run. If God would not accept him, he could not flee to Syria, nor to Rome, nor from God Himself, who would one day take him back.

Abu Bakr read the letter and judged him sincere. His repentance was accepted. Out of caution, Abu Bakr kept the returning apostates out of the army for a time, not to deny their faith but to protect the Muslims. Tulayha would have to wait, and prove what was in him. He was too ashamed, by his own admission, to come and stand before the very man he had raised an army against, and so he came back slowly, dragging his feet under the weight of what he had done.

The finish line

He made his way back, and one year he was seen making tawaf around the Kaaba: a strange sight for those he had once hurt. Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) met him with open anger. How can you hope to be saved from the Fire, he asked, when you killed Ukkashah and Thabit? I will never love you, he told him, for what you did.

Tulayha answered with humility. Those were two righteous men, he said, whom God honoured with martyrdom through my hand, and in His wisdom He did not let me die at theirs, which would have sent me to the Fire. He did not boast. He grieved with Umar, and asked only for a chance.

He got it, though carefully. When the request reached Khalid ibn al-Walid, the guidance was to consult Tulayha in matters of war but not to place him in command. Repentance was accepted; trust would be rebuilt in time. Sent into the wars against Persia, he fought as a man with no fear of death, always first into danger and last to leave, guarding the backs of the others rather than hanging safely behind them.

At Qadisiyyah he slipped alone into the night, reached the very tent of the enemy commander, scattered the horses and cut the ropes that held it, and returned with intelligence that shaped the battle. He was reckoned the equal of a thousand horsemen. At the Battle of Nahawand, the blow that broke the Persian Empire, he charged in, fought bravely, and was killed. He died a martyr, may Allah be pleased with him.

What Tulayha's life teaches us

His is not a gentle story. That is the point. If the door of return was open even to him, it is open to anyone who is honest enough to walk through it.

  • It is how you finish, not how you start. Tulayha began as a false prophet at war with the believers. He ended as a martyr in their ranks. The race is judged at the finish line.
  • Do not close the door of repentance on anyone. No one will commit a graver sin than he did, and yet his return was accepted. If his was, then so is the one you keep telling yourself is too late.
  • Repentance is something you act out, not just feel. He named his wrongs plainly, faced the people he had hurt, and then spent his courage defending the very community he once attacked.
  • Faith worn for advantage has not yet settled. When he carried Islam as a useful position, it slipped away. When he finally understood it, far from any power to gain, it held.

Questions

Who was Tulayha ibn Khuwaylid?

A chief of the Banu Asad tribe, a poet and horseman who accepted Islam, then claimed prophethood after the Prophet ﷺ fell ill, fought the Muslims, and later repented sincerely and returned to the faith.

Is Tulayha considered a companion of the Prophet?

Yes. The scholars hold that anyone who met the Prophet ﷺ as a Muslim and died as a Muslim counts as a companion. Tulayha met him as a believer in the year of delegations and, despite all that came between, died a Muslim and a martyr.

How did such a serious wrong end in his repentance being accepted?

After his defeat he fled to Syria, learned what Islam truly was, and wrote to Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) confessing everything. Abu Bakr judged him sincere and accepted his return, though he kept former apostates out of the army for a time as a precaution.

What can we learn from the life of Tulayha?

That the door of repentance is never closed, that what matters is how a life finishes rather than how it began, and that real return is shown in action, not only in feeling.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never fall as far as Tulayha did. But almost all of us carry some quiet conviction that one thing we have done is past forgiving, that a door has shut for good. His life answers that thought directly. The man who claimed prophethood and led armies against the believers was met at the gate, accepted, and gathered home as a martyr.

There is a smaller lesson tucked inside the larger one. He could have decided he was beyond return and simply disappeared into a foreign land. Instead he took one step, wrote one honest letter, and let it carry him the rest of the way. Whatever it is you have been putting off facing, the example here is not despair. It is the single step toward God, made before you feel worthy of it.
