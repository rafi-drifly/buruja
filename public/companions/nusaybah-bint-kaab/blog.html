Nusaybah bint Ka'b

The Woman Who Ran Toward the Danger

On the day of Uhud, when the line broke and most of the army was running, a small circle of people closed around the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ to keep him alive. Inside that circle, swinging a sword she had picked up off the ground, was a woman.

This is the story of Nusaybah bint Ka'b (may Allah be pleased with her), known as Umm Umarah: a mother from Madinah who ran toward the danger everyone else was fleeing, and who spent the rest of her life moving in the same direction.

Nusaybah bint Ka'b

The Woman Who Ran Toward the Danger

A family that answered first

Nusaybah bint Ka'b came from Banu Najjar, a branch of the Khazraj and the maternal relatives of the Prophet ﷺ in Madinah. We know almost nothing about her early years. What we do know is that when Islam reached the city, her whole household embraced it: both of her husbands, her brothers, and her children.

Her brothers Abdullah and Abdur-Rahman ibn Ka'b were among the first of the Ansar to believe, and they were present at the battles. Her sons from her first marriage, Habib and Abdullah ibn Zaid, would become known in their own right. Faith was not something she held alone. It was the air her family breathed.

She first heard the message from Mus'ab ibn Umayr, the teacher the Prophet ﷺ had sent ahead to Madinah. From that very first gathering, her character was already visible: she did not wait for the message to come to her.

The pledge she insisted on taking

When the second pledge of al-Aqabah was arranged in Makkah, seventy men from Madinah made the difficult journey to meet the Prophet ﷺ in secret. By custom, the men gave the pledge on behalf of their households. Nusaybah did not want it given on her behalf. She went herself.

She was one of only two or three women in that group. The men lined up and shook the Prophet's hand. When her husband told the Prophet that two women had also come to give the pledge, he accepted it from them with his word, saying that he did not shake hands with women. She had travelled a long and dangerous road to hear it for herself.

She brought her young children with her on that journey. Before there was any battle to fight, she was already raising her family on the idea of standing beside the Prophet ﷺ, whatever it might cost.

The day of Uhud

She lived long enough that people would come to her in Madinah and ask what the day of Uhud had been like. When she told it, she would say she could see it still, as if she were watching it happen. It was the most defining day of her life, and she never lost a detail of it. She remembered that the women on the other side came only to beat their drums and sing, and that when their men fled, they fled too. The women on her own side had come for something different.

She had set out with the other women, among them members of the Prophet's own family, carrying water and supplies to treat the wounded. Her husband and her two sons were near the Prophet ﷺ, ready to fight. Then the battle turned. After an early victory, some of the Muslims came down too soon to gather the spoils, the enemy circled around behind them, and the field became chaos. A rumour spread that the Prophet had been killed. Some put down their weapons and wept. Many fled.

She did not think first of her husband or her sons. She thought of the Prophet ﷺ. She put down her water skin, called to a fleeing man to hand his weapon to someone who would fight, and took up a shield and a sword. Then she pushed through to the Prophet, where she said only about ten people remained, her husband and both sons among them. Later the Prophet would say that wherever he looked that day, to his right and his left, in front and behind, he saw her swinging her sword.

There are men among the believers who honoured their pledge to God: some of them have fulfilled it by death, and some are still waiting. They have not changed in the least.Quran 33:23

Thirteen wounds, and a single request

She was wounded thirteen times. One blow to her shoulder, from a heavily armoured horseman who was charging at the Prophet ﷺ, was so deep it took her a full year to recover. She had stepped into his path on purpose, unable to harm him through his armour, and taken the strike herself. When her son Abdullah was struck and fell bleeding, she bound his wound, stood him back up, and told him to keep fighting. When the Prophet pointed out the man who had wounded him, she went after that man and brought him down, and the Prophet praised Allah that she had been allowed to see it.

In the middle of the fighting, the Prophet ﷺ looked at this one wounded family still holding their ground and said what a household they were. She heard him, and she did not ask for safety or rescue. She asked him to pray that her family would be with him in Paradise.

He raised his hands and prayed, "O Allah, make them my companions in Paradise." Her answer is one of the purest lines in the whole story. She said she no longer cared what happened to her in this world after that. The promise of his company was enough.

Years earlier, she had sat in Mus'ab ibn Umayr's first gathering in Madinah, the day the message arrived. At Uhud she was beside him again, and she watched him fall as a martyr. She had seen the cost of this path with her own eyes, and she had already decided it was worth paying.

The question that brought down a verse

Nusaybah had been in the thick of battle beside the Prophet ﷺ, yet she noticed something in the way the revelation often spoke. She came to him and asked, simply, why she kept hearing the men singled out for reward while the women were not mentioned in the same way.

She was not asking from the sidelines. This was a woman who had bled beside the Messenger of Allah. And the answer came down from above: a verse of Surah al-Ahzab that names the believing men and the believing women together, side by side, in every quality of faith.

It is one of the quiet honours of her life. A companion who asked sincerely, and an answer recorded in the Book of Allah for every believing woman who would come after her.

For men and women who are devoted to God- believing men and women, obedient men and women, truthful men and women, steadfast men and women, humble men and women, charitable men and women, fasting men and women, chaste men and women, men and women who remember God often- God has prepared forgiveness and a rich reward.Quran 33:35

A son lost, and a promise kept

After Uhud, no one was going to tell Nusaybah to stay home. She was present at Hudaybiyyah, at Khaybar, at Hunayn, and beyond. But the deepest test of her life came later, through her son Habib.

The Prophet ﷺ sent Habib as an envoy to Musaylimah, the false prophet who had risen near the end of the Prophet's life and tried to claim half the earth for himself. Musaylimah asked Habib whether he bore witness that Muhammad was the Messenger of Allah, and Habib said yes. He asked whether Habib bore witness that he, Musaylimah, was also a messenger, and Habib answered only, "I cannot hear you." He asked again, and got the same answer. For that, Habib was killed cruelly and slowly, and through all of it he kept repeating that Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah.

When the news of how her son had died reached Nusaybah, she did not break. She said she had prepared him for this and sought her reward with Allah, that he had given his pledge as a boy and kept it as a man. Then she vowed that if Allah ever gave her the chance to reach Musaylimah, she would take it.

She kept that vow. At the battle of Yamamah, more than sixty years old, she went forward with her son Abdullah, calling out to be shown where the enemy of Allah was. The spear that first struck Musaylimah was thrown by Wahshi, the same man who had killed Hamza at Uhud and who now used that very spear in repentance. It was her son Abdullah who finished him. She lost her arm that day and was wounded again and again. Of it she said only that she had sought her reward from Allah for one of her two sons, and now she sought it for one of her two hands.

An old woman in the streets of Madinah

She did not stop even then. When the battle of Yarmouk came, she went out and fought with the one hand that could still carry a sword. By then she had lived through battle after battle and carried more than thirty wounds on her body.

And yet she did not die on a battlefield. She lived into old age and passed away a natural death, buried in al-Baqi in Madinah. Picture a woman in her seventies walking those streets, having given a son, an arm, and a lifetime, still carrying herself with the calm of someone who had long ago settled her account with Allah.

Umar ibn al-Khattab did not forget her. When a gift of fine silk arrived, the most precious he had received, people suggested he keep it within his own family. He refused. He sent it to Nusaybah, calling her the one most deserving of it in the whole community, and as he spoke he recalled the Prophet's words about seeing her sword on every side at Uhud.

What Nusaybah's life teaches us

Her story is almost entirely a story of the battlefield, and yet the lessons in it are not about war. They are about what conviction looks like once it is tested.

  • Love can move you toward the danger. When others ran, her first thought was the Prophet ﷺ, not herself. We are shaped by what we run toward when we are afraid.
  • Hold your faith with your own hand. She would not let the pledge be given on her behalf. The commitment she wanted, she wanted to make herself.
  • Sacrifice at its purest does not bargain. After the Prophet prayed her family would be with him in Paradise, she said she no longer cared what happened to her. The promise was enough.
  • Patience can outlast loss. She lost a son and then an arm, and she answered both with the same words: she had sought her reward from Allah.

Questions

Who was Nusaybah bint Ka'b?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah, also known as Umm Umarah. She took the pledge at the second al-Aqabah herself and is remembered above all for defending the Prophet at the battle of Uhud.

What did Nusaybah do at the battle of Uhud?

When the army broke and many fled, she took up a shield and sword and fought her way to the Prophet ﷺ, defending him alongside her husband and two sons. She was wounded thirteen times, including a deep shoulder wound that took a year to heal.

Is Nusaybah connected to a verse of the Quran?

She asked the Prophet ﷺ why she heard the men mentioned but not the women. The verse in Surah al-Ahzab (33:35) names the believing men and women side by side in every quality of faith, and her question is among its reported occasions.

What can we learn from the life of Nusaybah?

That conviction shows itself when it is tested, that it is best held with our own hands, and that sacrifice at its purest asks for nothing in return.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never see a battlefield, and that is not what her story asks of us. What it asks is quieter and harder: when the moment of fear comes, which way do we turn? She turned toward the thing she loved most, again and again, until turning that way was simply who she was.

Our tests are smaller. A hard conversation we would rather avoid, a duty we would rather hand to someone else, a loss that tempts us toward despair. Her life is an invitation to face these things calmly, and to keep our small pledges the way she kept her great one. Carry that into one moment today, and her story has done its work.
