Abu Bakra

The Freed Slave of Allah

A man sat inside a fortress on a mountain, owned by people known for their hardness, with one chance to be free. Outside, an army was leaving. So he took hold of the wheel that drew water from the well, and lowered himself down the wall.

This is the story of Abu Bakra (may Allah be pleased with him): a slave who freed himself in body and soul on a single rope, and who lived the rest of his long life as a teacher and a careful keeper of the Prophet's ﷺ words.

The Companions

Abu Bakra

The Freed Slave of Allah

A city under siege

It was eight years after the Hijra. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had returned to Makkah, opened it, and turned back the tribes that had gathered against him. Their remnant had pulled back into Ta'if, a hard mountain city, difficult to climb and harder to storm.

The Prophet ﷺ laid the city under siege but held back from a full assault. The terrain itself made attack costly, but there was more to it than that. He had not lost hope in its people. When he was asked to pray against them, he would not pray for their destruction. He still believed something good would come out of them.

In the end he chose to lift the siege and return to Madinah rather than press the attack. But before he left, he made an announcement that opened a door: whoever came down out of the forts of Ta'if was free.

Free in every sense

The words carried more weight than they first seem. When the Prophet ﷺ had entered Makkah, he told people that whoever stayed in their homes was safe. Here he was offering something larger. To come down was not only to be spared. It was to walk back to Madinah a free person.

For an enslaved man, this was the one chance to break his chains. If he could find his way out of the city without being killed by his own masters, he would belong to no one again. The narrations say about twenty-three enslaved people took that chance and came out to join the Prophet ﷺ.

One of them was a man whose escape would become his name for the rest of his life.

The man on the wheel of the well

His given name was Nufay'. He was an enslaved man inside Ta'if, and when he heard the announcement, he did not wait. He took hold of the bakra, the wooden wheel used to pump water up from the well, and lowered himself down the wall of the fortress.

Picture it: a man throwing himself out of a stronghold, hanging on a well rope, climbing down to reach the Prophet ﷺ as the army turned for home. From that day he was called Abu Bakra, the man of the wheel. It is the name under which he appears in the books of hadith, 132 times.

His mother was an enslaved Abyssinian woman named Sumayyah. The man who owned him was named al-Harith, and people assumed Abu Bakra was his son and called him by that lineage. He did not accept it, and he had his own answer ready.

Whose slave am I now

When people attributed him to his old master, Abu Bakra refused. "My name," he would say, "is Abu Bakra, the freed slave of the Prophet ﷺ." He had freed himself from slavery and from idolatry on the same day, and he wanted to be known by the One who had freed him.

If people insisted on a lineage, he told them to call him by his own father, a man who had also been enslaved, not by the master who claimed him. The most beloved name to him was the one the Prophet ﷺ had given him.

When he reached Madinah, he was not held at a distance. He was welcomed in fully, a complete member of that society, though he was not from Makkah or Madinah, was not wealthy, and had no famous lineage. The very number of his narrations is the proof: a man kept far away does not learn so much.

Then the people of Ta'if accepted Islam, and a delegation came to the Prophet ﷺ. Al-Harith came too, and said: give him back, he is mine. The Prophet ﷺ gave Abu Bakra the description he carried forever after. "No," he said. "He is the freed slave of Allah, and the freed slave of the Messenger of Allah." He belonged to no master now. He would stay in Madinah, and he would prosper.

May Allah increase you in eagerness

Abu Bakra was a man of great enthusiasm, still new to his faith and eager to do everything right. One day he came into the mosque while the Prophet ﷺ was already bowing in prayer. Afraid of missing the bowing, he went down into it at the door and walked forward in that posture until he joined the rows.

Catching the prayer that way is a mistake. You are meant to settle into the row first, then bow. Many people make the same error today, hurrying through the door to catch the imam. So watch what the Prophet ﷺ said to a man who had clearly meant well.

He did not scold him. His first words were a prayer: "May Allah increase you in eagerness." Only then did he add, gently, "but do not do it again." He saw a good heart making an honest mistake, and he chose encouragement before correction.

The scholars draw a careful distinction here, between a mistake made out of good intention and one made out of malice or greed. To treat the two the same is an injustice. When a young person stumbles while reaching for something good, the first words out of our mouth should lift them, not crush them. Then the correction comes, and it lands. Abu Bakra never hid the story. He narrated it himself, because what could have been an embarrassment had become, for him, a moment of pride: the Prophet ﷺ had made a prayer for him.

A careful keeper of the words

Abu Bakra became a trusted narrator of the Prophet's ﷺ words. From him we have the description of the eclipse prayer, and the teaching that the eclipse is not an omen of any person's birth or death, but a sign meant to turn hearts back to Allah.

He carried a warning the Prophet ﷺ repeated until those listening wished he would stop, so heavy was it on him. Asked about the gravest of the major sins, the Prophet ﷺ named associating partners with Allah and disobeying one's parents. Then he sat up and said, again and again, that false testimony was among them.

He also narrated that on a journey the Prophet ﷺ did not pass a sleeping person without calling him to prayer or gently shaking him by the foot to wake him. From him too comes the prohibition of exchanging gold for gold and silver for silver unequally, a foundation of how Muslims trade.

These are not small reports. They are some of the most formative teachings in Islam, and they reached us through a man who came down a wall on a rope. Had the Prophet ﷺ chosen to wipe out the people of Ta'if for all they had done, this fruit would never have grown.

A long life, and a quiet end

Because he had heard the Prophet ﷺ warn so often against discord, Abu Bakra stayed out of the conflicts that later divided the community, and urged others to stay out of them too. His whole family rose with him. His sons and a daughter became narrators, and his line produced scholars and a governor, to whom he once wrote: never judge between two people while you are angry.

Think of how much the world had changed for him. A man from the enslaved class of Ta'if, with no lineage anyone valued, saw his children become some of the most respected people of their time. Of his city, Basra, it was later said that no two greater people had come to it than Abu Bakra and Hussein. Islam had quietly undone the old measures of rank and tribe and wealth.

He settled in Basra and taught there for decades. Imagine meeting him in that circle, the greatest of the next generation seated before him, and asking how he had become Muslim. The answer was always the same: I came out of a fortress, holding on to the wheel of a well.

In his old age he was hurt while helping to bury one of his wives, crushed in the press of people at the grave, and the injury weakened him. As his children wept around him, he told them he was content. He could see a time of trial coming that he would not be able to fix, and he would rather meet Allah than live through a day in which he could do no good. When they spoke of bringing a doctor, he said death cannot be pushed away when its time has come. He died in Basra, and by his own wish, the foster brother the Prophet ﷺ had given him long ago in Madinah led his funeral prayer: a bond of faith that held for more than four decades.

What Abu Bakra's life teaches us

His story begins with one decisive act and then settles into a lifetime of quiet faithfulness. Both halves are worth learning from.

  • Take the door when it opens. When freedom was offered, Abu Bakra did not hesitate or wait for an easier way. He grabbed the rope and climbed. Some chances come once.
  • Let people define you by the best thing about you. He refused the name of the man who owned him and chose to be known as freed by Allah. We can decide which part of our story we carry.
  • Correct gently, and praise first. The Prophet ﷺ met an honest mistake with a prayer before a correction. Starting with harshness can push a person away; starting with kindness keeps them close.
  • Guard what you pass on. Abu Bakra spent his long life carefully repeating what he had heard. A faithful memory and an honest tongue can serve people for centuries.

Questions

Who was Abu Bakra (RA)?

He was an enslaved man in Ta'if whose given name was Nufay'. During the siege of Ta'if he lowered himself from the fortress on the wheel of a well to reach the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, was freed, and became one of the well-known narrators of hadith.

Why is he called Abu Bakra?

The Arabic word bakra refers to the wooden wheel or pulley used to draw water from a well. He escaped Ta'if by climbing down on that wheel, and the nickname stayed with him for the rest of his life.

Is Abu Bakra the same person as Abu Bakr?

No. They are two different companions whose names look almost identical in writing. Abu Bakr al-Siddiq was the close friend and successor of the Prophet ﷺ. Abu Bakra was the freed slave of Ta'if. Many readers confuse the two.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Bakra?

To seize the chance to do what is right, to be known by the best of who we are, to correct others gently, and to faithfully guard and pass on what we have learned.

Why this story stays with us

Abu Bakra is remembered for one image: a man hanging on a well rope, climbing down a wall toward a better life. But the longer story is what he did with the freedom he won. He did not chase wealth or status. He spent decades carefully teaching others and keeping out of the quarrels that hurt people.

Most of us will not have a fortress to escape. Our chances look smaller: a habit to leave, a grudge to drop, a truth to say plainly, a name we have outgrown. His life asks a quiet question. When the door opens, do we take hold of the rope? And once we are free of the thing that held us, what do we choose to do with it? He answered with decades of teaching and peace. That answer is still within our reach today.
