The Companions

Ubadah ibn al-Samit

A Man Worth a Thousand Men

Some people are given a single gift. Ubadah ibn al-Samit seemed to be given several at once: he was a warrior, a teacher, a judge, a builder, and a worshipper who wept in the night. Yet the thing his companions remembered most was not his strength. It was that no one could say he had ever wronged them.

This is the story of Ubadah ibn al-Samit (may Allah be pleased with him): a leader of the Ansar, one of the first of Madinah to believe, and a man whose word the Muslims trusted more than their own.

The Companions

Ubadah ibn al-Samit

A Man Worth a Thousand Men

A leader before he was a Muslim

Ubadah came from the tribe of Khazraj, one of the two great tribes that had settled the city of Yathrib, later Madinah, long before Islam. His father had been one of their chiefs and had died in the wars that scarred the city. So leadership fell to Ubadah early, and he carried it well.

Madinah in those years was exhausted by feuding. A long civil war had killed off many of its seniors, and a small group of its leaders went looking for something steadier. They came to Makkah during the season of pilgrimage, half hoping the place itself might hold an answer.

There they met the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. Ubadah was among that first small group, and he became one of the very first people of Madinah to accept Islam.

The two pledges

Twelve men took the first pledge with the Prophet ﷺ at Aqabah. It was called the pledge of women, not because women took it, but because it carried no commitment to fight. It was a pledge of conduct, not of war.

Ubadah narrated its terms himself. They pledged not to worship anything besides Allah, not to steal, not to commit indecency, not to kill their children, not to slander the innocent, and not to disobey in what was right. This was the moral ground of Islam, handed to a people to carry home before a single law of prayer or fasting had been defined.

A year later he returned with a much larger group, more than seventy, and took the second pledge, the one that included standing in the Prophet's defence. Ubadah was present at both. Through him, his brothers, his wife, and his son would all come to believe.

The man everyone leaned on

Those who described Ubadah said he was tall, with striking features, and very dark of skin, one of the black Arabs of Yemen. When he walked into a room, people felt both admiration and a kind of awe. He had the presence of a chief, and they yielded the room to him without being asked.

He was praised for his beauty, his eloquence, his knowledge, and his wisdom. Before Islam and after it, he was the man sent to negotiate treaties, the one everyone could sit across from at the table. As the believers of Makkah began to migrate, Ubadah was one of those who set up the shelters that held them while the fragile new city of Madinah took them in. When the Prophet ﷺ reached Madinah, it was Ubadah he entrusted to negotiate with the tribes of the city.

He was made a collector of zakat. The Prophet ﷺ warned him: fear Allah regarding this wealth, take none of it for yourself, for any of it you take will testify against you on the Day of Judgement. Ubadah said he began to tremble, and from that day he never again wished to be placed in charge of even two people, nor to be responsible for anyone's wealth.

Yet because he was a man of integrity, that was exactly the place he was pushed into again and again. Every time he tried to step away from authority, the people pulled him back toward it. His honesty was so plain that they would not hear of anyone else. It is one of the quiet ironies of his life: the more he wished to be left alone, the more he was trusted.

Worshipper by night, lion by day

Ubadah was among the few who memorised the entire Quran in the lifetime of the Prophet ﷺ, and one of the small circle the Prophet trusted to write down the revelation as it came. He was counted among five of the Ansar who were the authorities of the Quran, the teachers from whom the later teachers learned.

In times of peace, they said, he was the most patient of teachers. He would sit among the homeless newcomers in the mosque, the ones others brought food and water to, and he would teach them the Quran, verse by verse, with no impatience at all.

At night he was an ascetic like no other, a worshipper who wept and gave himself to prayer in the dark. And in battle he was a lion no enemy wanted to be near. He accompanied the Prophet ﷺ in every campaign and was among the people of Badr. Few men ever held so many opposite strengths at once.

Worth a thousand men

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Ubadah served as a judge, a commander, and a builder of cities. He had a gift for architecture, and many famous mosques were planned by his hand. When reinforcements were needed against the Romans, Umar (RA) sent him with a written note for the general.

Umar was not a man easily impressed. The note said, simply, that this man was worth a thousand men, and that victory would come at his hand. It did, again and again.

When the people of greater Syria asked for teachers of the Quran, Ubadah was one of the three companions sent. He went to Palestine with his wife and settled there. He became, by himself, almost a school: he taught so many that the love of memorising the Quran took root in that land, and chains of recitation running back to the Prophet ﷺ pass through him to this day.

He was also a man of strong opinion. He fell into a sharp dispute with Muawiyah, then governor of the region, and told him plainly that the two of them could not live in the same place. He left for Madinah on his own. When Umar saw him there and asked why he had returned, Ubadah explained. Umar did not rebuke him. Instead he said: may Allah disgrace a land that has no man like you in it. He sent Ubadah back, but told him that no governor would ever be set over him again. He was, Umar said, his own man, answerable to Allah and to himself alone.

The insult at the fortress

In Egypt, Ubadah was one of the main commanders in the campaign against the Roman Empire. When the Muslims laid siege to the Babylon fortress, the Roman ruler, Al-Muqawqis, came out to negotiate. He took one look at Ubadah's dark skin and said: take this black man away from me, and send me someone else.

The Muslims did not flinch. This man, they answered, is the best of us. He is our leader, and we say nothing except what he says. The ruler pressed: how could you let a black man be the best of you? He should be the least of you. They replied again that he was their leader, and that to them black and white were the same: no one is better except through piety.

When Ubadah finally spoke, he was calm. You may fear my appearance, he said, but there are a thousand men as dark as me behind me. My youth is gone and I am old, yet I would not fear a hundred of your best soldiers. We are not fighting you for wealth or for this world. The ruler turned to his people, shaken, and said he feared this man's words far more than his looks. Ubadah led the army to a swift victory, and went on to build the first mosque in Egypt.

The last day

After Egypt came Cyprus, where the first naval expedition of Islam was fought. Ubadah's wife died there, as the Prophet ﷺ had once foretold, accepted as a martyr the moment she stepped off the ship. He still led the campaigns, then retired to Palestine and lived only two or three more years.

When death came near, Ubadah asked to be carried out into the courtyard of his house, and he asked his family to call every single person he had ever dealt with: every servant, soldier, neighbour, and family member. This is the last day of my life in this world, he told them, and the first night of my life in the hereafter.

Then he said: I may have hurt one of you with my hand or my tongue and not known it. Allah will take your right from me on the Day of Judgement, so come forward now and take it back before my soul leaves. Not one person came forward. They only spoke of him with love, and said they had never heard a harmful word from his mouth. O Allah, bear witness, he said, that not a single person had a complaint against me.

In his final hours a younger companion came to him and wept. Ubadah told him not to cry, and shared with him one last hadith he had been holding back, so that no one would grow complacent: that whoever bears witness there is no god but Allah and that Muhammad is His messenger, Allah will keep the fire from him. He died moments later, in Palestine, and was buried among many of the companions. His descendants are said to live in that land still, more than fourteen centuries on.

What Ubadah's life teaches us

Ubadah was given more gifts than most people receive in several lifetimes. What makes him worth imitating is how lightly he carried them, and how carefully he treated other people.

  • Trembling at responsibility is a sign of honesty. He feared every position of authority because he feared answering for it. That fear is what made people trust him with it.
  • Dignity does not need to raise its voice. Faced with an open insult, Ubadah did not rage. He answered with calm and let his conviction speak. His worth was never up for negotiation.
  • Give your knowledge away patiently. He sat with the homeless and the new, teaching the Quran without impatience, and that patience seeded a whole land with learning.
  • Settle your accounts with people while you still can. His final concern was not his record of victories, but whether he had wronged anyone. Live so that, asked plainly, no one can name a harm.

Questions

Who was Ubadah ibn al-Samit?

A leader of the Khazraj tribe of Madinah and one of the very first of the Ansar to accept Islam. He took both pledges of Aqabah, memorised the entire Quran, fought in every campaign with the Prophet ﷺ, and later taught the Quran across Palestine.

Why was he called a man worth a thousand men?

Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA), who was not easily impressed, sent Ubadah to reinforce an army with a note describing him as worth a thousand men and promising that victory would come at his hand. It repeatedly did.

What happened at the fortress in Egypt?

The Roman ruler refused to speak with Ubadah because of his dark skin. The Muslims insisted he was the best of them and their leader, telling the ruler that to them black and white were equal and only piety raised a person. Ubadah answered the insult with calm dignity and went on to lead the army to victory.

What can we learn from the life of Ubadah?

That responsibility is best held by those who fear misusing it, that dignity can stay quiet and still be unshakeable, and that we should settle our wrongs with others before we run out of time.

Why this story stays with us

Ubadah could have been remembered for any one of his gifts: the commander who never lost, the man who memorised the Quran, the teacher who filled a land with learning. Instead, the picture that lingers is an old man carried out to his courtyard, asking the people around him whether he had ever hurt them, and waiting for an answer.

Most of us will not lead armies or plan cities. But every one of us deals with people daily, with a hand and a tongue that can heal or harm. His life asks a quiet question we can carry into today: if I gathered everyone I have dealt with this week, what would they say of me? It is worth living so the answer is gentle, and worth fixing now what is not.
