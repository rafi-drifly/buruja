The Children of Ta'if

The Ones He Refused to Curse

On the worst day of his life, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was offered the one thing a person in his place might have wanted. An angel stood ready to crush a whole town between two mountains. The people of that town had set their children and their slaves on him until his body bled.

He said no. This is the story of the children of Ta'if (may Allah be pleased with those of them who believed): the ones who threw the stones, and the man who chose to spare them so that their children might one day worship Allah.

The Children of Ta'if

The Ones He Refused to Curse

The worst day of his life

Years into his mission, with little support left in Makkah, the Prophet ﷺ travelled to the town of Ta'if hoping its people would listen. They did not. They sent out their young ones and their slaves to drive him away, and these lined the road and pelted him with stones until he was wounded. The fools were unleashed on him, and the children threw, and the slaves beat him.

Long afterwards, when someone asked him whether he had ever known a day harder than any other, he looked back to this one. He said he had met his people as they were, and that the worst of it was the day of Ta'if. He had asked for an audience with its leaders, and it had not gone as he had hoped.

So he departed, his face heavy with grief, not finding rest until he reached a garden outside the walls. Everything he had carried out of Makkah, the loss, the rejection, the years of cruelty, had now been joined by this. He was alone, far from home, and turned away by strangers.

The angel who could move mountains

As he sat there, broken and alone, he looked up and saw a cloud move over him. From it the angel Jibril called to him with a message: Allah had heard what his people had said, and the way they had answered him. Allah had now sent the angel of the mountains, and placed him at the Prophet's command.

This was an angel the Prophet ﷺ had never met. He greeted him, then told him plainly that the choice was his. If the Prophet wished, he would crush the people of Ta'if between the two mountains that stood on either side of them. It could all be over. These people had given him too much trouble, and this had been the worst day of his life.

Think of how completely a person could be forgiven for saying yes. The wounds were still fresh. This was piled on top of years of cruelty in Makkah. By every human measure he was within his right to let it end there. And consider how beloved he was to Allah, that the heavens would answer his pain by placing a mountain at his word.

"I hope their children will worship Allah"

He said no. He told the angel that he hoped Allah would bring out of their offspring people who would worship Allah alone and associate no partner with Him. It was not their fault, and there might be good that came from them.

Notice what he did not say. He did not say he hoped their children would believe in him, or accept him, or love him after what their parents had done. He said he hoped their children would worship Allah. Even here, on his worst day, his concern was not himself. It was that Allah be known and worshipped.

This is one of the purest glimpses of his sincerity. He was not protecting his own name or soothing his own wounds. He had carried the same spirit before the battle of Badr, when he stood and pleaded not for his own honour but that the message itself not be lost from the earth. Whatever he had to endure to plant that seed, he was willing to endure it.

His call was never about his personal feelings. He was able to see through all of it, past the pain of that day, to a single hope: that these people one day worship their Lord. That is the spirit he was handing on to everyone who would carry a message after him.

The hope is always in the future

There is a quiet lesson in the way he reasoned. When a child is taught to hate, the hatred sits unnaturally on him. A child can be groomed into something terrible, raised in cruelty against his own nature, and still that nature can be reached.

If you are patient with that nature and nurture it, there is real hope of helping a person overcome what was put into him. That is why the work has to continue even when the adults in front of you are harsh. You look past them and you say, the hope is in the future.

He did not anticipate that he would live to see most of those children come to faith as he met them. His words pointed further out, to their offspring and their descendants, to people who might one day wake up to what was done to him and to the message he carried. He was content to be the seed and let others see the harvest.

The boy who never forgot what he heard

Did any of those children, even as they threw stones, feel something turn inside them? When you teach a child to hate, the hatred sits unnaturally on him. There is a narration that suggests at least one of them carried more away than anyone intended.

A man named Uthman ibn Abi al-As later told his own son the story of how he became a companion. He had been there at Ta'if, only a boy, when the Prophet ﷺ came. He remembered him as if he were still watching: a stranger with no people around him, desperate and lost, leaning on a stick or an arrow, reciting Qur'an aloud to the end of a surah.

The words moved him so deeply that he memorised them while he was still a disbeliever, and never forgot them. Part of the Prophet's call was simply to recite the Qur'an in the open, and the recitation reached a heart no one was watching.

When his elders interrogated the young ones afterward, demanding to know what they had heard from this man so they could drive it out of their minds, he gave them the words. They answered that they knew their own companion better, and that if he were truly what he claimed, they would have followed him first. They told the boy to forget it. But a thing like that cannot be taken out of a heart. You never know who is listening.

The youngest who became the leader

Years later, after the siege of Ta'if, a small delegation finally travelled to Madinah and embraced Islam, and the town became a Muslim town. The oldest among the delegation had once been one of the chiefs who mocked him. The youngest was that same Uthman ibn Abi al-As (may Allah be pleased with him).

Some scholars say he had quietly accepted Islam even before that delegation, hiding it from his people so he could keep his place among them and help where he could. He came carrying some of the Qur'an already, so he was not among the leaders who challenged the Prophet ﷺ on every point. The Prophet saw something in him and loved him. When the delegation was leaving, he appointed this youngest member as their leader, because he trusted his faith more than anyone else's.

Uthman went home as the leader of the very people he had arrived among as the youngest. He was known for his intellect, his zeal, his sincerity, and the purity of his character. Abu Bakr and Umar would later recall how dearly the Prophet ﷺ had loved him, as if they had been told to look after this one. He led expeditions, settled in Basra, and there he taught and passed on hadith, so that a town the Prophet ﷺ had once left in tears became a place from which his message spread.

The hidden fruit of one act of mercy

Most of those children grew up to become Muslims. Allah knows how many of their descendants went on to become scholars, narrators of hadith, and builders of communities. Some readers today may carry that very bloodline without knowing it.

Yet almost all of them came to faith without ever meeting the Prophet ﷺ as believers. The delegation reached Madinah as those who had not believed in him there, and he did not live long after they returned home. Imagine being one of those children, grown now, telling your own children: I was one of the boys who threw stones at the Prophet, but I did not know any better.

We cannot trace most of them. We do not know whose descendants come from those people, nor whose families today carry their story. But the fruit of that one refusal is undeniable, even where we cannot see it. The sincerity of his mission is undeniable too. He was dragged through the mud, and in that same soil he planted seeds that would bear fruit long after he was gone.

What this day teaches us

The day of Ta'if is remembered for the stones. It deserves to be remembered for the answer he gave when he held the power to end it all.

  • Mercy is strongest when revenge is justified. He was fully within his right to let the town be destroyed. He chose hope for their children instead. The harder the wrong, the more his mercy means.
  • Keep your cause bigger than your feelings. He did not ask that the people love him or accept him. He asked that Allah be worshipped. When we serve something, it helps to keep our own ego out of it.
  • The hope is in the future. He looked past the abrasive adults in front of him and saw the children behind them. Good work is often planted in soil we will never see flower.
  • You never know who is listening. A boy in a hostile crowd memorised his words and carried them for years. A quiet, sincere word can take root in a heart you never expected.

Questions

What happened to the Prophet ﷺ at Ta'if?

He travelled to the town of Ta'if hoping its people would accept his message. They refused and sent their young ones and slaves to drive him out, stoning him until he was wounded. He later described it as the hardest day of his life.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ refuse to destroy Ta'if?

When the angel of the mountains offered to crush the town between two mountains, the Prophet ﷺ declined. He said he hoped that Allah would bring out of their offspring people who would worship Allah alone and associate no partner with Him.

Who was Uthman ibn Abi al-As?

He was a young man, likely a boy at the time of Ta'if, who remembered the Prophet ﷺ reciting Qur'an there. He later embraced Islam and the Prophet ﷺ appointed him leader of the Ta'if delegation. He became a narrator of hadith and a respected leader.

What can we learn from the children of Ta'if?

That mercy can be greater than the wrong that provokes it, that sincere work serves the cause and not the ego, and that hope often rests in the next generation. A kind word can take root in hearts we cannot see.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to meet harm with the wish to harm back, especially when no one would blame us. The day of Ta'if shows a different way. With the power to end his enemies in his hands, the Prophet ﷺ chose to hope for their children instead. He saw past the people in front of him to the people who were not yet born.

Most of us will never face a crowd with stones. We will face smaller versions every day: the person who wrongs us, the child who does not yet understand, the door that closes in our face. His example asks us to hold our cause higher than our hurt, and to keep believing that good can grow even from hard ground. Carry that into one moment today, and this story has done its work.
