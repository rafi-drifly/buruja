The man who crossed the world to find the truth

Preview: He left a Persian house of fire and searched for years. When he arrived, he had no tribe to his name.

Assalamu alaikum,

Salman al-Farsi (may Allah be pleased with him) was not born into the faith. He was born far away, in a Persian house of fire, and as a boy his task was to tend the flame.

Something in him could not accept the answers he had been handed. So he left. He searched as a monk, moving from one teacher to the next. He was betrayed and sold into slavery, passed from owner to owner. He crossed half the world, all to reach one man in the desert he had heard the scriptures describe.

When he finally arrived in Madinah, he was already in his fifties, with no tribe and no family. The other companions could trace their pride to a clan. Salman had none. So he gave himself a lineage no one could take away. Asked his father's name, he said only: al-Islam. "My father is Islam," he would say, "I have no father but Islam."

His first battle was the trench dug around the city, on his own suggestion. Beside the Prophet ﷺ, who dug in the mud more than anyone, Salman saw a light flash from a struck rock: the palaces of Persia, of Rome, of Yemen. The light of Islam reaching the whole world, seen from the floor of a dark trench.

Later he was made governor of a palace, with a generous salary. He took a single room at the front, gave the money away, and planted date palms, selling fruit in the market like everyone else.

Dying in his eighties, he wept. Not for this world, he said, and not from fear of meeting his Lord. He wept because the Prophet ﷺ had told him to take from this world only what a traveller takes for the road, and he was afraid he had kept too much. In his house they found a blanket, a few utensils, and twenty coins.

There is a quiet question in his life. He crossed deserts for the truth, then asked for almost nothing once he had it. What do we truly belong to, and is it worth what he gave for it?
