The Companions

Miqdad ibn al-Aswad

The First Horseman

He came to Makkah as a stranger with no tribe and no name of his own. Within a few years he was among the seven who said, out loud and in public, that they were Muslim, and he paid for it with his body.

This is the story of Miqdad ibn al-Aswad (may Allah be pleased with him): one of the first to believe, the only horseman on the day of Badr, and the man whose words once lit up the face of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

The Companions

Miqdad ibn al-Aswad

The First Horseman

A stranger seeking shelter

Miqdad was from Hadramaut, in Yemen, a land the Prophet ﷺ loved for the soft and honest nature of its people. He was a big man among his tribesmen, and one day a fight with one of them ended in a death he had not meant to cause. Afraid of what would follow, he fled the only way he knew: away.

He arrived in Makkah with nothing to his name but his life. He went from clan to clan asking for protection, until a man of Banu Zuhra named al-Aswad took him in. The two grew so close that al-Aswad did more than shelter him: he adopted him as a son and announced it publicly by the Kaaba.

So the boy who came as Miqdad ibn Amr became Miqdad ibn al-Aswad, and the books of seerah still carry both names. He had found a place in a city that was not his. He did not yet know how soon he would risk losing it.

Among the first seven

When word of the Prophet ﷺ began to spread, Miqdad did not wait. He heard, and he went, and he believed, before the early Muslims even had a house to gather in. He kept it quiet at first, because he was vulnerable in every direction: no tribe of his own, and a guardian who might disown him the moment he found out.

That moment came. When al-Aswad learned that the man he had adopted was now a Muslim, he turned on him in public: cursing him, spitting on him, calling him a curse upon the household that had taken him in. Miqdad became one of only seven who declared their Islam openly in those first days, and one of the five with no powerful clan to shield them.

Quraysh would force those five into heavy coats of iron and drag them under the sun and beat them, again and again. You did not need to be their master to strike them. If a man was simply having a bad day, he could take it out on them like punching bags. Through it all, Miqdad and his companions stayed close to the Prophet ﷺ, learning what they could and bearing what they had to. About believers like him, a verse was revealed:

Do not drive away those who call upon their Lord morning and evening, seeking nothing but His Face. You are in no way accountable for them, nor they for you; if you drove the believers away, you would become one of the evildoers.Qur'an 6:52

A marriage the Prophet arranged himself

Miqdad migrated twice for his faith: first to Abyssinia, then to Madinah, where the Prophet ﷺ joined him in brotherhood with Jabbar ibn Sakhr of the Ansar. He was known, among other things, for his sense of humour. He carried heavy jokes, and one of them shaped the rest of his life.

Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf once suggested, kindly, that Miqdad should marry. Miqdad answered, half in jest, that Abd al-Rahman could marry him to his own daughter. Whatever was in the words, Abd al-Rahman did not like it, and he walked away. Hurt by that, Miqdad did what the companions did when something sat wrong with them: he went to the Prophet ﷺ.

The Prophet ﷺ did not weigh his lineage or his lack of one. He simply said, in effect, then become my relative. He took Miqdad to the home of his own cousin, Dubaah bint az-Zubayr ibn Abd al-Muttalib, a woman of standing, faith, and intellect, and proposed the marriage on his behalf. A man who had arrived in Makkah with no tribe was married, by the Prophet ﷺ himself, into the house of Banu Hashim.

The one horseman at Badr

At Badr the Muslims were not ready for an open battle. They had set out after a caravan and found an army instead. The enemy had horses by the score. The Muslims had one, and Miqdad was the man on it. The companions remembered him afterwards as the first horseman to ride in the path of Allah.

But it was something he said, not something he rode, that they never forgot. When the Prophet ﷺ told them they would have to fight, it was the kind of moment when one wrong word could break a people's nerve. Miqdad stood, and with his booming voice he said: we will not say to you what the people of Musa said to him, you and your Lord go and fight. We will fight on your right and your left, before you and behind you.

Those who were there said the face of the Prophet ﷺ lit up with joy at his words, and the men around him felt their courage return. One companion said for years afterwards that he wished he had been the one to bring that look to the Prophet's face. When the fighting came, they said that wherever you looked on the field, you saw the angels, and you saw the horse of Miqdad.

The night of the milk

Of all that is told about him, one story is simply called the incident of Miqdad. He and two companions were once so hungry in Madinah that they began to lose their sight and hearing. No one could host them; everyone was struggling. Then the Prophet ﷺ found them and took them to his home, where the whole of his family lived on the milk of three goats and little else.

He told Miqdad to milk the goats and divide the milk: a share each for the three of them, and one cup left for the Prophet ﷺ, who said he too was hungry. Miqdad did it. But after his companions slept, his own share had not been enough, and the cup saved for the Prophet ﷺ sat there in the dark. The whisper came: surely someone has already fed him on his way home. So Miqdad drank it, then lay down in dread of what would happen when the Prophet ﷺ returned and found the cup empty.

The Prophet ﷺ came in softly, greeting in a voice that would reach the awake without waking the sleeping. He prayed, then looked at the empty cup, then raised his hands. Miqdad braced for a curse. Instead the Prophet ﷺ said: O Allah, feed the one who fed me, and give drink to the one who gave me drink. Miqdad leapt up, took a knife to the goats, and found their udders full again. He milked them until the buckets overflowed, and carried the milk back to the Prophet ﷺ, laughing so hard he fell on his back. Asked if this was one of his jokes, he told the whole story. The Prophet ﷺ smiled and said it was nothing but a mercy from Allah.

The voice before the battle

Miqdad fought in every major campaign with the Prophet ﷺ, and later in the opening of Egypt and Sham, including Palestine. He was also a reciter of the Qur'an, and he loved to recite it before and during battle. He would walk between the ranks of the Muslims with that deep voice, carrying the verses to them, and there was hardly a battle where he did not. At Yarmouk, when the armies they faced were vast, Khalid ibn al-Walid called for Miqdad and sent him to recite Surat al-Anfal to the troops to steady them.

When Umar sent reinforcements to the conquest of Egypt, he named only one man. He told the commander: among them I am sending you Miqdad, who is better than a thousand men. There were few testimonies like it.

Yet leadership made him uneasy. He had seen how people began to place their leaders on a pedestal, and he feared what that might do to his own sincerity. So he asked the Prophet ﷺ never to put him in charge again, and he would not even lead the people in prayer. Once, sitting with Uthman, he watched a man pour out flattery, and he scooped up pebbles and pelted him with them, the way one pelts at Hajj, saying the Prophet ﷺ had told them to throw dust in the faces of those who heap up praise.

Do not wish you had been there

Years later, a man of the next generation sat with Miqdad and said what so many feel when they hear these lives: how blessed are these two eyes that saw the Prophet ﷺ. By Allah, we wish we had seen what you saw. It was a beautiful thing to say. Miqdad's answer was not what the young man expected.

He stood and said: what makes you wish to be present at a time Allah kept you absent from, when you do not know what you would have been had you been there? He reminded them that there were people who saw the Prophet ﷺ with their own eyes and were cast headlong into the Fire because they refused him. Then he told them the truth of those early years: how a believer would watch his own father, his own son, his own brother die rejecting the message, and could find no coolness in his heart for it. About that grief, he said, Allah revealed a verse:

Be grateful, he was saying, for the time and the family Allah placed you in. Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity, and Allah wrongs no one. You were given your own share of hardship and your own chance to stand for the truth. Do not envy another's test. Carry your own well.

those who pray, 'Our Lord, give us joy in our spouses and offspring. Make us good examples to those who are aware of You'.Qur'an 25:74

What Miqdad's life teaches us

Miqdad was not a chief or a man of famous lineage. He was a stranger who gave everything he had, and his life leaves a few things behind for ours.

  • Faith is worth more than belonging. Miqdad had only just found shelter when he risked it all to follow the truth, and let the man who adopted him turn against him rather than hide what he believed.
  • One steady voice can hold a crowd. When others might have broken, he stood and spoke, and a frightened gathering found its courage again. Words said at the right moment carry weight.
  • Guard your heart from praise. He stepped away from leadership and even from leading prayer, afraid that honour might quietly corrode his sincerity. He watched his intention more closely than his reputation.
  • Be grateful for your own time. Do not wish you had been tested in another age. Allah placed you here, with this family and these trials, and this is exactly where your faith is meant to be lived.

Questions

Who was Miqdad ibn al-Aswad?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, originally from Yemen, who became one of the first seven Muslims to declare their Islam openly. He is remembered as the only horseman at the Battle of Badr and as one of the great early believers.

Why is he called both ibn al-Aswad and ibn Amr?

His birth father was Amr, but after he fled to Makkah a man named al-Aswad gave him protection and then adopted him as a son. He became known as Miqdad ibn al-Aswad, and the seerah records both names.

Why is Miqdad called the first horseman?

At Badr the Muslims had only a single horse, and Miqdad was the one who rode it. The companions called him the first to ride a horse in the path of Allah, and he was placed in command of one flank of the battle.

What can we learn from the life of Miqdad?

To value faith above belonging, to guard the heart against praise, and to be grateful for the time and the test Allah has placed us in rather than wishing for another.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to read the lives of the companions and quietly think we would have done the same, that faith would have been simpler if only we had been there. Miqdad heard that wish spoken to his face and gently turned it around. You do not know what you would have been, he said. Be grateful for where Allah has placed you.

That is an invitation for an ordinary day. Most of us are not asked to ride into a battle. We are asked to be honest when it costs us, to steady the people near us with the right word, to keep our hearts clean of the praise we crave, and to thank Allah for the exact test we have been given. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
