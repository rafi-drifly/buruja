Umm Sulaym bint Milhan

Her Dowry Was Islam

A wealthy man came to her with gold and a proposal. She told him she did not want his gold. She wanted one thing only: that he believe in the One God. If he became Muslim, that would be her entire dowry.

This is the story of Umm Sulaym bint Milhan (may Allah be pleased with her): one of the first women of Madinah to accept Islam, the mother of Anas ibn Malik, and a woman whose quiet, steady faith ran through her whole household.

The Companions

Umm Sulaym bint Milhan

Her Dowry Was Islam

A woman of standing in Madinah

Before Islam reached Madinah, Umm Sulaym was already a woman people noticed. She was counted among the most beautiful women of the city, and she was known for something rarer: she was strong, assertive, and independent-minded. She could read and write at a time when few men or women could, and she knew poetry.

She is remembered most by her kunya, Umm Sulaym, the mother of Sulaym, though Sulaym was the name of her younger brother, not her son. Her own first name is reported as Rumaysa. Scholars note that she and her sister were maternal kin to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ through the clan of Banu Najjar, which would later explain an unusual closeness between him and her home.

She was married to Malik ibn an-Nadr, one of the wealthiest and most noble merchants of Madinah. He had been away on a trade route when the city's old wars destroyed so many of its men, and he matched her in standing. Together they had a son, Anas, a young boy from whom this ummah would one day learn a great deal of its religion.

The first to believe in her house

Islam did not enter her home through her husband. It entered through her. When Mus'ab ibn Umayr began his quiet teaching in Madinah, Umm Sulaym was present at one of those first gatherings, and she believed at once. Some scholars say she was the very first woman in the city to accept Islam.

Her husband was away when this happened. When Malik returned and found her practising the new faith, he was furious. He demanded that she abandon it. Had he not given her enough of this world, he asked, that she now had to trouble herself with another? She did not argue with him for long. She simply held to what she knew was true, and she began to teach their small son to say the words of faith.

Malik left again for Syria, still refusing the message, and on that journey he died. He died holding on to his disbelief. It is a quiet tragedy in her story: all the good that her son Anas and his descendants would go on to do, none of it would weigh in his father's favour, because he never gave the message even a moment of his attention.

Her last memories of her husband were of an argument over the very thing she held most dear. She was now a widow. It could have shaken her. Instead she stayed patient, stayed firm, and drew closer to Allah.

A dowry of one word

In time another man came to her: Abu Talha, from her own clan, a man as prominent as her late husband. He owned more property in Madinah than anyone, and he was known for his fine character and his kindness to the poor. He proposed, and he was the kind of man no woman would normally turn away.

But he was not yet a Muslim. She told him plainly: a man like you would not be refused, but you worship something other than God, and I cannot marry you as you are. He assumed she wanted a larger dowry, more gold and silver. She told him that was not it at all. "I call Allah and His Messenger to witness," she said, "that if you accept Islam, I will take that as my dowry, and I will ask for nothing else."

Abu Talha went away to think. He came back unchanged, and she sent him to think again. She began, gently, to point things out to him. What would happen, she asked, to the idol he worshipped if it were thrown into a fire? It would burn, he admitted. Did he not feel a little foolish, she asked, bowing to a piece of wood that a person could just as easily use to keep warm?

After some time he returned and said simply, "I believe in what you believe." Her son Anas, then about ten years old, performed the marriage. Anas would later say that he never knew of any woman whose dowry was nobler than Umm Sulaym's. Her dowry was Islam itself.

She gave her son to the Prophet

When a group from Madinah went out to give their pledge to the Prophet ﷺ, Umm Sulaym went too, with her young son in her arms. She watched men around her dedicate themselves to him, and she longed to give something of her own.

So she brought Anas forward. "O Messenger of Allah," she said, "this is my son. He is in your service." She did not offer gold or a gift. She offered her child. Anas would weep in later years simply telling this story, remembering the day his mother placed him at the side of the man he came to love most in the world.

The Prophet ﷺ made three supplications for the boy: that Allah would bless him in his wealth, bless him in his children, and bless him in everything he was given. Anas lived a long life, with many children and grandchildren and more wealth than he could account for, and he traced all of it back to that single moment when his mother said, please, make dua for him.

The home the Prophet kept returning to

Umm Sulaym set aside a corner of her house as a place to pray, and she asked the Prophet ﷺ to pray two units there. He did, and it gave her great joy. After that he came often. It was said that there was no home in all of Madinah he entered the way he entered hers.

When someone gently remarked on how often he visited, he answered with tenderness. Her brothers had been killed beside him in battle, he said, and this was a mercy he owed them. He would sit in her home, and her family would watch his manners up close: he would decline food when he was fasting, then go to the prayer corner and quietly ask Allah to bless Umm Sulaym and her children.

There was little in that house. Anas remembered a single mat that served as their bed at night, their floor by day, and their table at meals. His mother would wash it and they would use it for everything. This was the home of a woman who had been married to two of the richest men of Madinah, and she lived with the lightest of hands on the world.

One day Abu Talha told her he had heard hunger in the Prophet's voice. Umm Sulaym wrapped what little bread she had in a cloth and sent it with Anas. The Prophet ﷺ rose and brought the people of the mosque with him, and from that small amount of bread, blessed by his hand, around eighty men ate their fill. From the same bowl, the family ate for two months more.

Patience on the hardest night

Umm Sulaym and Abu Talha had a young son together, Abu Umayr, a child the Prophet ﷺ adored. He would ask the boy about his little pet bird, and he came to comfort him when the bird died. Then the child himself fell ill with a sudden fever, and within a short time he died.

Umm Sulaym was broken, as any mother would be. But her thoughts turned to how the news would land on her husband. She asked the household not to tell him, and she set herself to telling him in a way he could bear. She prepared a meal, she composed herself, and when Abu Talha came home she did not tell him at once. They spent the evening as husband and wife. Only in the morning did she speak.

She asked him a question. If someone lends you a trust and later asks for it back, how should you return it? Gratefully, he said, for the time you were allowed to benefit from it. Then she told him: Allah lent us our son, and now He has taken back what was His.

Abu Talha went to the Prophet ﷺ heavy with grief and confusion. The Prophet ﷺ smiled at him and prayed that Allah would bless the night they had spent. That same night, a child had been conceived. He was named Abdullah. Years later Anas swore that from that one son, Allah gave them seven grandsons, every one of them a memoriser of the Qur'an.

Always there, always near

Umm Sulaym did not stand at the edges of the great events of her time. In battle after battle she was there, carrying water, tending the wounded, doing whatever she could. On one campaign her husband laughed to find her with a dagger hidden in her garment. If anyone came near, she said, she would defend the Messenger of Allah ﷺ herself.

Her love showed in small, careful ways. When the Prophet ﷺ rested in her home on a hot day, she would gather his perspiration and mix it into the most fragrant perfume she had, telling him she wanted the blessing of it. When he shaved his head, she asked to keep some of his hair. None of this was for show. It was the devotion of a woman who simply wanted to be near goodness.

The Prophet ﷺ once said that he entered Paradise and saw Umm Sulaym there. Of all that her life held, the gold she gave up, the husband she lost, the son she handed over, the child she buried, this is how it is remembered: a woman whose steady faith carried her, and her whole household, all the way home.

What Umm Sulaym's life teaches us

Her story is not built on one famous moment. It is built on a faith so settled that it shaped every relationship she had: her marriage, her children, her home.

  • Some things are not for sale. She could have married wealth and security. She asked for faith instead, and held that line until it was met. She knew which of her treasures was the real one.
  • Faith is taught at home first. She did not preach in public. She taught her son the words of belief, set aside a corner to pray, and let her household watch her live what she believed.
  • Patience can be a gift you give others. On the hardest night of her life she carried her own grief quietly so that she could break it gently to the one she loved. She thought of his heart before her own.
  • Trust what God lends you, and return it well. She saw her child as a trust, given for a time and then asked back. That single way of seeing carried her through a loss that would break most of us.

Questions

Who was Umm Sulaym bint Milhan?

She was one of the first women of Madinah to accept Islam, the mother of the famous companion Anas ibn Malik, and the wife first of Malik ibn an-Nadr and later of Abu Talha. She was known for her intelligence, her strong character, and her deep devotion to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Why is it said that her dowry was Islam?

When Abu Talha proposed to her, he was not yet a Muslim. She refused his gold and told him that if he accepted Islam, that alone would be her dowry. He became Muslim, and her son Anas, then about ten, performed the marriage. Anas later said he knew of no woman with a nobler dowry.

What was her relationship to Anas ibn Malik?

Anas ibn Malik was her son from her first marriage. As a young boy she placed him in the service of the Prophet ﷺ, who prayed that Allah would bless him in his wealth, his children, and all he was given. Anas became one of the most prolific narrators of hadith.

What can we learn from the life of Umm Sulaym?

That conviction can be quiet and still unshakeable, that faith is shaped first in the home, and that trusting what Allah gives, and gracefully returning it when He asks, can carry a person through the deepest loss.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be tested in front of crowds. We are tested in our own homes: in what we ask of the people closest to us, in how we raise our children, in how we hold a loss when no one is watching. That was the whole ground of Umm Sulaym's faith, and she gave herself to it completely.

Her life is an invitation to want the right things, to teach what we believe by living it, and to trust that what Allah lends us was always His to take back. Carry one of those into today, quietly, and her story has done its work.
