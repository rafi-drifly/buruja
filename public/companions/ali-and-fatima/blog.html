The Companions

Ali and Fatima

A Love Built in Poverty

They had almost nothing. One bed that was also their couch and the place they fed their animals from. His back ached from work and her hands had blistered. And yet, when they asked the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ for help, he gave them something they would keep for the rest of their lives.

This is the story of Ali ibn Abi Talib and Fatima (may Allah be pleased with them): a young couple raised in the house of the Prophet ﷺ, tested by poverty, and bound by a love so deep that when it ended, the strongest man among them could not speak for days.

Ali and Fatima

A Love Built in Poverty

Which of us do you love more?

People often came to the Prophet ﷺ asking who he loved the most. One day it was Ali and Fatima themselves who asked: which of the two of us is more beloved to you?

He could not lie, and he did not want to wound either one. So he gave them an answer they could both hold. He told Fatima that she was more beloved to him, and he told Ali that he was more precious to him. He had raised Ali like his own son, and Fatima was the daughter he called the mother of her father.

It is a small moment, but it sets the tone for everything that follows. This was a household held together not by wealth or comfort, but by a careful, deliberate love.

A home close to him

The first trouble in the marriage was not an argument and it was not the poverty. It was distance. Once they married, their home sat far from the Prophet ﷺ, and both of them felt it. Fatima had grown up beside her father, and Ali had been raised in his care since boyhood.

All the homes around the mosque stood on land owned by a quiet companion named Haritha ibn Nu'man. The Prophet ﷺ was too embarrassed to ask him for more, because Haritha had already given so much. But Haritha noticed the longing in his face and asked, gently, if he missed his daughter.

When the Prophet ﷺ admitted that he did, Haritha said, "By Allah, the land I give to you is more beloved to me than the land I keep." He offered a plot right across from the Prophet's own home. So Fatima and Ali moved close, their one window facing the window of Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), and the Prophet ﷺ prayed that Haritha would be given a palace in Paradise.

Something better than a servant

Then came the poverty. The Prophet ﷺ himself lived with very little, never moving into a palace, often eating no more than dates and water in a day. His daughter's life was the same. Ali developed pain in his back, Fatima's hands blistered from the grinding and the work, and she was exhausted.

Ali suggested she ask her father for someone to help in the home. Fatima was too shy to ask directly, so when the Prophet ﷺ came to their house that cold night, he sat between them on their single bed and asked her gently why she had come looking for him. When she explained, he offered something else instead.

"Shall I not give you something better than a servant?" he said. Then he taught them words to say before sleep: to glorify Allah thirty-three times, to praise Him thirty-three times, and to declare His greatness thirty-four times. He would not take a servant for them while the poor of the mosque and the orphans of Badr had nothing. But he promised that these words would enrich them more than any help they were asking for.

Ali said afterwards that he never once missed those words again. And he swore that from that night on, they were given a strength and a sufficiency that meant they never felt the need for a servant at all.

Giving away the only meal

They were poor, but they were a generous family. Fatima was like her father, and Ali was like the man who had raised him. Food that came into their home would often leave before they tasted any of it.

Many scholars of tafsir connect this household to a verse in Surat al-Insan. On a night when they were hungry and had only a loaf of bread and some water, a poor family came asking, and they gave it all away.

The Quran preserved what people like this carry in their hearts:

they give food to the poor, the orphan, and the captive, though they love it themselves, saying, 'We feed you for the sake of God alone: We seek neither recompense nor thanks from you.Quran 76:8-9

Father of dirt

Like any marriage, theirs had its hard days. One evening the Prophet ﷺ came to their home and found Fatima alone and upset. He did not ask where her husband was. He asked, "Where is your cousin?" He had read the situation, and she told him they had quarrelled and Ali had gone to sleep in the mosque.

The Prophet ﷺ went and found him in a corner, his cloak slipped off, sand clinging to his back. He did not wake him with anger or play the offended father. He sat him up, brushed the dirt from his back, and put his shirt back on him. Then he smiled and called him by a new name: "Get up, Abu Turab, father of dirt."

Ali laughed. It became his favourite nickname, the one the Prophet ﷺ used to tease him with for the rest of his life. Then he sent him home to his wife. This was the Prophet's way of stepping into the marriage: not to take sides, but to mend it.

She is a part of me

After the conquest of Makkah, some of the elders suggested that Ali take a second wife: a daughter of Abu Jahl, to bind two tribes together in the custom of the time. When Fatima heard of it, it hurt her. She had watched Abu Jahl torture her father. She had cleaned his wounds with her own hands.

The Prophet ﷺ did not declare forbidden what Allah had made lawful. But he stood up and spoke from the heart. "Fatima is a part of me," he said. "What hurts her hurts me." By then she had lost her mother and her sisters, and he would not let anything deepen her grief.

Ali did not pursue it. There was never any doubt about the love between them. Over the next ten years it only grew. Ali, who was a poet, even wrote verses of love for Fatima, once playfully scolding the very toothstick she was using because he was jealous of how close it came to her.

Grandchildren and a cloak

Their family grew to four children: Hasan, Hussein, Zaynab, and Umm Kulthum. Twice Ali named a newborn son Harb, which means war, and twice the Prophet ﷺ gently changed it: to Hasan, and then Hussein, names that mean goodness.

No one looked more like the Prophet ﷺ than Hasan. When the Prophet prostrated in prayer, the boy would climb onto his back, and he would simply lengthen his prostration until the child climbed down. He would pause his sermons to come down from the pulpit and gather his grandsons into his lap.

One day, Aisha narrated, he wrapped Hasan in his cloak, then Hussein, then drew Fatima in, and finally Ali, holding all four of them close. He used to ask Fatima to call his two sons so he could squeeze them and breathe in the scent of their hair. He taught the believers to love Allah for His blessings, to love him for the love of Allah, and to love his family for their love of him.

The last words between a father and his daughter

Near the end, Fatima would weep to see how thin and worn her father had become, how he went hungry and refused to eat his fill. He comforted her as he always had, telling her that the message that had cost them so much would one day reach every home on earth.

When his final illness came, Fatima walked into the room. Every other time, he had risen to take her hand and seat her in his place. Now he could barely move, yet he gathered more strength to greet her than he had shown in all those dying days. He whispered something to her, and she wept. Then he whispered again, and she smiled.

Later she shared the secret. First he had told her he would not live past this illness, and she had wept. Then he told her she would be the first of his family to follow him, and that was what made her smile. She was only twenty-seven, and the thought of joining him soon brought her peace.

When he died in her lap, Fatima was the first to cry out. "O my dear father," she said, "how close you are now to your Lord." Ali was so stunned by grief that he did not speak for days. Of the burial, one companion told her plainly that the only way they could place dirt over the Prophet ﷺ was to numb their own hearts.

The first to follow him

Fatima did not wish to live long after her father, and she did not. In the first Ramadan after his death, she went out to her courtyard, lay back, and looked up at the sky, smiling.

She asked to be buried at night, simply and modestly, with few people present. She called for Ali and asked him to marry her niece Umamah afterwards, so the children would have a loving mother. Then she embraced her family and slipped away with peace on her face, the first of the Prophet's household to follow him, exactly as he had promised.

Ali washed her, wept through it, and lowered her into the grave himself, just as the Prophet ﷺ had once buried Khadijah. He said that nothing in his whole life had exhausted him more than losing the two people most beloved to him within so short a time. At her graveside he recited a poem of pure, unfiltered pain, imagining her answering his greeting from beneath the earth.

He did not turn against his faith. He knew how death and the afterlife work. But he felt the loss completely, and that was not a failure of patience. As the Prophet ﷺ had said when his own son died, weeping is mercy that Allah places in the heart.

What their story teaches us

Theirs was not a fairy tale of ease. It was a real marriage, with hardship and friction, held together by faith and by the patient hands that helped them mend.

  • Faith can carry a home through poverty. They were given words to say before sleep instead of a servant, and found that nearness to Allah was worth more than comfort.
  • Mend the people you love, do not take sides. The Prophet ﷺ brushed the dirt from Ali's back and sent him home with a joke. Repair was the goal, not winning.
  • Generosity gives even when it has little. On the night they had only one loaf, they handed it to a hungry family and asked for nothing, not even thanks.
  • Grief and faith can live together. Ali wept openly and Fatima longed to follow her father. Patience is not the absence of pain, but never saying anything that displeases Allah.

Questions

Who were Ali and Fatima?

Ali ibn Abi Talib was the cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, raised in his household, and one of the earliest believers. Fatima was the Prophet's youngest daughter. They married in Madinah and became the parents of Hasan and Hussein.

Why did the Prophet ﷺ refuse to give Fatima a servant?

When she and Ali asked for help in the home, he would not provide a servant for them while the poor of the mosque and the orphans of Badr had nothing. Instead he taught them words of remembrance to say before sleep, and they said they never felt the need again.

What does "Abu Turab" mean?

It means "father of dirt." The Prophet ﷺ gave Ali the name affectionately after finding him asleep in the mosque with sand on his back, and it became Ali's favourite nickname.

What can we learn from their marriage?

That faith can hold a home together through poverty, that loved ones should be helped to reconcile rather than divided, that generosity gives even from very little, and that real grief and real faith can coexist.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think a good marriage is one without hardship. Ali and Fatima had hardship in abundance: hunger, blistered hands, an aching back, even quarrels. What they also had was a love that gave instead of kept, and a faith that turned to remembrance when there was nothing else to turn to.

Most of our lives are lived in ordinary rooms, with the people who depend on us, far from any crowd. Their story asks us to be gentle there: to mend rather than win, to give without counting, and to hold the people we love so closely that, like Ali and Fatima, our nearness to them becomes part of our nearness to Allah.
