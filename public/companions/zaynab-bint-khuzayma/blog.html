Zaynab bint Khuzayma

The Mother of the Poor

We know very little about her. No long speeches survive, no famous debates, no dramatic scene at a turning point of history. What survives is a single quality, repeated by everyone who knew her: she gave everything away.

This is the story of Zaynab bint Khuzayma (may Allah be pleased with her): one of the Mothers of the Believers, remembered by one name above all others, the Mother of the Poor.

The Companions

Zaynab bint Khuzayma

The Mother of the Poor

A name that meant the best of things

Zaynab came from Banu Hilal, an Arab tribe of the desert. Her father was Khuzayma. Some of the scholars say her name carries the sense of something precious, the jewel of her father, a woman in whom people saw beauty of more than one kind.

She is one of those companions about whom the records are thin. There is so much we would love to know and cannot. She is often confused, even now, with another wife of the Prophet ﷺ who shares her first name, Zaynab bint Jahsh, about whom far more is written. Of all the wives of the Prophet ﷺ, Zaynab bint Khuzayma may be the one we know the least.

And yet she is one of the Mothers of the Believers. That alone is reason enough to want to learn everything we can about her, however little has been preserved.

What little there is has been kept with care, and it is all good. She is spoken of only in the best of ways, and one description of her has outlasted everything else that history forgot.

A family woven with believing women

On her mother's side, Zaynab belonged to a remarkable family. Her mother was Hind bint Awf, and through her, Zaynab was a half-sister to Maymuna bint al-Harith (may Allah be pleased with her), who would be the last woman the Prophet ﷺ ever married. The two sisters would each, at different times in their lives, be married to him, though never at the same time.

The women of this family read like a roll call of the believers of that age. Among her sisters and half-sisters were Salma bint Umays, the wife of Hamza; Asma bint Umays, the widow of Jafar who was later married to Abu Bakr and then to Ali; and Lubaba bint al-Harith, known as Umm al-Fadl, the wife of al-Abbas and the mother of Abdullah ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with them all).

This is only the surface of it. The Prophet ﷺ once described these women as the believing sisters. To come from such a household was to be surrounded by faith from every side: women who married the closest companions, who raised some of the great scholars of the ummah, who gave their lives over to the new religion.

Zaynab was raised among them. The generosity and the faith that would mark her did not appear from nowhere. They grew in soil that had been prepared long before she ever entered the house of the Prophet ﷺ.

Married, widowed, and married again

Zaynab's life before the Prophet ﷺ was marked by loss. Her first husband was Tufayl ibn al-Harith, a cousin of the Prophet ﷺ, who at some point divorced her.

She then married Tufayl's own brother, the great companion Ubaydah ibn al-Harith (may Allah be pleased with him), who was wounded at the Battle of Badr and died of those wounds. After him she married Abdullah ibn Jahsh (may Allah be pleased with him), another cousin of the Prophet ﷺ and the brother of Zaynab bint Jahsh. He was killed at the Battle of Uhud.

Three marriages, and each one ended: one in divorce, two in the loss of a husband on the field. By the time the Prophet ﷺ would come to her, she had already grieved more than most people grieve in a lifetime.

There is a quiet detail here that is easy to miss. Hamza and Abdullah ibn Jahsh were buried together in a single grave at Uhud, and the two widows they left behind, Salma bint Umays and Zaynab herself, were sisters. After Uhud, with so many women left without husbands, a renewed spirit moved through the community: the Prophet ﷺ encouraged his companions to step forward and marry the widows and care for the orphans, and he led the way himself.

A few months in the house of the Prophet

The Prophet ﷺ saw her, a woman three times widowed or divorced, and he proposed to her in the month of Ramadan, four years after the Hijra, shortly after his marriage to Hafsa (may Allah be pleased with her). The marriage was settled with a mahr of four hundred dirhams.

And so Zaynab moved into the small rooms beside the mosque in Madinah, into the home of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. After everything she had lost, she had arrived in the most honoured household on earth.

She would not have it for long. By the decree of Allah, Zaynab died only about three months after the marriage. One of those few months was the very Ramadan in which they had wed.

It is a short time, almost nothing by the measure we usually give a life. And yet of all that those months held, the one thing history keeps returning to, the thing it could not let go of, is her generosity.

The Mother of the Poor

Long before Islam, the people already had a name for her: Umm al-Masakin, the Mother of the Poor. This is worth sitting with. The name was not given to her by the new religion. It was given to her by a society that did not yet pray, that did not yet fast, and even they could see what she was.

The Prophet ﷺ taught that people are like precious metals, and that the best of them in the days of ignorance are the best of them in Islam, once they have understanding. Islam did not invent Zaynab's giving. It deepened it, and gave it a new purpose: the pleasure of Allah and the hope of the hereafter. The same hand that had always opened now opened for His sake.

They said that no extra food ever stayed in her home past the day it arrived. Whatever she had, she gave away, and she would often sleep hungry as a result. This was true in Makkah, it remained true in Madinah, and it remained true in the house of the Prophet ﷺ. It was not a phase or a season. It was simply who she was.

The Prophet ﷺ himself was known to be more generous than the blowing wind, and most generous of all in Ramadan, when the angel Jibril would come to meet him. It is worth pausing on what a home shared by the most generous of people and the Mother of the Poor must have been like in that month: how much went out of those doors, and how little was ever kept behind them.

The first in the row

When Zaynab died, the Prophet ﷺ buried her with his own hands. After Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), she is the only wife he would bury himself in his lifetime. There is a particular grief in that, and a particular honour: to be lowered into the earth by the hands of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ.

She was also the first of the wives of the Prophet ﷺ to be laid to rest in al-Baqi, the graveyard of Madinah. In time the others would join her there, all of them except Khadijah and Maymuna, lined up together in a single row. Zaynab was the first to lie in it.

When the Prophet ﷺ married Umm Salama (may Allah be pleased with her) some time afterwards, Umm Salama described it simply: she moved into the home of Umm al-Masakin, the Mother of the Poor. Even the house Zaynab left behind was known by her name.

The name followed her everywhere, before her marriage and after her death. It was, in the end, the truest thing anyone knew about her.

What charity is said to do

There is a teaching of the Prophet ﷺ that nothing extends a person's life like charity. In other narrations he named keeping good ties with relatives, and he named supplication, as things that increase a person's share of their time on earth, so that they may increase their share in the hereafter as well.

Zaynab was given only about three months with the Prophet ﷺ. We cannot claim to know the reasons Allah chooses, and the matter rests with Him alone. But it is hard not to wonder whether the giving she was known for, the food that never stayed the night, was itself part of the blessing that filled so short a life so completely.

Whatever the answer, the blessing in her life is plain to see. A woman whose own days were few has been remembered, with love and with honour, by a whole ummah for fourteen hundred years. There is a barakah in that which length alone could never buy.

The Prophet ﷺ used to ask Allah for the love of the poor. Zaynab was the woman who was given their name. She carried it before Islam, she carried it in Makkah and in Madinah, she carried it into the house of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, and she carries it still.

What Zaynab's life teaches us

Her story is almost entirely one quality. That is what makes it so clear, and so worth keeping.

  • Generosity can become your name. People called her the Mother of the Poor before any revelation reached her. A habit, repeated quietly for years, can become the truest thing anyone knows about you.
  • Give from what you have today. She did not wait for surplus or for an easier season. Whatever was in the house went out of it, even when it meant going to sleep hungry.
  • A short life can be a full one. She had only months in the house of the Prophet ﷺ, yet she is remembered with honour fourteen centuries later. The measure of a life is not its length.
  • Good character carries over. The best of people before Islam were the best of people within it. The kindness you build now is not wasted: it becomes the foundation faith is laid upon.

Questions

Who was Zaynab bint Khuzayma?

One of the wives of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and therefore one of the Mothers of the Believers. She was from the tribe of Banu Hilal and was known, even before Islam, as Umm al-Masakin, the Mother of the Poor, for her constant generosity.

Why was she called the Mother of the Poor?

Because she gave away whatever she had. It is said that no extra food ever stayed in her home overnight, and that she would often go hungry herself after giving it away. She carried this name before Islam, and her faith only deepened the habit.

How long was she married to the Prophet?

Only about three months. They married in Ramadan, four years after the Hijra, and she died shortly afterwards. After Khadijah (RA), she was the only wife the Prophet ﷺ buried with his own hands, and the first of the wives laid to rest in al-Baqi.

What can we learn from her life?

That generosity, repeated quietly over time, can define a person more than anything else. And that a short life given to others can leave a mark far larger than its length.

Why this story stays with us

It would be easy to feel that we know too little about Zaynab to learn from her. But sometimes a single, clear quality teaches more than a long life of details. She was the one who gave, again and again, until there was nothing left for the night, and she was content with that.

Most of us hold on to a little more than we need, just in case. Her life is a gentle question: what would it take, in our own day, to be the one the poor could count on? Not a grand gesture, but a steady habit, the kind that quietly becomes who we are. Carry that question into today, and her story has done its work.
