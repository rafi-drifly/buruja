The Companions of the Amwas Plague

Patient in the Face of the Decree

They were warriors and scholars, men who had walked away from a dozen battlefields without a scratch. Most of them never imagined they would die anywhere but in the field, sword in hand. Allah had decreed something quieter for them.

This is the story of the companions who died in the plague of Amwas (may Allah be pleased with them): a sickness that took around twenty five thousand companions in the land of Sham, and a generation that met it not with panic, but with a calm and complete acceptance of the will of their Lord.

The Companions of the Amwas Plague

Patient in the Face of the Decree

A different kind of martyrdom

Amwas was a town in the land of Sham, in what is now Palestine and Jordan. Around the year 18 after the migration, a plague spread through the Muslim armies and the people there, and it did not spare the great names. By the time it passed, it had taken close to twenty five thousand companions of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

These were people who understood martyrdom as something earned on the battlefield. Many of them had spent years expecting to fall to a sword or an arrow. Instead the decree of Allah reached them through sickness, in their homes and their camps.

The Prophet ﷺ had taught them that this, too, was a path to that high station: that the believer who dies in a plague is counted among the martyrs. So when the illness came, they did not see it only as a loss. They saw a door opening, and they walked through it with their hearts at rest.

What survives from those weeks is striking. Each of them, as Allah took his life, seems to have met the moment with the same complete contentment, a pleasure and a tranquillity with the decree that did not waver even as the people around them fell. To lose so many of the best of a young community in one season could have broken its spirit. Instead, because the Prophet ﷺ had named the plague a means of martyrdom, the grief carried hope inside it. The people could bury their best and still believe those they buried had been raised, not merely taken.

Abu Ubaydah, the trustworthy one of this ummah

The most beloved of those who died at Amwas was Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah (may Allah be pleased with him). He was among the first ten to accept Islam and among the ten given the glad tidings of Paradise. The Prophet ﷺ gave him a title he gave no one else: al-Amin, the trustworthy one of this ummah.

His trust was not just honesty. It was a heart that always carried the people. As governor of Sham he built mosques and some of the first hospitals, and he sent aid wherever the ummah was struck by hardship. When famine reached Madinah in the year of ashes, he sent thousands of camels of relief. Yet when Umar visited his home, he found it almost empty. The man who managed the wealth of a province had kept nothing for himself, and Umar wept.

When the plague broke out, Umar wrote to him, hoping to call him to safety without saying so. Abu Ubaydah understood at once. He wrote back that he knew what Umar was doing, but that he would not leave his army or abandon his people to die while he saved himself. He stayed. The plague took him, and Umar later said that had Abu Ubaydah still been alive, he would have named him the next leader without a second thought.

Running to the decree, not from it

There is a moment from those days that believers have repeated ever since. When Umar chose to turn back from the edge of the stricken land, someone asked him, are you running away from the decree of Allah? His answer became a principle.

Yes, he said, I am running from the decree of Allah to the decree of Allah. Then he explained it. If you have camels and you bring them to fertile land where they can graze, that is by the decree of Allah. If you bring them to barren land where they starve, that too is by the decree of Allah. You still choose. You still take the means. You simply do not know the outcome, and you trust the One who does.

It is a quiet correction of a confusion many of us carry. Trusting Allah does not mean refusing to act. It means acting with care, and then leaving the result in His hands without fear.

Muadh ibn Jabal, the master of the scholars

Among those taken was Muadh ibn Jabal (may Allah be pleased with him), a young man whom the Prophet ﷺ loved openly. The Prophet ﷺ once took him by the hand and said, by Allah, I love you, and then taught him a supplication to say after every prayer: O Allah, help me to remember You, to thank You, and to worship You well.

When Muadh was sent to Yemen to teach, the Prophet ﷺ told him it might be that he would not find him alive on his return. Muadh wept. He was the one the Prophet ﷺ called the most knowledgeable of his ummah in what is lawful and unlawful, the one who would be raised among the scholars on the Day of Judgment.

At Amwas the plague reached his own family. His last conversation was with his son, who lay dying. He told him to hold fast, and his son answered him with the words of the Quran:

This is the truth from your Lord, so do not be one of those who doubt.Quran 3:60

The shirtless warrior, and the brother who longed for the Prophet

Not all of them were scholars. Dirar ibn al-Azwar (may Allah be pleased with him) was a warrior of legendary skill. He once told the Prophet ﷺ that he owned a thousand camels and asked if he should give them all away for the sake of Allah, and the Prophet ﷺ told him it would be a profitable trade. He gave away every one, and turned his whole life to the field.

At the battle of Ajnadayn he removed his armour and even his shirt, and moved through the enemy lines so quickly that he cut down more than thirty soldiers without a single scratch. The Byzantines, unable to understand him, called him the shirtless devil. He survived battle after battle that killed everyone around him. He was too skilled to be touched by a sword. The plague of Amwas took him instead.

Remembered alongside them is a younger brother of Saad ibn Abi Waqqas, who had longed his whole life to be near the Prophet ﷺ but spent his early years far away in Abyssinia. He is reported to have died around this time, and some accounts count him among those Amwas took, though this is not certain. His own mother had once gone on a hunger strike to pull him from Islam, and he begged her to eat while refusing to leave his faith. About that struggle, a verse came down:

We have commanded people to be good to their parents, but do not obey them if they strive to make you serve, beside Me, anything of which you have no knowledge: you will all return to Me, and I shall inform you of what you have done.Quran 29:8

Two governors, one day

The plague took quiet men as well as famous ones. Yazid ibn Abi Sufyan (may Allah be pleased with him) was a commander who said little but fought hard, trusted enough that he stood second in command after Khalid ibn al-Walid at Yamamah and led forces into the conquest of Sham.

Beside him was Shurahbil ibn Hasana (may Allah be pleased with him), a man much of Jordan and Palestine owes its Islam to. He came to faith early in Makkah alongside his adopted mother, made the migration to Abyssinia and then to Madinah, and served as a central commander in army after army. A whole region still carries his name, because its people may never have received Islam without him.

To grasp how heavy Amwas was, hold this in your mind: these two governors of Sham died on the very same day. The ummah lost an enormous share of its best in a single season, and yet the narrations that survive are not of despair. They are of people leaving this world with their trust intact.

How they met the end

As Abu Ubaydah was dying, he did not speak of fear. He reminded the people that every child of Adam will taste death, and that the wisest among them are those who prepare for that day. He had spent his life preparing for it. He was only in his fifties when it came.

Muadh, having lost his family, was asked about the plague and is reported to have prayed for the largest possible share of it for himself, calling it the mercy of his Lord and the means by which the righteous before him had been taken. His last words asked Allah to take him, while his heart still loved Him.

These were not people numb to loss. They grieved. But underneath the grief was a settled certainty that what reached them came from a Lord who loved them, and that their job was to receive it well. That is the inheritance they left us from Amwas: not a story of a plague, but a lesson in how a believing heart meets whatever it cannot control.

Generations later, when a new sickness moved across the world and no one knew where it came from or how to stop it, Muslims returned to this very memory. They were not the first ummah to be tested by something they could not see. The companions of Amwas had walked this road first, and had left a map for how to walk it with faith.

What the companions of Amwas teach us

Their example is not really about plagues or battles. It is about the posture of a heart when life takes a turn it did not choose.

  • Trust does not cancel action. Run from the decree of Allah to the decree of Allah. Take your precautions and do your work, then leave the outcome to the One who already knows it.
  • Faithfulness means staying for people. Abu Ubaydah would not save himself and leave his community behind. The trustworthy heart is the one that carries others, even at a cost.
  • Prepare for the day you cannot avoid. The wisest, Abu Ubaydah said, are those who prepare for death. A life spent in service was simply that preparation, made daily and quietly.
  • Receive the decree with a steady heart. They grieved, but they did not rage against their Lord. They met loss the way the Quran taught them to: this is the truth from your Lord.

Questions

What was the plague of Amwas?

A plague that spread through the land of Sham around the year 18 after the migration, named after the town of Amwas. It took close to twenty five thousand companions of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, including some of the most senior among them.

Why is dying in a plague connected to martyrdom?

The Prophet ﷺ taught that a believer who dies in a plague is counted among the martyrs. The companions of Amwas had expected to die in battle, and they received this as a different door to the same high station.

Which famous companions died at Amwas?

Among them were Abu Ubaydah ibn al-Jarrah, the trustworthy one of this ummah; Muadh ibn Jabal, the great scholar; the warrior Dirar ibn al-Azwar; and the commanders Yazid ibn Abi Sufyan and Shurahbil ibn Hasana (may Allah be pleased with them all).

What did Umar mean by running from the decree to the decree?

He meant that taking precautions is itself part of God's decree, not a denial of it. We act with care because we cannot see the outcome, and then we trust Allah with the result. It is a balance between effort and reliance.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never stand on a battlefield. But all of us meet things we did not choose and cannot control: an illness, a loss, a door that closes when we expected it to open. The companions of Amwas show us what to do in that moment. Not to pretend it does not hurt, and not to stop taking sensible steps, but to keep the heart at rest under the hand of a Lord who knows what we do not.

Their quality worth imitating is acceptance without bitterness. They prepared for the day they could not avoid, they stayed loyal to the people who needed them, and when the decree came they met it with trust instead of fear. Carry a little of that into your own day, into your own worry, and their story has done its work.
