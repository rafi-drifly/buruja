Zaynab bint Muhammad

The First Daughter

Some tests come with a battle to fight or a journey to make. Hers came quieter, and harder: a husband she loved on one side, a father she believed in on the other, and years of waiting in between.

This is the story of Zaynab bint Muhammad (may Allah be pleased with her): the eldest daughter of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, who held her faith and her hope together when life pulled them apart.

The Companions

Zaynab bint Muhammad

The First Daughter

The eldest in the house

Zaynab was born when the Prophet ﷺ was about thirty years old, the first child of him and Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her). She was the oldest of the daughters, and the books of seerah remember how he loved her and praised her openly.

In the home, she was the helper at her mother's side and the mentor to her younger sisters, Ruqayyah, Umm Kulthum, and Fatimah (may Allah be pleased with them all). She carried the weight that comes with being the eldest, and she carried it well.

She married young, as was common in those days, to Abu al-As ibn al-Rabi. He was her cousin: his mother was Hala bint Khuwaylid, the sister of Khadijah. So the marriage drew two families that already loved each other even closer together.

A good man, and a hard line

Abu al-As was a merchant, like the Prophet ﷺ before him. He traveled the trade routes to Syria and was known for his honesty and his truthful word. The Prophet ﷺ loved his character, for he carried many of the same traits that had made the Prophet ﷺ beloved to his people.

When the revelation came, Abu al-As was away on a journey. He returned to find that Zaynab was among the first to accept the faith of her father and her mother. We are not told the exact moment she believed, but she is counted among the earliest to enter Islam.

His own people pressed him to divorce her, as the sons of Abu Lahab had been ordered to divorce her sisters to wound the Prophet ﷺ. Abu al-As refused. He loved his wife and would not let her go, and the Prophet ﷺ respected him for not bending to that pressure.

Yet he also would not become Muslim. His reason was not pride or hatred. It was the fear of what people would say: that he had only followed his wife and abandoned the faith of his father. He knew the Prophet ﷺ was trustworthy and special. The opinion of his tribe held him back all the same.

He was not a political man, not the head of any tribe, not expected to lead anything against the Prophet ﷺ. He was a merchant who loved his family and his people, trying to balance it all. So he held his ground, and Zaynab held hers, and they lived in that uneasy space together. In those early years, a marriage between a believer and one who had not yet believed was not yet forbidden.

Left behind in Makkah

They had two children. Their son, Ali, died very young, and the Prophet ﷺ grieved for him. Their daughter, Umamah, would later grow up in the shade of her grandfather in Madinah, carried and loved by him as he carried his other grandchildren.

Then came the Hijra, and the quiet arrangement could no longer hold. Her father was leaving for Madinah. Her husband was not ready to follow. Zaynab was caught between them.

The Prophet ﷺ gave her permission to stay in Makkah so that her family would not be torn apart. Think of what that cost him: he had buried Khadijah in that city, and now he was leaving his eldest daughter behind in it. There was pain on both sides of that parting.

She stayed, one of the believing women holding on to her faith in Makkah after the community had gone. It takes a quiet, deep kind of strength to keep your faith alive when almost everyone who shares it has left.

The necklace

At Badr, Abu al-As was driven out to fight on the side of the Makkans, against the very father-in-law he respected. He had no wish to be there. Like others who were pushed to the front by their tribes, he was taken captive early, and the Prophet ﷺ instructed the believers to treat their prisoners well.

Imagine the position this left Zaynab in. Her husband had gone out to fight her father, and now her husband was her father's prisoner. In Makkah, his people blamed her for everything that had happened. She had no army and no protector and had to prove her loyalty again and again.

So she did the only thing she could. She took the most precious thing she owned, the necklace her mother Khadijah had given her on her wedding night, placed it in a bag, and sent it to her father as ransom for her husband.

When the Prophet ﷺ opened the bag and saw the necklace, he could not hold back his tears. The companions did not understand why, until he told them: this was Khadijah's gift to Zaynab on her wedding night. He was careful never to favor his own family in the ransom of Badr, asking nothing for them that he did not ask for everyone. But he turned to the companions and said that if they were willing, they should free her prisoner and send the necklace back to her. She had asked only for her husband. The necklace returned to her too, out of a father's tenderness.

Six years of waiting

Freeing Abu al-As came with a condition that was not about his release. The command had come that a believing woman could no longer remain married to a man who rejected the faith. So the Prophet ﷺ asked that Zaynab be sent to him in Madinah, and Abu al-As, honest as ever, honored the request.

On the journey out of Makkah there was a violent encounter with the tribe of her husband. Some accounts say Zaynab lost the child she was carrying, and that the harm of it stayed with her for years. She reached Madinah, reunited with her father, and separated from the husband she still loved.

She refused to remarry. Not for a year or two, but for six years. She would have no one but Abu al-As, holding on to the hope that one day he would believe, come to Madinah, and their family would be whole again.

The morning she spoke up

Years later, the Prophet ﷺ sent a group of companions under Zayd ibn Harithah (may Allah be pleased with him) to intercept a Makkan caravan traveling toward Syria. Among the men taken was Abu al-As, brought back to Madinah once again as a prisoner. He reached Zaynab in the night before the dawn prayer. She asked him if he had become Muslim. He said no, he was a fugitive. She asked him to believe. He was still not ready.

So Zaynab waited until the dawn prayer. When the Prophet ﷺ had finished and turned to face the companions, she called out from the back of the mosque that she had granted protection to Abu al-As ibn al-Rabi. The Prophet ﷺ asked the companions if they had heard what he heard, and told them he had known nothing of it until that moment. Then he honored her word and freed her prisoner, with his possessions returned.

He told her gently that the man was the father of her children but not her husband while he remained outside the faith. Abu al-As returned to Makkah, paid back every debt and trust he owed so no one could say he had run from them, and only then declared his belief. He came back to Madinah at last, not as a captive, but as a son-in-law ready to embrace Islam.

Reunited, and farewell

Nearly twenty years after the first revelation, the Prophet ﷺ brought Abu al-As back to Zaynab. After all the separation, the waiting, and the loss, the family she had hoped for was finally whole. Her daughter was in the shade of the Prophet ﷺ, her husband was now a believer, and she was in the presence of the one she loved most.

It did not last even a year. Zaynab fell ill and passed away, and the grief broke over the whole household. The Prophet ﷺ wept for his eldest daughter. He gave instructions for her washing, then asked to be allowed in to her.

He took his own waist wrap and asked that she be shrouded in it. He led the prayer over her and went down into her grave himself, as he had once gone down into Khadijah's, comforting Abu al-As and little Umamah. Abu al-As died about a year later, never having stopped grieving for her.

What Zaynab's life teaches us

Her story is not won in a single moment. It is held together across years of patience, hope, and quiet courage.

  • Faith can stand alone. She kept her belief in Makkah after the community had left, with no one around to make it easy. Conviction does not always have company.
  • Loyalty and faith are not enemies. She loved her husband and stayed true to her father's message at the same time, refusing to let go of either while there was hope.
  • Patient hope is its own kind of strength. She waited six years for a reunion she could not force. She held the door open, and trusted the rest to Allah.
  • Mercy remembers the small things. A father wept over a necklace and sent it back to a daughter who had not even asked. Love notices what others overlook.

Questions

Who was Zaynab bint Muhammad (RA)?

She was the eldest daughter of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), and among the earliest to accept Islam. She is remembered for her patience through long years of separation from her husband, Abu al-As ibn al-Rabi.

Why was Zaynab separated from her husband?

She accepted Islam while her husband, Abu al-As, did not. When the command came that a believing woman could not remain married to a man outside the faith, she moved to Madinah and waited for him to believe. They were reunited only after he embraced Islam years later.

What is the story of the necklace?

When Abu al-As was captured at Badr, Zaynab sent her mother Khadijah's wedding necklace as his ransom. When the Prophet ﷺ saw it, he wept at the memory of Khadijah, freed the prisoner, and asked that the necklace be returned to his daughter.

What can we learn from the life of Zaynab?

That faith can stand even when it stands alone, that love and conviction can be held together, and that patient hope, the kind that waits without despairing, is a quiet and lasting strength.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be asked to choose between the people we love and what we believe. But almost all of us know the slower test Zaynab knew: holding on to something good while you wait, with no promise of when, or whether, it will come right.

Her life is an invitation to keep faith without bitterness and to keep hope without control. To stay loyal to the people in our care and true to what we believe at the same time. And to trust that the One who returned a weeping father's necklace sees the small, patient things we carry too. Hold one of those into today, and her story has done its work.
