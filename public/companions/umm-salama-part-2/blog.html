The Companions

Umm Salama

A Legacy of Wisdom

There is a moment when even the wisest people fall silent and do not know what to do next. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ once stood in such a moment, with hundreds of his companions frozen in grief, refusing to move. He turned for counsel to the woman beside him.

This is the second part of the story of Umm Salama (may Allah be pleased with her): not how she reached the Prophet's home, but what she gave the ummah once she was there. A scholar, an advisor, a teacher, and the last of the Mothers of the Believers to leave this world.

The Companions

Umm Salama

A Legacy of Wisdom

A place that was never taken from her

When Umm Salama married the Prophet ﷺ, she moved into one of the small chambers built beside his mosque in Madinah. The home had belonged to Zaynab bint Khuzayma, the only wife to pass away during his lifetime apart from Khadijah. Umm Salama settled there with her children, who were now raised in the shadow of the Messenger ﷺ.

She was the eldest of his wives at that time, and when he visited his family, he would visit her first. It was a quiet distinction, but it said something. She accompanied him on many of his journeys and became a trusted advisor in some of the most difficult moments along the way.

She arrived carrying everything her earlier life had taught her. We had already seen her trust in Allah, her patience, her courage, and the nobility of the family she came from. She had preceded her own brothers to Islam, and they would later narrate hadith from her. None of that was left behind at the door of his home; it became the ground she stood on as a Mother of the Believers.

Those who met her remembered more than her dignity. She was intelligent, knowledgeable, and unafraid to speak. She had such a presence that she occupied the place of a scholar in any gathering she entered. People came to her the way people come to a teacher.

The one who always said the right thing

Umm Salama was known for her command of the Arabic language. They said she always found the perfect words for a situation. When she described something, she described it exactly. When she asked a question, it was the clearest question. When she gave a verdict, it was concise and went straight to the point.

She rarely had to repeat herself, because her delivery was so precise. There was a beauty to the way she spoke, and people remembered it long after.

She did not only answer questions. She asked them too, including the difficult ones. Matters that others found awkward to raise, questions about family life, about women, about intimacy, she addressed in a way that was dignified yet left no confusion behind. The ummah understood these matters properly because she was willing to speak about them plainly.

The counsel at Hudaybiyyah

Of all the moments she shared with the Prophet ﷺ, none shows her wisdom more than the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah. The companions had set out hoping at last to reach Makkah and make the lesser pilgrimage. Many of them were emigrants who had been driven from that city years before, and the longing to return had never left them.

They were stopped short. A treaty was signed, and the Prophet ﷺ told them they would turn back to Madinah this year. He gave the order to sacrifice their animals, shave their heads, and leave the state of pilgrimage. No one moved. They were not defying him. They simply could not let go of the hope that something might still change.

Three times he gave the instruction, and three times nothing happened. He came into the tent and spoke to Umm Salama about the situation. She told him: do not say another word to them. Go out, offer your own sacrifice, and call for the barber to shave your head. Then you will see what happens.

He went out, said nothing, and did exactly as she advised. The companions watched him sacrifice and shave his head, and finally they rose and did the same. She had understood something in that crucial moment that calmed an entire camp: these were people who followed his example more than any command, and they needed to see him act.

The questions that brought revelation

Umm Salama once asked the Prophet ﷺ why it seemed the Quran spoke so often to the men, as though the women were not being addressed. She did not ask this to challenge anything. She was a woman of taqwa, asking from a sincere heart what must have been on the minds of many women of her time.

She said that some time passed, and then she heard the Prophet ﷺ answering her from the pulpit. She quickly tied up her hair and went to her door to listen. He recited a verse that placed the believing men and the believing women side by side, equal in their reward before Allah:

For men and women who are devoted to God- believing men and women, obedient men and women, truthful men and women, steadfast men and women, humble men and women, charitable men and women, fasting men and women, chaste men and women, men and women who remember God often- God has prepared forgiveness and a rich reward.Quran 33:35

A scholar for the whole ummah

She asked again on another occasion. The men go out to battle and we do not, she said, and they receive a larger share of inheritance. It was an honest question about how things were apportioned, and a verse came in answer, telling the believers not to wish for what Allah had given to some over others, since both men and women have what they have earned:

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Umm Salama gave the rest of her life to worship and to teaching. Her home became a kind of university. Companions and the generation after them came to her for the narrations she carried and for her understanding of the law. She was counted among the jurists of the companions, and even Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) came to her with his difficult questions.

Among the women, only Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) narrated more from the Prophet ﷺ than she did. Three hundred and seventy-eight authentic narrations are traced to Umm Salama. Much of what we know about the Prophet's home, about the early migration, about matters others were too shy to ask, reaches us through her. She lived into her nineties, the last of the Mothers of the Believers to leave this world, having seen both the brightest and the most painful chapters of her people's history.

Do not covet what God has given to some of you more than others- men have the portion they have earned; and women the portion they have earned- you should rather ask God for some of His bounty: He has full knowledge of everything.Quran 4:32

The water on a young girl's face

When Umm Salama married the Prophet ﷺ, her youngest daughter Zaynab was still a baby. The only father Zaynab would really know was the Prophet ﷺ, and he treated her exactly as his own. When he came home, he did not greet his wife and then notice the child. He would walk in asking, "Where is our Zaynab?" She would run to him, and he would lift her up and play with her.

He had a habit of splashing a little water from his ablution onto her face, a small playful thing, just to make her laugh and to give her memories of joy to carry. Few children can say their stepfather welcomed them like that, that they never once felt they were not his.

Zaynab grew up to become a scholar like her mother, remembered as the most knowledgeable woman of Madinah in her time. She taught some of the greatest scholars of the next generation. And those who knew her said that from the day the Prophet ﷺ sprinkled water on her face, her face never seemed to age. She lived to be old, yet kept the youth in it, and she gave the credit to him.

A house that shaped a stranger

There is one more thread in her legacy that is easy to miss. One of the greatest scholars of the generation after the companions was Hasan al-Basri, the child of two freed slaves. His mother, Khayra, had been freed by Umm Salama and stayed on in her house, becoming like a daughter and a student to her.

Hasan al-Basri was born in that home. When his mother went out to help with Umm Salama's affairs while she taught, the baby would cry, and Umm Salama would nurse him to calm him. She took him with her to meet the companions, so that a child of no rank by the standards of his world grew up among the closest people to the Prophet ﷺ.

Think about what that says. Islam came into a world ordered by tribe and lineage, where people were ranked by blood and status. And here, in the home of one of the noblest women of that society, the son of freed slaves was raised, nursed, and carried into the company of the companions, until he became a teacher to the whole ummah. Allah was not only planning for the family of the Prophet ﷺ. He was planning for the ummah that would benefit from this house for centuries to come.

What Umm Salama's legacy teaches us

Her gift to us was not a single act of courage. It was a lifetime of clear thinking, sincere questions, and a home open to whoever needed it.

  • Wisdom is knowing when to stop talking. At Hudaybiyyah she did not give a speech. She told the Prophet ﷺ to act and say nothing, and a frozen camp finally moved. Sometimes the right counsel is quiet.
  • Sincere questions are not a weakness. She asked the hard things from a heart full of taqwa, and her questions drew out verses the whole ummah still reads. Asking to understand is part of faith.
  • Knowledge is meant to be passed on. She turned her home into a place of teaching and gave her later years to it. What she learned did not stop with her; it reached generations.
  • Honour every child in your care. The Prophet ﷺ welcomed Zaynab as his own and left her with memories of joy. The way we treat the young in our homes can mark them for life.

Questions

Who was Umm Salama?

Umm Salama (may Allah be pleased with her) was one of the Mothers of the Believers, a wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. She was known as a scholar, an advisor, and a teacher, and she narrated the second largest number of hadith among the women companions.

What was Umm Salama's role at the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah?

When the companions hesitated to leave their state of pilgrimage and turn back from Makkah, the Prophet ﷺ asked her for counsel. She advised him to act in silence, offering his own sacrifice and shaving his head. The companions followed his example, and the difficult moment passed.

How many hadith did Umm Salama narrate?

She is credited with three hundred and seventy-eight authentic narrations from the Prophet ﷺ. Among the women companions, only Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) narrated more, which makes Umm Salama one of the most important sources we have for his life and teachings.

What can we learn from the life of Umm Salama?

The value of wise and timely counsel, the place of sincere questions in seeking knowledge, the duty to pass on what we learn, and the care we owe to the children in our homes.

Why this story stays with us

Umm Salama did not leave us many stories of her own life. She left us something rarer: a way of seeing. She watched carefully, she spoke only when her words would help, and she asked the questions others were afraid to ask. Her wisdom was not loud, and that is exactly why it worked.

Most of us will never advise a prophet or teach a generation. But we can practise the same things she did in an ordinary day: to think before we speak, to ask sincerely when we do not understand, to pass on the good we have been given, and to make the people in our care feel welcome and loved. Carry one of those into today, and her legacy lives on a little further.
