Hafsa bint Umar

The One Who Fasted and Prayed

Some people are remembered for the things they did in public. Hafsa is remembered for what she did when no one was watching: standing in prayer through the night, fasting through the day, year after year, until it was almost the whole shape of her life.

This is the story of Hafsa bint Umar (may Allah be pleased with her): the eldest daughter of Umar ibn al-Khattab, a wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and the woman the Quran was entrusted to.

Hafsa bint Umar

The One Who Fasted and Prayed

A daughter of an early house

Hafsa was born about five years before prophethood, around the year 605. Her father was Umar ibn al-Khattab, and she was his eldest child. Her full brothers were Abdullah and Abd al-Rahman, names her father chose after he heard the Prophet ﷺ say that the most beloved names to Allah are Abdullah and Abd al-Rahman.

She came from one of the earliest families touched by Islam. Her aunt, Zaynab bint Mazun, was among the first to believe, and the family's path ran through the migration to Abyssinia, where so many of the early Muslims fled to escape persecution in Makkah.

There is an old story that Umar buried daughters alive in the days of ignorance. It is a fabrication. His eldest daughter stood beside him, alive, and another daughter besides her. Hafsa was a copy of her father: the same God-consciousness, the same intensity, the same quiet devotion he was known for.

Married before her father believed

Most scholars hold that Hafsa accepted Islam before her own father did. Her first husband was Khunays ibn Hudhafa, counted among the first twenty people to embrace the faith, a man who believed even before the early Muslims began gathering in the house of al-Arqam.

Khunays and his family joined the migration to Abyssinia, and later they migrated to Madinah. He was the only man from his clan to fight at the Battle of Badr, a veteran of that small and decisive day, and he carried two honours at once: a fighter at Badr, and then a martyr. He died of his wounds not long after, and was buried beside Uthman ibn Mazun, the first migrant to be buried in the ground of Madinah.

Hafsa, still young and without children, was left a widow in a new city. It is an awkward, uncertain place to be, and the seerah does not look away from it.

Her father did not leave her alone in it. He set out, as a father in that society would, to find her a husband. What followed is one of the most human episodes in the whole seerah.

The proposal that wounded a proud man

Umar went first to Uthman, who had just lost his own wife, the Prophet's daughter. It seemed a perfect match. Umar offered Hafsa, and Uthman asked for time. When Umar came to him, Uthman slipped into a long prayer to avoid the conversation, then later said simply that he was not ready to marry. Umar was deeply hurt.

He went next to Abu Bakr, his closest friend and an eloquent man. This time he met only silence. Abu Bakr said nothing at all, and Umar walked away angrier than before, wondering why no one wanted his daughter.

Carrying that hurt, he went to the Prophet ﷺ. And the Prophet ﷺ told him gently that Hafsa would marry someone better than Uthman, and Uthman would marry someone better than Hafsa. It was the Prophet ﷺ himself who would marry her. Umar's anger turned to joy, and he said, "Allahu Akbar."

Only later did Abu Bakr explain his silence. He had already known the Prophet's intention, and would not give away the secret. "If the Prophet ﷺ had not mentioned her," he said, "it would have been an honour to marry her." The marriage took place around the third year after the migration.

What her worship looked like

Hafsa was not a public figure in the way some were. If you had met her, you would have found her doing the same two things: fasting through the day and standing in prayer through the night. She memorised the entire Quran at a time when that was rare, and learned to read and write when literacy itself was uncommon.

She narrated the worship of the Prophet ﷺ more than almost anything else. She described his recitation at night, slow and measured, every letter clear, a pause after each verse where he would weep and call upon his Lord. When they asked her to imitate it, she recited from behind a curtain, verse by verse, to show them they could not match it.

She also narrated his poverty without shame. Asked what his bed was like, she said it was only a folded woollen blanket. One night she folded it into four to make it softer for him. He slept more deeply, and then told her to put it back the way it was: the comfort had made him slower to rise for the night prayer.

Her younger brother Abdullah, too shy to ask the Prophet ﷺ directly, once sent his dream to her to relay. The Prophet ﷺ answered, "What a good young man Abdullah is, if only he would pray a little more at night." Hafsa carried that advice to her brother, and lived it herself: she was the one already standing through the nights she was telling him about.

Two friends in the house of the Prophet

Hafsa and Aisha grew close, as their fathers were close. Abu Bakr and Umar were the Prophet's two dearest companions, the men he once called "my sight and my hearing," and now each had a daughter in his household. The friendship between the two young women had its warmth and its mischief both.

One day the two of them were keeping a voluntary fast when food arrived, and being hungry, they ate. Unsure of the ruling and afraid they had done wrong, they worried over how to tell the Prophet ﷺ. Hafsa, with the guilty conscience and the plain speech of her father, blurted it out the moment he arrived. He set them at ease: a voluntary fast that is broken is simply made up another day, and there is no sin in it.

It is a small, ordinary scene, and that is its charm. The house of the most influential man in the world held two young women who fasted, who feared to displease their Lord over a missed day, and who still leaned on each other the way friends do.

The hardest year, and the words that saved her

There came a time when the Prophet ﷺ withdrew from his wives, and word raced through Madinah that he had divorced them all. Umar, who feared above all things to displease the Prophet ﷺ, found Hafsa weeping and did not know what her fate would be. He went in to the Prophet ﷺ and found him lying on a mat of palm fibre that had marked his side, in a room that held almost nothing.

The Prophet ﷺ had not divorced them. And when the matter passed, he came to Hafsa. "Take Hafsa back," the angel had told him, "for she is a woman who fasts and a woman who prays, and she is your wife in Paradise."

Her rescue was not because she was the daughter of Umar. It was because of how she spent her nights and her days. Her worship, done quietly and for no audience, was known in the heavens, and it was her worship that spoke for her when she could not speak for herself.

This was the verse her nephew Ibn Abbas waited a whole year to ask Umar about, the verse that named the two wives by implication and called them gently back:

If both of you [wives] repent to God - for your hearts have deviated - [all will be well]; if you collaborate against him, [be warned that] God will aid him, as will Gabriel and all righteous believers, and the angels too will back him.Quran 66:4

The keeper of the Quran

When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Hafsa grieved as her father grieved, and drew back into a quiet life of worship. She did not become a public teacher. She waited, fasting and praying, for the day she would meet him again.

Years on, when Umar was the leader of the believers, she saw him walking about in a garment that had been patched and mended, and asked why he would not wear something finer and easier. He answered her with a question: did she not remember how the Prophet ﷺ used to live? At that, father and daughter held each other and wept, remembering the poverty of the man they both loved.

Her home was given an honour few homes have ever held. The first compilation of the Quran was kept in her house. When Uthman gathered the copies that would be sent across the lands, he borrowed that compilation from her and returned it to her when the work was done.

Like her father, she kept orphans in her home and cared for them. And when the great trials came, the disputes that split the community after Uthman, she stayed out of all of it. She would say, in gratitude, that she never dipped her hands in any of it.

She lived the rest of her years exactly as she had lived them before, until it was as if she fasted every single day. She died around the year 45 after the migration, and her brother Abdullah laid her in the grave.

What Hafsa's life teaches us

Her story has its dramatic hour, but its real weight is in the years no one was looking. That is what makes it worth carrying.

  • Private worship is never hidden. Hafsa fasted and prayed for no audience. It was that quiet devotion, not her lineage, that was named in the heavens and spoke for her when she needed it most.
  • Steadiness beats intensity. She did not have a single famous moment of zeal. She had a lifetime of the same two acts, repeated until they became who she was.
  • A small comfort can cost a great deal. When a softer bed made the Prophet ﷺ slower to rise at night, he gave it up. Hafsa understood that ease can quietly loosen the grip of good habits.
  • Peace can be a choice. When the community tore itself apart, she stepped back and kept her hands clean of it. Not every dispute needs another voice in it.

Questions

Who was Hafsa bint Umar?

The eldest daughter of Umar ibn al-Khattab and a wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. She is honoured among the Mothers of the Believers and was known for her fasting, her night prayer, and her memorisation of the Quran.

Why is Hafsa called a woman who fasts and prays?

When the Prophet ﷺ took her back after withdrawing from his wives, the angel described her to him as a woman who fasts and a woman who prays, and his wife in Paradise. She kept those habits all her life, until it was as if she fasted every day.

What happened to the first compiled Quran?

It was kept in Hafsa's home. When Uthman ibn Affan prepared the standard copies to send across the Muslim lands, he borrowed that compilation from her and returned it to her afterwards.

What can we learn from the life of Hafsa?

The quiet strength of private worship, the value of steadiness over occasional zeal, and the wisdom of stepping back from conflict rather than adding to it.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to measure faith by its big moments: the public stand, the dramatic sacrifice. Hafsa's life points somewhere quieter. Hers was built from things no one applauded: a night of prayer, a day of fasting, a blanket folded back so it would not make her soft. None of it was for show. All of it was counted.

Most of our own worship happens the same way, unseen, in the small hours and the ordinary days. Hafsa's life is a reminder that those hidden acts are not lost. Choose one small thing to keep faithfully, the way she did, and let it quietly become who you are.
