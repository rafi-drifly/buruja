Abu Ahmad Abd ibn Jahsh

The Other Blind Companion

There was a man in Makkah who could walk its hardest paths with no one to lead him. He had lost his sight, yet he knew its valleys and its hilltops by heart, and he loved the city the way a person loves a home that holds everyone they belong to.

This is the story of Abu Ahmad Abd ibn Jahsh (may Allah be pleased with him): the blind companion who was among the very first to leave that city for the sake of Allah, and who carried one quiet grief for the rest of his life.

The Other Blind Companion

A family that believed early

Abu Ahmad was the brother of Abdullah ibn Jahsh, the man who would become the first leader given command in Islam. Theirs was a household that accepted the message early, converting as a family on the hands of Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him). When one of them believed, it seems, the others were not far behind.

When the persecution in Makkah grew heavy, this family did not wait. They were among the first batch of believers to leave for Abyssinia, the first migration in the history of Islam. Ibn Ishaq records that the first person to go out on that migration was Abdullah ibn Jahsh, who took his family and his brother with him.

So Abu Ahmad belongs to the first of the first: not only one of the earliest to believe, but one of the earliest to act on that belief by leaving. These were the people who were escaping persecution and undertaking the very first hijrah, before any of it had a name. They had no example to follow. They were the example.

Think about what that means. Before there was a state, before there was safety anywhere, these were the people who packed up their lives and walked away from everything they knew, simply because they believed.

The man who walked Makkah alone

Abu Ahmad does not appear to have been born blind the way his relative Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum was. He lost his sight early in life. Yet he was known across Makkah for something remarkable: he could move through the city with no support at all.

He would go to its hilltops and its mountaintops and walk through its valleys with no guide and no hand to hold. Makkah is not an easy surface to cross even with sight, with its rock and its rising ground, and people were struck by the way he found his own way through all of it.

He even put his love for the city into verse. He called Makkah a blessed, beloved valley: a place that held his family, his guests, and his visitors, the people who came back to him and to whom he returned. His roots were there, he said, and there he walked without any guide.

There is something quietly moving in that. A man who could not see had memorised an entire city out of love, and moved through it as if it were holding him up. His limitation had not made the world smaller. It had made one place his own.

A marriage between two houses

In Abyssinia, Abu Ahmad was married to al-Farya bint Abi Sufyan, a daughter of Abu Sufyan. Her sister was Umm Habibah, who would later become one of the wives of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. So this family was closely tied, again and again, to the house of Abu Sufyan.

The marriage served two purposes that we can see. It strengthened the allegiance between the Jahsh family and the tribe of Abu Sufyan, and it joined two houses of standing, for the Jahsh family was known for its wealth. To many in Makkah it would have seemed an ordinary arrangement of its time.

It is one of the quiet patterns of these years: bonds being formed across families that history, and the choice to believe, would later pull in very different directions.

The home that was taken

What Abu Ahmad is most remembered for is what happened to his home. The houses of the Jahsh family sat among the most valuable in Makkah. When word reached the city that the family had left for Abyssinia, Abu Jahl went into their homes, took what was inside, and began to sell it off.

The Prophet ﷺ saw how deeply this wounded the family. He comforted Abdullah ibn Jahsh with a question that turned the loss inside out: would you not be pleased that Allah will give you a home in Paradise in place of the home that was taken from you?

There is a tenderness in that question. He did not tell them the loss did not matter. He acknowledged it fully, and then he set against it something far greater and far more lasting. A house of stone for a house in Paradise.

But for Abu Ahmad there was a sharper edge to the grief. The man who took his house, sold it, and kept the money was his own father-in-law, Abu Sufyan. The betrayal was not only the loss of property. It came from inside his own family, from the very house his marriage had been meant to bind him to. That wound stayed with him for the rest of his life.

Excused from battle, equal in reward

In time Abu Ahmad moved to Madinah, and there Allah arranged something none of them expected. Of all his siblings, he was the one who would outlive them all. The brother who might have seemed most in need of care, the one without his sight, was the one given the longest life.

His blindness meant he could not go out to fight in the battles. There is a well-known account of Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum and the verse that excused those unable to fight, while making clear that the believer held back by such a hardship is given the same reward as the one who goes out. Some of the commentaries mention that this applied to Abu Ahmad as well.

It is worth sitting with that for a moment. The reward was not reduced because the deed was impossible. The intention, the faith, the willingness that had no outlet: all of it was counted in full. The door that was closed to his body was left wide open in the sight of Allah.

He could not carry a sword, and he was not asked to. His place in the reward of Allah was not lessened by the limits of his body.

The wali of his sister

Abu Ahmad's sister was Zainab bint Jahsh, who would become one of the wives of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. When that marriage took place, it was Abu Ahmad who had the honour of serving as her wali, the one who gave her in marriage to the Prophet ﷺ.

It is a striking honour for a man whose own father-in-law had wronged him. The same family ties that had brought him pain now brought him close to the household of the Prophet ﷺ. He stood, on that day, in a place few people in history would ever occupy.

He lived in Madinah through the whole of that period. When Makkah was finally opened and the believers returned to the city that had once driven them out, Abu Ahmad returned with them, and the old grief about his home rose up in him once more.

By then Abu Sufyan had repented and was among those granted amnesty, and what was past was meant to be left in the past. Even so, Abu Ahmad still carried the hurt of the home that had been stolen from him. Some wounds do not close just because the world has moved on.

A grief laid to rest

Standing near the Kaaba, Abu Ahmad called out to the clan of Abd Manaf, reminding them by Allah of their old allegiance and of the home he had lost. He spoke long lines about the regret that ought to be felt for the property that had been taken from him. It was a raw, public moment: a blind man calling his people to account for an old wrong.

The Prophet ﷺ spoke to him. Whatever was said brought him down off his camel to sit with the people, and from that day he never mentioned the home again until he met Allah. His compensation had been placed where no one could ever take it from him, and that was enough.

It is a small detail, but a telling one. The grief did not vanish because he forced it down. It was answered, gently, by the one person whose word could reach it, and then he let it go. There is a difference between burying a hurt and being healed of it, and what he received was the second.

He even outlived his sister Zainab. At her funeral he insisted on being one of those who carried her body to the grave, refusing to be relieved of it. She was, he said, the one through whom they had gained every blessing, by her marriage to the Prophet ﷺ, and remembering that comforted them in their grief. He died sometime after, and the circumstances are not recorded. May Allah be pleased with him.

What Abu Ahmad's life teaches us

His story is short, and most of it is carried in a few quiet details: a man who could not see, a home that was taken, a grief finally laid down. That is exactly why it stays with us.

  • Limits do not lower your standing with Allah. He could not fight, yet he was promised the same reward as those who went out. What he could not do never counted against him.
  • Faith can mean walking away from home. He was among the very first to leave Makkah for Abyssinia, giving up the city he loved most because he believed.
  • Some wounds are healed by trust, not by getting even. He carried the loss of his home for years. In the end he laid it down and left his compensation with Allah.
  • Honour is given, not grasped. He gave his sister in marriage to the Prophet ﷺ and carried her to her grave. He was lifted by the people he loved, not by anything he seized for himself.

Questions

Who was Abu Ahmad Abd ibn Jahsh?

He was an early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ and the brother of Abdullah ibn Jahsh. He was blind, yet known for moving through Makkah without a guide, and he was among the first believers to migrate to Abyssinia.

Why is he called the other blind companion?

He is remembered alongside Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum, the well-known blind companion. Some narrations connect Abu Ahmad to the same verse that excused those unable to fight while granting them an equal reward.

What happened to his home in Makkah?

When his family migrated to Abyssinia, their homes were seized and sold off. In Abu Ahmad's case the one who took and sold his house was his own father-in-law, Abu Sufyan, and the loss grieved him for years until the Prophet ﷺ comforted him.

What can we learn from his life?

That a hardship we did not choose never lowers our worth before Allah, that faith can ask us to leave what we love, and that some wounds are best healed by trusting Allah rather than seeking to settle the score.

Why this story stays with us

Abu Ahmad lived with two things many of us quietly fear: a limit in his own body, and a lasting wrong done to him by someone close. He met the first without bitterness, and he learned, in the end, to set the second down. Neither defined him. His blindness did not lower his reward, and his loss did not harden his heart.

Most of us carry something similar: a thing we cannot do, a hurt we keep returning to. His life is a gentle invitation to hold both differently. To trust that what we lack is seen and weighed by Allah, and to leave an old wound in His hands rather than carry it forever. Take one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
