The Companions

Abdullah ibn Jahsh

An Accepted Prayer

On the morning of Uhud, two men stood side by side with their hands raised. One of them asked Allah for something most people would never dare to ask for: a hard battle, a hard death, and to be returned to his Lord having given everything for His sake. The friend beside him, whose own prayers were famous for being answered, would later say this prayer was better than his own.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Jahsh (may Allah be pleased with him): an early believer, the first man to be called a commander in Islam, and the cousin of the Prophet ﷺ whose final prayer was answered down to the last detail.

A household already turning toward the truth

Abdullah ibn Jahsh was the son of Jahsh ibn Ri'ab al-Asadi. His mother was Umaymah bint Abdul Muttalib, the paternal aunt of the Prophet ﷺ. That made Abdullah a cousin of the Prophet, and it placed him inside a family that was quietly moving toward belief before the message was ever made public.

All of Umaymah's children would become Muslim. His sister Hamna would marry Mus'ab ibn Umayr, and she would weep deeply for him when he was martyred. His sister Zaynab would one day become a wife of the Prophet ﷺ. His brother Ubaydullah was among those who had searched for the one God even before the call to Islam began. Another brother, Abd, also known as Abu Ahmad, carried his own remarkable story.

Most of the biographers say Umaymah herself passed away before she could see her children embrace the faith. But the ideas of Islam were already moving through the household.

So when faith reached Abdullah, it did not reach a stranger. It reached a home where the search for the one God was already in the air.

An early answer to the call

Abdullah embraced Islam very early, at the hands of Abu Bakr as-Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him). There is a quiet beauty in that detail. Every good deed Abdullah would go on to do, every prayer, every migration, every act of courage, became part of Abu Bakr's reward too, because he was the one who had guided him to the faith.

Abdullah joined the small circle of believers who gathered in the house of al-Arqam, learning the religion in its first and most fragile days. When the persecution grew heavy, he was among those who made the second migration to Abyssinia, and later he migrated again to Madinah.

Few companions carried that distinction. He was a man of the two migrations, someone who left his home not once but twice for the sake of his faith. He had already given up comfort and safety long before he was ever asked to give his life.

The first commander in Islam

After Abdullah reached Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ sent him out at the head of a small party. The companion who recorded what happened was Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (may Allah be pleased with him), who himself was present.

Sa'd narrated that the Prophet ﷺ said he would place over them a man who was the most patient of them with hunger and thirst: someone steady, capable, and fit to lead. Then he appointed Abdullah ibn Jahsh over them.

And so, Sa'd said, Abdullah was the first commander in Islam. Some narrations even remember him as the first to be called Amir al-Mu'minin, the commander of the believers, though that title would later belong to Umar ibn al-Khattab as a settled office.

The honour did not come from his lineage or his wealth. It came from a quiet strength of character that the Prophet ﷺ could trust under hunger, thirst, and hardship. The leader was chosen for what he could bear, not for how he looked.

The expedition that went wrong

This first command was the expedition of Nakhla, in the days before Badr. The Prophet ﷺ used to send out small parties to watch the caravans of Makkah, which carried goods toward Syria, much of it taken from the Muslims who had been driven out of the city. He gave Abdullah a sealed letter with instructions and told him to wait before opening it.

The orders were to go to Nakhla, between Makkah and Ta'if, to observe Quraysh and gather news, but not to fight. It was the sacred month of Rajab, and even though the people of Makkah honoured little, the Prophet ﷺ still honoured what Allah had made sacred.

Fighting broke out anyway. The party returned with captured goods, two prisoners, and one man killed on the other side. The Prophet ﷺ was displeased, and he said plainly that he had not commanded them to fight in that month, only to gather news and disrupt the caravans.

Abdullah was left fearing the worst. He believed he had earned the displeasure of the Prophet ﷺ, and the people of Makkah seized on the moment, mocking the Muslims and accusing them of violating a sacred month. He waited, hoping for some word that would set things right.

A verse that lifted the burden

The people of Makkah were not honest accusers. They had driven the believers from their homes, seized their property, and were now trading it for profit. They even rearranged the sacred months of the calendar when it suited them, so they could fight whenever they pleased. Yet here they spoke as though they held the moral high ground.

Then a verse was revealed that settled the matter. It acknowledged that fighting in the sacred month was a serious thing, while naming the far greater wrong: barring people from the path of Allah, denying access to the Sacred Mosque, and driving its people from their homes.

The verse lifted the weight from Abdullah and his companions. They had no moral defeat to answer for. The heavier burden, Allah made clear, rested on those who had persecuted and expelled the believers in the first place.

They ask you [Prophet] about fighting in the prohibited month. Say, 'Fighting in that month is a great offence, but to bar others from God's path, to disbelieve in Him, prevent access to the Sacred Mosque, and expel its people, are still greater offences in God's eyes: persecution is worse than killing.'Qur'an 2:217

The prayer at Uhud

Abdullah fought bravely at Badr and became one of its veterans. Then came the day that his name is most remembered for: the battle of Uhud.

Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas narrated that before the fighting began, Abdullah turned to him and asked, "Will you not make dua to Allah?" So the two of them raised their hands together. Picture them there, on the edge of a battle, two friends with their palms open to the sky. Sa'd prayed first, asking Allah for a strong opponent, that he might fight him and be granted martyrdom. Abdullah said ameen to his friend's prayer.

Then Abdullah made his own. He asked for a fierce opponent and for martyrdom, and then he added something more. He asked that his body be left for the sake of Allah, so that when he met his Lord on the Day of Judgement and was asked in what cause this was done to him, he could answer, "For You, and for Your Messenger," and his Lord would say to him, "You have told the truth."

Sa'd stood listening to every word of it, still holding his hands up beside his friend. And Sa'd, a man whose own prayers were known to be answered, said simply: "The dua of Abdullah was better than mine." It takes a rare sincerity to ask Allah for something so heavy, and to mean it completely.

Buried beside Hamza

Abdullah fought until he was killed at Uhud. The man who killed him then mutilated his body, cutting off his nose and his ears, just as had been done to Hamza (may Allah be pleased with him), the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ. Hamza was Abdullah's uncle too, for Abdullah's aunt was Hamza's sister.

When the Prophet ﷺ found him on the field, he was deeply moved. Hamza's grave already stood apart from the rest. The Prophet ﷺ ordered that Abdullah be buried in that same grave. So the two of them, nephew and uncle, were laid to rest together, side by side.

His prayer had been answered exactly as he had asked it. He had wanted a hard fight, a martyr's death, and a body given wholly for the sake of Allah, and he was granted all three. His widow, Zaynab bint Khuzayma, would later become one of the Mothers of the Believers.

He had asked his Lord for the heaviest thing a believer can carry, and his Lord had said yes.

What Abdullah ibn Jahsh teaches us

His life is short in the record, but it carries a weight far beyond its length. Most of it comes down to one quality: sincerity that asks for nothing but the pleasure of Allah. These are a few of the things his story leaves with us.

  • Character earns trust before status does. He was made the first commander not for his name or his wealth, but because he was the most patient with hunger, thirst, and hardship. The Prophet ﷺ looked past the surface and chose the man who could bear the most. The inner quality came first.
  • Ask Allah boldly, and mean it. His prayer at Uhud was daring and specific, and it was answered word for word. We often shrink our requests to what feels reasonable. Sincere certainty in dua, asking Allah for the things that truly matter, is its own kind of strength.
  • Sincerity is between you and Allah alone. He did not ask to be seen or praised by anyone. He asked only that his Lord would one day say to him, "You have told the truth." The audience he cared about was an audience of One.
  • A burden you did not deserve can be lifted. When Abdullah feared he had failed and lost the approval of the Prophet ﷺ, relief came from the highest place of all. What looked like disgrace was turned, by a single verse, into vindication. Allah does not abandon the sincere.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Jahsh?

An early Muslim and a cousin of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ through his mother Umaymah bint Abdul Muttalib. He accepted Islam at the hands of Abu Bakr, made the migrations to Abyssinia and Madinah, and was killed at the battle of Uhud.

Why is he called the first commander in Islam?

When the Prophet ﷺ sent out the expedition of Nakhla, he appointed Abdullah to lead it. Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, who was present, narrated that Abdullah was the first man placed in command of a party in Islam.

What was special about his prayer at Uhud?

Before the battle, he asked Allah for a fierce opponent, for martyrdom, and to be returned to his Lord having given everything for His sake. He was granted exactly what he asked. Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas said Abdullah's prayer was better than his own.

Where was Abdullah ibn Jahsh buried?

After he was killed and mutilated at Uhud, the Prophet ﷺ ordered that he be buried in the same grave as Hamza, his uncle, who had been treated in the same way. The two were laid to rest together.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to keep our prayers small and safe, asking for ease and protection and little more. Abdullah ibn Jahsh asked for the hardest thing he could imagine, because he wanted his whole life to point in one direction. He was not performing for anyone. He simply wanted to stand before Allah one day and hear the words, "You have told the truth."

We may never face a battlefield. But every ordinary day asks the same quiet question: who are we trying to please? His example invites us to make our intentions cleaner, to ask Allah for what truly matters, and to do the work that no one sees with the same care as the work that everyone watches. It invites us to pray with the kind of certainty that expects an answer. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
