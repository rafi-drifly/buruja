He asked Allah for the hardest thing, and it was granted

Preview: Before the battle, two companions raised their hands. One of them prayed something most of us never would.

Assalamu alaikum,

Most of us keep our prayers careful. We ask for ease, for safety, for things to be made light. Abdullah ibn Jahsh (may Allah be pleased with him) did the opposite, and his story has stayed with us ever since.

He was an early believer and a cousin of the Prophet ﷺ. He accepted Islam at the hands of Abu Bakr, learned the faith in the house of al-Arqam, and migrated twice for the sake of it: first to Abyssinia, then to Madinah. When the Prophet ﷺ needed someone to lead the first expedition, he chose Abdullah, not for his name or his wealth, but because he was the most patient of them with hunger and thirst.

That first command did not go as planned. Fighting broke out in a sacred month when the orders had been only to watch, and for a while Abdullah feared he had failed his Prophet. Then a verse was revealed that lifted the burden from him, making clear that the heavier wrong lay with those who had driven the believers from their homes.

The morning of the battle of Uhud is what he is most remembered for. Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas narrated that Abdullah turned to him and said, "Will you not make dua to Allah?" So they raised their hands together. Sa'd prayed first, asking for a strong opponent and for martyrdom. Then Abdullah prayed, and he asked for more.

He asked Allah for a fierce opponent, for martyrdom, and to be returned to his Lord having given everything for His sake. He wanted that on the Day of Judgement, when he was asked in what cause this was done to him, he could answer, "For You, and for Your Messenger," and hear his Lord say, "You have told the truth."

Sa'd, a man whose own prayers were known to be answered, said simply: "The dua of Abdullah was better than mine."

He was granted exactly what he asked. He fought until he was killed, and the Prophet ﷺ, deeply moved, ordered him buried in the same grave as his uncle Hamza.

There is a quiet lesson in him that is easy to miss. He was not performing for anyone. His sincerity was between him and Allah alone. We may never face a battlefield, but every ordinary day asks the same question: who are we really trying to please?
