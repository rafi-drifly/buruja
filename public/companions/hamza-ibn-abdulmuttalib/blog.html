Hamza ibn Abdulmuttalib

The Lion of Allah

He was the man every young person in Makkah wanted to be: a hunter who could track and take down a lion, a wrestler, an archer, the one the crowds gathered around when he came back into town. For years, none of it pointed toward faith.

This is the story of Hamza ibn Abdulmuttalib (may Allah be pleased with him): uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, called the Lion of Allah, and the one the Prophet ﷺ named the master of the martyrs.

The Companions

Hamza ibn Abdulmuttalib

The Lion of Allah

A name that meant a lion

Banu Hashim chose their children's names with care, and Hamza was no exception. His name comes from a word for the lion: not the lion stalking its prey, but the lion at the height of its strength. It was a name about power, and it fit him exactly.

He was tall, strong, and popular, a warrior and a hunter known across the city. He liked to hunt the animals no one else dared to, lions among them. When he walked into Makkah, the youth would crowd around him, and the people told stories about his strength.

He was also the uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, though uncle is almost the wrong word. They were close in age, nursed by the same woman, Thuwaybah, in infancy, and bound together their whole lives. The Prophet ﷺ would later say of him simply: he is my brother.

His full standing among the companions is easy to underestimate, because he died so early. Yet some of the scholars said that had he lived, he would likely have been counted among the rightly guided caliphs. That tells you the rank the companions understood him to hold.

The one who stayed away

When the Prophet ﷺ began to call his family to Islam, Hamza was not there. The scholars give a plain reason: he was rarely in Makkah. He was out in the desert, hunting, exploring, lost in his craft the way some people lose themselves in a sport, as if nothing else in the world counted.

He was also, they say, simply not a man who thought much about religion. He loved the Prophet ﷺ, his nephew and his brother, but the talk of faith and the hereafter did not reach him. He kept to his bow and his arrows.

So he was distant from it all, in body and in heart. As the early Muslims gathered and suffered, his name does not even come up. He was somewhere else, and faith had not yet found him.

It is a familiar kind of distance. Not hostility, just absence: a good man who loved good people, busy with the things he was good at, never quite stopping long enough to ask the larger questions. His story is a reminder that such a person can still be reached.

The blow that changed him

Abu Jahl had been tightening his cruelty against the Muslims, moving from insult to violence. One day he found the Prophet ﷺ alone near the Kaaba, with none of his clansmen nearby, and threw a vile insult at him in front of the people. The Prophet ﷺ, as always, did not answer it.

A servingwoman from the household of Abdullah ibn Jud'an saw the whole thing. When Hamza came back from a hunt and went, as he always did, to sit by the Kaaba, she came to him and told him what had happened: his nephew, insulted, with no one to defend him. The anger rose in his face until he was sweating with it.

He walked straight through the gatherings to Abu Jahl, in the middle of his own tribe, and struck him across the head with his bow until it split open and bled. Then he stood over him and said the words that surprised even himself: you dare insult him when I am on his religion, and I say what he says.

A heart that asked for guidance

He did not really know what that religion was. He had said it in fury, to wound Abu Jahl. That night he went home unsettled and could not sleep. He turned the words over and over, pulled between them and the whispers that asked what he was doing to the way of his forefathers.

Hamza believed in God. He was not a religious man, but the belief was there in his nature. So he turned to Allah and asked, plainly, to be guided: if this is the truth, let it settle in my heart. The Prophet ﷺ, when Hamza came to him, told him to do exactly that, to ask Allah alone.

He prayed on it, and faith settled. He came to the Prophet ﷺ and said: I testify that you are telling the truth. Then announce your religion openly, my nephew, for I will protect you. He said he would not trade his new faith for the whole world. The lion now had Islam in his heart.

There is a quiet lesson in how it happened. Allah used Abu Jahl, who feared Islam more than anyone, as the very means of bringing Hamza to it. The insult meant to wound the Prophet ﷺ became the doorway through which his strongest protector walked in.

The first to carry the flag

His faith changed the balance. Until Hamza, no warrior of his standing had become Muslim, and Abu Jahl had felt free to torment the believers. Now the people of Makkah began, for the first time, to think about negotiating with the Prophet ﷺ rather than only crushing him.

In Makkah, Hamza became a regular of Dar al-Arqam and the guard of the Muslims. If there was a knock or a noise at the door, he was the one who stood with his sword. In Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ made him the first to carry the flag of Islam, sending him out as general on the earliest expeditions and counting him among his closest advisers.

At Badr he was the first to strike, and when the enemy called for their best opponents, it was Hamza who answered: I am the uncle of the Prophet, the Lion of Allah and of His Messenger. He fought with two swords, and a feather pinned to his chest so that anyone who wished to face him would know exactly where he stood.

In Madinah he had been paired as a brother with Zayd ibn Harithah, and before each battle he would entrust Zayd with his will, always ready for the moment Allah would take his life. He carried himself as a man who had made his peace with death long before it came for him.

The day of Uhud

Makkah came back for Uhud with a larger army and a settled hatred. Among them was Hind bint Utbah, whose father Hamza had killed at Badr, and who had promised a man named Wahshi his freedom if he would kill Hamza. Wahshi was a master of the spear. He hid behind a rock and kept his eyes on no one else.

Hamza fought through the battle like a lion. The narrations say that as much as two thirds of the enemy losses that day fell to his hand. Then his armor lifted for a moment, and Wahshi threw. The spear passed through him, and even then Hamza rose and tried to reach his attacker before the wound took him.

When the Prophet ﷺ found his body, mutilated where it lay, he wept in a way those around him had never seen before. He praised his uncle: you always kept the ties of kinship, you were always at the forefront of good. Then his first thought was for another's pain, and he sent Hamza's nephew Zubayr to keep his sister Safiyyah from seeing the sight.

Patience, and an unimaginable mercy

Standing over a brother mutilated in front of him, the Prophet ﷺ at first said he would answer it in kind. Then the angel Jibril came down with a verse, and the Prophet ﷺ chose patience instead and held to the higher ground.

Hamza was buried as the master of the martyrs, the one all the martyrs will stand behind on the Day of Judgement. Every family in Madinah had lost someone at Uhud, and when the Prophet ﷺ heard them weeping for their dead, he grieved that his own uncle had no one to weep for him. The people of Madinah came to his door and wept for Hamza.

The most astonishing part came later. Wahshi, who threw the spear, and Hind, who mutilated him, both became Muslim, and their faith became sincere and strong. The Prophet ﷺ forgave Hind. Wahshi carried his guilt for years, then took the very spear that killed Hamza and used it to kill Musaylimah, the false prophet. One for one, he said: I killed the best of men, and now I have killed the worst.

When some of the companions saw Wahshi after that, they thought of the verse that tells those who have wronged themselves never to despair of Allah's mercy. Picture it: Hamza, the master of the martyrs, and the man who killed him, both gathered in the same Paradise. That is the size of the mercy this story holds.

What Hamza's life teaches us

His story moves quickly: a late faith, a few short years, an early death. What stays with you is not the strength, but what the strength was finally given to.

  • It is never too late to turn. For years Hamza kept his distance from faith. One sincere prayer for guidance changed everything. No one is too far along, or too set in their ways, to ask Allah for the same.
  • Ask, and then watch your heart. He did not argue himself into belief. He asked Allah to settle the truth in his heart, and it settled. A humble request can do what debate cannot.
  • Strength is meant to protect. Hamza's courage was loved before Islam, but it only became great when he stood between the Prophet ﷺ and harm. What we are good at is worth most when it shields others.
  • No sin is beyond Allah's mercy. The two who harmed Hamza most were both forgiven and became sincere believers. If they were not beyond mercy, neither are we, and neither is anyone we are tempted to write off.

Questions

Who was Hamza ibn Abdulmuttalib?

He was a paternal uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, close to him in age and like a brother, having been nursed by the same woman. Known before Islam as a strong warrior and hunter, he became one of the most important early Muslims and is honoured as the Lion of Allah and the master of the martyrs.

Why is Hamza called the Lion of Allah?

His name itself comes from a word for the lion at the height of its strength, and he was famous for his courage, his hunting, and his power in battle. After he accepted Islam and defended the Prophet ﷺ, he became known as Asadullah: the Lion of Allah and of His Messenger.

How did Hamza become Muslim?

After Abu Jahl insulted the Prophet ﷺ near the Kaaba, a woman who had witnessed it told Hamza. In his anger he struck Abu Jahl and declared he followed the Prophet's religion. That night he prayed for guidance, and faith settled in his heart. He then pledged to protect the Prophet ﷺ openly.

How did Hamza die?

He was killed at the Battle of Uhud by a spear thrown by Wahshi, after fighting bravely and accounting for much of the enemy's losses that day. The Prophet ﷺ grieved deeply for him. Remarkably, both Wahshi and Hind, who had wronged Hamza, later accepted Islam and were forgiven.

Why this story stays with us

It would be easy to remember Hamza only for his strength, for the bow and the two swords and the feather on his chest. But the turning point of his life was quiet: a man who had ignored faith for years, lying awake one night, asking Allah to guide his heart. Everything that made him the Lion of Allah grew from that small, honest prayer.

Most of us are not warriors. But all of us have something we are good at, and a heart that can be turned. His life asks two things of an ordinary day: to ask sincerely for guidance and trust Allah to give it, and to spend our strength, whatever form it takes, in protecting and lifting the people around us. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
