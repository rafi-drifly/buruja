The Companions

Talha ibn Ubaydillah

The Living Martyr

On the worst day of the early Muslim community, when the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ had fallen and arrows were coming from every side, one man stood over him and caught them with his bare hand. By the end of that day his body carried more than twenty wounds, and his hand would never fully work again.

This is the story of Talha ibn Ubaydillah (may Allah be pleased with him): a wealthy young merchant who gave up his comfort early, kept a promise he never wavered on, and was called by the Prophet ﷺ a living martyr while he still walked the earth.

The Companions

Talha ibn Ubaydillah

The Living Martyr

A young man with everything to lose

Talha was born into Banu Taym, a respected tribe of Makkah. It was the tribe of Abu Bakr (RA), and Talha was his second cousin by birth and later his son-in-law. He grew up with lineage, wealth, and the protection of his people.

He was a cloth merchant, and a successful one, traveling the trade routes north to Syria and south to Yemen. Those who knew him described him as tall and dark, with plenty of hair, handsome, and quick on his feet. He wore fine clothes and good scents, and on his hand sat a gold ring set with a ruby.

He was only about eighteen when the Prophet ﷺ began to receive revelation. He had everything a young man could want in this world, and everything to lose by becoming a Muslim.

The monk on the road to Syria

When the message came to Makkah, Talha was not there. He was away on his trade route in Syria, where he met a Christian monk, one of the people of the Book. The monk asked whether anyone in his group was from the sacred city of Makkah, and Talha said he had just come from there.

Then the monk asked a strange question: had Ahmad appeared yet? Talha had never heard the name used this way, and did not yet know it pointed to Muhammad ﷺ. He asked who Ahmad was. The monk told him: the son of Abdullah, the seal of the prophets, from the land of the sanctuary, who would migrate to a place of palm trees, black soil, and many springs. He urged Talha to hurry and follow him as soon as he had come out.

The words landed somewhere deep. Talha would later say they penetrated his heart. As soon as he reached Makkah he asked the people what had happened while he was away. Something, he felt, had changed.

Following his kinsman into Islam

They told him that Muhammad ibn Abdullah had announced his prophethood, and that Abu Bakr had followed him. Talha already knew the Prophet ﷺ, knew his character, and had no reason to doubt it. But it was his relative Abu Bakr he went to first.

He asked him plainly whether he had followed this man. Abu Bakr said yes, and told him he should too, because the Prophet ﷺ called to the truth and to what is good. Talha went with him, heard the message of the oneness of God from the Prophet ﷺ himself, and accepted it.

He is counted among the first eight people to embrace Islam, at the age of eighteen. He would also become one of the ten companions given the glad tidings of Paradise by the Prophet ﷺ. In all of this, Abu Bakr was his guide, and the reward of bringing him followed Abu Bakr too.

Tied to Abu Bakr in hardship

Wealth and standing often spared the powerful of Makkah from the open torture inflicted on the weak. Talha was prominent, yet his own tribe still turned on him. A man named Nawfal, whom the Prophet ﷺ called the devil of Quraysh, took Talha and Abu Bakr and bound them together with a single rope.

He beat them, brought others to beat them, and left them tied by their hands and feet, deprived of food and water. Because of this they were nicknamed the two tied ones. Much of it happened behind closed doors, but not all of it.

One companion remembered seeing a young man dragged through the streets, his hands bound behind his back, struck on the head while an old woman lashed him with a whip and shouted abuse. He asked who the woman was. They told him she was the young man's own mother, and the young man was Talha. Even with all this, he was spared the worst of Makkah's persecution, in part because his trade kept him out of the city for long stretches.

The portion of Badr, and the day of Uhud

When the Prophet ﷺ migrated, Talha met him on the road, returning from Syria, and gave him clothes he had brought. He then escorted the family of Abu Bakr to Madinah. He missed the Battle of Badr, not because he was trading, but because the Prophet ﷺ had sent him to scout one of the caravans of Abu Sufyan. He returned to find the battle already over.

He came grieving that he had missed it. The Prophet ﷺ told him he still had his portion of the spoils. Talha said he did not care about the spoils, he wanted the reward. Then came Uhud. Abu Bakr would later call the whole battle the day of Talha. As the Prophet ﷺ fell and was struck in the face, Talha lifted him, carried him on his back up the mountain, and stood guard. Arrows rained toward the Prophet ﷺ, and Talha caught them with one hand while fighting with the other, taking wound after wound. When it ended he had more than twenty wounds, and his hand was left partly paralyzed for the rest of his life.

There are men among the believers who honoured their pledge to God: some of them have fulfilled it by death, and some are still waiting. They have not changed in the least.Quran 33:23

The living martyr, and the open hand

When they reached the Prophet ﷺ, he was breathing hard, having nearly died himself. He looked at Talha, whose blood was flowing from every part of him, and said that whoever wished to see a martyr still walking on the earth should look at Talha. From that day he was known as the living martyr. He never missed a battle after, fighting on despite a hand that could no longer fully work.

His courage was matched by his generosity. His trade made him immensely wealthy, yet his wealth never reached his heart. It stayed in his hand. He would give the greater part of his earnings away in charity, and he forgave the debts owed to him, once as much as thirty thousand dirhams at a stroke. At Tabuk he gave a vast sum for the cause.

For this the Prophet ﷺ gave him names beyond the living martyr: the good one, and the generous one. Those who traveled with him said they had never met a man more generous with his money, his clothes, and his food.

His character carried into his home. He named his sons after the prophets, Muhammad, Imran, Musa, Yaqub, Ismail, and Isa. And his daughter Aisha bint Talha grew up to become one of the great scholars of Madinah, learning from her aunt, our mother Aisha (RA), and passing on much of what she carried.

A covenant kept to the last

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Talha fought in the wars that followed and in the conquests toward Syria, the lands he knew well from his trading days. When Umar (RA) was dying, he named Talha to the council of six who would choose the next leader. Talha had no interest in the position himself and gave his vote away, trusting another to make the best choice.

Years later, when Uthman (RA) was under siege, Talha was not in Madinah. He had not believed it would reach the point of bloodshed, and he carried a heavy guilt for not being there to defend him. He said a line that still holds weight: O Allah, take from me for Uthman until You are pleased.

He went out seeking justice for Uthman. He and his close friend Zubayr (RA) had even come to an agreement to pursue the wrongdoers together. But the same people who had stirred up the unrest attacked both camps in the night, so each side believed the other had struck first, and in that confusion the Battle of the Camel began. Talha was killed that day, with as many as seventy-four wounds on his body.

When Ali (RA) found him afterward, he laid Talha's head in his lap and wiped the dirt from his face, weeping, and said he wished he had died twenty years before seeing this day. He prayed over him, and later sat Talha's son beside him and prayed that he and Talha would be among those whose hearts are cleansed of every grudge:

and We shall remove any bitterness from their hearts: [they will be like] brothers, sitting on couches, face to face.Quran 15:47

What Talha's life teaches us

Talha is remembered for one extraordinary day, but the day was only possible because of who he already was. Three things made him.

  • Sincerity counts the same as presence. He missed Badr on the Prophet's own orders, yet his reward was full, because his heart had already gone out to it. What you fully intend is written for you, even when the moment passes you by.
  • Give from the hand, not from the heart. He was one of the wealthiest of his time, and he let almost none of it settle on his heart. He kept his money in his hand, open, and forgave what others owed him.
  • Courage is standing where it counts. On the worst day, he did not step back. He put his own body between the Prophet ﷺ and the arrows. Real courage is not the absence of danger, it is staying when others scatter.
  • A promise is kept by not changing. From eighteen to his last breath, his resolve never wavered. The verse he is remembered by praises those who do not change in the least.

Questions

Who was Talha ibn Ubaydillah?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the tribe of Banu Taym, a successful cloth merchant, and the second cousin and son-in-law of Abu Bakr (RA). He was among the first eight to accept Islam and one of the ten given the glad tidings of Paradise.

Why is Talha called the living martyr?

At the Battle of Uhud he shielded the Prophet ﷺ with his own body, catching arrows with his hand and carrying him to safety, taking more than twenty wounds. The Prophet ﷺ said that whoever wished to see a martyr still walking the earth should look at Talha.

How did Talha ibn Ubaydillah die?

He was killed at the Battle of the Camel, during the unrest that followed the death of Uthman (RA), having gone out to seek justice for him. He is said to have had as many as seventy-four wounds on his body, and Ali (RA) wept over him.

What can we learn from the life of Talha?

That sincere intention earns a full reward even when the moment is missed, that real generosity keeps wealth in the hand and not the heart, and that a promise is kept by never changing your resolve.

Why this story stays with us

Talha had every reason to keep his distance. He was young, wealthy, well connected, with a comfortable life on the trade routes. He could have believed quietly and protected all of it. Instead he stepped forward, again and again, until he was standing over the Prophet ﷺ with arrows in his hand, asking for the reward and not the spoils. The Prophet ﷺ once said that Talha and Zubayr would be his two neighbors in Paradise, and it is not hard to see why: they were the two who rushed to his side when it cost them the most.

Most of us are not asked for that. We are asked for the smaller version of it: to give without keeping count, to stay steady when it would be easier to drift, to keep a promise long after the feeling that made it has faded. The hardest battles in an ordinary life are rarely loud, and they are usually fought alone. Talha's life is an invitation to be the one who does not change, to keep showing up for what is right when no one is counting and the moment is no longer dramatic. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
