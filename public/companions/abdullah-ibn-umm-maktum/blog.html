The Companions

Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum

The One for Whom Heaven Spoke

Most people know him through a single moment: a man hurrying toward the Prophet ﷺ, a slight frown, and a verse of the Qur'an that followed. But that moment was never meant to shame him. It was sent to honour him, and to change the way an entire community would look at people forever.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum (may Allah be pleased with him): a man born without sight, of noble Makkan blood, for whom the Qur'an was revealed not once but twice, and who would one day die on a battlefield holding the banner he could not see.

Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum

The One for Whom Heaven Spoke

The man behind one famous moment

He came from good standing in Makkah. Both sides of his family belonged to Quraysh, and he was a first cousin of the Prophet's wife Khadijah (RA): her aunt was his mother. So when people picture him as one of the poor and the powerless, they only have half the picture. He carried a respected lineage. What set him apart was something else entirely.

Abdullah was the only companion we know of who was born blind. Others lost their sight in old age, but he never saw the world at all. His mother became known as Umm Maktum, from a word that means concealed, because her son's sight was hidden from him. People called her that out of sympathy for the child who could not see.

Because of his standing, he was spared the open torture that fell on companions like Bilal. We know of no persecution against him. He had heard of the Prophet's character long before the call began, and as soon as he heard the message, he believed. He was among the very earliest Muslims, accepting Islam before the first believers even gathered in the house of al-Arqam.

The day the Prophet frowned

The Prophet ﷺ was sitting with the leaders of Quraysh: Utbah, his brother Shaybah, Umayyah ibn Khalaf, Abu Jahl, and al-Walid. These were powerful men who had mocked him and persecuted those around him, and now they seemed willing, for once, to listen. He hoped that if such men accepted the message, others would follow, and the early Muslims might finally find some relief.

Into that gathering came Abdullah, rushing, calling out, "O Muhammad, teach me from what Allah has taught you." He could not see who else was there. This was simply how he and the Prophet ﷺ were with one another: the Prophet had made himself so available that Abdullah would come, find him, place a hand on him, and be answered for as long as he wished.

The Prophet ﷺ did not push him away or tell him to leave. He simply drew his brows together, a flicker of being interrupted, and turned back to the men he was hoping to reach. Abdullah never saw it. But the leaders saw it. And then the revelation came.

He frowned and turned away when the blind man came to him: for all you know, he might have grown in spirit, or taken note of something useful to him.Quran 80:1-4

Sixteen verses, and a changed heart

Allah revealed sixteen verses for this one moment. Not a private word to the Prophet ﷺ, but Qur'an that would be recited until the end of time. The same revelation that gently corrected him also raised Abdullah, asking why the man full of eagerness and awe should be passed over for those who felt no need of guidance at all.

This was never humiliation for the Prophet ﷺ. It was an honour, a sign of how carefully Allah was teaching him, and through him, teaching everyone who came after. The lesson was a shift in vision: that the worth of a person is not measured by status or strength, but by the heart turning toward God.

From then on, whenever the Prophet ﷺ saw Abdullah, he would stand to greet him and say, "Welcome to the one on whose behalf my Lord admonished me." He did not hold the moment against him. He treated him as someone special, because Allah had spoken from above the seven heavens on his behalf.

There is a quiet footnote to the story. Every one of those proud men in the gathering, except al-Walid who died earlier, was later killed at the Battle of Badr. The world had honoured them and overlooked the blind man. Allah honoured the one the world had overlooked.

Among the first to Madinah

When the door to Madinah opened, Abdullah was among the very first to walk through it. He was the second person to make the migration, after Mus'ab ibn Umayr. The two of them were sent ahead of the Prophet ﷺ for one purpose: to teach the people the Qur'an.

So while the Muslims of Madinah waited for the Prophet ﷺ to arrive, it was these two men, going from house to house, who taught them the verses being revealed in distant Makkah. A blind man and a young noble, carrying the words of God into homes that had never heard them.

Then came Badr, and with it his first taste of being left behind. He longed to go out with the Prophet ﷺ, but he was not placed among the fighters, and it grieved him deeply. He was one of the earliest Muslims, the Qur'an had already been revealed on his behalf, and yet he could not stand in the line.

It would be easy to read his sadness as wounded pride. It was the opposite. A man who feels no closeness to God is relieved to be excused. A man whose heart is full of Him aches to be counted in. Abdullah ached to be counted in.

O Allah, reveal my excuse

He did not look for ways out of striving. He looked for ways in. When the heart is truly in awe of Allah, it does not search for permission to do less. It searches for any way to do more. So Abdullah made a simple, aching dua: "O Allah, reveal my excuse." He wanted to hear his own situation answered in the Qur'an.

One day the verse came that those who sit at home are not equal to those who strive in the path of Allah. Abdullah heard it and his heart sank further. He said, "O Messenger of Allah, what about me? I cannot see." Revelation came over the Prophet ﷺ again, and when it lifted, a few words had been added to the verse:

Those believers who stay at home, apart from those with an incapacity, are not equal to those who commit themselves and their possessions to striving in God's way.Quran 4:95

The blind man Allah honoured

In Madinah, Abdullah was given honours that overturned every assumption of the time. The Prophet ﷺ made him a caller to prayer alongside Bilal. He could not see the dawn break in the sky, so someone would come and tell him when Fajr had entered, and only then would he call. The Prophet ﷺ chose him anyway, because the point was to honour him, and to teach a community that worth is not in the eyes but in the heart.

He was appointed to lead the people in prayer, at a time when many assumed a blind man could not. And when the Prophet ﷺ left Madinah, he placed Abdullah in charge of the city, not as a symbol but in truth, some thirteen times. The man no one would have looked twice at before Islam was left to govern the Prophet's own city.

Years after the Prophet ﷺ had passed, his family still honoured Abdullah. Our mother Aisha (RA) was once seen serving him with her own hands, cutting citrus fruit and bringing him honey. When a student asked who this man was, she told him the whole story of the frowning, and said this was how the family of the Prophet ﷺ had been instructed to treat him ever since.

The banner he could not see

His life ended where his heart had always wanted to be. Around fourteen years after the migration, the Muslims faced the Persian empire at the Battle of Qadisiyyah: roughly thirty thousand against an army of more than a hundred thousand. A call went out across the lands for anyone who could help, and Abdullah, old and blind, asked to go. When they wondered what he could do, he said he would carry the Qur'an to the people and pray with them through the nights.

On the battlefield he asked for more. "Do you not need someone to carry the banner?" he said. They warned him he could not defend himself. So he put on his armour, took the flag, and planted his feet firmly in the ground so that nothing could move him back. He stood in the middle of a battle he could not see, refusing to fall.

The fighting raged for three days. Thousands of Muslims were martyred, and the tide turned against the Persian empire for good. When they searched among the fallen, they found Abdullah lying still, the banner of the Prophet ﷺ clutched to his chest. The man who once called the people to a dawn he could not see had carried the flag to a victory he would never witness.

The eyes are not what go blind. It is the hearts. And his heart had always seen clearly.

What Abdullah's life teaches us

His story is not really about blindness. It is about a heart that kept reaching for God, and a faith that refused to settle for less than it was capable of.

  • Worth is not what others can see. The world measured men by status and sight. Allah honoured the one it overlooked, because He looks at the heart turning toward Him.
  • Do not search for excuses to do less. Abdullah was excused, and still begged for a way in. Real love of God reaches for more, not for permission to stop.
  • How we treat the overlooked is a test. The Prophet ﷺ rose to greet him. His family served him for decades. The way we honour those society ignores says everything about us.
  • Eagerness matters more than ease. He could not fight, so he carried the flag and planted his feet. Sincerity finds a place to stand even when the body cannot.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Umm Maktum?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, a first cousin of Khadijah (RA), and the only companion known to have been born blind. He is remembered as the man for whom Surah Abasa was revealed, a muezzin of Madinah, and a martyr at the Battle of Qadisiyyah.

Why was the Qur'an revealed about him?

When he came eagerly to learn, the Prophet ﷺ briefly turned toward the leaders of Quraysh instead. Allah revealed the opening verses of Surah Abasa, teaching that a sincere seeker matters more than a powerful but indifferent one. A second revelation, in Surah An-Nisa, answered his prayer to be excused from battle because of his blindness.

What roles did he hold in Madinah?

He was among the first two sent to teach the Qur'an in Madinah, was made a caller to prayer alongside Bilal, led the people in prayer, and was left in charge of the city around thirteen times when the Prophet ﷺ was away.

What can we learn from his life?

That a person's worth is not in what others can see, that sincere faith reaches for more rather than less, and that how we treat the people society overlooks is a measure of our own hearts.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to assume that the people who matter most are the ones everyone notices: the strong, the influential, the ones with a seat at the table. Abdullah's life turns that quietly on its head. The room was full of powerful men, and Heaven spoke for the blind one rushing in from the edge.

Most of us know what it is to feel overlooked, or to be the one doing the overlooking. His story asks something gentle of both. Look again at the person you might have passed over. And when you feel you have an excuse to do less for God, remember a man who was genuinely excused, and who still planted his feet in the ground and refused to be moved. Carry one of those into your day, and his story has done its work.
