The Companions

Bilal ibn Rabah

The Voice of Certainty

A young man lay pinned to the burning sand of Makkah, a rock too heavy for one man to lift pressed onto his chest. The men who put it there only wanted a single word back: the name of one of their idols. He had every reason to say it. He said something else instead.

This is the story of Bilal ibn Rabah (may Allah be pleased with him): born into slavery, broken in body but never in faith, and chosen by the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ to be the first voice to call the world to prayer.

Bilal ibn Rabah

The Voice of Certainty

Born into chains in Makkah

Bilal was born around the year 580, about ten years after the Year of the Elephant, which made him roughly a decade younger than the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. His mother, Hamamah, had been an Abyssinian princess, captured and brought into slavery in Makkah. His father, Rabah, was a black Arab who was himself enslaved.

So Bilal grew up owned by other men, in a city where status was decided by tribe and lineage. He was tall and dark, with hazel eyes and thick hair that, it is said, never greyed until the very end of his life. People remembered him as strikingly handsome and quietly strong.

It is mentioned that as a boy he watched his own mother beaten, and could do nothing. Picture the heart of a child who grows up seeing the people he loves treated as property. That was the world into which the message of one God would one day arrive.

He understood early that the only way to rise in such a place was to be useful, so he sharpened his skills until he became one of the favoured among the enslaved. Yet no skill could change his station. However valued he was, he remained owned, and he knew it. Long after, a companion would comfort a young black man by reminding him that three of the best people to walk the earth were black, and Bilal was the first name he spoke.

One word he would not give up

Bilal was among the favoured of the enslaved. He was intelligent and capable, and his master, Umayyah ibn Khalaf, was a wealthy custodian of the idols at the Kaaba. By the standards of Makkah, Bilal had found a tolerable place. Then he heard of the Prophet ﷺ and his call to worship God alone, and something in him answered at once.

He may not have heard it first from the Prophet ﷺ himself. He likely heard it from Umayyah complaining about this new message. However it reached him, Bilal believed, and he was counted among the first seven to declare their Islam openly. He had no clan to shield him, and his master decided to make an example of him.

Umayyah put a heavy collar on him, tied him by the wrists and ankles, and left him in the desert sun from morning until night with no water. Out of cruelty they would let a few drops fall on his chest, only to take them away. "Just say their names," Umayyah told him, "and this ends." Bilal answered with one word, again and again: Ahad, Ahad. One, One.

Ahad, One

The torture deepened. He was handed to the reckless youth of Taif to be assaulted in any way they wished. He was dragged through the streets of Makkah until his blood marked the stones. Still the answer did not change. A companion later said that of all those put in chains and tortured, Bilal was the one who never showed his pain. He had given himself away entirely for the sake of Allah.

It would have been simple to say the word and stop the suffering. Umayyah was not even demanding that Bilal curse the Prophet ﷺ, only that he name an idol. To Umayyah, this was a generous offer, and Bilal's refusal infuriated him. But Bilal would not. The simplicity of his answer was the whole of it: one God, and therefore one humanity, and therefore a dignity that no master could take from him.

Years later, when Umar ibn al-Khattab asked him why all he ever said was Ahad, Ahad, Bilal answered that he knew nothing else of Allah to say. He had no memorised prayers, no long list of names. He had one truth, and he held it with his life.

But there is also a kind of man who gives his life away to please God, and God is most compassionate to His servants.Quran 2:207

Bought for freedom

At last Umayyah ordered a great rock, one that took several men to carry, laid upon Bilal as he was stretched out on the sand. He said Ahad, Ahad until he lost consciousness. The story might have ended there, with Bilal as a martyr.

But Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) kept coming back, asking to buy him. Umayyah, a man who loved money, finally relented and sold him. As the deal was done, Umayyah sneered that he would have taken a single coin for such a worthless slave. Abu Bakr answered that he would have paid a hundred times the price.

That was the measure of the community Bilal was joining. They did not value him for his tribe or his skin or his former station. They valued him for his love of Allah, and for nothing else. When Quraysh later told the Prophet ﷺ to drive away the poor and the formerly enslaved if he wanted the leaders to sit with him, the answer came that he was never to push away those who call upon their Lord.

The first voice to call to prayer

In Madinah, when the words of the adhan were given through a dream, the Prophet ﷺ chose Bilal to be the first to call them out. It was not because of what Bilal symbolised. It was because the man who had said the testimony of faith under a crushing rock had earned the right to proclaim it from above the mosque. It was his certainty that won him the honour, not his past.

Bilal had a strong, beautiful voice. Before each call he would come to the Prophet ﷺ and ask permission, which meant five quiet meetings a day with the one he loved. He would say, "The prayer, O Messenger of Allah," and one of the gentlest replies in all the narrations would come back: "Comfort us with it, O Bilal."

His whole life began to turn around the Prophet ﷺ. He was the gatekeeper and the confidant. He woke the Prophet ﷺ for Fajr, set up his camp on journeys, guarded his solitude, and became the first treasurer of the young community. Once the Prophet ﷺ told him he had heard Bilal's footsteps ahead of him in Paradise, and asked what deed he hoped for most. Bilal answered that whenever he made wudu, by night or by day, he prayed with it.

Above the Kaaba

At Badr, Bilal saw the man who had tortured him for years. Umayyah had tried to ransom his way out, and Abdur-Rahman ibn Awf was shielding him as a captive. Bilal could not bear it. "I will not live if he lives," he cried, and called the others to him. The justice of that day was his, and Umayyah fell. By a quiet turn, Bilal would later marry Abdur-Rahman's sister, and the two men became family.

Then came the conquest of Makkah. The same city that once watched Bilal dragged bleeding through its streets now watched him climb onto the roof of the Kaaba, the idols cleared away, to call the adhan. Where he had once lain beneath a rock saying One, One, he now stood above the holiest house calling Allahu Akbar, Allahu Akbar. The scholars say this was harder on the hearts of the proud than the smashing of the idols themselves.

Some who watched made bitter remarks about a black man raised so high. According to the scholars, it was about this scene that a verse of the Quran was sent down, settling the matter of who is truly noble for all time.

People, We created you all from a single man and a single woman, and made you into races and tribes so that you should recognize one another. In God’s eyes, the most honoured of you are the ones most mindful of Him: God is all knowing, all aware.Quran 49:13

The call he could not finish

When the Prophet ﷺ passed away, the grief nearly undid him. His whole life had been arranged around that one presence. He stood to call the adhan, and when he reached the name of Muhammad ﷺ, he broke down and wept, and could not go on. All of Madinah wept with him. He came down and said he would never call the adhan for anyone again.

He asked Abu Bakr to send him out, because everywhere he looked in Madinah he saw the Prophet ﷺ: his smile, his warmth, his face. Abu Bakr let him go, and Bilal joined the campaigns toward Sham. He is said to have called the adhan only twice more: once in Jerusalem at the request of Umar, becoming the first to call it in all three of the holy sanctuaries, and once again in Madinah at the asking of the Prophet's grandsons. Both times, the companions wept, carried back to the days they had heard his voice.

Bilal died in Sham around the year 639, during a great plague that took many of the companions. As death neared, his wife cried out in grief. He told her not to grieve but to rejoice, for tomorrow he would meet his loved ones, Muhammad ﷺ and his companions. He had spent his life longing for that meeting, and he went to it at peace.

What Bilal's life teaches us

His story is remembered everywhere, yet its heart is small enough to hold: one truth, held without flinching, in a body that had every reason to give in.

  • Conviction is not loud, it is constant. Bilal did not argue or debate. Under torture he simply repeated what he knew to be true, and his steadiness outlasted his masters' cruelty.
  • You are not your past or your status. Born into slavery and insulted for his birth, Bilal was raised above the Kaaba for one reason: his certainty in Allah. What is owned, inherited, or assumed about you is not what counts.
  • Honour is measured by God, not by people. The men of Makkah did not write his name among their great ones. The Prophet ﷺ called him a leader of those who call to prayer, and heard his footsteps in Paradise.
  • Hold one thing well. Bilal knew almost nothing to say but Ahad, One. A single sincere truth, lived fully, can carry a whole life and reach the highest places.

Questions

Who was Bilal ibn Rabah?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, born into slavery in Makkah to an Abyssinian mother and a black Arab father. He was among the earliest Muslims, was tortured for his faith, and was freed by Abu Bakr. He became the first person to call the adhan.

Why was Bilal tortured?

He accepted Islam early and had no clan to protect him. His master, Umayyah ibn Khalaf, wanted to make an example of him. Bilal was left in the sun without water, beaten, and crushed under a heavy rock, yet he kept answering only with Ahad, Ahad: One, One.

Why is Bilal known as the first muadhin?

When the call to prayer was established in Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ chose Bilal to call it. He later became the first to call the adhan in Makkah and in Jerusalem as well, making him the first muadhin in all three of the holiest places in Islam.

What can we learn from the life of Bilal?

That conviction held quietly and constantly can outlast any pressure, that a person's worth is set by God and not by birth or status, and that one sincere truth, lived fully, can raise a person to great heights.

Why this story stays with us

We are not often asked to hold our faith under a rock in the desert. We are asked to hold it in smaller, quieter ways: in the words we refuse to say, in the prayer we get up for when no one is watching, in staying steady when it would be easier to give in. Bilal's certainty was not a single brave moment. It was the same word, repeated, until it became who he was.

His life invites a simple question into our own day. What is the one truth we would not trade, and are we living as though we believe it? When you next hear the adhan, remember the voice that first carried it, and the certainty underneath it, and let a little of that steadiness into your own heart.
