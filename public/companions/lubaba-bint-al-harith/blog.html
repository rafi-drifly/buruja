Lubaba bint al-Harith

The First Woman to Believe After Khadijah

She is in almost everyone's story, and almost no one tells hers. Pull the name of nearly any early Muslim from the records and somewhere in the family tree you find her: a sister-in-law of the Prophet ﷺ twice over, an aunt of Khalid ibn al-Walid, the mother of the great scholar Abdullah ibn Abbas.

This is the story of Lubaba bint al-Harith (may Allah be pleased with her), known to the community as Umm al-Fadl: the first woman to accept Islam after Khadijah, and a quiet pillar in the lives of the people we remember most.

The Companions

Lubaba bint al-Harith

The First Woman to Believe After Khadijah

A daughter of the desert

Lubaba came from a tribe called Banu Hilal, and she was sometimes called Lubaba al-Hilaliyya. It was a Bedouin tribe known for surviving the hardest conditions of the desert. They were skilled at growing crops where little would grow, and trusted to care for people's cattle, sheep, and camels.

Families would send their children to live among Banu Hilal so they could learn to handle rough circumstances. To have been raised among them was something you could be proud of. Some say this is where her nephew Khalid ibn al-Walid first learned the toughness that later defined him.

Lubaba carried the marks of that upbringing. She was strong and courageous, a woman with a presence that could unsettle even a man like Abu Lahab. She would marry al-Abbas, the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, and become, in time, the great aunt of the whole Muslim community.

The first to follow Khadijah

Above every honour carried in her family ties, Lubaba held one distinction she treasured most: she was the first woman to accept Islam after Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her). She even said she embraced Islam on the same day that Khadijah did.

She did not need to be argued into it. She knew the character of the Prophet ﷺ, she loved him, she admired him. As soon as she heard what had happened to him, she went straight to him and accepted his message. The scholar al-Dhahabi records that no woman is said to have believed before her except Khadijah, and Ibn Hajar notes the only possible exception is Fatima bint al-Khattab, the sister of Umar.

She had been close to Khadijah, and always spoke of her warmly. Through that bond, and through her marriage to al-Abbas, Lubaba became one of the few who could sit and learn from the Prophet ﷺ both in his own house and in the house of al-Arqam. In Makkah, that was a rare privilege.

Believing while the household held back

Her son Abdullah ibn Abbas later remembered his mother as someone who declared her faith openly from the start. He said that he and his mother were counted among the weak and vulnerable believers of Makkah, the ones who suffered for what they believed.

Her husband was a different matter. Al-Abbas wanted to follow the path of Abu Talib: the loyal uncle who protected the Prophet ﷺ out of family and tribal duty, while staying back from the faith itself. He would not openly accept Islam for nearly two decades after his wife.

So Lubaba prayed. She made dua that al-Abbas would come to Islam, and she made dua that he would give up riba, the lending practice he was known for. Her nature recoiled from it long before any verse forbade it. She kept urging him, gently and persistently, toward the religion of his nephew.

Born in the years of the boycott

When Quraysh boycotted the Prophet's clan, the whole family was made to suffer, whether they believed or not. They were cut off, left to go hungry, pressured to abandon the message so the cruelty would end. It was in those bitter days that Khadijah died, and that Abu Talib's strength gave way.

In the middle of all of it, Lubaba was carrying a child. During the boycott she gave birth to Abdullah ibn Abbas, the boy who would grow into the great scholar of this ummah. A blessing arriving in the hardest season is felt all the more, and the Prophet ﷺ loved him deeply.

When the baby was born, Lubaba asked for him to be brought to the Prophet ﷺ for the tahnik, the gentle rubbing of a date on the roof of the mouth. But there were no dates to be found in the boycott. So the Prophet ﷺ placed only his own saliva in the child's mouth. Mujahid said Abdullah was the only child with that distinction.

The woman who was not afraid

Years later came the Battle of Badr. Al-Abbas was forced out with the Makkans, though he had no wish to fight, and the Prophet ﷺ had told his companions to spare him and capture him rather than kill him. Word of the defeat reached Makkah before the captives did.

Abu Rafi, the freed slave of al-Abbas and a vulnerable believer, heard the news that huge unseen warriors had fought beside the Muslims and could not be touched. He cried out in joy that these were the angels. Abu Lahab, enraged, threw him to the ground and beat him until he bled.

Then Lubaba acted. She seized a tent pole and struck Abu Lahab across the head, splitting it open, and stood over him. "Do you think you can overpower him just because his master is away?" The blow was so severe that he could not rise. Within days, Abu Lahab was dead. The man the Quran names with his punishment met his end at the hand of this woman.

A ransom, and a verse

When al-Abbas was brought as a captive, the Prophet ﷺ would show him no favour. He told him to ransom himself like everyone else. Al-Abbas protested that he had no such money. The Prophet ﷺ then asked about the wealth he had secretly left with Umm al-Fadl, telling her to keep it for their sons if he did not return.

No one had known of that conversation but the two of them. Al-Abbas said, "I bear witness that you are the Messenger of Allah, for no one knew of this except myself and Umm al-Fadl." He ransomed himself with the wealth he had hidden away with her.

It was about these captives that the verse came down, telling the Prophet ﷺ what to say to the men held in his hands:

Prophet, tell those you have taken captive, 'If God knows of any good in your hearts, He will give you something better than what has been taken from you, and He will forgive you: God is forgiving and merciful.'Quran 8:70

Close to the Prophet to the end

After the Treaty of Hudaybiyya, al-Abbas finally accepted Islam openly, and the family moved to Madinah for the last years of the Prophet's life. There her sons stayed near him: Abdullah shadowing him, fetching his water, watching his night prayer, carrying so much of what we now know. Much of what we have about the farewell Hajj comes through her son al-Fadl, who rode behind the Prophet ﷺ.

On the day of Arafah, the companions did not know whether they should be fasting. The heat was heavy and they would not interrupt the Prophet's long dua to ask. Lubaba understood. She sent him a cup of milk. When he raised it and drank, the answer was clear to everyone, and they drank too. Her quiet wisdom had eased them out of their difficulty.

She also became the wet nurse of al-Husayn, the beloved grandson of the Prophet ﷺ, after she dreamed that a part of his flesh was in her home. Once, when she lightly smacked the crying infant, the Prophet ﷺ told her, "You have hurt me by hurting my son." Lubaba lived until the time of Uthman, and was buried in al-Baqi.

What Lubaba's life teaches us

Her name sits in the background of a hundred famous stories. What she shows us is how much good a steady, faithful person can carry without ever standing in the spotlight.

  • Believe the moment you know the truth. Lubaba did not wait to be persuaded or to see which way her household would lean. She knew his character, and she went straight to him.
  • Keep praying for the people you love. For nearly twenty years she made dua for her husband to find faith, and kept gently urging him. Patience with those close to us is its own kind of strength.
  • Courage is standing up when it is needed. She defended a beaten servant against a powerful man without a moment's hesitation. Quiet people can be the bravest in the room.
  • Small acts of wisdom serve everyone. A single cup of milk, offered at the right moment, settled a question for a whole crowd. Attentiveness to others is a quiet form of service.

Questions

Who was Lubaba bint al-Harith?

She was the wife of al-Abbas, the uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and the mother of Abdullah ibn Abbas. Known as Umm al-Fadl, she is remembered as the first woman to accept Islam after Khadijah (RA).

Why is she called the first woman to believe after Khadijah?

As soon as she heard about the Prophet's message she went to him and accepted Islam, saying she did so on the same day as Khadijah. Scholars record that no woman is known to have believed before her except Khadijah, with the possible exception of Fatima bint al-Khattab.

What is the story of Lubaba and Abu Lahab?

When Abu Lahab beat the believing servant Abu Rafi for celebrating the victory at Badr, Lubaba struck Abu Lahab on the head with a tent pole and stood over him. He never recovered and died within days.

What can we learn from the life of Lubaba?

To accept the truth without delay, to keep praying patiently for those we love, to find courage when it is needed, and to serve others through quiet, attentive wisdom.

Why this story stays with us

Lubaba is easy to overlook. She is the relative in the margin of the famous accounts, the one who hands over a cup of milk, the one who prays for years before anyone sees the answer. And yet so much of the good we inherit passed through her hands and her home.

Most of us will live that way too: not in the headlines, but in the steady work of believing early, praying for our families, standing up when someone is wronged, and noticing what the people around us quietly need. Her life is an invitation to take that work seriously. Carry one piece of it into your day, and her story has done its work.
