Abu Mahdhura

The Boy Who Mocked the Adhan

He was one of a handful of boys standing at the edge of a Muslim camp, laughing at the call to prayer. He did not know the man calling it, and he did not want to. He only knew the rumours his parents had passed down.

This is the story of Abu Mahdhura (may Allah be pleased with him): a young man who mocked the adhan, and was handed it to carry for the rest of his life.

The Companions

Abu Mahdhura

The Boy Who Mocked the Adhan

A name that was almost lost

We are not even certain of his first name. Some say it was Samura, some say Aws, some say Salama. What survived was his title, Abu Mahdhura, and the one thing he became known for.

He came from the area around Makkah and Taif, a region that the message of Islam had only just begun to reach. His own corner of the story begins in the worst possible way: as one of the people who could not stand the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

He was young. His dislike was not really his own. It was handed to him by his elders, who knew the Prophet ﷺ only as the man from Makkah who had come to cause them trouble, and then come back again. The boy had never heard the message. He had only heard the complaints.

It is worth pausing on that. He was not a hardened opponent who had weighed the truth and refused it. He was a child who had absorbed the mood of the adults around him. His heart had simply never been given a fair chance, and that, it turns out, is a very different thing from a heart that is closed.

The journey back from Taif

The Prophet ﷺ had lifted the siege of Taif and was making his way back toward Madinah. With him travelled a group of people who had escaped the city and come over to him, and among them was Abu Mahdhura.

It was a tense road. These were the same lands where ambushes were common, and a sneak attack could come at any moment. The Prophet ﷺ travelled fully armoured, a general as much as a messenger, alert to everything around him.

There is a quiet detail worth noticing here. The Prophet ﷺ never actually entered Taif. He had once been driven from it and stoned, and now he was leaving again without setting foot inside. He was not going back to the place that had hurt him. He was simply bringing people out of it, and moving on.

Even so, the prayer did not stop. When the time came, the Prophet ﷺ ordered the adhan to be called. And out at the edge of the camp stood a small group of youths who decided this was something to laugh at.

Caught laughing

Abu Mahdhura later described it plainly. As the adhan was called, the boys began to mock it from a distance, calling out the words back in a mocking way, laughing among themselves.

Then a stern voice cut across the camp. The Prophet ﷺ called them over. This was not the moment of someone wandering in to ask about faith. This was the opposite: a group of children who had not thought about the consequences, and had now been caught.

They came forward, all of them terrified, and stood before him. He told them to do again what they had been doing, to repeat the call. They glanced at each other and began, quietly this time, with none of the mockery left in their voices.

The one they pointed to

The Prophet ﷺ listened, then asked which of them had been the loudest in making fun of the call. Abu Mahdhura remembered the answer with a kind of rueful honesty: they all pointed to me.

It is a small, human detail that anyone who was ever a child will recognise. They had mocked together, but the moment there were consequences, the other nine pointed at the one beside them. There is a lesson in that alone: when fear arrives, friends will often hand you over to save themselves. The Prophet ﷺ let the nine go, and kept Abu Mahdhura.

Picture the scene from the boy's side. He was the one singled out, alone before a man he had just insulted, a man who was also a commander on a dangerous road. Think of what a general in that position could have done: a punishment, a captivity, something worse. He had every reason to expect the worst, and no reason at all to expect what actually came.

A hand on his head, and his heart

What came next was nothing he could have imagined. The Prophet ﷺ drew him closer and told him to call the adhan again, the way he had been calling it, out loud. He began low, then his voice rose and filled out, and it became clear the boy had a beautiful voice.

When he finished, the Prophet ﷺ handed him a small pouch of silver and smiled. A gift for you, he said. The boy who had braced for punishment now stood there stunned, silver in his hands.

Then the Prophet ﷺ placed his hand on the boy's forehead and made a quiet prayer for him. He moved his hand down to his chest, over his heart, and prayed for him again, and the boy watched him the whole time. He prayed that Allah would bless him, again and again.

Abu Mahdhura would say afterward that the Prophet ﷺ had been the most hated person in the world to him before that. By the time those hands lifted, he was the most beloved person in the world to him. The change did not come from an argument or a threat. It came from being treated with a gentleness he had done nothing to deserve.

He asked to be taught the call properly, and the Prophet ﷺ went through the whole adhan with him, line by line, until he had memorised it. The boy who had been mocking those words an hour earlier now wanted to get every one of them right.

Make me the muadhin of Makkah

What he said next took some nerve. This boy, who minutes earlier had been mocking the call, now asked the Prophet ﷺ to let him be the muadhin of Makkah. He had not yet prayed a single prayer.

The Prophet ﷺ agreed. The city had only just been opened, and a boy who had arrived hating him would leave carrying its call to prayer.

Before he had even prayed a single prayer, the Prophet ﷺ trusted him with the call of a whole city. He sent him back to where a leader had already been appointed over Makkah, and told that leader the call to prayer was now to be Abu Mahdhura's. From mocking it to owning it, in the space of a single afternoon.

In Makkah his voice became something people looked forward to. They would ask him to repeat the call just to hear it. He used a singer's voice for the sake of Allah, and he never gave up the post. As long as the Prophet ﷺ lived, Abu Mahdhura would not let anyone else take his place, calling all five prayers himself, afraid that if he stepped aside even once the honour might pass to someone else.

The hair he never cut

There was one part of him he would never touch again. The place on his head where the Prophet ﷺ had laid his hand, Abu Mahdhura refused to ever cut or even part. He trimmed everything else and left that alone, because the Prophet ﷺ had touched it and prayed for him there.

He lived a long life and taught students who carried his narrations forward. All eight of the reports that come from him are about the adhan, and nothing else. He had been given one task, and he kept to it. The way he learned the call, repeating certain phrases the way the Prophet ﷺ had taught him, became one of the recognised ways of giving the adhan, traced back through him to that road outside Taif.

The honour stayed in his family. Just as the key of the Kaaba remained with its keepers, the call of Makkah remained with the children of Abu Mahdhura, his sons and grandsons after him, for generations. For a long time, if you came to Makkah, the adhan you heard was given in his way, by his descendants.

He guarded it so fiercely that, late in life, when a new muadhin was sent during the time of Muawiya, he is said to have pushed the man into the well of Zamzam rather than give up his place. He even put his stubbornness into verse, swearing by his Lord and by the Prophet ﷺ that this call was his and no one would come between him and it. It is half funny and half moving: a man so in love with a single gift that he would not let it go for anything.

What Abu Mahdhura's life teaches us

His story turns on a single choice the Prophet ﷺ made: to look at a boy who showed no interest in being guided, and see what he could become.

  • See the potential before the person does. Abu Mahdhura showed no willingness to be guided. The Prophet ﷺ looked past the mockery, heard the voice, and saw a muadhin.
  • Gentleness disarms more than force. A pouch of silver, a hand on the head, a quiet prayer. The boy who expected punishment was won over completely.
  • Do not break a young person. He had every reason to crush a child who had insulted him. Instead he gave him a task worth owning, and the boy owned it for life.
  • A gift, once entrusted, is worth guarding. He was given the call to prayer and never let it go, holding it with a love that lasted the rest of his days.

Questions

Who was Abu Mahdhura?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ who became the muadhin of Makkah. He is counted among the two most famous callers to prayer alongside Bilal (RA). His given name is uncertain, but his title, Abu Mahdhura, is well known.

Why did Abu Mahdhura first dislike the Prophet?

He was young and had grown up among people in the region of Makkah and Taif who opposed the Prophet ﷺ. He had never heard the message itself, only the hostility passed down from his elders, so at first he mocked the call to prayer.

How did Abu Mahdhura become a muadhin?

After catching the boys mocking the adhan, the Prophet ﷺ had Abu Mahdhura repeat the call, heard his beautiful voice, gave him a gift, and prayed for him. The boy's heart changed completely, and he asked to be the muadhin of Makkah. The Prophet ﷺ taught him the full adhan and sent him to do it.

What can we learn from the life of Abu Mahdhura?

That potential can be seen in someone who shows none of it yet, that gentleness changes hearts more surely than punishment, and that a trust given with love can be carried faithfully for a lifetime.

Why this story stays with us

It would have been so easy to deal with that boy harshly. He had mocked something sacred, in front of everyone, on a dangerous road. No one would have blamed a tired commander for putting him in his place. Instead the Prophet ﷺ heard a gift in the very voice that had insulted him, and he handed that boy something to be proud of.

Most of us meet a difficult young person sooner or later, in our families, our work, our own homes. The instinct is to correct, to shame, to write them off. This story asks for the harder thing: to look for the good buried under the behaviour, and to trust someone with it before they have earned it. There is also a quieter invitation in it for us. The next time the adhan reaches you, remember that the call you are hearing was once carried by a boy who began by laughing at it, and was changed by a single act of kindness. A single act of mercy turned a mocker into the man who called Makkah to prayer for the rest of his life. Carry that patience into one difficult moment today, and his story has done its work.
