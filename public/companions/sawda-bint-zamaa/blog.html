Sawda bint Zama'a

The One Who Brought Joy

She is one of the most overlooked of the Mothers of the Believers. Her students could not always remember her name, and even the year of her death is uncertain. It is almost as if she wanted it that way.

This is the story of Sawda bint Zama'a (may Allah be pleased with her): one of the earliest to believe, the wife who came to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in his deepest grief, and the woman everyone remembered for one thing above all, the joy she brought wherever she went.

Sawda bint Zama'a

The One Who Brought Joy

An early believer in a divided family

Sawda came from a powerful clan of Quraysh, with family ties in both Makkah and Madinah. Through her father she was related to Khadijah, and through her mother she had a link to the Prophet ﷺ as well. Her father, Zama'a, was an elderly, blind man. These connections meant that when she embraced Islam, she would be shielded from the worst of the physical persecution that others suffered.

She and her husband, Sakran ibn Amr, were among the very first batch of Muslims. They believed in the days of Dar al-Arqam, the early house where the Prophet ﷺ taught in secret, and they were disowned almost at once. We are told little about that early time, only that they were among the earliest to believe and that they paid for it.

Her family was split by the new faith. One brother, Malik, believed early and migrated for the sake of Allah, making two migrations in his lifetime. Another brother, Abd, opposed Islam fiercely at first, then accepted it years later and looked back with regret on his early opposition. Her husband's brothers, too, were divided, some believing early and some only much later. In time, all of them died upon Islam.

Two migrations and a quiet dream

When persecution grew heavy, Sawda and Sakran left everything and migrated to Abyssinia. There, far from home, they had a son, Abd al-Rahman. He would one day grow into a brave companion and warrior, and die as a martyr in the path of Allah at the Battle of Jalula.

While they were still in that distant land, Sawda saw a dream. The Prophet ﷺ came to her and knelt beside her. She told her husband, puzzled, and his answer startled her. If your dream is true, he said, then I will die soon and the Prophet ﷺ will marry you. The next night she dreamt the moon descended into her home and settled beside her, and he gave the same gentle reading.

Nothing seemed to point to it. They were healthy, together, with a child, and a whole ocean away from Makkah. Then Sakran died in Abyssinia, one of the earliest companions, leaving Sawda a widow in a strange land.

A widow waiting in Makkah

Sawda waited for a safe caravan and made her way back to Makkah. She returned to a hard life: now in her fifties, with an aged and blind father, a young son, and other children to care for, and a family that had already turned away from her.

She was a convert and a widow, settled almost as an outcast among her own people, though her tribal ties spared her from open punishment. She was not considered marriageable, and there seemed no end to the waiting in sight.

Quietly, she began to plan out the rest of her life. She expected to grow old as a widow, to raise what was left of her children, and to seek her reward with Allah. She asked for nothing more.

It would have been easy, in that situation, to grow bitter. She had given up her home for her faith, buried her husband far away, and come back to a city that no longer welcomed her. Yet nothing in her story suggests resentment. She simply carried on, and kept her warmth.

Joy brought into the year of grief

Meanwhile, the Prophet ﷺ had entered the hardest season of his life. He had lost Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), the love of his life, and his uncle Abu Talib within days of each other. He had been rejected at Ta'if. They call it the Year of Grief, and he carried that grief for months, alone with two young daughters at home.

A trusted matchmaker named Khawla bint Hakim came to him and asked, gently, about the grief she saw in him since Khadijah's death. She suggested two people: young Aisha, who would not move in for some years, and Sawda bint Zama'a. Why Sawda? Because she was one of the first to believe, she had suffered greatly for it, and she carried a warm, easygoing, humorous spirit that brought joy to everyone around her.

She was a woman who never argued, never quarreled, and got along with everyone. In a house heavy with sorrow, this was exactly what was needed. When Khawla brought the proposal, Sawda was astonished, and she accepted with all her heart. Her old father called it a noble match, and the marriage was made.

Three years as his only companion

For three years, Sawda was the only wife of the Prophet ﷺ. It is striking that a woman so unknown to many of us today held such a close place in his life. She was his wife at the time of the Night Journey. She was the only wife living with him when he migrated to Madinah, and the first to have her home beside him there. So much history sits quietly between them.

There was history with her relatives, too. At the Battle of Badr, her former brother-in-law Suhail was taken prisoner and brought to her house. Seeing him humiliated, she scolded him for surrendering instead of fighting to the death, forgetting for a moment that he had come to fight the Muslims. The Prophet ﷺ heard her and asked, gently, whether she was urging him on against Allah and His Messenger. She apologized at once, and he smiled at her to put her at ease.

Her humor is what people remembered most. One day she saw him in distress and wanted to lift him. She told him she had prayed behind him the night before and that his bowing was so long she had to hold her nose, afraid it might start to bleed. He laughed until the sadness lifted from his face.

Another time Aisha had cooked a dish, and when Sawda would not eat it, the two of them ended up gently smearing food on each other's faces, with the Prophet ﷺ smiling and the whole house laughing. There was even a night they spoke so much about frightening matters that Sawda hid in a corner of the room, and again the house filled with laughter. These small, warm moments show a tender, human side of his household that is easy to forget.

An older sister to Aisha

When Aisha came of age and joined the household, she did not see Sawda as a rival. She looked to her as an older sister, and in some ways as a mother and a role model. Sawda nurtured that bond with care.

Aisha later said there was no one whose company she loved more, and whose example she more wished to follow, than Sawda. She spoke of a firmness and strength of resolve in her that she wanted to imitate. The two of them became close friends.

Sawda also lived long into old age, and her body slowed. On the farewell Hajj, because she could not move quickly through the crowds, she asked the Prophet ﷺ for permission to leave Muzdalifah early. He granted it, and that small permission for an elderly woman has eased countless pilgrims ever since. After that, she never made Hajj again.

Generous in life, selfless in death

After her laughter, Sawda is remembered most for her generosity. Aisha said she was the most generous of people. Whatever came to her, a gift or her own allotted share, she gave it away at once in charity. Once she was sent a whole bucket of coins, and she did not even pause to look at it before passing it on. She would prepare what she had and send it as gifts to young women who were getting married.

As she grew old, she went to the Prophet ﷺ and told him she no longer sought the life of a wife in that way, but she asked, with love, to be gathered among his wives on the Day of Judgement. So she gave her night to Aisha, whom she loved, seeking the pleasure of the Prophet ﷺ and that honored place in the Hereafter.

She withdrew almost entirely from public life. She narrated only five hadith, took no part in the disputes of her time, and rarely left her home. When she died, she asked that her room be merged with Aisha's, to give her more space. The companion Ibn Abbas was so moved at the news of her passing that he fell into prostration, calling the loss of a wife of the Prophet ﷺ a sign worth bowing before. There was not a single complaint about her. Everyone remembered only her loving, generous spirit.

What Sawda's life teaches us

Her story is not built on famous battles or long lists of narrations. It is built on a warmth that made other people's burdens lighter, and that is why it stays with us.

  • Bringing joy is a real act of service. Sawda lifted a grieving heart with gentle humor. Making the people around you laugh and feel at ease is not a small thing. It is mercy.
  • Give without keeping count. Whatever reached her she passed on at once, often without even looking. Real generosity holds things loosely and asks for nothing back.
  • A quiet, private life can be a great one. She sought no fame and left almost no trace by choice. A life lived sincerely for Allah needs no audience to have weight.
  • Love can make room for others. She gave her night to Aisha and asked for her room to be joined to hers. Her love was the kind that made space rather than competing for it.

Questions

Who was Sawda bint Zama'a?

She was one of the earliest Muslims and one of the Mothers of the Believers. After being widowed, she became the wife of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, the first woman he married after Khadijah (RA), and for three years she was his only wife.

Why is Sawda remembered for joy?

She married the Prophet ﷺ during the Year of Grief, after he had lost Khadijah and Abu Talib. She was known for her warm humor and easygoing spirit, and she made him laugh when he was weighed down by sorrow.

Why did Sawda give her night to Aisha?

In her old age she told the Prophet ﷺ she no longer sought that part of married life, but she wished to remain his wife in the Hereafter. So she gave her night to Aisha, whom she loved, while keeping her honored place as his wife.

What can we learn from the life of Sawda?

That bringing joy to others is real service, that true generosity gives without counting, and that a quiet, sincere life lived for Allah carries its own great weight.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never be remembered by crowds. We live in our homes, among a few people who depend on us, doing small things that no one records. Sawda's life says that this is enough, and that it can be beautiful. She lifted a grieving heart, gave away whatever reached her hands, and asked for nothing in return.

Her story is an invitation to be the person who makes a room lighter, who gives quietly, who loves in a way that makes space for others. We often think our faith must be proven in some great public moment. Sawda's life answers softly that it is proven instead in how we treat the people closest to us. Carry one of those into your own day, into the people at your table and the moments only Allah sees, and her example has done its work.
