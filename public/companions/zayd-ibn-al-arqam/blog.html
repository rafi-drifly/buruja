The Companions

Zayd ibn al-Arqam

The Truthful Witness

He was young enough that the Prophet ﷺ once turned him away from the battlefield for being too small to fight. He was an orphan, with no great wealth and no famous father of his own. And yet, when he reported a few quiet words he had overheard, the answer came down from the heavens.

This is the story of Zayd ibn al-Arqam (may Allah be pleased with him): an orphan of the Ansar, raised by one of the most beloved companions, whose truthfulness was confirmed in the Qur'an itself.

Zayd ibn al-Arqam

The Truthful Witness

An orphan in a noble house

Zayd ibn al-Arqam came from the Ansar of Madinah, from the Khazraj, one of the elite tribes of the city. He took his name from his grandfather, al-Arqam, a senior figure among his people. But his own beginning was hard: his father died while he was still a child, and Zayd grew up an orphan.

He was not left alone. He was raised in the house of Abdullah ibn Rawahah (may Allah be pleased with him), one of the first handful of people to bring Islam to Madinah and one of the most beloved companions of the Prophet ﷺ. Abdullah was a poet, a scribe, and a man who fought in every battle and fasted when others did not.

So righteous was he that his own brother once prayed to be spared any sin that would shame him before Abdullah after death. This was the man who became a father to Zayd. And much of what made Abdullah remarkable, his learning, his courage, his love of the Prophet ﷺ, would pass quietly to the boy in his care.

There is a second lesson folded into this one. Abdullah ibn Rawahah already had a long record with the Prophet ﷺ and a high station among the believers. He was also a man who took in an orphan and raised him, and that orphan grew up to matter in the history of Islam. The good a person does for a child can outlast them in ways they never see.

Too young for the front line

Because he was raised in the family of Abdullah ibn Rawahah, Zayd was counted among the early believers of Madinah from the very start. He learned to read and write. He grew into someone with a natural pull toward knowledge, and a clear wish to stand and fight alongside the Prophet ﷺ as the man who raised him did.

When the Battle of Badr came, Zayd offered himself. But the Prophet ﷺ sent him back with a small group of boys who were judged too young to fight that day. It must have stung. Yet the Prophet ﷺ did not simply dismiss them. He gave them a task: take up your arms and guard the city, the women and the children of Madinah.

Zayd would later count it carefully. The Prophet ﷺ went out on nineteen expeditions, and Zayd was at his side for seventeen of them. He missed only Badr, and one other. His first real campaign would also become the most famous chapter of his life.

The words he could not ignore

It happened on the campaign of Banu al-Mustaliq. On the way back, two men quarrelled at a well over water, one from Makkah and one from Madinah. The chief of the hypocrites, Abdullah ibn Ubayy ibn Salul, saw his opening. He had once expected to rule Madinah, and he never forgave the Prophet ﷺ for the city's turning to Islam instead.

It was an old grievance for him. He liked to tell people that Madinah had been at peace until the Makkans arrived and brought their troubles with them. If you fatten up a dog, he would say, it will turn and bite you. So when the two men clashed at the well, he reached for the same poison again.

He spoke bitter words to those around him. Do not spend on the people who are with the Messenger of Allah, he said, until they leave him. And then, of himself: the honoured one will surely drive out the humiliated one, meaning he would expel the Prophet ﷺ from Madinah.

Zayd was sitting close by, and he heard every word. He was young, but he understood exactly what he had heard: not a complaint, but treason. He told Ibn Ubayy plainly that he would report it to the Prophet ﷺ. The man dismissed him as a boy. Zayd answered that even if his own father had said such a thing, he would still report it.

They are the ones who say, 'Give nothing to those who follow God's Messenger, until they abandon him', but to God belong the treasures of the heavens and earth, though the hypocrites do not understand this.Qur'an 63:7

Confirmed from above the seven heavens

Zayd went to the Prophet ﷺ and told him everything. But when Ibn Ubayy was questioned, he swore by Allah that he had said nothing of the kind, and his standing among the Ansar gave his oath weight. The Prophet ﷺ took him at his word, and Zayd was left looking like a liar.

It crushed him. A young man who had tried to do the right thing now sat at home, weighed down by grief, while even his own family scolded him for the trouble he seemed to have caused. He could do only one thing: he turned to Allah and prayed to be cleared.

Then the Prophet ﷺ came to him, took him gently by the ear, and smiled into his face. It was not a laugh out loud; it was that quiet, reassuring smile. Soon after, the opening verses of Surah al-Munafiqun were revealed, naming the hypocrites as the liars and confirming, word for word, what Zayd had reported. The Prophet ﷺ said to him: Allah has confirmed what you said.

Ibn Ubayy was not punished. The Prophet ﷺ had refused even to let him be killed, saying he did not want people to claim that Muhammad kills his own companions. He left him to sink instead, lower and lower in the eyes of the people. It was the orphan boy, not the chief, whom Allah had honoured.

They say, 'Once we return to Medina the powerful will drive out the weak,' but power belongs to God, to His Messenger, and to the believers, though the hypocrites do not know this.Qur'an 63:8

A keeper of the Prophet's words

Zayd lived a long life, and he carried the memory of the Prophet ﷺ into the years that followed. Because he saw the religion take shape over time, he could explain how things once were. He remembered when people still spoke during the prayer, greeting one another, until the practice was forbidden and the believers learned to stand in silent devotion.

He narrated some of the supplications Muslims still say today: the words to seek refuge when entering the place where one relieves oneself, and a long, beautiful du'a the Prophet ﷺ taught with great emphasis. In it, he sought refuge from incapacity and laziness, from miserliness and cowardice, from a soul that is never satisfied, a heart that is not humble, knowledge of no benefit, and a prayer that is not answered.

He also remembered the Prophet ﷺ stopping during a walk to comfort the companions about the Day of Judgement, telling them they were not even a fraction of the vast multitude who would come to him at his fountain. Through Zayd, close to a hundred of the Prophet's sayings reached the ummah.

Take care to do your prayers, praying in the best way, and stand before God in devotion.Qur'an 2:238

Blindness, and a vision returned

Zayd was at the Battle of Mu'tah, carried on the back of the saddle by Abdullah ibn Rawahah, the man who had raised him. There he watched that father figure ride forward into the fighting and fall as a martyr. It was a deep grief, for him and for all of Madinah.

Near the end of the Prophet's life, another trial came: Zayd lost his sight. The Prophet ﷺ visited him and asked what he would do if he stayed blind for the rest of his days. Zayd gave the answer of a believer. I will be patient, he said, and seek my reward from Allah. The Prophet ﷺ told him that if he did so, he would enter Paradise with no sin upon him.

Then something quiet and remarkable happened. After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Allah restored Zayd's sight. He had been ready to bear the loss for life and to be rewarded for it. The lesson stayed: the one who resolves to be patient in hardship keeps that reward, even if the hardship is later lifted.

A scholar to the end

After the Prophet ﷺ, Zayd became a man people came to for knowledge. When Ibn Abbas was asked about a question of currency exchange, he sent the questioner to Zayd, saying he was more knowledgeable and better than himself. Zayd settled in Kufa and taught in its mosque, and some of the great scholars of the next generation learned at his feet.

He loved the family of the Prophet ﷺ and narrated their virtues, and when discord broke out among the Muslims, he held to what he had heard directly from the Prophet ﷺ. He was among those who remembered the Prophet ﷺ saying that whoever held the Prophet ﷺ as a close and beloved friend, Ali too was a close friend to him. He carried no taste for that strife.

When tragedy struck the people of Madinah, he wrote to console them with glad tidings he had heard from the Prophet ﷺ: that Allah would forgive the Ansar, and the children of the Ansar, and the children of their children. Even in grief, his instinct was to reach for something the Prophet ﷺ had said, and to pass on comfort rather than bitterness.

In old age, when people asked him to narrate, he began to hold back. We have grown old, he said, and to speak on behalf of the Prophet ﷺ is a heavy thing. He would not risk a single word. He died a quiet death in Kufa, an orphan who had been raised in faith and lived his whole life inside it.

What Zayd's life teaches us

His story is not built on rank or wealth. It is built on telling the truth when it was costly, and staying patient when it was hard.

  • Tell the truth even when no one believes you. Zayd reported what he heard, was called a liar, and stayed silent in his grief. He left the vindication to Allah, and it came.
  • Standing is not the only way to serve. Turned away from Badr for being too young, he took up the quieter task of guarding the city. Faithfulness shows up wherever you are placed.
  • Resolve to be patient, and the reward is already yours. He decided to bear the loss of his sight for life. The intention to be patient in trial is rewarded, even if the trial is later lifted.
  • Be careful with the truth you carry. In old age he held back rather than risk misquoting the Prophet ﷺ. Some words are too weighty to repeat unless you are sure.

Questions

Who was Zayd ibn al-Arqam?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah. Orphaned young and raised by Abdullah ibn Rawahah, he became a narrator of close to a hundred hadith and is best known for the words he overheard from the hypocrites, which Allah confirmed in the Qur'an.

Why is Zayd ibn al-Arqam linked to Surah al-Munafiqun?

On the campaign of Banu al-Mustaliq, Zayd overheard the chief hypocrite Abdullah ibn Ubayy threaten to drive the Prophet ﷺ out of Madinah. When he reported it and was disbelieved, the opening verses of Surah al-Munafiqun were revealed, confirming exactly what Zayd had said.

Did Zayd ibn al-Arqam fight at Badr?

No. He was judged too young to fight at Badr and was sent back, along with other boys, to help guard Madinah. He later took part in seventeen of the Prophet's nineteen expeditions, beginning with the campaign that followed.

What can we learn from the life of Zayd ibn al-Arqam?

To tell the truth even when it is costly, to serve faithfully wherever you are placed, and to meet hardship with patience while trusting Allah with the outcome.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never stand before crowds or change the course of history. Zayd was a boy with no fortune and no famous name, told he was too young, then told he was a liar. What he had was the willingness to say a true thing out loud and then leave the rest to Allah. That is within reach of anyone.

There is a particular ache in being doubted when you know you are right, and a particular temptation to defend yourself loudly or give up the truth altogether. Zayd did neither. He sat with his grief, kept his integrity, and prayed. Carry that into an ordinary day, the quiet honesty and the patience behind it, and his story has done its work.
