As'ad ibn Zurara

The First of Madinah

He was perhaps nineteen or twenty years old. He had no long biography, no books of sayings, and no son to carry his name. He died before the first mosque was even finished.

Yet without him, there may have been no Madinah waiting for the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ at all. This is the story of As'ad ibn Zurara (may Allah be pleased with him): the first of his city to believe, and the one who quietly prepared the ground for everything that came after.

The Companions

As'ad ibn Zurara

The First of Madinah

A young chief in a tired city

As'ad ibn Zurara was from Banu Najjar, one of the largest sub-tribes of the Khazraj in Yathrib, the town that would later be called Madinah. His father, Zurara, had died before Islam. His mother, Suad bint Rafi, was from the same tribe.

The people of Yathrib were not like the people of Makkah. Their fathers had worn themselves out in long wars between the two main tribes, the Aws and the Khazraj, and most of the older chiefs had been killed in the fighting. As'ad was not an old man. None of the leaders were. He rose because of who he was: known for his wisdom, his gentle manner, and his good character. He was not hot-headed. He looked for peace and for reconciliation.

He had little attachment to the idols of his people. Some accounts even suggest he leaned toward the worship of one God before Islam, though that is not certain. What is clear is that he had no deep loyalty to the ways of his forefathers. Through his dealings with the Jewish tribes who had settled near Yathrib, he kept hearing them speak of a prophet who was soon to come. The word stayed with him.

He became the chief of Banu Najjar about ten years after the Prophet ﷺ first received revelation. That made him the spokesman for one of the largest sub-tribes in the city, and by extension one of the most respected young men in all of Yathrib. There is a tie here worth noting: the Prophet's own great-grandmother, Salma, was from Banu Najjar. When he later called them his maternal uncles, this was the bond he meant.

An insult that planted a seed

The first time As'ad heard of the Prophet ﷺ, it was not from a believer. He had travelled to Makkah with another man to settle a dispute, and he sought the help of Utbah ibn Rabi'ah, one of the fiercest opponents of the new message.

Utbah spoke of the Prophet ﷺ with scorn. But As'ad listened more carefully than Utbah intended. Behind the mockery he could make out what this man in Makkah was actually calling to: the oneness of God, an extension of the pure way of Ibrahim.

It resembled what those Jewish tribes back home had been waiting for. Sometimes people who set out to turn others away from the truth end up pointing them toward it instead. A seed had been planted, and As'ad carried it back to Yathrib.

Let us be the first to reach him

During the days of Hajj, the Prophet ﷺ would move among the visitors looking for anyone who would listen. He came upon six young men from Yathrib, As'ad among them. He asked if they were the allies of the Jews of Madinah, and when they said yes, he invited them to sit and hear him.

Their answer is one of the most beautiful in the whole story. They turned to one another and said: let us hasten and reach this prophet before the others do. We knew a prophet was coming. Let us be the first to embrace him.

Among six young men, there is always one the rest look to. As'ad was the youngest, yet he was the one they trusted. He spoke what they were all feeling. He was the first of them to extend his hand and his belief to the Prophet ﷺ, and the others followed.

The pledge that severed every other tie

Those six became twelve. The twelve took the first pledge of Aqaba with the Prophet ﷺ and asked him to send someone back with them to teach Islam. He sent Mus'ab ibn Umayr. Together, As'ad and Mus'ab went from house to house in Yathrib. As'ad opened the doors of the city, calmed its leaders, and made the way for Mus'ab to teach, and Islam entered home after home. Some accepted and some kept to their old ways, but the message reached them all.

When they returned the next year, they were seventy. At the second pledge of Aqaba, the spokesman of the Ansar was As'ad himself. He stood and made the cost plain to his people: take this man in, and every tie you have with the Arabs of Makkah is cut. They will all come against you. You may lose everything of this world.

If you mean to walk away, he told them, walk away now, for that is better before Allah. Do not take this pledge and then forsake your prophet. And what do we gain in return, they asked. Paradise, he said. Then he was the first to put his hand in the Prophet's hand, and his people followed him again.

Think of how much rested on the shoulders of a man barely twenty. On that day the Prophet ﷺ chose twelve leaders from among the seventy, and As'ad was appointed the leader of Banu Najjar.

The first to break the idols, the first to gather for Friday

Back in Yathrib there was hesitation. The idols still stood. No one wanted to be the first to touch them. As'ad went to his own people and broke their idols, knowing that if anyone would face the worst of the consequences, it would be him. When nothing struck them down, the other chiefs took courage and did the same.

Then came something the Muslims of Makkah could not yet do openly. The Jewish tribes kept Saturday and the Christians kept Sunday. As'ad was the first to gather the people on Friday, week after week, to remember Allah and to pray together behind Mus'ab. This was before Friday prayer was even formally commanded, and before the call to prayer existed.

They were about forty men, meeting in an open stretch of land, and Mus'ab would lead them in prayer. Years later, scholars would point back to those forty when they spoke of the smallest number for which a Friday gathering could be held. A whole practice of the faith traces, in part, to a single young man who would not wait.

He prepared the city, then he was gone

When the Prophet ﷺ finally made his way into Madinah, he let his camel choose, saying Allah would guide it to its place. It knelt on a plot of land held in trust for two orphan boys. The boys offered the land as a gift, but the Prophet ﷺ refused to take it for nothing and paid them a generous price. That same land was where As'ad had been gathering the people for Friday before the Prophet ﷺ ever arrived. On it the Prophet's mosque would be built.

There is a quiet scene that captures what this meant. A blind old man named Khabib was led to the prayer by his son. Every time he heard the call for Jumu'ah, he would say: O Allah, forgive As'ad. O Allah, have mercy on As'ad. His son had never met As'ad and did not understand. The father explained: the first to gather us for Friday, the first to lead us in it, was As'ad ibn Zurara.

As'ad did not live to see the city he had prepared. While the mosque was still being built, a severe illness took hold of his throat. He died in the very first year of the Hijrah, before the first Ramadan in Madinah, before so much of what he had made possible.

We do not have a single confirmed saying from him. The man who could have told us how all of it began was the one who passed away first, with his work standing unfinished around him.

What the Prophet ﷺ gave back

The Prophet ﷺ washed As'ad himself, wrapped him in three garments, and prayed over him. Many scholars say this was the first funeral prayer in Islam, and certainly the first in Madinah. As'ad was the first to be buried in al-Baqi, the cemetery that would one day hold so many of the family and companions of the Prophet ﷺ.

As'ad had no sons, only three young daughters. The Prophet ﷺ took them into his own household, raised them among his wives, cared for them until they were of age, and saw them married. This was how he answered the loyalty of one who had given so much.

When the people of Banu Najjar came asking who would now lead them, they had no mechanism yet to choose a successor. So they asked the Prophet ﷺ to appoint one. He said: I will be your leader. Some scholars read this as the highest praise of As'ad, that no one could simply step into his place, and so the Prophet ﷺ himself stood in it.

This was the loyalty of the Prophet ﷺ. If you did something for him and you lived, he would point you out and praise you. And if you died and people began to forget you, he would revive your mention. As'ad died early and almost silent, but he was not forgotten. May Allah be pleased with him, forgive him, and gather us with him.

What As'ad's life teaches us

His name is barely a few lines in the books, yet the whole of Madinah rests on what he quietly built. That is the heart of his story.

  • Be the first to move toward good. When others hesitated, As'ad did not wait to be sure it was safe. He reached for the truth, broke the first idol, and gathered the first prayer.
  • Quiet groundwork outlasts the spotlight. He prepared a whole city and died before he could enjoy any of it. The work mattered far more than being seen to do it.
  • Youth is no excuse to stay small. Barely twenty, he carried his people on his shoulders. Age was never the measure of what he could give.
  • Loyalty is remembered, even when we forget it. An old man kept praying for As'ad long after others moved on. Good that is done sincerely is not lost, even when its doer is gone.

Questions

Who was As'ad ibn Zurara?

A young chief of Banu Najjar in Yathrib (Madinah) and the first person of his city to accept Islam. He led the early believers there, gathered the first Friday prayer, and prepared the ground for the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ before the Hijrah.

Why is As'ad ibn Zurara remembered as a first?

He was among the first six of Madinah to believe and the one who spoke for them, the spokesman at the second pledge of Aqaba, the first to break the idols of his people, the first to gather the people for Friday prayer, and the first to be buried in al-Baqi.

How did As'ad ibn Zurara die?

He fell severely ill with an ailment of the throat while the Prophet's mosque was still being built, and he died in the first year of the Hijrah, before the first Ramadan in Madinah. The Prophet ﷺ washed him, prayed over him, and buried him.

What can we learn from the life of As'ad ibn Zurara?

To be the first to move toward what is right, to do quiet groundwork without needing to be seen, and to give fully even when young. His loyalty was remembered long after his death.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to measure a life by what we get to see finished. As'ad measured his by what he could begin. He believed early, spoke when speaking was costly, broke the first idol, gathered the first prayer, and then died before the mosque was even complete. He never saw the city he made possible.

Most of us will plant things we do not live to harvest: in our families, our communities, the people we quietly help. His life is an invitation to begin the good thing now, to be the first to move, and to trust that sincere work is never wasted, even when no one remembers our name. Carry that into today, and his story has done its work.
