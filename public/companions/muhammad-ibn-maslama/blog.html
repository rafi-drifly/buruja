Muhammad ibn Maslama

The Knight of the Prophet

There may be no other companion quite like him. He carried the name Muhammad before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ received revelation. It was a rare name among the Arabs, and that one fact tells you most of his story: a man so easily overshadowed that history almost lost him.

This is the story of Muhammad ibn Maslama (may Allah be pleased with him): the knight of the Prophet ﷺ, the watchman who guarded Madinah through every danger, and the man who broke his own sword when the danger turned out to be other Muslims.

The Companions

Muhammad ibn Maslama

The Knight of the Prophet

A man of few words

He was from the tribe of Aus in Yathrib, the city that would become Madinah. He accepted Islam early, at the hands of Mus'ab ibn Umayr, the teacher the Prophet ﷺ had sent ahead. He was among the very first of the Ansar, believing before even some of the great names of his own people.

He was eighteen years younger than the Prophet ﷺ. He was tall, very strong, and dark-skinned, counted among the black companions in the early collections. People who saw him were struck by his presence. He had a quiet dignity about him, and others felt shy to speak in front of him.

Above all, he spoke only when he had to. He was a man of few words and great actions. That habit cost him in the records, because history remembers the talkers, and he was not one. But it did not cost him with the people who knew him, and it did not cost him with his Prophet ﷺ.

The watchman

When the Prophet ﷺ reached Madinah, he gave this quiet man a standing assignment: to watch. Muhammad ibn Maslama became the perpetual guard, the eye on the borders, the one who patrolled the city while others slept.

He was made for it. In battle he was skilled on horse and on foot, and they said they never once saw him take a step back. He could read a battlefield before it began, count the enemy, and sense who would do what. So the Prophet ﷺ would send him to study a situation and report back honestly.

He attended every battle alongside the Prophet ﷺ except one. He missed Tabuk, and the reason itself is the honour: the Prophet ﷺ left him in charge of Madinah while the army was away. He used to tell his children to ask him about any battle they wished, and he would recount it in full, every battle except Tabuk, for he had been guarding the city.

There is a quiet teaching in this. The Prophet ﷺ praised the eye that weeps from the fear of Allah and the eye that keeps watch over the borders of the Muslims, and said neither will be touched by the Fire. Every saying about the one who holds the fort was sweetness to his ears, because that was the life he was given, and he never once asked for a different one.

The trustworthy one

The Prophet ﷺ liked to pair people of similar character, and he paired Muhammad ibn Maslama with Abu Ubaidah ibn al-Jarrah, the trustworthy one of this ummah. If you laid their lives side by side, you would think you were reading about the same man. Two trustworthy souls in one circle, both sent to read a situation, both trusted for their piety.

This is the heart of who he was. He was safe from hypocrisy, and that is why the Prophet ﷺ trusted him in ways he did not trust others. He accepted the role he was given and never tried to push past it into something else. He knew what Allah had made him for, and he stayed there.

He brought his whole family to Islam: his wives, his sixteen children, his brothers, his sister. He named his children after the early emigrants he admired, which tells you plainly what he loved. His brother Mahmud also believed, and would be the first to fall at Khaybar, a brother Muhammad ibn Maslama would later avenge in a duel so fierce the narrators say the fighters cut down the trees around them.

The hardest order

There was a man in Madinah named Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf: a poet, a warlord, and very rich. From the day the Prophet ﷺ arrived, he worked to wreck the peace. He wrote ugly verses about the Muslims, and after the defeat at Badr he rode to Makkah and urged its leaders to rise and attack again. His incitement helped light the fire that became the Battle of Uhud.

The Prophet ﷺ forgave many enemies, even men caught trying to kill him. This was the rare exception, a present danger in a dangerous time. He stood and asked, 'Who will deal with Ka'b ibn al-Ashraf, for he has harmed Allah and His Messenger?' Muhammad ibn Maslama answered, then paused to be sure: 'O Messenger of Allah, are you asking me to deal with him?' 'Yes,' came the reply.

It was a delicate, dangerous mission against a guarded man who lived like a king. Muhammad ibn Maslama carried it out with patience and care, and returned without his companions even being detected. Even Ka'b's own tribe found they had no grounds to complain, for the man had been inciting war.

When Ka'b had gone to Makkah, its leaders asked him which faith he found closer to his own, theirs or the way of the Prophet ﷺ. He chose the idol-worshippers over the believers. The Prophet ﷺ had hoped for the kind of recognition the Negus and others had shown, yet some hearts refused it. Scholars connect this to a verse about the strange sickness of a heart that holds Scripture and still chooses the wrong side:

أَلَمْ تَرَ إِلَى ٱلَّذِينَ أُوتُوا۟ نَصِيبًا مِّنَ ٱلْكِتَـٰبِ يُؤْمِنُونَ بِٱلْجِبْتِ وَٱلطَّـٰغُوتِ وَيَقُولُونَ لِلَّذِينَ كَفَرُوا۟ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ أَهْدَىٰ مِنَ ٱلَّذِينَ ءَامَنُوا۟ سَبِيلًا أُو۟لَـٰٓئِكَ ٱلَّذِينَ لَعَنَهُمُ ٱللَّهُ ۖ وَمَن يَلْعَنِ ٱللَّهُ فَلَن تَجِدَ لَهُۥ نَصِيرًاDo you not see how those given a share of the Scripture, [evidently] now believe in idols and evil powers? They say of the disbelievers, 'They are more rightly guided than the believers.' Those are the ones God has rejected: you [Prophet] will not find anyone to help those God has rejected.Quran 4:51-52

Break your sword

Of everything narrated about him, one moment stands above the rest. The Prophet ﷺ once gave him a sword and said, 'Use this to fight the disbelievers in battle.' Then he added something unexpected: 'But when you see the Muslims fighting one another, take it to Mount Uhud and strike it against the rock until it breaks. Then sit in your house until death comes to you.'

Notice he was not told only to stop fighting. He was told to destroy the very means of it. The lesson is hard and clear. If you fear you will not be able to control yourself when trial comes, do not edge close to it. Step away from it entirely.

It is the kind of instruction that reaches across the centuries. When people begin tearing into one another, withdraw from the argument altogether. If a place keeps leading you into sin, leave the place. If you cannot sit with certain company without trouble, stop sitting with them. Sometimes wisdom is not winning the fight, but refusing to pick up the sword at all.

He took the advice to heart and kept it for the rest of his life. The Prophet ﷺ once said that there was a man fitna would never harm, and named Muhammad ibn Maslama. So when the trials came and Muslims raised swords against Muslims, he did exactly as he had been told.

The man Umar sent

Because he was safe from hypocrisy, the man who loved him most after the Prophet ﷺ was Umar ibn al-Khattab. Umar made him his eyes: whenever someone complained about a governor, he sent Muhammad ibn Maslama to investigate. The Companions loved having him beside them in battle, and dreaded having him arrive to inspect their affairs.

He held the most senior men to account without fear. Sent to question Amr ibn al-Aas, he refused the fine meal laid out for him and ate his own bread from his pocket, because he had come to investigate, not to dine. He weighed everything, down to a single sandal of a pair. Yet the accountability ran both ways: he once told Umar plainly that if the leader ever strayed from justice, the people would straighten him like an arrow, and Umar thanked Allah for people like that.

He was not above correction himself. When a poor mother of orphans was missed in the distribution of zakat, Umar summoned him and reminded him of their lean early years, of how vulnerable they had once been. Muhammad ibn Maslama wept and said he had not meant to forget her, and made sure she received her share that year and the share she had missed before.

Keeping his word to the end

When the great fitna came and the Companions argued over what to do, this was the man who had spent his life scanning the ummah for danger. And yet, when the danger became other believers, he remembered his Prophet's words ﷺ. He took the sword he had been given, carried it to Mount Uhud, and broke it. Then he went home.

He withdrew from it all. He kept only a small wooden sword in his house, he said, in case someone came to harm him. He moved to a remote place far from Madinah and pitched his tent there, asking only to be kept away from the fighting. When travellers urged him to come out and lead, he refused, and repeated the command he had been given: break your sword, and sit in your house.

It was his decree that even this did not spare him. A man, angry that he had stayed out of the conflict, entered his tent and killed him. He was martyred at seventy-seven and buried in Madinah: the companion named Muhammad, the quiet knight who guarded everyone but himself.

What the life of Muhammad ibn Maslama teaches us

His was not a loud life. It was a life of trust kept quietly, in war and in peace, and that is exactly why it is worth sitting with.

  • Be trustworthy in the dark. He was trusted with the riskiest missions and the most sensitive inspections because he was safe from hypocrisy. Integrity that holds when no one is watching is the rarest kind.
  • Know your role, and stay in it. He had one gift and he gave it fully, never grasping for a part that was not his. There is real peace in serving where you are placed instead of envying where you are not.
  • Walk away from what you cannot control. Break your sword. If a place, a conversation, or a habit keeps pulling you into harm, the safest move is not to manage it but to leave it entirely.
  • Accept correction, even at the top. One of the strongest men of his time wept when a poor woman was overlooked, and made it right. Strength and a soft, correctable heart can live in the same person.

Questions

Who was Muhammad ibn Maslama?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah, known as his knight and watchman. He was one of the people of Badr, guarded Madinah through its battles, and was trusted with the most sensitive missions.

Why did he carry the name Muhammad before the Prophet?

He was one of only a handful of Arabs who happened to bear the name Muhammad before the Prophet ﷺ received revelation. Scholars suggest some families chose it after hearing that a foretold prophet would carry that name.

What does it mean that he broke his sword?

The Prophet ﷺ told him that when Muslims began to fight one another, he should break his sword and stay home rather than take part. When that trial came, he did exactly that, withdrawing completely from the conflict.

What can we learn from his life?

The weight of being genuinely trustworthy, the peace of knowing and keeping your role, the wisdom of walking away from what you cannot control, and a heart soft enough to accept correction.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to measure a life by how loud it was, how often it was seen, how much it was praised. Muhammad ibn Maslama spoke little, sought no spotlight, and was nearly forgotten by history. Yet he was the one the Prophet ﷺ trusted to guard the city while everyone slept, and the one fitna could not touch.

His quiet faithfulness is something most of us can actually live. To be reliable when no one is checking. To serve where we are placed without resentment. And to know, when something keeps dragging us toward harm, that the bravest thing is sometimes to break the sword, step back, and keep our hearts whole. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
