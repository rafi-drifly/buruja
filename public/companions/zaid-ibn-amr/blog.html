The Companions

Zaid ibn Amr ibn Nufayl

A Nation by Himself

Before the first revelation, before anyone had heard the Quran, there was a man in Makkah who would stand with his back against the Kaaba, look at a crowd surrounded by idols, and say that not one of them was on the religion of Abraham. Only him.

This is the story of Zaid ibn Amr ibn Nufayl (may Allah be pleased with him): the man who searched his whole life for the truth, refused to follow a path he could not believe in, and died before he could meet the Prophet he was waiting for.

The Companions

Zaid ibn Amr ibn Nufayl

A Nation by Himself

Four men who could not pretend

Zaid lived in Makkah a generation before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ was called. He belonged to Banu Adi of Quraysh, a respected clan, and the elder al-Khattab, the father of Umar ibn al-Khattab, was his uncle. That made Zaid the first cousin of the man who would one day become the second caliph of Islam. He looked at the world he had been born into, a city built around idols, and something in him refused to settle.

He was one of four men who reached the same conclusion at the same time. They could not accept the idol worship of their people, and they decided, quietly, to look for something older and truer: the way of Ibrahim (peace be upon him), the way of pure monotheism. They called themselves the hunafa, the upright. They were not Muslims, for the Prophet ﷺ had not yet been sent, but they were monotheists who sensed that the truth of Abraham had been buried under generations of idols.

One by one, three of them drifted into versions of Christianity. One left for Rome and disappeared from the story. Another, Waraqah ibn Nawfal, became a learned Christian priest who would later be among the first to recognise the Prophet ﷺ. Zaid alone was never satisfied. He would take from any path whatever matched the way of Abraham, and reject the rest. His motto was simple: my Lord is the Lord of Abraham, my religion is the religion of Abraham.

The lone voice at the Kaaba

It is worth pausing here on how unusual Zaid was. The Prophet ﷺ himself never worshipped an idol, and neither did Abu Bakr, but in those years neither of them was publicly challenging the idols of Makkah, because Allah had not yet called them to do so. There is a wisdom in that. For forty years the Prophet ﷺ would build a reputation for honesty so deep that no one could deny it. Zaid had no such cover. He confronted his people head on, and he paid for it.

He did not keep his conviction to himself, and he did not hide it. He would raise his hands toward the Kaaba and say, in front of everyone: O Allah, I bear witness that I am on the religion of Abraham. When the season of pilgrimage came, and the crowds filled the city for what had become a festival of idols and commerce, he would press his back against the Kaaba and call out: O assembly of Quraysh, I swear that not one of you is on the religion of Abraham except me. They prided themselves on being descendants of Ibrahim. He was telling them they had lost the very thing they boasted about.

He refused to eat meat slaughtered in the name of idols. He would say it plainly: Allah created the animal, Allah sent down the rain that fed it, and then you sacrifice it to other than Him. There is a moment the Prophet ﷺ never forgot. Near a place called Tan'im, just outside Makkah, he met Zaid at a gathering, and Zaid said to him gently: O son of my brother, do not eat from this, for it was slaughtered for other than Allah. From that day, it is narrated, the Prophet ﷺ never again ate from the meat that Quraysh dedicated to their idols. The very rituals Zaid was defending were the ones the Quran would one day restore to their true purpose:

Say, ‘My prayers and sacrifice, my life and death, are all for God, Lord of all the Worlds;Quran 6:162

The prostration of a man with no guide

What moves you most about Zaid is that he had no book and no teacher. He did not know what bowing was, or what prostration was, or how a person was meant to pray. He only had a heart that kept reaching toward God.

Asma bint Abi Bakr (may Allah be pleased with her) remembered watching him place his cheek to the ground near the Kaaba and weep. She heard him say: O Allah, if only I knew which way is most pleasing to You, I would worship You in that way, but I do not know. And then, almost as an apology: so I worship You like this.

Think about what he was doing. He was asking himself, with no help at all, what Ibrahim would have done, and reaching for it. He prostrated, even though the Christians of his time had long since dropped prostration from their prayer. His intuition led him back to it. There is a word the scholars use for this: rushd, a kind of innate guidance. His God-given nature was pointing him home.

He was guessing, and he was sincere, and he was alone. He had taken the rituals that Makkah had twisted into pagan custom and tried, with nothing but his own intuition, to return them to the One they were always meant for. The Kaaba built for the worship of God, the tawaf meant to centre Him alone, the sacrifice meant to glorify Him: all of it had been handed over to idols, and Zaid was quietly trying to take it back.

The girls he carried home

Zaid understood something that the people around him had forgotten: that the way of Abraham was never only about how you worship. It was about how you treat the weak. In his Makkah, newborn girls were carried out at night and buried alive, because a daughter was seen as shame and as poverty.

Asma said that he used to give the buried girls life. She did not mean he raised the dead. She meant that he would go out to the families as they took their daughters to the pits and stop them. He would say: do not kill her. I will take her, and I will pay for everything she needs.

So he raised them. And when a girl he had saved grew up, he would return to her family and offer them a choice: take her back now, or let me find her a husband and marry her off honourably. He kept away from adultery and from wine, and he warned others against them, all of this before a single verse had been sent down. He was living the religion that had not yet arrived.

The journey that ended too soon

His search cost him dearly. His own uncle, al-Khattab, the father of Umar, turned on him. First he cursed him, then he spat at him, then he beat him, then he set the reckless men of the city on him, until Zaid could only enter Makkah in secret. He was hated, not for harming anyone, but for refusing to bow. The Prophet ﷺ, still a young man with no idea of his own future, once asked Zaid why his people hated him so much. Zaid answered: it is because I have left their religion and their idols for the religion of Abraham.

So he travelled, even as his wife begged him to stay and his uncle warned him not to return. He went north to Mosul, in Iraq, and then on to Syria, searching for the truth among rabbis and priests, holding every one of them up against the religion of Abraham. When he reached Syria he asked to be taken to the most knowledgeable monk they had, and he told that monk everything he was looking for. The answer changed his course. The monk told him that the faith he sought had no teachers left, but that a prophet was about to appear, in the very land he had come from. Go home, he was told. He is being sent now.

Zaid turned back toward Makkah, full of hope, an old man near eighty-five. He had no idea that the noble young man he used to call son of my brother would be that very prophet. He never made it home. At a place called Balqa, not far from his destination, robbers seized him, took everything he had, and killed him. He died perhaps five years before the Prophet ﷺ himself knew he would be called, around the year the records place near 605.

The dua that was answered

As death came for him on that road, Zaid did not pray for himself. He prayed for his child. O Allah, he said, if You have kept me from the company of Your Prophet, then do not keep it from my son. Allah answered him. His son Saeed ibn Zaid became one of the first to accept Islam and one of the ten given glad tidings of Paradise. Years later, Umar ibn al-Khattab would beat Saeed for his faith, just as al-Khattab had once beaten Zaid, and that very story would end with Umar himself becoming Muslim.

After the call began, Saeed and Umar came to the Prophet ﷺ afraid to ask about Zaid, afraid the answer would break their hearts. They did not ask whether he was in the Garden or the Fire. They asked only whether they could seek forgiveness for him. The Prophet ﷺ told them yes, seek forgiveness for him. Then he described what he had seen: that on the Day of Judgement, as the prophets stand with their nations lined up behind them, Zaid would be raised as a whole nation by himself, standing alone between the Prophet ﷺ and Isa, the son of Maryam, peace be upon him. It is the same word the Quran uses for Ibrahim, that he too was a nation in himself. The man who loved the way of Abraham was raised in Abraham's likeness.

There is more, and it only grows more tender. A companion named Amr ibn Rabi'ah recalled, in tears, that Zaid had once said to him: I am waiting for a prophet from the children of Isma'il, who will follow the way of Abraham and pray toward this Kaaba. If I live to see him I will believe in him. But I fear I will not. So if you meet him, give him my salaam. When that companion later accepted Islam, he conveyed the greeting, and the Prophet ﷺ returned the salaam and asked Allah to have mercy on Zaid. In other narrations the Prophet ﷺ said he had seen Zaid in Paradise, walking and trailing his garments, and that Allah had given him not one but two ranks within it.

His old friend Waraqah, hearing of his death, mourned him in verse. He said that Zaid had been guided to the right path and had saved himself from the blazing Fire by worshipping a Lord who has no equal and abandoning the idols, and that the mercy of his Lord reaches a man even if he lies many layers beneath the earth. May Allah be pleased with him and have mercy on him.

What Zaid's life teaches us

Zaid never met the Prophet ﷺ, never heard a verse, never prayed a prayer as we know it. And yet his heart found its way. That is the heart of his story.

  • A sincere heart is guided. With no book and no teacher, Zaid was drawn toward worship, toward mercy, toward the truth. When the inner compass is honest, it points the right way.
  • Refuse to be complacent. Everyone around him had settled into a comfortable lie. He could not. The willingness to keep asking, and to keep looking, is itself a kind of faith.
  • Faith is how you treat the weak. Zaid did not only reject the idols. He went out at night to save the daughters no one wanted. Belief that does not move toward mercy is missing its heart.
  • You may not see the harvest. He died on the road, still waiting, still hoping. His reward came anyway, and his prayer for his son was answered. Sincerity is not wasted, even when the ending is hidden from us.

Questions

Who was Zaid ibn Amr ibn Nufayl?

A man of Makkah from Banu Adi of Quraysh, a generation before the Prophet ﷺ was called. He rejected idol worship and searched for the original monotheism of Ibrahim (peace be upon him). He was the father of Saeed ibn Zaid and the first cousin of the father of Umar ibn al-Khattab.

Was Zaid a Muslim?

He died about five years before the first revelation, so he never met the Prophet ﷺ or heard the Quran. But the Prophet ﷺ said he had seen Zaid in Paradise, raised on the Day of Judgement as a nation by himself, and permitted his family to seek forgiveness for him.

What does it mean that he saved baby girls?

In that society, families would bury newborn daughters alive out of shame and fear of poverty. Zaid went to those families and offered to take and raise the girls at his own expense, and later to return them or marry them off honourably.

What can we learn from the life of Zaid?

That a sincere heart is guided even without a teacher, that complacency is a danger, and that true faith always reaches toward mercy for the weak.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us were handed our faith. We did not have to search a lifetime for the direction of prayer, or guess in the dark which way might please God. Zaid did. He had every excuse to drift along with everyone else, and he refused, and the refusal cost him almost everything he had.

His life asks one quiet question of our day: would we still reach for the truth if it were not already in our hands? Would our worship still bend toward the weak and the unwanted? You can carry one piece of him into this ordinary day. Be sincere when no one is checking, refuse to grow comfortable with what is wrong, and let your faith show itself in mercy. That is the heart Zaid had, and it is the heart that was answered.
