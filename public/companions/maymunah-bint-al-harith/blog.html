The Companions

Maymunah bint al-Harith

The Blessed One

She was widowed, no longer young, and not counted among the powerful of Makkah. She had no wealth to speak of and no one to protect her. By the standards of her city, she was easy to overlook.

This is the story of Maymunah bint al-Harith (may Allah be pleased with her): the last woman the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ married, whose marriage came in one of the happiest weeks of his life, and who asked, at the very end, to die in the place where it began.

The Blessed One

A family of believing sisters

Maymunah came from the tribe of Banu Hilal, a desert people known as the in-laws of the Prophet's own clan, Banu Hashim. Her mother, Hind bint Awf, was remembered as one of the most honoured women in Arabia, not for her own wealth, but for who her daughters married. Through her line ran a whole web of the early believers.

Maymunah's sisters were remarkable. One of them, Lubabah, the mother of Abdullah ibn Abbas, was said to have accepted Islam the same day as Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her). Another, Zaynab bint Khuzaymah, had already been married to the Prophet ﷺ before she passed away. The Prophet ﷺ gave these women a single name together: al-Akhawat al-Mu'minat, the believing sisters.

It is not clear exactly when Maymunah herself accepted Islam. She seems to have come to it later than her sisters. She was living in a Makkah that was hostile to the faith, married to a man who opposed the Prophet ﷺ and died without believing. Around her, the people closest to her were being drawn in one by one, while she waited in a harder place.

But at some point, quietly and in difficult circumstances, she believed. Hers was not the dramatic early faith of Khadijah or the open declaration of her sister Lubabah. It was a faith that took root slowly, in a household that gave it no encouragement, and it held.

The return to Makkah

Seven years had passed since the Prophet ﷺ and his companions had been driven out of Makkah. They had not seen their homeland. They had not made the pilgrimage. They had only defended themselves, year after year, against the city that had expelled them.

When he had left Makkah, he turned back to look at it and wept. "You are the most beloved of Allah's earth to me," he said. "And had your people not expelled me, I would never have left you." He loved that city, even as it hunted him.

Now, after a treaty was made at Hudaybiyyah, he was allowed back for three days to perform a make-up pilgrimage. He came with two thousand companions. The men who had once fled for their lives walked into the city again, calling out together, "Here I am, O Allah, here I am."

The people of Quraysh withdrew to the surrounding hills and watched. They had spent years trying to destroy this man, and now they saw the Muslims circling the Kaaba in peace and worship. Some of those watching felt their hearts move toward Islam simply from the sight of it. The whole week became a quiet invitation, and Maymunah's story unfolds in the middle of it.

A woman who hoped

Maymunah was now widowed again, and she was vulnerable. She had no husband, no fortune, and little standing. The closest thing she had to protection was her brother-in-law al-Abbas, the Prophet's uncle, and a young nephew named Khalid ibn al-Walid, who was not yet a Muslim.

Quietly, she let her family know what she hoped for. She told Ja'far, her brother-in-law, that she wished she could be tied to Banu Hashim the way her sisters were. She told her sister Lubabah the same. It was her gentle way of asking a question she could barely say aloud: would the Prophet ﷺ ever consider someone like me?

So al-Abbas went to the Prophet ﷺ and spoke plainly. His sister-in-law had been widowed once more. She was alone, and no longer sought after. "Are you interested in marrying her, O Messenger of Allah?" The Prophet ﷺ accepted. She is the woman that several early commentators understood the Qur'an to be describing:

Also any believing woman who has offered herself to the Prophet and whom the Prophet wishes to wed.Quran 33:50

The camel and all that was on it

Maymunah was waiting on her camel, a little way off, for the answer. She knew her uncle had gone to the Prophet ﷺ. She knew Ja'far, so beloved to him, had spoken on her behalf. And then she saw Ja'far returning, and she could tell from the way he came that the news was good.

He told her the Prophet ﷺ had accepted. She climbed down from her camel in joy and said, "The camel and all that is on it belong to the Messenger of Allah." She had nothing else to give, so she gave everything she had to show what this meant to her.

The Makkans refused to let the wedding feast be held inside the city. They wanted him gone. So the Prophet ﷺ held it just outside, at a place called Sarif, about thirteen miles from the Kaaba on the road to Madinah. Some relatives still came, and among them was the young man Khalid ibn al-Walid, who would soon become Muslim. This marriage, the scholars say, may have been part of what finally softened his heart.

Her birth name had been Barrah, a name the Prophet ﷺ disliked because it claimed purity for a person before Allah. So he gave her a new one: Maymunah, which means blessed. Many scholars say he chose it because her marriage fell in the very days of his joyful return to Makkah after seven years away. Her name became a glad tiding, sealing the happiness of that week.

The student at her feet

She returned with him to Madinah, and so did the whole family of al-Abbas. Among them was a boy named Abdullah ibn Abbas, who would spend the last three years of the Prophet's life shadowing him, fetching his water, learning everything a child could from a man he loved.

One night, Abdullah asked his aunt Maymunah if he could stay in her home on the night the Prophet ﷺ was with her. He wanted to watch how the Prophet ﷺ prayed in the dark hours. Maymunah asked, and the Prophet ﷺ agreed. The boy lay across the foot of their bed and watched.

From that single night came one of the most detailed accounts we have of how the Prophet ﷺ rose, made wudu, and stood in the long night prayer. It reached the whole ummah because Maymunah opened her home to a curious child on what was her own night.

Maymunah narrated much herself: how the Prophet ﷺ prayed, how he washed, the small private details of his worship that only someone in his household could know. She passed on around seventy of his sayings. Once, on the burning day of Arafah, the companions were afraid to ask whether he was fasting, so she sent him a cup of milk. He raised it where all could see and drank, and the exhausted companions, relieved, drank too.

A life lived simply

After the Prophet ﷺ died, Maymunah lived plainly, as his wives did. She often had to take loans, and some companions felt this was beneath a Mother of the Believers. She answered that she had heard her dear friend the Prophet ﷺ say that whoever takes a loan, intending to repay it, will be helped by Allah to pay it back. She always repaid.

She freed many slaves, and when she did, she would stop to make a prayer for each one before letting them go. It was not enough for her simply to set them free; she wanted them to leave with a blessing. Many of those she freed went on to become scholars and narrators of hadith in their own right.

She kept the ties of family carefully, reaching even her distant relatives, spending on them and caring for them. Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) said of her that she was among the most God-fearing of them, and the most devoted to keeping family ties.

Yet she did not let kinship override the limits of Allah. When a relative once came to her smelling of wine, she would not let him near, and told him gently to go and purify himself first.

Back to Sarif

Maymunah outlived all the other wives of the Prophet ﷺ. She outlived the great caliphs. The generation that had known her was passing away, and many of the younger Muslims no longer recognised who she was.

When she fell ill, in her eighties, she remembered something the Prophet ﷺ had once told her: that she would not die in Makkah. So she asked to be carried out to Sarif, the place thirteen miles outside the city where, more than sixty years earlier, she had married him. They took her to the exact spot, and there she died, in the place of the happiest memory of her life.

Abdullah ibn Abbas led her funeral prayer, and her nephew Khalid ibn al-Walid helped lower her into the grave. As they carried her, Ibn Abbas reminded the people who they were burying. "This is Maymunah. This is your mother. When you lift her, do not be rough, do not shake her." She was buried in the very ground where her wedding tent had once stood.

What Maymunah's life teaches us

Her story has no battles and no grand speeches. It is the story of a quiet, overlooked woman whose faith and steadiness lasted a lifetime, and that is exactly why it stays with us.

  • Hope is allowed, even when you feel small. Maymunah had no wealth and no standing, yet she dared to hope for something good and spoke it to the people who could help her. Her circumstances did not decide her worth.
  • Quiet faithfulness is a whole life. She is remembered for keeping family ties, freeing slaves with a prayer, repaying every loan, and guarding the limits of Allah. None of it was loud, and all of it was real.
  • Open your home, and good travels far. Because she let one curious boy stay the night, we know how the Prophet ﷺ prayed. Small acts of generosity carry farther than we can see.
  • Some joys are worth returning to. At the end of her life she asked to die where she had been married. She knew which moment had been the most blessed, and she let it be her last.

Questions

Who was Maymunah bint al-Harith?

She was the last woman the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ married, and one of the Mothers of the Believers. She came from the tribe of Banu Hilal and was the sister of Lubabah, the mother of Abdullah ibn Abbas.

Why was she named Maymunah?

Her birth name was Barrah, which the Prophet ﷺ disliked because it claimed purity for a person. He renamed her Maymunah, meaning blessed, because her marriage came during his joyful return to Makkah after seven years in exile.

When did the Prophet ﷺ marry Maymunah?

He married her in the year after the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, during the make-up pilgrimage (Umrah al-Qada), about seven years after the migration to Madinah. The wedding feast was held at Sarif, just outside Makkah.

What can we learn from the life of Maymunah?

That a quiet, overlooked person can hope for good and receive it, that faith shows itself in steady everyday righteousness, and that simple generosity, like opening your home, can carry farther than you ever imagine.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to feel that we are too ordinary to matter: not wealthy enough, not important enough, not noticed by anyone. Maymunah was all of those things in the eyes of her city, and none of it touched her real worth. She hoped quietly, she believed, and she gave what little she had.

Her life invites us to live the unseen parts well: to keep the ties we are tempted to drop, to repay what we owe, to be kind to the people who pass through our homes, and to hold to the limits of Allah even when no one is watching. Carry one of those into today, and her story has done its work.
