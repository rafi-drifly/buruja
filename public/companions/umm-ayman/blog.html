Umm Ayman

The Mother After His Mother

There is one person who can claim something no other human being can. She held the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ in her arms on the day he was born, and she was still by his side on the day he died.

This is the story of Umm Ayman (may Allah be pleased with her), born Baraka: a freed servant from Abyssinia whom the Prophet ﷺ called "my mother after my mother," and the second woman in history to believe in him.

The Companions

Umm Ayman

The Mother After His Mother

A girl named Baraka

She was born around the year 557, roughly thirteen years before the Prophet ﷺ. Her name was Baraka, which means blessing. She came from Abyssinia, in what is modern-day Ethiopia, and as a young girl she was brought to Makkah and sold in its slave market.

She was bought by Abdullah ibn Abd al-Muttalib, the father of the Prophet ﷺ. She was the only servant brought into that household. So when Abdullah married Amina, it was the three of them in one home: Abdullah, Amina, and this young girl Baraka.

Soon after the marriage, Abdullah left for the trade route to Syria. He did not yet know that Amina was expecting a child. He never returned. He died on that journey, and it was Baraka who was given the task of telling Amina that her husband was gone. She stayed with her, and consoled her, and kept her company through the long months that followed.

The first to hold him

While Abdullah was away, Amina had a dream. She saw a light come from her, lighting up the hills and valleys all the way to Syria. There was only one other person in the house she could tell, and that was Baraka. Baraka said she hoped it was good news, a blessed child.

When the day came for Amina to give birth, the only other person in the room was this young girl. Baraka was the first human hands to hold the Prophet ﷺ. She said that she saw the light when he was born and knew it was the meaning of Amina's dream. She cleaned him, and she handed him to his mother.

She is one of only three women known to have nursed the Prophet ﷺ: his mother Amina, this woman Baraka, and later Halima of the open desert. From the very first hour of his life, she was there.

Take care of him as if you are his mother

When the Prophet ﷺ was six years old, his mother Amina fell ill on a journey and began to die in front of him. As she was dying, she whispered something to Baraka. She told her to take care of him as if she were his mother. To stay with him, and to make sure he would know no sadness beyond this.

So Baraka, then only around nineteen, held a six-year-old boy as he lost the last parent he had. Three years later, when his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib died, she was there again, consoling a nine-year-old who had lost the man who sheltered him.

She was the one figure who never left. Others knew the Prophet ﷺ as a man, or as a youth, or in his later years. She alone had known him from his very first breath. The Prophet ﷺ would later free her from servitude, so that she was no longer anyone's property.

And when he married Khadijah (may Allah be pleased with her), he introduced this woman with words she would carry for the rest of her life: "She is my mother after my mother." It was not a passing kindness. It was how he would speak of her for the rest of his days: not a servant to be overlooked, but family, and the closest thing he had to a mother on this earth.

I never left him, and he never left me

For years she had stayed unmarried, the same way the Prophet ﷺ had waited, content to serve and to care rather than to be distracted. When he married, he gently told her it was time she had a life of her own. "You donated your youth for my cause," he said. "Now go and have a life."

She refused at first. "I never left him, and he never left me," she said. It is a small line, but it shines through her whole story. Khadijah offered to find her the best of men and to pay for the whole wedding herself, the way she did for women in need. Only then did Baraka agree.

Her first husband was a good man from Yathrib named Ubayd ibn Zayd. They married before Islam, and she had a son. His name was Ayman, and from him she took the name the world would know her by: Umm Ayman, the mother of Ayman. Her son would live to see Islam, believe in it, and one day die as a martyr.

The second to believe

When revelation came to the Prophet ﷺ, the family who knew his character did not hesitate. After Khadijah, Umm Ayman was the second woman to believe in him. She heard the message and accepted it at once, exactly as anyone who had loved him that long would.

Her first husband had passed away by then. The Prophet ﷺ saw her, older now and widowed, and he said: whoever wishes to marry a woman from Paradise, let him marry Umm Ayman. On paper there was little to draw a suitor. She was no longer young, her lineage was unknown, and she had no wealth. But he had named her a woman of Paradise, and that was enough. The man who rushed forward was Zaid ibn Haritha, himself freed by the Prophet ﷺ, and at least twenty years younger than her.

She was not expected to have more children. But Allah blessed them with a son: Usama ibn Zayd, whom the Prophet ﷺ loved as his own. Usama would sit on his lap as a small child, and one day the Prophet ﷺ would place him in command of the Muslim army, young as he was. Through this marriage, two people the Prophet ﷺ had loved and freed became the parents of one of the people dearest to his heart.

When the migration to Madinah came, she made the hard journey in her old age, near seventy. On the road she found no water and feared she would die of thirst. She said a bucket was lowered to her from the sky on a rope whose top she could not see. She drank until she was satisfied, and there was still water left. From that day, she said, she never felt thirst again, not even fasting on the hottest days.

As long as Islam is good, I am good

She arrived in Madinah with swollen feet and her face covered in dust. The Prophet ﷺ looked at her and told her, a second time, that a place in Paradise was hers. She witnessed his battles. She moved across the field keeping her eyes on him like a watchful mother, then bandaged the wounded and tore her own clothes to wrap them. At Uhud, when many fled, she was among those who took up a sword and stood by him.

He visited her almost every day. Once he asked her simply, "How are you?" Her answer holds the whole shape of her life. "As long as Islam is good," she said, "I am good." As long as his message was safe, she needed nothing more.

She was also the one who could make him laugh. Her Arabic was imperfect, and sometimes a word came out with the opposite meaning, and he would smile. Once she came asking him to give her a mount, and he teased that he would only carry her on the child of a she-camel, until she realized that every camel is the child of a camel. He called her his mother, he joked with her, and he once said of her: "This is all that is left of my family."

The grief that was for the revelation

She outlived almost everyone she loved. Her first husband, her son Ayman, her second husband Zaid, the Prophet ﷺ himself, and later her son Usama. Some say she lived to be a hundred years old, burying one beloved face after another.

When the Prophet ﷺ died, she grew quiet. She kept her distance from the crowds, no longer joking, no longer speaking much. After his death, Abu Bakr and Umar went to visit her, as he used to. When they sat with her, she began to cry. They assumed she was crying because she missed him, and they reminded her that what Allah had given him now was better than this world.

"I know that," she said. "That is not why I am crying. I am crying because the revelation from the heavens has ceased." And the two men began to weep with her, and the three of them wept together for a long time. She died about twenty days after Umar was struck down, and she was buried in al-Baqi, placed in line with the grave of the Prophet ﷺ she had cared for all his life.

What Umm Ayman's life teaches us

Her name is rarely on the famous lists. Yet she was present for the beginning and the end of the most important life in human history, and she did it by caring, not by being seen.

  • Faithful care is its own greatness. She held a frightened orphan, stayed when others left, and watched over one person for a lifetime. The Prophet ﷺ raised her in honour for exactly that.
  • Serve without keeping count. She gave her youth and her whole life to a cause and never once complained about the hardship, because she understood it was for something greater.
  • Your worth is not your status. She had no tribe, no wealth, and no high birth, and the Prophet ﷺ called her his mother. Allah measures people by their hearts, not their labels.
  • Stay good as long as the cause is good. Asked how she was, she answered: as long as Islam is good, I am good. A heart anchored to something larger than itself does not crumble easily.

Questions

Who was Umm Ayman?

She was Baraka, a freed servant from Abyssinia who raised and cared for the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He called her "my mother after my mother." She was the only person present at both his birth and the years of his death, and the second woman to accept Islam.

Why is she called Umm Ayman?

The name means "mother of Ayman." Ayman was her son from her first marriage, before Islam. He grew up to believe in the Prophet ﷺ and was later martyred. She became known throughout her life by his name.

What was her connection to Usama ibn Zayd?

Usama was her son from her marriage to Zaid ibn Haritha. The Prophet ﷺ loved Usama as his own and appointed him to command the Muslim army while he was still very young.

What can we learn from the life of Umm Ayman?

That quiet, faithful care matters as much as any public act, that we should serve without keeping count, and that a person's true worth has nothing to do with their wealth, tribe, or rank.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never lead an army or stand on a famous stage. We will spend our days caring for the people in front of us: a child, a parent, a community, a cause that asks more of us than it gives back. Umm Ayman lived that life entirely, and it placed her at the very heart of history.

Her example is an invitation to look again at the small, unseen work of caring for others and to treat it as something sacred. When you are tired and someone asks how you are, her answer is a quiet model: not a list of complaints, but a heart still anchored to what matters. She measured a good day by whether the thing she loved was safe, not by whether she was comfortable. Carry that into today, and her story has done its work.
