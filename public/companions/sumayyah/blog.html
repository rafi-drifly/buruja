Sumayyah bint Khayyat

The First Martyr

She had no tribe to protect her, no wealth to shield her, and no name that the people of Makkah thought worth remembering. She was old, she was enslaved, and she was far from the land of her birth. There was almost nothing in her circumstances that the world would call honourable, and almost nothing that would have made anyone expect her to be remembered at all.

This is the story of Sumayyah bint Khayyat (may Allah be pleased with her): the first person in this ummah to die for the truth, and the first to be raised among the martyrs on the Day of Judgement. If Khadijah (RA) is likened to Maryam, then Sumayyah is the Asiyah of this ummah, the believing woman whose own life was taken by a Pharaoh who could not bear her faith.

Sumayyah bint Khayyat

The First Martyr

A life with no easy years

Sumayyah was an Abyssinian woman, from the land we now call Ethiopia. She was born about twenty years before the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, which means she was already an elderly woman, well into her sixties, by the time her story reaches us. She was older even than Khadijah (RA).

She was enslaved to Abu Hudhayfah ibn al-Mughirah, a man of Banu Makhzum, one of the most powerful tribes of Makkah. He gave her in marriage to Yasir, a man who had come from Yemen searching for a lost brother and had stayed, living under Abu Hudhayfah's protection. Yasir himself had aged to the point where it was said he had no eyebrows left.

Together they served that household, and in truth the whole tribe, all their lives. Sumayyah gave birth to a son, Ammar (RA), born around the same time as the Prophet ﷺ. This was a family with no standing, no voice, and no claim on anything in the world. Sumayyah had never known a comfortable day.

Among the first seven

When the call to Islam came quietly to Makkah, the first to make their faith known were only a handful. The scholar Mujahid counted seven: the Prophet ﷺ, Abu Bakr, Bilal, Khabbab, Suhayb, Sumayyah, and her son Ammar. Yasir accepted Islam soon after his wife and son.

When Sumayyah embraced the message, she said something to the Prophet ﷺ that would carry her through everything to come. She bore witness that he was the Messenger of Allah, and that his promise was true. It was that certainty in the promise of Allah that would hold her steady.

The Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr had families and tribes who shielded them from the worst. The other five did not. They were the ones with no one to defend them, and so they were the ones made into a public example. They were forced into metal armour and left to burn under the sun, beaten, and starved of water. The whole purpose of the spectacle was to frighten others away from the faith, and yet not one of those five ever turned back. Every one of them would go on to be counted among the noble of Islam.

The tyrant of Makkah

The man who led this cruelty was Abu Jahl, a chief of Banu Makhzum. His tribe feared that to accept the Prophet ﷺ was to lose their standing to a rival clan, Banu Hashim. Abu Jahl said openly that they could compete with the Prophet's people in everything, in lineage, in wealth, in poetry, in generosity, except in one thing: they could not compete if they admitted a prophet walked among them.

So he set out to crush every sign of faith. He was always the one who pushed the persecution to its next, lower level. The Prophet ﷺ called him the Pharaoh of this ummah, and the comparison fit. He beat a young enslaved woman until she lost her sight. He was a huge and powerful man, of the same build as Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA).

Set him beside Sumayyah: an old, frail woman, enslaved, foreign, with no one on earth to speak for her. By every worldly measure he held all the power, and she held none. And yet, of all the people he tortured, it was Sumayyah who seemed the most unshakeable, and that is what enraged him most.

She would not curse him

Abu Jahl wanted more than for them to leave Islam. He wanted them to stand in public and curse the Prophet ﷺ. He seemed to single Sumayyah out and to experiment his cruelty upon her, trying it in every form he could invent, because he knew that no one on earth would come to her aid. She was tied to a tree and beaten. She was dipped in water, buried in the sand, kicked, and lashed.

Every time, she answered his torture with the remembrance of Allah. When he demanded she curse the Prophet ﷺ, she would not. Instead she returned the curse to him and to his idols, keeping the name of her Lord and the honour of His Messenger ﷺ untouched on her tongue. The more he hurt her, the more her conviction seemed to grow, as though every blow only drew out the sweetness of her faith rather than breaking it.

The Prophet ﷺ could do nothing to save this family. He could only pass them as they were tortured and say: "Be patient, family of Yasir. Your appointed place is Paradise." In another narration he would pray, "O Allah, forgive the family of Yasir." He did not promise them rescue, or that their circumstances would change, or that their oppressor would be dealt with. He promised them only Paradise, and for them that was enough.

The first to fall

By now Abu Hudhayfah had died, and the family was left at the mercy of the whole tribe, caught in a strange limbo. They were not quite slaves who could be bought and freed, as Abu Bakr had freed Bilal and Khabbab, and they were not free either. Abu Jahl had no wish to see them ransomed; he wanted to prove a point, and so the torture went on. It came to a breaking point with Sumayyah tied to the trunk of a tree, barely holding to her life, and her husband Yasir tortured before her, near death himself.

Sinking lower still, Abu Jahl taunted her with a vile insult, suggesting she only longed for Yasir to die so she could marry the Prophet ﷺ. She spat at him. She told him that he was, by Allah, smaller in her sight than a beetle she would crush underfoot, and she asked Allah to humiliate him. At that, Abu Jahl took a spear and drove it through her. She was the first soul of this ummah to be killed for the truth.

Yasir watched it happen, and did not live long after. He died soon, the second martyr of Islam. Their son Ammar (RA), under the same torture, once said with his tongue the words he was ordered to say, and was left so ashamed that he lowered his face from the Prophet ﷺ. The Prophet ﷺ told him he was forgiven, and that if they forced him again, he could say it again, for it had come from his tongue and not his heart.

[Prophet], do not think of those who have been killed in God's way as dead. They are alive with their Lord, well provided for,Qur'an 3:169

Honoured beyond every distinction

Sumayyah alone, of those first seven, never lived to see the better days of Islam. The others saw Madinah, saw victory, saw the religion take root. She saw none of it in this world. Hers was not a life that had known ease before Islam and then lost it; she had never tasted comfort at all, and what came to her in Islam was harder still. The Prophet ﷺ once said that the person who had suffered the most in this life would be dipped just once into Paradise, and then asked if he had ever known a single day of hardship, and he would answer that he never had. Imagine, then, the one who entered first of all.

Years later came the Battle of Badr. Abu Jahl, the Pharaoh of this ummah, was killed by the very kind of people he had spent his life humiliating. The Prophet ﷺ went to Ammar (RA) and told him plainly: Allah has killed the killer of your mother. Ammar would carry her memory for the rest of his life and be reunited with her, himself a martyr, about fifty years on.

That the very first martyr of Islam was an elderly, enslaved, foreign woman is not a detail to pass over. It is the point. The honour of Allah does not follow the rankings of this world. Sumayyah, who never tasted its comfort, was the first of this whole ummah to cross into the gardens of the next.

What Sumayyah's life teaches us

Her story is short, and almost all of it is its ending. What she leaves us is not a list of deeds, but a single, unbreakable stance.

  • The truth is worth standing alone on. She had no tribe, no wealth, and no protector. She held to what was true anyway, with nothing in this world to gain by it.
  • Honour is given by Allah, not by people. Makkah saw an old, enslaved foreigner of no account. Allah made her the first martyr of the entire ummah.
  • A promise of Paradise can carry a soul through anything. The Prophet ﷺ did not promise her rescue, only Paradise. She held to that promise until her last breath, and it was enough.
  • Conviction can grow under pressure. The harder she was pressed, the steadier she became. Hardship did not wear down her faith; it revealed how deep it ran.

Questions

Who was Sumayyah bint Khayyat?

An elderly, enslaved Abyssinian woman in Makkah, the wife of Yasir and the mother of Ammar ibn Yasir (RA). She was among the first seven to openly accept Islam and is honoured as the first martyr of Islam.

Why is Sumayyah called the first martyr of Islam?

She was the first person in this ummah to be killed for refusing to give up the faith. Abu Jahl killed her under torture when she would not renounce Islam or curse the Prophet ﷺ. She is the first shahid, not only the first woman to be martyred.

How did Sumayyah die?

After enduring prolonged public torture and refusing to break, she was killed by Abu Jahl, a chief of Makkah, who drove a spear through her. Her husband Yasir died soon after as the second martyr.

What can we learn from the life of Sumayyah?

That the truth is worth standing on even when you stand alone, that real honour comes from Allah and not from people, and that the promise of Paradise can hold a believer steady through the hardest trial.

Why this story stays with us

It is tempting to think that the people who hold firmest are the ones with the most behind them: the support, the standing, the strength to push back. Sumayyah had none of that. She was old, alone, and powerless by every measure the world keeps. And she did not bend. What held her was not anything she possessed, but something she trusted. She believed the promise of Allah was true, and she let that single certainty outweigh everything that was being done to her body.

There is a mercy in that for the rest of us. It means the thing that steadied her is available to anyone, in any state, with empty hands. We are not asked to be strong the way the world counts strength. We are asked to trust the One who counts differently. Most of us will never face anything like what she faced, but all of us know the smaller moments: the pressure to stay quiet, to go along, to let go of something true because holding it costs us. Her life is a quiet question for those moments. What would it look like, today, to hold to what is right because we believe Allah's promise more than we fear the cost? May Allah grant us a measure of her certainty, make us firm when we are tested, and gather us with her among those who held to the truth.
