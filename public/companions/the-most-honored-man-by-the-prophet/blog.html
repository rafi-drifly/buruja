The Companions

Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib

The Uncle of the Prophet

If a certain old man had walked into Madinah in the years after the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ passed away, every companion in the room would have stood. They would have lowered themselves, kissed his hand, and asked him for his prayers. Not because of an army he led or a conquest he won, but because of who he was to the one they missed.

This is the story of Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib (may Allah be pleased with him): the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, the bridge between Makkah and Madinah, and a man whose faith took its time but never let go.

The Companions

Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib

The Uncle of the Prophet

The last of the forefathers

When revelation came to the Prophet ﷺ, four of his uncles were alive. Two were much older: Abu Talib, who protected him, and Abu Lahab, who fought him. The other two were close to him in age, more like older brothers than uncles. They were Hamza and Al-Abbas, and they were the only two of his uncles to enter Islam.

Their names tell you something. Both Al-Abbas and Hamza mean lion, but each names a different posture. Al-Abbas is the lion as it circles its prey and begins to roar. Hamza is the lion once it has struck. The names fit the men. Hamza was the fearless one who threw himself into the enemy. Al-Abbas was the careful one, who studied a situation and took his time.

Al-Abbas was about three years older than the Prophet ﷺ, but he never used that to place himself above him. Asked once which of them was older, he answered with words people still repeat: "He is greater. I just happened to be born before him."

A man people noticed

Al-Abbas was hard to miss. He was one of the tallest men of Makkah, fair, with two braids that fell to his shoulders, and he carried a presence that made people quiet when he entered a room. He was also clever, eloquent, and known for a voice that could be heard from far away. Even in old age, instead of sending for his servants, he would stand on a high place and call out, and his voice would reach them.

He was wealthy, a trader who travelled to Syria and Yemen and was famous for the spices and fine things he brought back. He was entrusted with the care of Zamzam after his father, which made him a man of hospitality, the one who looked after the pilgrims who came to Makkah.

Because he knew the wider world, he was the first person a visitor would ask about news of Makkah. When a man saw the Prophet ﷺ praying near the Kaaba with a boy and a woman beside him and did not understand what he was seeing, it was Al-Abbas who told him: that is my nephew Muhammad, and beside him Ali and Khadijah, and he says no one on earth follows this religion but them.

The uncle who held off

Al-Abbas was never hostile to the Prophet ﷺ. He defended him, testified to his honesty, and offered him protection. But for a long time he did not declare himself a Muslim. Of all the companions, the question of when exactly he believed is the hardest to pin down. Some narrations place his belief much earlier than it appeared, kept quiet inside him.

His wife, Lubabah bint al-Harith (Umm al-Fadl), was among the first women to accept Islam after Khadijah. She prayed openly, and she prayed for her husband. Their son Abdullah and their servant Abu Rafi were Muslims too. In his own home, faith was already alive while he stayed outwardly undecided.

When Abu Talib died, the role of protecting the Prophet ﷺ fell to Al-Abbas. So you saw the strange split inside one family: while one uncle, Abu Lahab, followed the Prophet ﷺ to stone him and call him a liar, another uncle walked beside him, shielding him and looking for a people who would take him in.

The bridge to Madinah

That search led to the Ansar, a small group of mostly young men from Yathrib. Al-Abbas was cautious. He did not doubt their sincerity, but he wanted to be sure they understood what they were taking on. When they returned the next year, more than seventy strong, ready to pledge themselves to the Prophet ﷺ, it was Al-Abbas who spoke first.

He warned them plainly. Be discreet, he said. Know that if you take this man in, all of Arabia may turn against you. If you are people who can carry that, then carry it. If not, walk away with no blame, and his own family will keep him. They answered that they were people of war and they would protect him. They asked what they would gain, and they were told: Paradise. We accept, they said, and want nothing else.

Here is the quiet irony of that night. Al-Abbas stood there overseeing the pledge, holding it together, sending the Prophet ﷺ off to a new home, and he had not yet declared his own belief. He sent the Prophet ﷺ from Makkah to Madinah. Years later he would receive him back into Makkah. He was the bridge at both ends.

Badr, and the verse that followed

At Badr, Al-Abbas was on the wrong side of the field, pushed out by Makkah against his will. The Prophet ﷺ knew it. He named him to the Muslims and said: do not kill him; he was forced out and does not want to fight us. A companion found him standing on that day like a statue, motionless, his eyes wet, a large man undone by where he had ended up.

He was taken captive by Abu al-Yusr, a small man whom Al-Abbas towered over. When the Prophet ﷺ asked how he had managed it, the answer was that a stranger had helped, a man never seen before or after, and the Prophet ﷺ smiled and said a noble angel had helped him. That night the Prophet ﷺ could not sleep. He heard his uncle groaning in his chains. When the companions loosened them, he was pleased, but told them to loosen the chains of every prisoner, not only his uncle's.

When it came to ransom, he would not treat his uncle differently from anyone else. Al-Abbas said he was already a Muslim, and the Prophet ﷺ answered that Allah knew the truth of his heart and would reward him for it, but outwardly he would be treated like the rest. He paid the largest ransom of Badr, freeing himself and others. And about the captives, a verse was revealed:

Prophet, tell those you have taken captive, 'If God knows of any good in your hearts, He will give you something better than what has been taken from you, and He will forgive you: God is forgiving and merciful.'Quran 8:70

Brought all the way back

After Badr, Al-Abbas grew even wealthier, and he never again let himself be forced out against the Prophet ﷺ. When the conquest of Makkah came, he was at the centre of it, negotiating the terms, advising the Prophet ﷺ on how to enter, going to the very people who had once cut the Prophet ﷺ off and bringing them back. The man who had sent him to Madinah now opened Makkah for his return.

Not everyone understood. Some still saw Al-Abbas as a latecomer. The Prophet ﷺ would not have it. He called him what is left of my forefathers, and said the uncle is like the father, and warned: whoever hurts Al-Abbas has hurt me. At Hunayn, when many fled, it was Al-Abbas who stood firm holding the reins of the Prophet's mount, and it was his great voice that called the people back to the Prophet ﷺ until the battle turned.

Back in Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ built him a home beside his own. Aisha (RA) said she never saw anyone the Prophet ﷺ honoured more than Al-Abbas. He would keep him close, kiss his forehead, and treat him with the tenderness owed to a father. He prayed that Allah forgive him every sin, hidden and open, and bless him and his children.

Honoured to the very end

After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, Al-Abbas became the anchor of the family in Madinah. Ali was seen kissing his hands and feet, saying, "O my uncle, be pleased with me." In a year of terrible drought, when the rain would not come, Umar (RA) did something striking: he gathered the people to pray, and he brought Al-Abbas out before them. The Prophet ﷺ used to honour this man, he said, so seek Allah's mercy through the uncle of your Prophet. They prayed, Al-Abbas prayed, and the rain filled the valleys.

Umar honoured him in small things too. He once broke a waterspout from Al-Abbas's roof, and when he learned the Prophet ﷺ had allowed it, he bent down and insisted Al-Abbas stand on his own back to set it right. When Al-Abbas walked down a road, caliphs would step off their mounts so as not to sit higher than him.

He died in the time of Uthman (RA), eighty-six years old, soon after his wife. So many people poured into Madinah to pray over him that it became hard to carry him to his grave; guards had to clear a path so the family could bury him. It is remembered as one of the largest funerals of any companion. His grandson Abdullah ibn Abbas became a great scholar of this ummah, and from his line came generations of Muslims, scholars, and leaders.

What the life of Al-Abbas teaches us

His story is not built on a single battle or a famous speech. It is built on nearness, patience, and a love that he never let his pride get in the way of.

  • Greatness can mean stepping lower. He was older, taller, and richer than his nephew, and he said plainly: he is greater, I was only born first. Honour, for him, meant putting someone worthier ahead of himself.
  • Quiet usefulness is its own service. He rarely held the spotlight. He negotiated, advised, protected, and steadied things from the side, and the work mattered as much as anything done out front.
  • Fairness begins at home. The Prophet ﷺ would not treat his own uncle more gently than the other captives. Real justice does not bend for the people we love most.
  • Hold the bond, even when it is hard. He kept the ties of kinship through war, exile, and reconciliation, and the Prophet ﷺ kept him close to the end. Few things outlast a person like the relationships they refused to drop.

Questions

Who was Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib?

He was a paternal uncle of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, a wealthy Makkan merchant and caretaker of Zamzam, and one of only two of the Prophet's uncles to accept Islam. He was honoured above almost anyone else in Madinah for his closeness to the Prophet ﷺ.

Why was Al-Abbas honoured so much?

Because he was the uncle of the Prophet ﷺ, and in that culture the paternal uncle stood in the place of the father. After the Prophet ﷺ passed away, the companions saw Al-Abbas as the last living piece of that family and showed him deep respect.

When did Al-Abbas become a Muslim?

This is one of the most disputed points about any companion. He protected and defended the Prophet ﷺ for years without declaring his faith publicly. Some narrations say he had already believed in private well before he made it known around the conquest of Makkah.

What can we learn from the life of Al-Abbas?

To put worthier people ahead of ourselves, to serve quietly without needing the credit, to be fair even with those we love, and to hold on to family ties through every kind of hardship.

Why this story stays with us

Al-Abbas was a man who could have made everything about himself. He was older, taller, wealthier, and more established than the nephew who was now being followed by thousands. Instead he chose, again and again, to stand a little lower: to negotiate, to protect, to carry messages, to hold the reins while others fled. The honour came to him precisely because he never demanded it.

Most of us will not lead an army or settle a treaty. But all of us decide, in small daily moments, whether to step forward for the credit or quietly do the thing that needs doing. We decide whether to keep a difficult bond or let it break. Al-Abbas is an invitation to be the steady one in the family, the one who serves without being asked and loves without keeping score. Carry that into today, and his story has done its work.
