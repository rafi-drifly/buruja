The Companions

Asma bint Yazid

The Orator of the Women

When the women of Madinah had something to say to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, they did not all crowd around him. They chose one woman to carry their words, because they knew she would say it plainly and without fear, and that she would not soften the question on the way.

This is the story of Asma bint Yazid (may Allah be pleased with her): the Orator of the Women, the daughter of a martyr, and the companion who asked, on behalf of every woman she knew, whether they too had a share in the reward. It is a small story in the records, and a large one in what it settles.

The Asmas of the seerah

There is a thread that runs through the women named Asma in the early community. They were known, almost without exception, for a particular kind of strength. It is a small thing to notice, but once you see it, it is hard to miss.

There was Asma bint Abu Bakr, who tied her waist belt for the Hijra and stood firm before Abu Jahl when he demanded the hiding place of her father and the Prophet ﷺ. She was slapped for her silence and gave nothing away. Even at a hundred years old she stood without fear before a tyrant, beside her son. People spoke of her as an unusually strong woman, and they meant it.

There was Asma bint Umays, who insisted on going out for the farewell Hajj while nine months pregnant, and gave birth on the journey rather than miss its reward. There was even the mother of Abu Jahl, an Asma who left her own son in Makkah to follow the Prophet ﷺ when many of the elders held back until the very end.

Asma bint Yazid belongs to this company. She came from Madinah, from the Ansar, and she carried that same steadiness into a life of service that lasted from the Prophet's time long into the years after him. Hers is the most famous of the Madinan Asmas, and she earned the name people gave her.

The daughter of a martyr

To understand Asma, it helps to know her father. He was a man of the Ansar who did not flee. In the difficulty of battle, when others scattered, he threw himself toward the Prophet ﷺ and defended him from every direction until he was killed. His body carried so many wounds that he was barely recognised.

When people remembered him afterward, they would recite the verse about those who sell themselves seeking the pleasure of Allah. That was the house Asma was raised in: a home where loyalty to the Prophet ﷺ was not a slogan but a price someone had already paid in full.

It is worth pausing on what she inherited. Not wealth, not status, but an example. She grew up knowing exactly what it looked like to give everything for something you believed in, and she did not treat that example as something to admire from a distance. She lived as though it were hers to continue.

A seat in the Prophet's gathering

The Prophet ﷺ used to set aside a time each week for the women of Madinah, a gathering of their own where he would teach them and answer their questions. Asma was there, and she remembered it carefully. Through her, much of what we know about those sessions comes down to us. She narrated tens of hadith from them.

She described how the Prophet ﷺ greeted the women when they came into the mosque as a group, signalling to them with his hand. She passed on his teaching about the verse of forgiveness, and his warnings against backbiting and against extravagance. When we read about how he spoke to the women, gently and directly, we are often reading her memory of it.

In one of these gatherings he asked, "Shall I not tell you who the best of you are?" They said yes. The best of you, he said, are those who, when you see them, you remember Allah. Then he asked, "Shall I not tell you who the worst of you are?" The worst, he said, are those who go about slandering, who break apart people who love each other, and who lead the innocent into ruin through gossip. It is a teaching she carried out of that room and into the centuries after.

Bread, raisins, and a quiet miracle

Asma was not only a student of the gathering. She used to prepare food and stand outside the mosque, and after the afternoon prayer she would serve the Prophet ﷺ what she had cooked. It was a small, regular act of care, the kind no one writes down.

One day she was waiting with some bread and some raisins and invited him into her home to eat. He came, and he brought a number of the Ansar with him: around forty men, gathered around one small dish. She watched them eat from her plate and drink from her water skin, and there was enough for all of them.

She said afterward that from that day their sustenance never stopped increasing. She had offered what little she had, in front of the Prophet ﷺ, and the giving came back to her many times over. It is the kind of story that turns an ordinary afternoon into something she remembered for the rest of her life.

The ambassador of the women

Her most famous moment is the reason people called her the Orator of the Women. She came to the Prophet ﷺ while he sat among his companions and spoke for all of them at once. May my mother and father be sacrificed for you, she began. I am the ambassador of the women to you, and there is not a woman in the East or the West, hearing of my coming, but would say exactly what I am about to say.

Then she laid it out with care. We believed in you, she said, and in the One who sent you. We are the pillars of our homes. We care for our husbands, we raise your children, we guard the house and the wealth. And the men have been given so much: the Friday prayer, the congregation, the five daily prayers in the mosque, visiting the sick, attending funerals, Hajj after Hajj. And more than any of that, they get to go out and fight by your side.

So we are left at home with all the unseen work, she said, while we hear of all these rewards going to the men. Do we not get a share of it too? It was an honest question, asked without apology, and it landed in a quiet room.

The companions sitting there were taken aback. They were not used to a woman speaking so directly, and it showed on their faces. But the Prophet ﷺ turned his face to them and asked, "Have you ever heard a woman ask about her religion better than this?" In one sentence he reframed what they had just heard: not as boldness to be corrected, but as faith worth admiring.

The answer she carried back

Then he turned back to Asma and gave her the reply she had come for. Go back, he said, and tell the women: when a woman tends her home well, and supports her husband, and seeks what is good, she has all of that reward too. The one who enables good is like the one who does it.

It was not a softer version of the reward for the men. It was the same reward, reached by a different road. The deeds looked different from the outside, but Allah was counting them the same.

She turned and walked away saying takbir, pleased with what she had heard. She had not come to argue, and she did not leave in triumph over anyone. She had come because the women she spoke for did not want to be left out of the reward of Allah, and she went home with the assurance that they were not.

Underneath the whole exchange was a single spirit, the spirit of the Ansar. Men and women, rich and poor, they could not bear the thought of being left out of any good that was on offer. Asma simply gave that longing a voice.

A tent pole at Yarmuk

The same strength that spoke up in a quiet gathering did not stay there. Years after the Prophet ﷺ had passed, the Muslims met the Byzantines at Yarmuk, one of the hardest battles of that era.

Asma was there. She was carrying water to the wounded and tending to them in the heat of the fighting. This was the work she knew, the work of service, only now it was done on a battlefield rather than outside a mosque.

When the Byzantines charged, she did not retreat. She took up a tent pole and is said to have struck down nine soldiers with it. The woman who had asked, years earlier, whether women had a share in the reward of the battlefield, was now standing on one. The strength of the gathering and the strength of the field were, in her, the same strength wearing different clothes.

What Asma's life teaches us

Her story is not about a single bold speech. It is about a person who refused, in every setting, to be left out of doing good.

  • Ask the question you are afraid to ask. She voiced what many felt but no one had said. The Prophet ﷺ did not rebuke her for it; he praised the question and answered it.
  • Ordinary work is not a lesser path. She was told plainly that the care she gave at home carried the same reward as the deeds done in public. Where you are is not a smaller place to earn it.
  • Give what little you have. She offered bread and raisins, and the giving came back to her for the rest of her life. Generosity does not wait for abundance.
  • Strength shows up wherever it is needed. The same woman who spoke for others in a quiet gathering stood firm on a battlefield. One steady character, many settings.

Questions

Who was Asma bint Yazid?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Madinah, known as the Orator of the Women because she would speak to him on their behalf. She narrated many hadith and later fought at the battle of Yarmuk.

Why is Asma bint Yazid called the Orator of the Women?

When the women of Madinah wanted to put a question or a concern to the Prophet ﷺ, they sent Asma, because she was the one who could speak plainly and clearly on their behalf. Her famous address asking whether women shared in the reward earned her the title.

What did Asma bint Yazid ask the Prophet?

She asked, on behalf of all the women she knew, whether women had a share in the great rewards that men could earn through prayer in congregation, Hajj, and going out to fight. The Prophet ﷺ answered that a woman who cares for her home and supports her husband shares fully in that reward.

What can we learn from the life of Asma bint Yazid?

To ask honest questions about our faith, to value the reward in ordinary daily work, to give from what little we have, and to bring a steady character to every part of our lives.

Why this story stays with us

It is easy to feel that the meaningful work belongs to someone else, somewhere more visible, and that our own days of cooking, caring, answering the same small needs over and over, and quietly holding a household together do not count for much. Asma asked that exact worry out loud, in front of the Prophet ﷺ, and the answer she carried back was that none of it is wasted.

Her life is an invitation to do two things. First, to ask the questions that sit heavy on the heart, instead of carrying them in silence. And then, once the answer is clear, to trust it enough to keep going. Whatever your ordinary work is today, the work no one will see or thank you for, do it as if it counts with Allah, because she was told, plainly, that it does.
