Abdullah ibn Abdullah ibn Ubayy

The Son Who Chose the Prophet

Imagine being raised to inherit a city, and watching your own father become the most dangerous man in it. Imagine loving that father, and loving even more the Prophet your father was working to destroy.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Abdullah ibn Ubayy (may Allah be pleased with him): the son of the chief of the hypocrites of Medina, who gave his loyalty, his body, and his heart to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

Abdullah ibn Abdullah ibn Ubayy

The Son Who Chose the Prophet

Born into the wrong house

His father was Abdullah ibn Ubayy, the chief of the hypocrites in Medina. A significant part of the Quran is devoted to the evil and corruption of this man and to all that he managed to stir. He was the one who got people to abandon the Prophet ﷺ in battle, who tried to spread division during the siege of the Trench, who fed the slander against the Prophet's own household. It was one harm after another, from within.

Before the Prophet ﷺ arrived, this was effectively the royal family of the city. The father expected to be crowned king of Yathrib, and the son was being groomed as the prince who would one day succeed him on the throne. By the measure of his world, the boy had everything to gain by following his father and everything to lose by leaving his path.

The son carried his father's name. The Prophet ﷺ gave him a new one: Abdullah, the servant of Allah. From the very beginning, his name announced the choice he had made. He would not be the heir of a hypocrite. He would be a servant of his Lord.

First to step forward

When Islam reached Medina, Abdullah did not hesitate. He embraced it sincerely and dedicated himself to the Prophet ﷺ without reservation. There was no slow softening, no waiting to see which way the city would turn. He simply believed, and stayed.

He was present at Badr, and not everyone among the Ansar was. Those who fought at Badr were a small and honored group, and he was one of them. He stayed firm at the Trench when others wavered. He took the pledge under the tree at Hudaybiyyah. He was there, again and again, in exactly the moments that asked the most of a person and offered the least reward in this world.

He was also one of the few who could read and write. The boy who had been prepared to rule instead became one of the scribes of the Prophet ﷺ. Some accounts say he was among those who wrote down the Quran: the son of the chief hypocrite, recording the very verses that exposed his father's plotting.

Gold for what he gave

He did not only serve with his pen. Fighting alongside the Prophet ﷺ, he lost part of his nose and two of his teeth.

The Prophet ﷺ permitted him to replace the lost part of his nose with gold, and his two teeth with gold as well, because that was the treatment that held the repair together. Scholars later took from this very case the permissibility of using gold to set right what the body has lost. So he carried gold on his face for the rest of his life, a quiet record of where he had stood and what it had cost him.

The Prophet ﷺ trusted him with more than tasks. He appointed him as a commander in battle. While his father worked from within to weaken the Prophet ﷺ, the son was given authority and earned it.

Living inside a paradox

It is worth pausing on how strange his life must have felt from the inside. The father was doing everything he could to push people away from the Prophet ﷺ and to weaken him from within. The son was among the most reliable men around the Prophet ﷺ, proving over and over that he would be there at his side.

When the father got people to flee in battle, the son was one of those who stayed. When the father stirred slander and division in the city, the son carried the Prophet's letters and wrote his words. The same household produced both the deepest wound and one of the steadiest hands.

He never let his father's reputation become his own excuse. He could have hidden behind his name, or grown bitter, or kept one foot in each camp to protect his future. He did none of that. He simply decided, early and clearly, where he belonged, and stayed there for the rest of his life.

It would have been easy to assume that a poisoned tree could only bear poisoned fruit. His life says otherwise. A person is not sentenced to the choices of the house they were born into. The same roof that produced the chief of the hypocrites also produced one of the most trusted of the believers.

A father's treason, a son's restraint

On one journey his father said the words that crossed every line: that once they returned to Medina, the honored one would drive out the humiliated one, meaning that he himself would expel the Prophet ﷺ. It was treason of the highest order, and the punishment in any society of the time would have been death. Allah recorded the boast:

Word reached Abdullah that his father might be executed. The expectation in any society would have been simple: the man had been caught in open treason, and he would pay for it with his life. So the son came to the Prophet ﷺ, and what he said is one of the most striking things a son has ever said. He did not ask for mercy. He made a request that almost no one expects.

He said that no son loved his father more than he did, and that he had always been obedient to him, always honored him, always treated him well. This was not the bitterness of a child who hated his parent. But he feared that if someone else were ordered to carry out the execution, he would not be able to bear the sight of the man who had killed his father. He did not know whether he could ever forgive that man.

And so he was honest about the danger inside himself. He feared his own grief and pride might rise up, that the old ignorance might overtake him, and that he might one day raise his hand against that man and end up killing a believer while telling himself he was defending his father's honor. To close that door before it could open, he said: if my father must be put to death, command me to do it.

They say, 'Once we return to Medina the powerful will drive out the weak,' but power belongs to God, to His Messenger, and to the believers, though the hypocrites do not know this.Quran 63:8

Treat him with the best of manners

The Prophet ﷺ refused. There would be no execution, and certainly not by the son's hand. He told Abdullah that they would treat his father with the best of manners and keep good company with him for as long as he remained among them.

And he told the son something a son needed to hear: that is still your father, so treat him well. The Prophet ﷺ had not come to turn a child against his parent, even a parent like this one. The loyalty Abdullah owed to the Prophet ﷺ did not cancel the duty he owed to the man who raised him. He could hold both at once, and he was being asked to.

Later, when the father tried to enter Medina after his boast, it was his own son standing at the gates who stopped him. Abdullah told him plainly that he would not let him pass until he admitted who was truly honored and who was truly humbled. The son drew the line his father never would.

The shirt, the prayer, and the limit of mercy

This is where the story shows its deepest human complication. When Abdullah ibn Ubayy died, his son was devastated. Whatever his father had been to the city, he was still a father to his son, and the grief was real. He came to the Prophet ﷺ and asked for the Prophet's own shirt to use as his father's burial cloth. The Prophet ﷺ took it off and gave it to him.

Then the son asked the Prophet ﷺ to come and pray over his father and to seek forgiveness for him. This was the man who had slandered the Prophet ﷺ, who had tried to overthrow him on more than one occasion, who had worked to destabilize the city from the very start. The Prophet ﷺ went out to lead the funeral prayer anyway, and someone tried to stop him: you would pray for this man, after everything he did?

That is when Allah revealed that even if the Prophet ﷺ were to seek forgiveness for him seventy times, it would not be granted, because the man had never truly believed. The verse drew a line that mercy itself could not cross. Yet the Prophet ﷺ had still answered a grieving son with his own shirt and his own presence.

It makes no difference [Prophet] whether you ask forgiveness for them or not: God will not forgive them even if you ask seventy times, because they reject God and His Messenger. God does not guide those who rebel against Him.Quran 9:80

What Abdullah's life teaches us

His story is full of twists and turns, but underneath them all is a single, steady choice: he stayed where the truth was, even when the line ran straight through his own family. A few things he leaves us are worth carrying into an ordinary life.

  • Truth comes before lineage. He was born to inherit a throne. He chose to be a servant beside the Prophet ﷺ instead, and never used his father's name as an excuse for anything.
  • Loyalty and kindness can coexist. He gave his whole heart to the Prophet ﷺ and still loved and honored his father. He was not asked to hate one in order to follow the other.
  • Know your own heart, and guard it. He did not trust his own anger. He feared what grief might make him do, and arranged things to keep himself from a sin he had not yet committed.
  • Mercy can be given even where it is not deserved. The Prophet ﷺ answered a hostile man's funeral with his own shirt and his own presence, simply because a grieving son had asked.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Abdullah ibn Ubayy?

A sincere and devoted companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from among the Ansar of Medina. He was the son of Abdullah ibn Ubayy, the chief of the hypocrites, yet he himself was loyal to the Prophet ﷺ from the beginning.

Why did the Prophet change his name to Abdullah?

His original name was changed by the Prophet ﷺ to Abdullah, meaning the servant of Allah. The new name reflected the faith he had chosen, in contrast to the path of his father.

Did he really offer to execute his own father?

When he heard his father might be put to death for treason, he asked that he be the one to carry it out, so that no other man would do it and leave him unable to forgive. The Prophet ﷺ refused and chose to treat the father with kindness instead.

What can we learn from his life?

That truth can be chosen over family pride, that loyalty to the truth does not require cruelty to those we love, and that we should guard our own hearts against anger before it leads us into sin.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are not asked to choose between a crown and the truth. But many of us are quietly pulled between what is right and the people we are tied to by blood, habit, or loyalty. Abdullah shows that it is possible to hold both honesty and tenderness at once: to refuse what is wrong without abandoning the person who is wrong, and to keep honoring a parent even while refusing to follow him.

His deepest fear was not his father. It was his own heart, and what grief and pride might do to it if he was not careful. That is a fear worth borrowing. Today, ask where your loyalties are really pointed, and ask Allah to keep your heart soft toward people and firm toward the truth at the same time. That balance is hard to hold for even a single day, and it is exactly what his life was made of.
