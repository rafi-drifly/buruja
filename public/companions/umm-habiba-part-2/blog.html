Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan

Royalty Redefined

She had left Makkah a young woman and stayed away for thirteen years. In that time she lost her livelihood, lost her husband, raised a daughter alone in a foreign land, and watched her own father become the chief enemy of the faith she had chosen. At any point, she could have walked back into comfort. She never did.

This is the second part of the story of Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan (may Allah be pleased with her): a woman whose steadiness through exile was answered with marriage to the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and who, when real power finally came within reach, quietly turned away from it.

Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan

Royalty Redefined

A faith that would not bend

Before we follow her home, it is worth pausing on what she had already endured. She became Muslim in Makkah and faced the anger of Abu Sufyan and her family for it. When the call came to migrate, she did not hold back, even though her position could have shielded her from persecution if she had stayed.

She kept her faith through the long migration to Abyssinia. She kept it when her husband, Ubaydullah ibn Jahsh, reportedly left Islam and then died there. She kept it while her father led the opposition against the Prophet ﷺ back home. And she kept it through years of exile, alone with her young daughter, far from everything familiar.

There were many doors out of her faith, and she walked through none of them. She only grew firmer. This was a woman whose istiqamah, whose steadiness, was tested from every side, and held.

The journey home

Her marriage contract to the Prophet ﷺ had been written while she was still in Abyssinia, but six years passed before they would actually meet as husband and wife. In that long wait, her daughter Habiba grew up before her eyes.

At last the time came. Seven years after the migration, in the calm that followed the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah, Jafar ibn Abi Talib (may Allah be pleased with him) led a delegation of sixteen families from Abyssinia to Madinah. Now there was safety on the road, and joy in the destination.

For Umm Habiba the joy was layered. She had been away from her land, her family, and her home for thirteen years. She had sacrificed her livelihood, lost her husband, and had a child in exile. And now she would meet the Prophet ﷺ again, this time not only as a believer, but as his wife.

Reunited friends, remembering Abyssinia

In Madinah she was reunited with Umm Salama (may Allah be pleased with her), with whom she shared a deep friendship. Their lives had run in parallel: both had left Makkah for Abyssinia with their husbands, both had children there, both were widowed, and both, by very different paths, came to be wives of the Prophet ﷺ.

They would sit and reminisce about Abyssinia together, and describe it to the Prophet ﷺ. In one narration carried by Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), Umm Habiba and Umm Salama told him about a church they had seen there, with its images and tombs.

He warned them about a people who, when a righteous person among them died, would build a place of worship over the grave and fill it with such images. Those, he said, would be among the worst of people before Allah. In that single hadith, three of the most prolific narrators of the Prophet ﷺ are gathered: Aisha, Umm Salama, and Umm Habiba.

There was a tenderness in Umm Habiba that shows in another moment. She once asked the Prophet ﷺ whether he might also marry her sister. Surprised, he asked if that was truly what she wanted, and she said she would be glad for her own sister to share in this honour. He told her gently that it was not lawful for him to marry two sisters. Even where Allah had widened the rules of marriage for the Prophet ﷺ alone, the prohibition on marrying sisters still stood, and he kept to it.

The father at the door

After the Makkans broke the treaty, Abu Sufyan came to Madinah to try to repair it. He went to the Prophet ﷺ, who rose and walked away. He went to Abu Bakr, then Umar, then Fatima (may Allah be pleased with them). Every door closed. At last he came to the home of his own daughter, whom he had not seen in years.

Inside, Umm Habiba was sitting on the Prophet's simple bed, a palm mattress with a single blanket, used as a couch by day. As her father moved to sit beside her on it, she quietly folded the mattress away and let him sit on the floor.

He asked her whether the bed was too good for him, or he too good for the bed. She answered plainly: this was the bed of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, and he was not worthy to sit where the Prophet ﷺ sat. She would not let the man who still opposed her faith share that place. The doors had closed on Abu Sufyan completely.

It is worth noticing what kind of place she was guarding. The home of the most honoured man in Arabia held a palm mattress and one blanket. Her father, a chief used to comfort and rank, found in the Prophet's house neither, and a daughter who would not trade her faith for his approval.

A long prostration for her family

When Makkah was finally opened, the Prophet ﷺ met its people not with revenge but with mercy. He gave a general amnesty to those who had spent decades fighting him, sparing only a few who posed an active danger. He even gave Abu Sufyan a measure of honour, declaring that whoever entered his house would be safe. This was never about ego or revenge. It was about the mission, and about leaving every door open for people to change.

It was then that Abu Sufyan, and her brother Muawiyah, accepted Islam. Umm Habiba had never stopped loving her family or longing for their faith. It is said that the two longest prostrations of her life were the day her father accepted Islam and the day her brother did. She wanted for them exactly what she had wanted for herself.

Once the Prophet ﷺ found her making a tender supplication, asking Allah for a long life in the company of her husband, her father, and her brother. Gently, he taught her something deeper: that lifespans and provisions are already fixed, and that she would do better to ask Allah for protection from the punishment of the grave and the Fire. He turned her hope away from the days of this world and toward the next.

Choosing worship over power

Umm Habiba lived a long life, long enough to see her brother rise and the Umayyad house come to power before her eyes. Privilege was now hers for the taking. She did not take it.

She avoided politics. She avoided the civil strife of her time. She kept clear of the trials that pulled others in, and gave her years instead to worship, to teaching, and to carrying the words of the Prophet ﷺ. When her family rose to the height of worldly power, she stepped quietly in the other direction.

Yet standing apart from the strife did not make her cold to suffering. When Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) was besieged and even denied water, she rode out on a mule with water hidden under her garment, hoping her standing might let her reach him. A young man blocked her, snatched the water and threw it to the ground, then cut the reins so the mule bolted and she fell before everyone. She used what privilege she had not for herself, but to help a man being wronged.

When she was dying, she called for Aisha and asked forgiveness for any small disputes that had passed between them, the ordinary friction of shared lives. Aisha forgave her and asked Allah to release her from any account. Umm Habiba smiled and said, You have made me happy, may Allah make you happy. Then she sent for Umm Salama and said the same. Three of the great mothers of the believers, settling every account before she went.

A legacy carried in prayer

Umm Habiba narrated sixty-five hadith of the Prophet ﷺ, ten in Bukhari and eight in Muslim. Her brothers learned from her, her daughter Habiba learned from her, and students like Zaynab bint Umm Salama and Urwa ibn al-Zubayr carried her narrations forward.

Among them is one of the best loved practices in Islam. She narrated that the Prophet ﷺ said whoever prays twelve units of voluntary prayer in a day and night, Allah will build for them a house in Paradise. The way she taught it is unforgettable. I never left those twelve, she said, since I heard them from the Messenger of Allah ﷺ. Each narrator after her added the same vow: I never left them since I heard them.

It was not a chain of words passed along. It was a chain of people who lived what they learned. Those twelve units, two before Fajr, four before and two after Dhuhr, two after Maghrib and two after Isha, reach us today through her.

What Umm Habiba's life teaches us

Her story is not built on a single moment. It is built on holding a position, year after year, when no one would have blamed her for letting go.

  • Steadiness is its own kind of strength. Door after door opened back toward comfort and safety. She kept her faith through all of them, and only grew firmer for it.
  • Love your family and still hold your line. She longed for her father's faith and rejoiced when it came, yet she would not let his opposition seat him in a place that was not his.
  • Power is not the prize. When her own family reached the height of worldly power, she turned toward worship and teaching instead, and avoided the trials that pulled others in.
  • Live what you learn. She did not just pass on the reward of twelve units of prayer. She never once left them, and that is why the practice still reaches us.

Questions

Who was Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan?

An early Muslim and one of the Mothers of the Believers. She was the daughter of Abu Sufyan, migrated to Abyssinia for her faith, and later married the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. She became one of the most important female narrators of hadith.

Why did she refuse to let her father sit on the bed?

At that time Abu Sufyan was still leading the opposition to the Prophet ﷺ. When he came to her home in Madinah, she folded away the Prophet's mattress so he could not sit on it, telling him it was the Messenger's place and he was not worthy of it.

What is Umm Habiba best known for narrating?

She narrated the hadith that whoever prays twelve voluntary units in a day and night, Allah will build them a house in Paradise: two before Fajr, four before and two after Dhuhr, two after Maghrib, and two after Isha.

What can we learn from the life of Umm Habiba?

Steadfastness through long hardship, loving your family without compromising your faith, choosing worship over worldly power, and living what you learn rather than only repeating it.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are never tested by a single dramatic choice. We are tested slowly, by the option to quietly let go: of a habit we know is right, of a boundary that has become inconvenient, of a faith that would be easier to set down. Umm Habiba was offered that exit again and again, across thirteen years and then across a lifetime, and she kept choosing to hold.

Her life asks a gentle question of an ordinary day. When ease pulls one way and conviction the other, which do we follow? She loved her family and still kept her line. She had every reason to chase status and chose worship instead. Carry even a little of that steadiness into today, and her story has done its work.
