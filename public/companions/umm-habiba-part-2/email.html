She had every reason to walk away, and never did

Preview: Thirteen years in exile, a father who opposed her faith, and a steadiness that never broke.

Assalamu alaikum,

Some people are tested by one dramatic moment. Umm Habiba bint Abi Sufyan (may Allah be pleased with her) was tested slowly, for years, by the simple option to quietly let go.

She became Muslim in Makkah and faced her family's anger for it. When the call to migrate came, she did not hold back, though her standing could have protected her if she had stayed. She kept her faith through the long exile in Abyssinia. She kept it when her husband died there. She kept it while her own father led the opposition to the Prophet ﷺ back home.

Thirteen years she was away, alone with her young daughter, far from everything familiar. There were many doors back to comfort. She walked through none of them. She only grew firmer.

Her steadiness was answered. In the peace after Hudaybiyyah she came home to Madinah, not only as a believer but as the wife of the Prophet ﷺ. There she was reunited with her old friend Umm Salama, and the two would sit and remember their years in exile.

Then her father came to her door, still opposing the faith, hoping to use her. She was sitting on the Prophet's simple bed, a palm mattress with a single blanket. As he moved to sit beside her, she quietly folded the mattress away and let him sit on the floor. This is the bed of the Messenger of Allah ﷺ, she told him, and you are not worthy to sit where he sits. She loved her father, and longed for the day he would believe. But while he opposed the truth, she would not move her line.

Years later, her family rose to the height of worldly power. Umm Habiba turned the other way. No politics, no privilege. She gave her life to worship and to teaching instead.

There is a lesson in her that is easy to miss: faith is often kept not in one grand stand, but in the quiet refusal to let go. When ease pulls one way and conviction the other, she kept choosing to hold.

May Allah be pleased with her, and grant us a measure of her steadiness.
