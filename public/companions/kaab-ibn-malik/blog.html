Ka'ab ibn Malik

The One Who Told the Truth

He had stood with the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ when it cost everything: at the secret pledge in Makkah, in the worst hour of Uhud, in battle after battle. Then, one summer, he stayed home for no good reason at all.

This is the story of Ka'ab ibn Malik (may Allah be pleased with him): a poet and a soldier whose single mistake, and the way he answered for it, became the most famous account of repentance in the whole of our history.

The Companions

Ka'ab ibn Malik

The One Who Told the Truth

A poet from the first believers of Medina

Ka'ab ibn Malik was one of the earliest people of Medina to accept Islam. Both his parents had died before the message reached the city, and he brought his two brothers, Sahl and Abdullah, into faith with him. He was there on the night of the second pledge at Aqabah, when a small group from Medina slipped into Makkah and gave their allegiance to a man his own people had cast out.

He was one of the three poets of the Prophet ﷺ. His was war poetry: verse of strength and courage that he carried onto the battlefield to lift the spirits of the believers. It was said that some who came to fight heard the confidence in his words and laid down their arms before a single blow was struck.

When the believers of Makkah and Medina were paired as brothers, the Prophet ﷺ joined Ka'ab with az-Zubayr ibn al-Awwam (may Allah be pleased with him), his own close companion. To be chosen as the brother of such a man told everyone, from the very beginning, the standing that Ka'ab held.

He never forgot that night at Aqabah. People spoke of Badr for the rest of their lives, but Ka'ab used to say he would not trade the night of the pledge for the day of Badr, famous as Badr was. To slip into Makkah in secret, with no safety at all, and give his word to a man hunted by his own people: that, he said, was the most beloved night of his life.

The day he would not leave the Prophet's side

Ka'ab joined every major expedition with the Prophet ﷺ but two. On the day of Uhud, when the Muslims were struck from behind and a rumour spread that the Prophet ﷺ had been killed, the field fell into confusion and despair. Ka'ab was the first to catch sight of him alive, and he called out the good news so the believers would not lose heart.

Then he did something almost no one would. He swapped his own armour with the Prophet ﷺ, drawing the enemy toward himself and away from him. He carried seventeen serious wounds out of that day. He and az-Zubayr, both badly hurt, held each other up and kept fighting.

This is why his later mistake stung so deeply. A man who had given that much, who had stepped in front of danger for the Prophet ﷺ, was not the kind of man anyone expected to fall short.

When words are arrows

The Quran does not speak gently of poets, and this worried Ka'ab. He came to the Prophet ﷺ and said that Allah had revealed about the poets what He had revealed, as if to ask whether the gift he used was a thing to be ashamed of.

The Prophet ﷺ answered him plainly. The one who strives, he said, strives with his sword and with his tongue. And he told Ka'ab that the words he sent against the enemy were like arrows loosed at them. His poetry was not a lesser kind of service. It was service.

On another day the Prophet ﷺ recalled a verse Ka'ab had once composed and said that Allah had not forgotten it, for Allah does not forget any good a person does. When Ka'ab asked which words he meant, the Prophet ﷺ turned to Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him), who recited the lines back from memory. It was a quiet honour, and a reminder that no good word is ever truly lost.

The summer he stayed behind

Years passed. The call came to march on Tabuk: a long road in the burning heat, through land with no water, toward a far larger army. The Prophet ﷺ told the people to prepare, and to keep preparing.

Ka'ab meant to go. But for the first time in his life he had a little ease, two camels and a garden whose fruit was ripening, and he told himself he would get ready when the time came. He went out one morning and came back having done nothing. He would go tomorrow, he thought. Then one morning the army was simply gone, and he had missed it.

He walked out into a Medina emptied of its best, left among the openly insincere and those excused by illness. Forty years later, blind and old, telling the story to his son, he still paused in the middle of it to say plainly: I wish I had gone.

Nothing would save me but the truth

When word came that the Prophet ﷺ was returning, Ka'ab thought of all the excuses he could give. He was a poet; he knew how to soften the truth. But the closer the return came, the clearer it grew to him that no story would save him. Only the truth would.

The Prophet ﷺ reached Medina, prayed two units in the mosque, and sat. Around eighty men came forward with their excuses, and he accepted them at face value and left their hearts to Allah. Then it was Ka'ab's turn. The Prophet ﷺ smiled the smile of a man holding back anger and asked what had kept him back.

Ka'ab answered honestly. "By Allah," he said, "if I were sitting before anyone else, I could talk my way out of his anger. But I know that if I lie to you today to please you, Allah will soon make you angry with me. And if I tell you the truth that angers you, I can hope for a good end from Allah." He had no excuse, he admitted. He had never been stronger or better able to go, and he simply had not. The Prophet ﷺ said he had told the truth, and told him to wait for Allah to decide his case.

Fifty nights, and the test that came with them

Two other sincere men, Murarah ibn ar-Rabi and Hilal ibn Umayyah, had done the same and refused to lie. The Prophet ﷺ forbade the Muslims from speaking to the three of them. The city Ka'ab loved turned silent around him. He greeted people and they would not answer. He prayed near the Prophet ﷺ and watched to see whether his lips moved in reply. When he asked his own dear cousin, Abu Qatadah, whether he knew that Ka'ab loved Allah and His Messenger, the answer was only silence, and then: "Allah and His Messenger know best." It broke him.

Into that loneliness came a letter from a foreign king: leave the man who has abandoned you, come to us, and we will honour you. Ka'ab saw it at once for what it was, another test, and he took it to the oven and burned it.

On the fiftieth night, after the dawn prayer, sitting on his roof with the wide earth feeling too tight to hold him, he heard a voice carry across the hills: rejoice, Ka'ab, rejoice. He fell at once into prostration. Allah had accepted his repentance, and the Prophet ﷺ, his face shining like the moon, told him: rejoice in the best day of your life since the day your mother bore you.

And to the three men who stayed behind: when the earth, for all its spaciousness, closed in around them, when their very souls closed in around them, when they realized that the only refuge from God was with Him, He turned to them in mercy in order for them to return [to Him]. God is the Ever Relenting, the Most Merciful.Quran 9:118

A tongue that never lied again

In his joy, Ka'ab offered to give away all his wealth as thanks. The Prophet ﷺ told him gently to keep some of it, that this was better for him. So Ka'ab kept only what he had at Khaybar and gave the rest for the sake of Allah.

Then he made a vow that would shape the rest of his days. Allah had saved him through honesty, so he resolved never to tell a lie again as long as he lived. He used to say that he knew of no one more severely tested for telling the truth than himself, and that no blessing in his life was greater than that one truth he had spoken before the Prophet ﷺ.

Allah confirmed all of it in the Quran: He had turned to the three who stayed behind, and He counted them among the truthful. Ka'ab lived a long and peaceful life after this, going blind in his old age, his sons leading him to the mosque where people came to ask him about the days he had seen.

He stayed clear of wrongdoing to the end. When unrest later reached its worst and Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him) was besieged, Ka'ab refused to take any part in the bloodshed, and grieved at the abandonment of a righteous man. The one who had learned the cost of a single mistake spent the rest of his days being careful with his deeds and his words.

You who believe, be mindful of God: stand with those who are true.Quran 9:119

What Ka'ab's life teaches us

His story is not really about a battle he missed. It is about the moment he chose, with everything to lose, to tell the truth.

  • The truth costs less than it seems. A lie would have ended his trial in a minute. The truth took fifty hard nights, and then it lasted forever. He never regretted which one he chose.
  • Small delays grow into great regret. He did not refuse the call. He just put it off, one comfortable day at a time, until the moment was gone. Forty years later it still ached.
  • Owning your mistake is the way back. He did not blame his garden or his camels or the heat. He sat before the Prophet ﷺ and said plainly: I had no excuse. That honesty was the door Allah opened.
  • Hold fast when the test arrives in disguise. At his lowest, an easier life was offered to him by those who wished him harm. He recognised it and burned it, and trusted that relief would come from Allah alone.

Questions

Who was Ka'ab ibn Malik?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from Medina, one of the first believers there and one of his three poets. He fought at Uhud and most other expeditions, and is best known for the account of his repentance after staying behind from Tabuk.

Why did Ka'ab ibn Malik stay behind from Tabuk?

He had no real excuse. For the first time he had some wealth and a ripening garden, and he kept putting off his preparations until the army left without him. He admitted this openly to the Prophet ﷺ rather than offer a false excuse.

What happened to Ka'ab after Tabuk?

The Prophet ﷺ instructed the Muslims not to speak to him and two others for fifty nights, until Allah revealed the acceptance of their repentance in the Quran. Ka'ab then vowed never to lie again, and lived a long life as a respected narrator of the Prophet's life.

What can we learn from Ka'ab ibn Malik?

That honesty is worth its cost, that small delays can lead to lasting regret, and that the surest way back to Allah is to own our mistakes truthfully rather than excuse them.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will never miss an army marching to Tabuk. But all of us know the smaller version of Ka'ab's mistake: the thing we meant to do, kept putting off, and let slip away while life felt comfortable. And all of us know the moment afterward, when it would be so easy to reach for an excuse.

Ka'ab's life leaves us one quiet question for the day ahead. When the truth is harder than the lie, which one will we choose? He chose the truth, carried its weight, and was never sorry. That choice is still open to every one of us, in the ordinary moments no one else may ever see.
