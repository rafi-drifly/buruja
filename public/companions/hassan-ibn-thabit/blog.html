The Companions

Hassan ibn Thabit

The Poet of the Prophet

If you had walked into the mosque of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, you would have seen his pulpit, the place from which he taught. A few feet away you would have seen a second one, lower and plainer, and you might have wondered who was allowed to stand there.

This is the story of Hassan ibn Thabit (may Allah be pleased with him): a man who never carried a sword, who defended the Prophet ﷺ with words instead, and who shows us that even a deep fall can end in mercy.

The Companions

Hassan ibn Thabit

The Poet of the Prophet

The poet the whole world knew

Hassan was from Banu Najjar, the maternal relatives of the Prophet ﷺ in Medina, a subtribe of Khazraj. In a sense he was a distant cousin of the Messenger ﷺ from his mother's side of the city.

He was a famous poet from a young age, and his fame was not limited to Medina. He was perhaps the only poet from that region known across the wider world. Kings sent for him. He travelled to the courts of the Ghassanids in Syria, and he composed for the Romans and the Persians, rivals who would each call on the same gifted tongue.

In Medina, the Ansar were two tribes, Khazraj and Aws, with old quarrels between them. Hassan was the poet of Khazraj, and his poetry was sharp. He would take an opponent apart with his words, expose their flaws, and leave them humiliated. That was his trade, and no poet stood a chance against him.

A family that believed

When Islam reached his household, something quietly remarkable happened. Both of his parents and all eight of his siblings became Muslim.

His father, Mundhir, was in his eighties when he accepted Islam, and he was counted among the veterans of Badr, one of the oldest companions on that day. His mother was known for her intellect, so much so that Hassan would sometimes call himself the son of his mother. His brothers fought in the battles, and two of them died as martyrs.

His sisters, too, were companions. One of them narrated a single, tender hadith: the Prophet ﷺ once visited her home and drank from a water skin hanging there, and she cut away the part he had drunk from and kept it for the rest of her life. Two of his sisters were poets in their own right.

Hassan would be one of this believing family too. But his path would look very different from theirs, because no two people, even from the same home, come out the same way.

The man sent to mock him

Hassan was sixty years old when the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Medina, seven years older than the Messenger ﷺ himself. He was one of the few elders of his age still living, for many men of his generation had been killed in the tribal wars. He, the poet who insulted rival tribes for a living, had somehow survived them.

Some of those unhappy with the Prophet's rise paid Hassan to do what he did best. Go and see this man, they said, and find his flaws. Hold nothing back. So he went out, unimpressed, expecting to do his usual work.

Then he saw the Prophet ﷺ, and instead of an insult he found himself in love. He had nothing bad to say. His famous lines about that meeting have come down to us: my eye has never seen anyone like you, and no woman has given birth to one more beautiful. You were created free of every flaw, as if you were made exactly as you wished. He looked at the Prophet ﷺ and said, in effect, this is him. There is nothing here to mock.

The man who would not fight

Hassan never fought in a single battle beside the Prophet ﷺ. This was not laziness or half-hearted faith. He had a genuine, deep fear of the battlefield that he could not overcome, and the Prophet ﷺ allowed him to stay back because of it.

There is a telling moment from the Battle of the Trench. Safiyya bint Abd al-Muttalib (may Allah be pleased with her), the aunt of the Prophet ﷺ, was sheltering with the women and children, and Hassan, by then nearly seventy, was with them. When an enemy scout approached, she asked Hassan to deal with him. He answered, gently, that this was not his task.

So Safiyya took a pole herself. She waited until the man was close, struck him down, and dealt with him alone. When his companions saw the body thrown out, they assumed an army was inside and fled. Hassan simply could not enter that world. His battle was his poetry, and to that he held.

Respond, and the angel will support you

As the enemies of the Prophet ﷺ began to attack him in verse, some of the companions wanted to reply in kind. The Prophet ﷺ looked around the room. What stops the men who defended me with their weapons, he asked, from defending me with their tongues? Hassan said: I am the one for this.

The Prophet ﷺ told him, respond, and the angel will support you. Then he gave him a careful instruction. How will you attack their tribes when I am one of them? Hassan answered that he would draw the Prophet ﷺ out from among them the way a single hair is drawn from dough. To get the lineage exactly right, he was sent to Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him), who knew the tribes of Makkah completely.

So Hassan sat with Abu Bakr, taking notes, asking after every family and subfamily of Makkah: whose father was this, whose daughter was that. When he finally stood and spoke, he dissected every clan of Quraysh with precision, naming the maternal and paternal sides exactly where it mattered, yet never once touching the Prophet ﷺ. His opponents said there was no way a poet from Medina had worked all of this out alone.

In one of these poems, preserved in the collection of Muslim, he answered those who had mocked the Prophet ﷺ. You mocked a man of virtue and righteousness, he said, the Messenger of Allah, whose nature is loyalty. The scholars explain his meaning simply: the lion does not answer the barking dog. Of such poetry the Prophet ﷺ prayed, O Allah, support him with the Holy Spirit. Hassan had given his gift, once sold to kings, to the service of the Messenger ﷺ.

The mistake, and the way back

Then came the part of his story that is hardest to tell. When a cruel slander was spread about Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), Hassan, the very man who defended the Prophet ﷺ, did not hold his tongue when he heard it, and he repeated it. He was not the one who started it, but he passed it on, and it was a grave error.

There is a lesson here worth sitting with: sometimes our greatest strength is also our greatest weakness. Hassan had a quick tongue and a quick mind, trained to respond, and in that moment the very gift that served the Prophet ﷺ tripped him. He was also an older man by now, and a careless word slips more easily through fewer filters. When the truth was made clear, he was punished for it. But he was not cast out of the community. He sought forgiveness, and he was forgiven.

The harm rippled, as such things do. Safwan ibn al-Mu'attal, an innocent man caught up in the same slander, heard that Hassan had repeated it and waited for him on a path. I am not a poet like you, he told him, I only know how to give a beating, and he struck him. It was the wrong response, and the Prophet ﷺ defused it gently. Let it go, Hassan, he said, even though it was his own wife who had been wronged.

Hassan poured his regret into a poem praising Aisha (RA): a pure woman who never backbites, who would never touch the honour of the innocent. And Aisha herself became his defender. Years later, when people tried to speak ill of him in front of her, she would stop them. Do not say that, she would say. He used to defend the Prophet ﷺ. She hoped Allah would admit him to Paradise for the verses he composed in the Prophet's honour.

What history kept

Hassan lived to be a hundred and twenty years old, sixty years before Islam and sixty within it, and in his last years he went blind. He carried the mark of his mistake to the end of his life, and there were always some who would not let him forget it. But those closest to the matter would not allow him to be defined by it.

When a young man once called him cursed, Aisha (RA) refused to let it stand. He is not cursed, she said. This is a man who strove alongside the Prophet ﷺ with himself and with his tongue. He should not be put down for one mistake.

There is one more quiet detail. None of the poetry Hassan composed for the tyrants and kings of his earlier life has survived. What was not done for Allah simply fell away. What remains is the poetry he gave to the Messenger ﷺ. In the end he goes down in history not as the man who slandered, but as the poet who defended the Prophet.

What Hassan's life teaches us

His story is not a straight climb. It is a real life, with a real gift, a real fall, and a real return, and that is exactly why it speaks to us.

  • Your skill is meant for something higher. Hassan could have sold his words to anyone. He gave his finest gift to the cause he believed in, and that is the part that lasted.
  • Your greatest strength can become your weakness. The quick tongue that defended the Prophet ﷺ was the same tongue that repeated the slander. Watch the gift you are proudest of.
  • Guard your tongue against what you cannot verify. He did not invent the rumour. He simply did not stop it at his own lips, and that was enough to be a serious wrong.
  • A person is more than their worst moment. He fell gravely, sought forgiveness, and was forgiven. He is remembered for what he defended, not for the day he slipped.

Questions

Who was Hassan ibn Thabit?

A companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ from the Ansar of Medina, and a famous poet known across the wider world. He is remembered as the poet who defended the Prophet ﷺ with his verses.

Why did Hassan ibn Thabit never fight in battle?

He had a genuine and deep fear of the battlefield that he could not overcome, and the Prophet ﷺ allowed him to stay back. His contribution was his poetry, which he used to defend the Prophet ﷺ against those who attacked him in verse.

What mistake did Hassan ibn Thabit make?

He was one of those who repeated the false slander against Aisha (RA). He did not begin it, but he passed it on. He was punished, he sought forgiveness, and he was forgiven, and Aisha later defended him from those who tried to shame him for it.

What can we learn from the life of Hassan ibn Thabit?

To use our gifts for what matters most, to guard the tongue against what we cannot verify, and to remember that a person who falls and turns back is more than their worst moment.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us are not warriors, and few of us will ever take a public stand. But every one of us has some gift, large or small, and a tongue that can build or wound in a single careless breath. Hassan's life sits in that ordinary tension. He gave his best to something good, and he also slipped in the place he was strongest.

His story is an invitation to take whatever you have been given and spend it on what is worth spending it on, and to be slow with your words when something unverified reaches you. And it is a quiet reassurance: a mistake, even a serious one, is not the end of the road. The way back is forgiveness sought and forgiveness given. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
