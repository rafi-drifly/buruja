The man with a pulpit beside the Prophet's

Preview: He never carried a sword. He defended the Prophet with words instead.

Assalamu alaikum,

If you had walked into the mosque of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, you would have seen his pulpit. A few feet away you would have seen a second one, lower and plainer. The man allowed to stand there was Hassan ibn Thabit (may Allah be pleased with him).

Hassan was a poet the whole world knew. Kings in Syria, Rome, and Persia sent for him, and no opponent stood a chance against his words. Some of the enemies of Islam once paid him to go and mock the Prophet ﷺ and find his flaws. He went out unimpressed. Then he saw the Prophet ﷺ and found, to his own surprise, that he had nothing bad to say. "My eye has never seen anyone like you," he said. "You were created free of every flaw."

Hassan never fought in a single battle. This was not weakness of faith but a deep fear of the battlefield he could not overcome, and the Prophet ﷺ let him stay back. His battle was his poetry. When enemies attacked the Prophet ﷺ in verse, Hassan stood and replied. "Respond," the Prophet ﷺ told him, "and the angel will support you."

And then he fell. When a cruel slander spread about Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her), Hassan, of all people, did not hold his tongue. He did not begin the rumour, but he repeated it, and it was a grave wrong. He was punished. He sought forgiveness. He was forgiven.

There is a lesson in him that is easy to miss: sometimes our greatest strength is also our greatest weakness. The quick tongue that defended the Prophet ﷺ was the same tongue that slipped. Yet a person is more than their worst moment. Years later Aisha herself defended him: "He is not cursed. He strove alongside the Prophet ﷺ with himself and his tongue."

None of the poetry he once sold to kings has survived. What remains is what he gave to the Messenger ﷺ. He goes down in history not as the man who slandered, but as the poet who defended the Prophet.

May Allah be pleased with him, and teach us to use what we have been given for what truly matters.
