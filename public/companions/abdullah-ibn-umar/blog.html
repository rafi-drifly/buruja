The Companions

Abdullah ibn Umar

The One Who Followed Every Footstep

Imagine a man walking through Madinah with his eyes down, watching where his feet land. He is not lost. He is trying to set his own foot in the exact place he once saw the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ set his.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with him): the son of a great companion, and the man who held on to the smallest details of the Prophet's ﷺ life as though his own depended on them.

The Companions

Abdullah ibn Umar

The One Who Followed Every Footstep

The boy the Prophet asked about

Abdullah was born about a year after the revelation began, around 611. His father was Umar ibn al-Khattab, who at first stood against Islam and later became one of its greatest defenders. His mother was Zaynab bint Mazun, and his full sister was Hafsa, who would become one of the Mothers of the Believers.

He grew up watching his father make every decision around one question: what does Allah and His Messenger want. That question became his own. The Prophet ﷺ used to ask after him by name. When Umar came without his son, he would say, bring Abdullah, bring Abdullah.

He was too young to fight at Badr and Uhud. At fourteen he stood ready, and the Prophet ﷺ, seeing how young he was, sent him home. At fifteen he presented himself again, and this time he was allowed to take his place at the Battle of the Trench. Of all the young people around the Prophet ﷺ, it was said that none was more disciplined or more devoted than him.

He had a way into the Prophet's ﷺ life that few others had. His sister Hafsa was married to the Prophet ﷺ, so one of those homes was open to him. He would slip in behind the Prophet ﷺ and pray the voluntary prayers at his side, then carry out to the rest of the community what the Prophet ﷺ was like inside his own house.

A hand on the shoulder

One day the Prophet ﷺ put his hand on the young man's shoulder and drew him close to say something. It became one of the most repeated pieces of advice in Islam: be in this world as if you are a stranger, or a traveller passing through.

Abdullah took it to heart for the rest of his life. He would add his own words to it afterward: if you reach the evening, do not wait for the morning, and if you reach the morning, do not wait for the evening. Take from your health before your sickness, and from your life before your death.

He longed for any reason to be near the Prophet ﷺ. Once he saw a difficult dream of the fire, and angels who told him not to be afraid. He was too shy to tell the Prophet ﷺ himself, so he sent it through his sister Hafsa. The reply came back gently: what a good young man Abdullah is, if only he would pray a little in the night. From that night, his son later said, his father never slept a full night again.

The Prophet ﷺ handled this sensitive young man with great care. On one journey Abdullah's camel kept pushing ahead of the Prophet's ﷺ, and the boy was mortified, sure he looked arrogant. The Prophet ﷺ simply stopped, bought the camel from Umar, and gave it back to Abdullah as a gift: do with it as you wish. A small kindness, recorded in passing, so the young man would not carry the weight of an accident.

The man who counted the grey hairs

If another companion was known for his love of the Quran, Abdullah was known for his love of the way the Prophet ﷺ lived. He counted the grey hairs in the Prophet's ﷺ beard and could point to exactly where they were. He narrated more than two thousand hadith, and it was said he never spoke of the Prophet ﷺ without tears coming to his eyes.

He was painfully careful. When he passed on a hadith he would not add a single word or drop one. He prayed in every spot he had seen the Prophet ﷺ pray. He once heard the Prophet ﷺ say a certain door should be left for the women, and though the door was never formally assigned, Abdullah never walked through it again as long as he lived.

There is a small story that says everything. People once found him watering a tree in the desert. Why, they asked. He said he had seen the Prophet ﷺ sit beneath it once, and he did not want it to die.

His care reached into the heart of it. There were hundreds of his reports that ended with the same quiet line: and Abdullah used to do this as well. If anyone ever wondered how the Prophet ﷺ had really done something, the answer was settled, because everyone knew this man never strayed from the sunnah. People began to pray that he would live long, so the way of the Prophet ﷺ would stay alive among them.

Is it not time for believers to humble their hearts to the remembrance of God and the Truth that has been revealed, and not to be like those who received the Scripture before them, whose time was extended but whose hearts hardened and many of whom were lawbreakers?Quran 57:16

Following the truth, even past his own father

His care for the sunnah was not blind copying. When someone praised a practice on the authority of his father, but Abdullah had heard the Prophet ﷺ say something else, he chose the Prophet ﷺ without hesitation. Is this religion built on following my father, he asked, or on following the Messenger of Allah ﷺ?

He loved his father deeply. He still chose the higher source. His own son Salim later did the same, and explained it simply: my father parted with his father when he heard the Prophet ﷺ say something, so this is how we were taught.

He was just as firm against going too far. He corrected a man who stretched and decorated the words of the call to prayer, and another who had picked up a habit the Prophet ﷺ never had. To him, holding to the sunnah meant not exaggerating in any direction, only walking the straight path the Prophet ﷺ had walked.

A son at his father's side

When Umar was stabbed, Abdullah was there. He stayed with his father through the long hours as he bled, and carried his final instructions. Umar asked him to count his debts and settle them, then sent him to Aisha (RA) with a request, told humbly: not from the leader of the believers, but simply from Umar, asking permission to be buried beside the Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr.

As Umar lay dying, some companions said the natural thing was to make his copy, Abdullah, the next leader. Umar refused outright. It is enough, he said, for one person from the family of al-Khattab to carry this. He would not let the position become an inheritance.

Their bond had never traded on privilege. As leader, Umar fixed a smaller pension for his own son than for the other early emigrants, because Abdullah had come to faith carried along by his parents rather than on his own. When a famine struck, Umar would not let his children eat a piece of fruit that the wider community could not have. That was the responsibility Abdullah was raised inside, and it stayed with him.

After his father was gone, people would look at Abdullah just to remember Umar. He carried the resemblance, the bearing, the love of fine scent, and above all the same plain sincerity that both men were known for.

Honouring a father by honouring his friends

One day in the marketplace Abdullah came across a poor bedouin man. He climbed down from his camel, took the turban from his own head and placed it on the man, told him to keep the camel too, and walked away on foot. People were puzzled. A few dates would have satisfied the man, they said. Why your turban and your mount?

Abdullah wept, as he often did, and explained. He had heard the Prophet ﷺ say that one of the finest acts of devotion to your parents is to keep ties with the people they loved after they are gone. This bedouin, it turned out, had once been a friend of his father. To honour him was to honour Umar.

He had his father's courage too. On one journey a lion blocked the road and the travellers froze. Abdullah walked up to it, took it by the ear, said something to it, and it turned and left. Those, the others said, were the kinds of things we only ever saw from his father.

Slaves freed, orphans fed, fitna avoided

He lived with very little and gave away a great deal. By the end of his life he had freed more than a thousand people from slavery with his own wealth. He would not sit down to a meal without an orphan beside him. He once said he had not eaten his fill in forty years.

He kept his intentions clean to a degree that is hard to imagine. He would not even drink a sip of water in a place where he had come only for the reward of prayer, in case it diluted why he was really there. When money came to him, he gave it away, sometimes the same day it arrived.

He loved to test, and reward, sincerity in others. On a journey he once asked a shepherd to sell him a sheep and then suggested the man could simply tell his master a wolf had taken it. The shepherd answered with the words that stayed with Abdullah: and what would I say to Allah? Abdullah found the man's master, bought the shepherd, and set him free. His own heart stayed soft. Once he held a cup of water, then began to weep, remembering that the people of the fire would long for nothing more than a sip of it.

When the community split into conflict, he stepped away from the fighting, but he was never unclear about where he stood. He confronted a tyrant to his face. In the end he was poisoned on the orders of a corrupt ruler. He died with three regrets only: the long days of fasting he would miss, the long nights of prayer, and the times he had not stood against oppression.

What Abdullah's life teaches us

His story is not built on one great battle or one famous speech. It is built on attention: the patient, daily care of a man who did not want to miss anything.

  • Love shows in the small things. He counted grey hairs and watered a tree. Real devotion lives in details most people would never notice.
  • Follow the truth, not the familiar. He loved his father and still chose the Prophet's ﷺ words over him. Loyalty to what is right comes before loyalty to habit.
  • Guard your intention. He would not even drink water if it might cloud why he had come. What we do matters, but so does the heart we bring to it.
  • Stand clear without being drawn into harm. He stepped back from fighting his fellow Muslims, yet no one ever doubted where he stood. Principle and restraint can live in the same person.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Umar?

He was the son of Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA) and a companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. He is remembered as one of the most devoted followers of the Prophet's ﷺ sunnah and one of the greatest narrators of hadith, with more than two thousand reports attributed to him.

Why is Abdullah ibn Umar so closely linked to the sunnah?

He followed the Prophet's ﷺ example in the smallest details and narrated his actions with great care. People used to pray for him to live long so that the way of the Prophet ﷺ would stay visible among them, and that prayer was answered: he was among the last of the major companions to pass away.

Did Abdullah ibn Umar ever become a ruler?

No. When his father was dying, some suggested making Abdullah the next leader, but Umar refused so the role would not become an inheritance. Abdullah himself never sought a government position and even declined to serve as a judge.

What can we learn from the life of Abdullah ibn Umar?

That devotion shows in small and steady care, that we should follow the truth even when it parts from the familiar, that intention matters as much as action, and that a person can hold firm to principle while keeping their hands clean of harm.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us will not be remembered for one dramatic act. We will be made, quietly, by the small choices no one sees: how carefully we keep a promise, whether we water the tree, whether we tell a story exactly as it happened. Abdullah ibn Umar built a whole life out of that kind of attention, and people loved him for it.

His example asks a gentle question of an ordinary day. What would change if we treated the small good as worth getting right, and guarded our hearts as closely as our deeds? Carry that into your prayer and your evening, and his story has done its work.
