The Companions

Jarir ibn Abdullah

The Yusuf of this Ummah

A tall man stepped into a crowded mosque in the last Ramadan of the Prophet's life. He had dressed in his finest clothes for the moment. The whole room turned to look at him, and the Prophet ﷺ, in the middle of speaking, paused.

This is the story of Jarir ibn Abdullah (may Allah be pleased with him): a Yemeni chief the Prophet ﷺ called the best of Yemen, who had barely forty days in his company, and who spent the rest of his life keeping one quiet promise.

The Companions

Jarir ibn Abdullah

The Yusuf of this Ummah

The chief who came late

Jarir was a leader of the Bajila, a cluster of tribes from Yemen. He was a man of standing among his people, though not one of the great political players of Arabia. His tribe had been fractured by long disputes, and he carried that worry with him.

Word of Islam had been spreading south along the road from Madinah to Yemen, especially after the Prophet ﷺ sent teachers there to call the people. Some chiefs came reading the wind, allying themselves with the rising power. Jarir was not one of them. He heard about Islam, and his own heart moved him to come.

He set out for Madinah with a hundred and fifty of his men. It was Ramadan in the final year of the Prophet's life, and the city was fuller than it had ever been. The walls of the mosque seemed ready to burst from the crowds gathered in those blessed nights.

The best of Yemen

Jarir was strikingly handsome. He was so tall that standing on the ground he could reach the hump of a camel, and he carried himself with a calm, imposing presence. He had dyed his beard with saffron, and when he entered a room, every eye turned to him.

Near Madinah he stopped, stepped aside, and prepared himself: he washed, put on his best clothes, and made himself ready to meet the Prophet ﷺ. Then he walked into the mosque while the Prophet was giving the sermon. The room fell silent and turned toward him. The Prophet ﷺ stopped speaking.

Jarir did not yet know whether this was good or bad. He quietly asked a man beside him whether the Prophet had said anything about him. The man told him yes: in the middle of the sermon the Prophet ﷺ had paused and said that the best of Yemen was about to walk through the door, and that his face was as if it had been touched by an angel.

It was a heavy thing to say. In that congregation were people of great standing. Yet the Prophet ﷺ, who had never met Jarir, was not praising a face. He was praising a heart and a character he already knew.

A cloak on the floor

The mosque was so crowded that no one moved to make room for the newcomer. The people held their places close to the Prophet ﷺ, unwilling to give up their nearness to him. They glanced back at Jarir, standing tall at the rear.

So the Prophet ﷺ took off his own cloak, folded it, and tossed it across to him. Sit on this, he told him, and come close. Jarir caught the cloak and held it to his chest. He kissed it and pressed it to himself, deeply moved. May Allah honour you, he said, as you have honoured me.

Then the Prophet ﷺ turned to the gathering and said: if a person of nobility comes to you, then show him honour. This was their first meeting. They had not yet spent a single hour together.

In the Prophet's home it was the same. He owned a single cushion in that room, and his habit was to give it to his guest and sit on the bare floor himself. He gave the cushion to Jarir, and Jarir sat on the ground. He did not arrive like a king, and the more he lowered himself, the more the Prophet ﷺ raised him in the gathering.

Beautify your character

As the delegations of the Arabs streamed into Madinah, the Prophet ﷺ would send for Jarir to be present, and would keep him close, honouring him before the visitors. But he also knew the danger that beauty and status can carry for a man, and so he gave Jarir an advice meant to last.

You are a man, he told him, whom Allah has made beautiful in your creation, so make your character beautiful too. The meaning was gentle and clear: if people are impressed by what they can see, let what Allah sees be more impressive still.

When the Prophet ﷺ took a pledge from a person, he would often add a line shaped to that person's life. From Jarir he took the pledge on one condition above the rest: that he would always have goodwill toward every Muslim.

The word is nasihah. It is sometimes flattened into giving advice, but its root is well-wishing: wanting good for your brother and your sister, and acting on it. Of all the companions, more stories of this quality are told about Jarir than anyone.

The price of a horse

That pledge was not a phrase he recited and forgot; he let it shape the way he lived. He never took more than his share of the spoils of war, though he led the caravans. I am just one of you, he would say. When charity came to him as a leader of his people, it was passed to the poor before he could even take it in.

Once Jarir sent a servant to buy a particular horse. The servant returned having bought it for four hundred dirhams, a clear bargain. Most people would say alhamdulillah and keep the discount.

Jarir did not. He had the servant take him back to the seller. You sold this for four hundred, he said, but it is worth more than that. Would you be pleased with five hundred? The man said yes. Six hundred? Of course. Jarir kept raising the figure until they agreed the horse was truly worth eight hundred, and he paid the difference.

The seller stood baffled. Who returns to pay twice what he was charged? Jarir explained: I gave the Prophet ﷺ my pledge of goodwill to every Muslim, and your horse is worth more than you asked. I did not want to take advantage of you. This was simply how he traded: if he was buying he put himself in the seller's place, and if he was selling he put himself in the buyer's. He carried the other person inside every dealing.

Forty days, and a lifetime after

The Prophet ﷺ sent Jarir on a mission to remove an old idol in Yemen and the indecent gathering that had grown around it, a sign of the days the Prophet had warned would return. Jarir was clumsy on a horse, too tall, his feet too large for the saddle, and he often fell. So the Prophet ﷺ placed a hand on his chest and prayed that Allah make him firm in the saddle, and make him guided and guiding. After that, Jarir said, he and the horse and the wind moved as one.

Jarir said the Prophet ﷺ never looked at him without smiling, and never once made him feel excluded, though Madinah was now bustling with people from across the world. He came to Jarir, and counted him among his own.

Then Jarir returned from another mission carrying good news, and learned that the Prophet ﷺ had died. He was crushed. He had been given barely forty days, some say up to ninety, yet even a week with the Prophet had felt like a lifetime. Among those few days he gathered over a hundred narrations, including the one many scholars rely on for wiping over the socks: a practice from the very end of the Prophet's life, which is part of why it carries the weight it does.

Carrying goodwill into a hard time

Jarir lived on through the years of Abu Bakr and Umar. He stayed close to Umar, who once told him: what a good leader you are. The praise was earned in small, telling moments.

In one gathering a man broke wind, loudly and by accident. The leader of the gathering kept asking, three times, that whoever did it should get up and make wudu, and the embarrassed man would not move. Jarir said simply: let us all get up and make wudu together. He covered one man's shame by spreading it across the whole room. Even that was his goodwill at work.

Under Umar he is remembered for helping establish the first postal stations, so the growing community could send word quickly and safely. When the Persians pressed hard, he led his people from Madinah to Iraq in under two weeks, a speed they traced back to the Prophet's prayer over his horse.

But when Muslims later began to fight one another, Jarir wanted no part of it. He withdrew rather than stand in that fire. I did not become Muslim for this, he said. He kept his pledge of goodwill to the end, and when he died in the year 51 after the Hijrah, the companions wept for the man the Prophet ﷺ had loved in so short a time.

What Jarir's life teaches us

His whole companionship was about forty days. What he did with the years afterward is the lesson.

  • Make your inside match your outside. Jarir was given beauty, so the Prophet ﷺ told him to beautify his character. What people admire fades; what Allah sees lasts.
  • Goodwill is a daily practice, not a feeling. He paid a seller more than was asked because the horse was worth it. He wished for others what he wished for himself, in every dealing.
  • Humility lifts you. The more Jarir lowered himself, the more he was honoured. He never walked in like a king, and he was treated like family.
  • Protect people's dignity. He covered one man's embarrassment by sharing it with the whole room. Sparing someone shame is its own quiet form of care.

Questions

Who was Jarir ibn Abdullah?

A chief of the Bajila tribe of Yemen who came to Madinah and accepted Islam in the final year of the Prophet Muhammad's life. The Prophet ﷺ called him the best of Yemen, and he is remembered as the Yusuf of this ummah for his beauty and character.

Why is Jarir called the Yusuf of this ummah?

Because of his striking beauty and noble bearing, which reminded the companions of the Prophet Yusuf. The Prophet ﷺ described his face as if it had been touched by an angel, and praised his character even more than his appearance.

How long did Jarir spend with the Prophet?

By most accounts only about forty days, some say up to ninety, near the end of the Prophet's life. In that short time he narrated over a hundred hadith and was treated as one of the family.

What can we learn from the life of Jarir?

To match our character to whatever Allah has given us, to make goodwill toward others a daily practice in our dealings, and to protect the dignity of the people around us.

Why this story stays with us

Jarir had every reason to coast on what people could see: his height, his looks, his standing among his tribe. Instead he took the one instruction the Prophet ﷺ gave him, to beautify his character, and built a whole life on it. He turned a single pledge of goodwill into the way he bought a horse, led an army, and sat in a gathering.

Most of us will never be called the best of anywhere. But the test Jarir passed is the same one we meet every day: in how we treat the person across the counter, how we cover a stranger's mistake, how we wish for others what we wish for ourselves. Carry one of those into today, and his story has done its work.
