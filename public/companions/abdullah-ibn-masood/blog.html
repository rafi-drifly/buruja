Abdullah ibn Mas'ud

The Voice of the Quran

He was a thin, short shepherd boy with no tribe to protect him, tending another man's sheep on the edge of Makkah. By the world's reckoning he counted for almost nothing.

This is the story of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with him): the sixth person to accept Islam, the first to recite the Quran aloud before the Kaaba, and a man whose small body the Prophet ﷺ said would weigh heavier than a mountain.

The Companions

Abdullah ibn Mas'ud

The Voice of the Quran

A boy on the margins of Makkah

Abdullah ibn Mas'ud came from the tribe of Hudhayl, not a tribe of Makkah but outsiders, counted among the Bedouin. His family had no standing in the city. His father had come to Makkah to care for people's livestock and had tied himself by allegiance to the clan of Banu Zuhra.

Abdullah followed the same path. He took up shepherding, one of the lowest jobs in the city, and lived the quiet life of someone with no place in Makkah's politics and no powerful name behind him. He was among the people the city called weak: the unprotected, the ones nobody had to answer for.

He was unusually short and very thin, so short that a man sitting down was nearly his height standing, and in a crowd you could lose him entirely. He had dark skin and the look of the desert. Yet he loved good scent and the cleanest white robes, and Allah had set something in his heart that the city could not see.

The sheep that gave milk

He was a boy of about thirteen, tending the flocks of Uqba ibn Abi Mu'ayt, one of the cruelest enemies of the Prophet ﷺ. One day two travellers approached him and asked for milk. Abdullah refused. The sheep were not his, he said, they were a trust, and it would not be honest to give from their milk.

The older traveller smiled and asked instead for a young sheep that had never given milk. Abdullah brought one. The man placed his hand on it, said Bismillah, and the udder filled. He drank, gave his companion to drink, and gave the boy to drink, and then the sheep returned to giving nothing at all.

Abdullah had never seen anything like it. "Uncle," he said, "teach me these words." The man, who was the Prophet ﷺ travelling with Abu Bakr, wiped his hand over the boy's chest and said, "You are a knowledgeable young man." Abdullah went and asked who these strangers were, learned the truth, and returned to declare that there is no god but Allah and that this man was His Messenger.

The first voice to carry the Quran

He became, by the well-known count, the sixth person to enter Islam, so early that he was nicknamed Sudus al-Islam, one sixth of Islam. And he drew close to the Prophet ﷺ in a way few ever did. He carried his sandals, woke him from sleep, guarded his privacy, and was trusted with his secrets, so close that some new Muslims thought he was a member of the household.

The early believers wanted the Quran recited openly at the Kaaba for the first time. They named the powerful men who might survive it. The last name anyone would choose was Abdullah ibn Mas'ud: no tribe, no protector, no body that could take a beating. He volunteered anyway. "Allah will protect me," he said.

He stood before the idols and began to recite Surah Ar-Rahman in a clear voice. The people of Makkah turned, realised these were the words of Muhammad ﷺ, and fell on him. They beat him until his face bled and he was left half conscious. When the believers pulled him out and asked why, he said the enemies of Allah had never seemed smaller to him, and that he wanted to go back the next day and do it again.

The Quran, fresh from the source

His bond with the Quran became the mark of his whole life. The Prophet ﷺ said that whoever wished to hear the Quran fresh, as it was revealed, should listen to the recitation of Ibn Mas'ud. He named him first among four from whom the Quran should be learned, and Abdullah himself said he had taken seventy chapters directly from the mouth of the Prophet ﷺ.

So central was he to this that, in the later books of knowledge, the name Abdullah on its own, without anyone after it, came to mean him. He used to say that whoever loves the Quran will love Allah and His Messenger, and he carried that love into every place he travelled, teaching it to students whose chains of recitation reach us to this day.

Once the Prophet ﷺ asked him to recite. Abdullah was startled: "Shall I recite to you, when it was revealed to you?" The Prophet ﷺ said he loved to hear it from someone other than himself. So Abdullah recited from Surah An-Nisa, until he reached the verse about the witnesses of the nations:

فَكَيْفَ إِذَا جِئْنَا مِن كُلِّ أُمَّةٍۭ بِشَهِيدٍ وَجِئْنَا بِكَ عَلَىٰ هَـٰٓؤُلَآءِ شَهِيدًاWhat will they do when We bring a witness from each community, with you [Muhammad] as a witness against these people?Quran 4:41

The legs heavier than a mountain

One day Abdullah was reaching up into a tree to gather a twig for the siwak, when the wind lifted his garment and bared his thin legs. Some of the companions laughed. The Prophet ﷺ saw the hurt on his face and would not let it pass.

"You laugh at his legs?" he said. Then he gave his answer, an oath: by Allah, those two legs are heavier on the scales on the Day of Judgement than the mountain of Uhud. The man the city had ignored, whose body the desert had worn thin, weighed more in the sight of Allah than a mountain.

The Prophet ﷺ had taught that on that Day a huge, imposing man may arrive and not weigh, with Allah, even the wing of a mosquito. Here was the opposite. Allah does not look at our shapes or our standing or our strength. He looks at our hearts.

The shepherd who finished Pharaoh

Abdullah was with the Prophet ﷺ through everything: the migration to Abyssinia and to Madinah, Badr, and the pledge of Ridwan. He had once been the shepherd of Uqba ibn Abi Mu'ayt, and he had seen the day Uqba threw filth on the back of the Prophet ﷺ while others laughed.

At Badr, Abu Jahl, the great tyrant of Makkah, lay struck down but still alive, a huge man, a mountain of a man. It was Abdullah, small and thin, who climbed onto his chest. Even then Abu Jahl sneered down at him: "You have climbed a difficult climb, you shepherd of sheep." Abdullah answered that victory belongs to Allah and His Messenger, and finished him.

His closeness held through the hardest days. At Uhud, when the Prophet ﷺ was struck and bleeding, it was Abdullah who carried to us the words the Prophet ﷺ said of the very people wounding him: "O Allah, forgive my people, for they do not know." He learned his prayer and his ablution from the Prophet ﷺ, and he used to say the Prophet ﷺ taught him the words of the prayer holding his hand between his palms, the same way he taught him a chapter of the Quran.

These were the people Makkah had told the Prophet ﷺ to send away before they would sit with him, the ones they called weak. Abdullah was the first name on that list. About the likes of him, Allah revealed His command to His Messenger:

وَٱصْبِرْ نَفْسَكَ مَعَ ٱلَّذِينَ يَدْعُونَ رَبَّهُم بِٱلْغَدَوٰةِ وَٱلْعَشِىِّ يُرِيدُونَ وَجْهَهُۥ ۖ وَلَا تَعْدُ عَيْنَاكَ عَنْهُمْ تُرِيدُ زِينَةَ ٱلْحَيَوٰةِ ٱلدُّنْيَا ۖ وَلَا تُطِعْ مَنْ أَغْفَلْنَا قَلْبَهُۥ عَن ذِكْرِنَا وَٱتَّبَعَ هَوَىٰهُ وَكَانَ أَمْرُهُۥ فُرُطًاContent yourself with those who pray to their Lord morning and evening, seeking His approval, and do not let your eyes turn away from them out of desire for the attractions of this worldly life: do not yield to those whose hearts We have made heedless of Our Quran, those who follow their own low desires, those whose ways are unbridled.Quran 18:28

A well people still drink from

When Abdullah finished reciting that verse, he looked up and saw the eyes of the Prophet ﷺ streaming with tears. Hudhayfah, who knew the Prophet ﷺ best, would later say that of all the companions Abdullah was the closest to him in guidance and conduct, and that the most upright among them knew he was among the nearest of all to Allah.

He once made a quiet supplication, and the Prophet ﷺ stopped to listen and told him, "Ask, and you will be given." Abdullah asked for faith that would not fade, blessings that would not be taken away, and the company of the Prophet ﷺ in the highest paradise. The Prophet ﷺ said, "Amen."

After the Prophet ﷺ passed, Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) loved and honoured Ibn Mas'ud, called him to his councils for advice, and once said he had given him to the people of Kufa over himself, though he could not bear to live without him. Through that, the schools of Quran and knowledge took root in Kufa.

He died in the time of Uthman (may Allah be pleased with him), before the trials he had feared could reach him. He was a shepherd from the edge of Makkah with no rank among men, and a supreme rank in the sight of Allah. We are still drinking from his well of knowledge today, in ways we rarely stop to notice.

What the life of Ibn Mas'ud teaches us

His story is the answer to a quiet fear most of us carry: that we are too small, too unnoticed, to matter. He shows that the world's measures are not God's.

  • Allah does not weigh you by your size. The man others overlooked, and even laughed at, was told his thin legs would outweigh a mountain. Worth, in the end, is measured in the heart.
  • Honesty holds even when no one is watching. A boy alone with another man's sheep would not give away a drop that was not his. That trust was the first thing the Prophet ﷺ saw in him.
  • Someone has to be willing to go first. When the strong measured the risk and stayed silent, the weakest man in the room stepped forward and said, Allah will protect me.
  • A love of the Quran shapes a whole life. He took it fresh from the Prophet ﷺ, carried it everywhere, and taught it to a generation. His nearness to it became his nearness to Allah.

Questions

Who was Abdullah ibn Mas'ud?

An early companion of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, from the tribe of Hudhayl. He was a shepherd of no standing in Makkah who became one of the first to accept Islam and one of the greatest authorities on the Quran among the companions.

Why is Ibn Mas'ud associated with the Quran?

The Prophet ﷺ said that whoever wished to hear the Quran recited fresh, as it was revealed, should listen to Ibn Mas'ud. He was the first to recite it aloud at the Kaaba, and he taught it to a generation whose chains of recitation reach us today.

How did Abdullah ibn Mas'ud become Muslim?

As a teenage shepherd he met two travellers who asked for milk. One placed his hand on a sheep that gave none, said Bismillah, and it filled. The traveller was the Prophet ﷺ. Abdullah sought him out and accepted Islam soon after.

What can we learn from the life of Ibn Mas'ud?

That Allah does not judge by size, wealth, or rank, but by the heart; that honesty matters most when no one is watching; and that a deep love of the Quran can shape an entire life.

Why this story stays with us

Most of us, at some point, feel like Abdullah on the edge of Makkah: small, unseen, sure that the people who matter are someone else. His life is a gentle correction. The same boy the city walked past was the one the Prophet ﷺ kept closest, the one whose legs would outweigh a mountain, the one chosen to be the first voice to carry the Quran into the open air.

You do not need a name people know or a place in anyone's councils. You need an honest heart that will not betray a trust, and the courage to do the right thing when others go quiet. Carry one of those into your day, and let the rest rest with Allah, who weighs what no one else can see.
