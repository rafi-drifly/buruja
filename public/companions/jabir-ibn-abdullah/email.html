The boy who was left with debts and a houseful of sisters

Preview: The night before Uhud, a father told his son he would not come home. What he asked of him stayed for a lifetime.

Assalamu alaikum,

The night before the battle of Uhud, a man woke his teenage son. "O my son," he said, "I have a feeling I will be among the first of the companions to be killed." Then he told him there were heavy debts to repay, and many sisters to protect. "Take care of my debts. Be good to your sisters."

By morning he was the very first man to fall.

The boy was Jabir ibn Abdullah al-Ansari (may Allah be pleased with him). When his father's body was carried back, mutilated by the enemy, the people tried to stop him from looking at it. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said, gently, "Let him see him." And when Jabir wept, he was not silenced.

Some time later the Prophet ﷺ found him still broken and asked him why. Jabir spoke of the debts, the sisters, the father he had lost. And the Prophet ﷺ told him a secret: Allah had spoken to his father with no veil between them, and offered him any wish at all.

His father did not ask for ease. He asked to be sent back to this life, to die for Allah a second time. He was told the dead do not return. So he asked only this: "My Lord, tell those I left behind how I am." And a verse came down in answer, that those killed in Allah's way are not dead, but alive with their Lord, with no fear and no grief.

Jabir was a frightened, grieving boy handed far too much. He did not become great in a single moment. He stayed. The Prophet ﷺ sat with him when creditors came, fed him, visited him when he was ill. Jabir went to nineteen battles and never left his side, and lived to carry more than fifteen hundred of the Prophet's sayings to us.

There is a quiet lesson in him that is easy to miss: faithfulness is mostly staying. Most of us are not asked for one heroic act, but for the long, unseen work of carrying the people and the duties left to us, day after ordinary day.

May Allah be pleased with him, and gather us in the company of those who stayed.
